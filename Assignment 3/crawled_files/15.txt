Clubs</b>, or simply the <b>American League</b> (<b>AL</b>), is one of two leagues that make up <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball" title="Major League Baseball">Major League Baseball</a> (MLB) in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> and <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>. It developed from the <a href="/wiki/Western_League_(original)" class="mw-redirect" title="Western League (original)">Western League</a>, a minor league based in the <a href="/wiki/Great_Lakes_region" title="Great Lakes region">Great Lakes states</a>, which eventually aspired to <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball" title="Major League Baseball">major league</a> status. It is sometimes called the <b>Junior Circuit</b> because it claimed Major League status for the 1901 season, 25 years after the formation of the <a href="/wiki/National_League" title="National League">National League</a> (the "Senior Circuit").\n</p><p>At the end of every season, the American League champion plays in the <a href="/wiki/World_Series" title="World Series">World Series</a> against the National League champion; two seasons did not end in playing a World Series (1904, when the National League champion <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Giants" title="San Francisco Giants">New York Giants</a> refused to play their AL counterpart, and 1994, when a players\' strike prevented the Series). Through 2017, American League teams have won 65 of the 113 World Series played since 1903, with 27 of those coming from the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Yankees" title="New York Yankees">New York Yankees</a> alone. The New York Yankees have won 40 American League titles, the most in the league\'s history, followed by the <a href="/wiki/Oakland_Athletics" title="Oakland Athletics">Philadelphia/Kansas City/Oakland Athletics</a> (15) and the <a href="/wiki/Boston_Red_Sox" title="Boston Red Sox">Boston Red Sox</a> (14).\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" data-file-height="800" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Teams_of_the_American_League_(2350728586).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Johnson">Ban Johnson</a>  (1864-1931), at age 39, in the third season of the League, 1903</div></div></div>\n<p>Originally a <a href="/wiki/Minor_league_baseball" class="mw-redirect" title="Minor league baseball">minor league</a> known as the <a href="/wiki/Western_League_(1885-1899)" class="mw-redirect" title="Western League (1885-1899)">Western League</a> which existed 1885 to 1899, with teams in mostly <a href="/wiki/Great_Lakes" title="Great Lakes">Great Lakes</a> states, the newly organized Western League later developed into a rival major league after the previous <a href="/wiki/American_Association_(19th_century)" title="American Association (19th century)">American Association</a> (1882-1891) disbanded after ten seasons as a competitor to the older <a href="/wiki/National_League" title="National League">National League of Professional Baseball Clubs</a> (usually known as the <a href="/wiki/National_League" title="National League">National League</a>) which was founded in 1876. In its early history of the late 1880s, the minor Western League struggled until 1894, when <a href="/wiki/Ban_Johnson" title="Ban Johnson">Ban Johnson</a> (1864-1931) became the president of the league. Johnson led the Western League into elevation   as claiming major league status and soon became the president of the newly renamed American League of Professional Baseball Clubs (also simply called the American League) in 1901. <a href="/wiki/Babe_Ruth" title="Babe Ruth">George Herman ("Babe") Ruth</a> (1895-1948), noted as one of the most prolific hitters in <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball" title="Major League Baseball">Major League Baseball</a> history, spent the majority of his career in the American League with the <a href="/wiki/Boston_Red_Sox" title="Boston Red Sox">Boston Red Sox</a> and the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Yankees" title="New York Yankees">New York Yankees</a> (plus his first year with his hometown team, the <a href="/wiki/Baltimore_Orioles_(minor_league)" title="Baltimore Orioles (minor league)">Baltimore Orioles</a> of the minor level <a href="/wiki/International_League" title="International League">International League</a>). The American League has one notable difference versus the rival <a href="/wiki/National_League" title="National League">National League</a>, in that in modern times since 1973 it has had the <a href="/wiki/Designated_hitter" title="Designated hitter">designated hitter</a> rule. Under the rule, a team may use a <a href="/wiki/Batter_(baseball)" class="mw-redirect" title="Batter (baseball)">batter</a> in its <a href="/wiki/Batting_order_(baseball)" title="Batting order (baseball)">lineup</a> who is not in the field defensively, replacing the <a href="/wiki/Pitcher" title="Pitcher">pitcher</a> in the batting order, compared to the old rule that made it mandatory for the pitcher to bat. In the last two decades, the season schedule has allowed occasional interleague play.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Walter_Johnson_and_Calvin_Coolidge_shake_hands_FINAL.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/Walter_Johnson_and_Calvin_Coolidge_shake_hands_FINAL.jpg/220px-Walter_Johnson_and_Calvin_Coolidge_shake_hands_FINAL.jpg" width="220" height="91" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/Walter_Johnson_and_Calvin_Coolidge_shake_hands_FINAL.jpg/330px-Walter_Johnson_and_Calvin_Coolidge_shake_hands_FINAL.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/Walter_Johnson_and_Calvin_Coolidge_shake_hands_FINAL.jpg/440px-Walter_Johnson_and_Calvin_Coolidge_shake_hands_FINAL.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4549" data-file-height="1873" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Walter_Johnson_and_Calvin_Coolidge_shake_hands_FINAL.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>30th President <a href="/wiki/Calvin_Coolidge" title="Calvin Coolidge">Calvin Coolidge</a> (1872-1933, served 1923-1929), and <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Washington_Senators_(1901%E2%80%9360)" title="History of the Washington Senators (1901\xe2\x80\x9360)">Washington Senators</a> pitcher <a href="/wiki/Walter_Johnson" title="Walter Johnson">Walter Johnson</a> (1887-1946, played 1907-1927, managed 1929-1932 for Senators; managed Cleveland Indians 1933-1935), shake hands following the Senators\' 1924 American League championship at <a href="/wiki/Griffith_Stadium" title="Griffith Stadium">Griffith Stadium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a></div></div></div>\n<p>Until the late 1970s, league umpires working behind home plate wore large, balloon-style chest protectors worn outside the shirt or coat, while their brethren in the National League wore chest protectors inside the shirt or coat. In 1977, new umpires (including <a href="/wiki/Steve_Palermo" title="Steve Palermo">Steve Palermo</a>) had to wear the inside chest protector, although those on staff wearing the outside protector could continue to do so. Most umpires made the switch to the inside protector, led by <a href="/wiki/Don_Denkinger" title="Don Denkinger">Don Denkinger</a> in 1975 and <a href="/wiki/Jim_Evans_(umpire)" title="Jim Evans (umpire)">Jim Evans</a> the next year, although several did not, including <a href="/wiki/Bill_Haller" title="Bill Haller">Bill Haller</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lou_DiMuro" title="Lou DiMuro">Lou DiMuro</a>, <a href="/wiki/George_Maloney" title="George Maloney">George Maloney</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Jerry_Neudecker" title="Jerry Neudecker">Jerry Neudecker</a>, who became the last MLB umpire to use the outside protector in 1985.\n</p><p>In 1994, the league, along with the National League, reorganized again, into three divisions (East, West, and <a href="/wiki/American_League_Central" title="American League Central">Central</a>) and added a third round to the playoffs in the form of the <a href="/wiki/American_League_Division_Series" title="American League Division Series">American League Division Series</a>, with the best second-place team advancing to the playoffs as a wild-card team, in addition to the three divisional champions. In 1998, the newly franchised <a href="/wiki/Tampa_Bay_Rays" title="Tampa Bay Rays">Tampa Bay Devil Rays</a> joined the league, and the <a href="/wiki/Arizona_Diamondbacks" title="Arizona Diamondbacks">Arizona Diamondbacks</a> joined the National League: i.e., each league each added a fifteenth team. An odd number of teams per league meant that at least one team in each league would have to be idle on any given day, or alternatively that odd team out would have had to play an <a href="/wiki/Interleague_play" title="Interleague play">interleague game</a> against its counterpart in the other league. The initial plan was to have three five-team divisions per league with inter league play year-round\xe2\x80\x94possibly as many as 30 interleague games per team each year. For various reasons, it soon seemed more practical to have an even number of teams in both leagues. The <a href="/wiki/Milwaukee_Brewers" title="Milwaukee Brewers">Milwaukee Brewers</a> agreed to change leagues, moving from the AL Central to the NL Central. At the same time, the <a href="/wiki/Detroit_Tigers" title="Detroit Tigers">Detroit Tigers</a> were moved from the AL East to the AL Central, making room for the <a href="/wiki/Tampa_Bay_Rays" title="Tampa Bay Rays">Devil Rays</a> in the East. Following the move of the <a href="/wiki/Houston_Astros" title="Houston Astros">Houston Astros</a>, which had been in the NL for 51 years since beginning as an expansion team in 1962, to the American League in 2013, both leagues now consist of 15 teams, a far cry from their original 8 for the first half-century of the <a href="/wiki/20th_century" title="20th century">20th century</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Permanent_Interleague_play">Permanent Interleague play</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_League&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Permanent Interleague play">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>For the first 96 years, American League teams faced their National League counterparts only in exhibition games or in the World Series. Beginning in 1997, interleague games have been played during the regular season and count in the standings. As part of the agreement instituting interleague play, the designated-hitter rule is used only in games where the American League team is the home team.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Teams">Teams</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_League&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Teams">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Charter_franchises">Charter franchises</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_League&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Charter franchises">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>There were eight charter teams in 1901, the league\'s first year as a major league, and\nthe next year the original <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_St._Louis_Browns" title="History of the St. Louis Browns">Milwaukee Brewers</a> moved to St. Louis to become the <a href="/wiki/St._Louis_Browns" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Louis Browns">St. Louis Browns</a>. These franchises constituted the league for 52 seasons, until the Browns moved to Baltimore and took up the name <a href="/wiki/Baltimore_Orioles" title="Baltimore Orioles">Baltimore Orioles</a>.
