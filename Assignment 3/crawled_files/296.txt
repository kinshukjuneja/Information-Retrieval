id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>\t\t\t\t<div id="contentSub"></div>\n\t\t\t\t<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>\t\t\t\t<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Not to be confused with <a href="/wiki/Content_management" title="Content management">Content management</a> or <a href="/wiki/Information_management" title="Information management">Information management</a>.</div>\n<p><b>Knowledge management</b> (<b>KM</b>) is the process of creating, sharing, using and managing the <a href="/wiki/Knowledge" title="Knowledge">knowledge</a> and information of an organisation.<sup id="cite_ref-Davenport_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Davenport-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> It refers to a multidisciplinary approach to achieving organisational objectives by making the best use of knowledge.<sup id="cite_ref-2UNC_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2UNC-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>An established <a href="/wiki/List_of_academic_disciplines" class="mw-redirect" title="List of academic disciplines">discipline</a> since 1991, KM includes courses taught in the fields of <a href="/wiki/Business_administration" title="Business administration">business administration</a>, <a href="/wiki/Information_systems" class="mw-redirect" title="Information systems">information systems</a>, management, library, and <a href="/wiki/Information_science" title="Information science">information sciences</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-37HBR_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37HBR-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-39Nonaka_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39Nonaka-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Other fields may contribute to KM research, including information and media, <a href="/wiki/Computer_science" title="Computer science">computer science</a>, <a href="/wiki/Public_health" title="Public health">public health</a> and <a href="/wiki/Policy" title="Policy">public policy</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17Mental_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17Mental-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Several universities offer dedicated <a href="/wiki/Master%27s_degree" title="Master&#39;s degree">master\'s degrees</a> in knowledge management.\n</p><p>Many large companies, public institutions and non-profit organisations have resources dedicated to internal KM efforts, often as a part of their <a href="/wiki/Strategic_management" title="Strategic management">business strategy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Information_technology" title="Information technology">IT</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Human_resource_management" title="Human resource management">human resource management</a> departments.<sup id="cite_ref-18Addicot_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18Addicot-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Several consulting companies provide advice regarding KM to these organisations.<sup id="cite_ref-18Addicot_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18Addicot-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Knowledge management efforts typically focus on organisational objectives such as improved performance, <a href="/wiki/Competitive_advantage" title="Competitive advantage">competitive advantage</a>, <a href="/wiki/Innovation" title="Innovation">innovation</a>, the sharing of lessons learned, integration and <a href="/wiki/Continuous_improvement" class="mw-redirect" title="Continuous improvement">continuous improvement</a> of the organisation.<sup id="cite_ref-16Gupta_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16Gupta-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> These efforts overlap with <a href="/wiki/Organisational_learning" class="mw-redirect" title="Organisational learning">organisational learning</a> and may be distinguished from that by a greater focus on the management of knowledge as a strategic asset and on encouraging the <a href="/wiki/Knowledge_sharing" title="Knowledge sharing">sharing of knowledge</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2UNC_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2UNC-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Maier_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Maier-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> KM is an enabler of organisational learning.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-1Sanchez_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1Sanchez-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Knowledge_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Knowledge management efforts have a long history, including on-the-job discussions, formal <a href="/wiki/Apprenticeship" title="Apprenticeship">apprenticeship</a>, <a href="/wiki/Internet_forum" title="Internet forum">discussion forums</a>, corporate libraries, professional training, and mentoring programs.<sup id="cite_ref-2UNC_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2UNC-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-1Sanchez_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1Sanchez-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> With increased use of computers in the second half of the 20th century, specific <a href="/wiki/Adaptation" title="Adaptation">adaptations</a> of technologies such as <a href="/wiki/Knowledge_base" title="Knowledge base">knowledge bases</a>, <a href="/wiki/Expert_system" title="Expert system">expert systems</a>, <a href="/wiki/Information_repository" title="Information repository">information repositories</a>, group <a href="/wiki/Decision_support_system" title="Decision support system">decision support systems</a>, <a href="/wiki/Intranet" title="Intranet">intranets</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Computer-supported_cooperative_work" title="Computer-supported cooperative work">computer-supported cooperative work</a> have been introduced to further enhance such efforts.<sup id="cite_ref-2UNC_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2UNC-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1999, the term <a href="/wiki/Personal_knowledge_management" title="Personal knowledge management">personal knowledge management</a> was introduced; it refers to the management of knowledge at the individual level.<sup id="cite_ref-48Wright_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48Wright-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the enterprise, early collections of case studies recognised the importance of knowledge management dimensions of strategy, <a href="/wiki/Business_process" title="Business process">process</a> and <a href="/wiki/Measurement" title="Measurement">measurement</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-25Booker_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25Booker-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-35Morey_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35Morey-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Key lessons learned include people and the cultural norms which influence their behaviors are the most critical resources for successful knowledge creation, dissemination and application; cognitive, social and organisational learning processes are essential to the success of a knowledge management strategy; and measurement, <a href="/wiki/Benchmarking" title="Benchmarking">benchmarking</a> and incentives are essential to accelerate the learning process and to drive cultural change.<sup id="cite_ref-35Morey_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35Morey-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> In short, knowledge management programs can yield impressive benefits to individuals and organisations if they are purposeful, concrete and action-orientated.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Research">Research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Knowledge_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>KM emerged as a scientific discipline in the early 1990s.<sup id="cite_ref-34JAS_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34JAS-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> It was initially supported by individual practitioners, when <a href="/wiki/Skandia" title="Skandia">Skandia</a> hired Leif Edvinsson of Sweden as the world\'s first <a href="/wiki/Chief_Knowledge_Officer" class="mw-redirect" title="Chief Knowledge Officer">Chief Knowledge Officer</a> (CKO).<sup id="cite_ref-6KU_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6KU-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Hubert Saint-Onge (formerly of <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Imperial_Bank_of_Commerce" title="Canadian Imperial Bank of Commerce">CIBC</a>, Canada), started investigating KM long before that.<sup id="cite_ref-2UNC_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2UNC-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The objective of CKOs is to manage and maximise the intangible assets of their organisations.<sup id="cite_ref-2UNC_2-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2UNC-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Gradually, CKOs became interested in practical and theoretical aspects of KM, and the new research field was formed.<sup id="cite_ref-10Bray_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10Bray-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> The KM idea has been taken up by academics, such as <a href="/wiki/Ikujiro_Nonaka" title="Ikujiro Nonaka">Ikujiro Nonaka</a> (<a href="/wiki/Hitotsubashi_University" title="Hitotsubashi University">Hitotsubashi University</a>), Hirotaka Takeuchi (Hitotsubashi University), <a href="/wiki/Thomas_H._Davenport" title="Thomas H. Davenport">Thomas H. Davenport</a> (<a href="/wiki/Babson_College" title="Babson College">Babson College</a>) and Baruch Lev (<a href="/wiki/New_York_University" title="New York University">New York University</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-37HBR_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37HBR-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27Davenport_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27Davenport-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 2001, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_A._Stewart" title="Thomas A. Stewart">Thomas A. Stewart</a>, former editor at <i><a href="/wiki/Fortune_(magazine)" title="Fortune (magazine)">Fortune</a></i> magazine and subsequently the editor of <i><a href="/wiki/Harvard_Business_Review" title="Harvard Business Review">Harvard Business Review</a></i>, published a cover story highlighting the importance of intellectual capital in organisations.<sup id="cite_ref-57Stewart_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57Stewart-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> The KM discipline has been gradually moving towards academic maturity.<sup id="cite_ref-2UNC_2-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2UNC-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> First, is a trend toward higher cooperation among academics; single-author publications are less common. Second, the role of practitioners has changed.<sup id="cite_ref-10Bray_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10Bray-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Their contribution to academic research declined from 30% of overall contributions up to 2002, to only 10% by 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-44Serenko_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44Serenko-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Third, the number of academic knowledge management journals has been steadily growing, currently reaching 27 outlets.<sup id="cite_ref-45Serenko_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45Serenko-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Multiple KM disciplines exist; approaches vary by author and school.<sup id="cite_ref-10Bray_16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10Bray-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-51PPH_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51PPH-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> As the discipline matured, academic debates increased regarding <a href="/wiki/Theory" title="Theory">theory</a> and practice, including:\n</p>\n<ul><li><i>Techno-centric</i> with a focus on technology, ideally those that enhance <a href="/wiki/Knowledge_sharing" title="Knowledge sharing">knowledge sharing</a> and creation.<sup id="cite_ref-19Harv_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19Harv-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-54Springer_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54Springer-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li><i>Organisational</i> with a focus on how an organisation can be designed to facilitate knowledge processes best.<sup id="cite_ref-18Addicot_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18Addicot-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li><i><a href="/wiki/Knowledge_ecosystem" title="Knowledge ecosystem">Ecological</a></i> with a focus on the interaction of people, <a href="/wiki/Identity_(social_science)" title="Identity (social science)">identity</a>, knowledge, and environmental factors as a <a href="/wiki/Complex_adaptive_system" title="Complex adaptive system">complex adaptive system</a> akin to a natural <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystem</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8Bray_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8Bray-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-53rst_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53rst-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>\n<p>Regardless of the <a href="/wiki/School_of_thought" title="School of thought">school of thought</a>, core components of KM roughly include people/culture, processes/structure and technology.  The details depend on the  <a href="/wiki/Perspective_(cognitive)" class="mw-redirect" title="Perspective (cognitive)">perspective</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> KM perspectives include:\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Community_of_practice" title="Community of practice">community of practice</a><sup id="cite_ref-3Teacher_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3Teacher-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Social_network_analysis" title="Social network analysis">social network analysis</a><sup id="cite_ref-4Groth_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4Groth-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>intellectual capital<sup id="cite_ref-24Bontis_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24Bontis-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Information_theory" title="Information theory">information theory</a><sup id="cite_ref-34JAS_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34JAS-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6KU_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6KU-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Complexity_science" class="mw-redirect" title="Complexity science">complexity science</a><sup id="cite_ref-7Snowden_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7Snowden-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>constructivism<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9citeseer_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9citeseer-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>\n<p>The practical relevance of academic research in KM has been questioned<sup id="cite_ref-28Ferg_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28Ferg-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> with <a href="/wiki/Action_research" title="Action research">action research</a> suggested as having more relevance<sup id="cite_ref-21TLO_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21TLO-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> and the need to translate the findings presented in academic journals to a practice.<sup id="cite_ref-25Booker_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25Booker-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dimensions">Dimensions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Knowledge_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Dimensions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Different <a href="/wiki/Conceptual_framework" title="Conceptual framework">frameworks</a> for distinguishing between different \'types of\' knowledge exist.<sup id="cite_ref-1Sanchez_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1Sanchez-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> One proposed framework for categorizing the <a href="/wiki/Dimensions" class="mw-redirect" title="Dimensions">dimensions</a> of knowledge distinguishes <a href="/wiki/Tacit_knowledge" title="Tacit knowledge">tacit knowledge</a> and <a href="/wiki/Explicit_knowledge" title="Explicit knowledge">explicit knowledge</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7Snowden_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7Snowden-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> Tacit knowledge represents internalised knowledge that an individual may not be consciously aware of, such as to accomplish particular tasks. At the opposite end of the spectrum, explicit knowledge represents knowledge that the individual holds consciously in mental focus, in a form that can easily be communicated to others.<sup id="cite_ref-10Bray_16-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10Bray-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20Alavi_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20Alavi-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Knowledge_spiral.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Knowledge_spiral.svg/350px-Knowledge_spiral.svg.png" width="350" height="184" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Knowledge_spiral.svg/525px-Knowledge_spiral.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Knowledge_spiral.svg/700px-Knowledge_spiral.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="689" data-file-height="362" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Knowledge_spiral.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Knowledge Spiral as described by Nonaka &amp; Takeuchi.</div></div></div>\n<p>Ikujiro Nonaka proposed a model (<a href="/wiki/SECI_model_of_knowledge_dimensions" title="SECI model of knowledge dimensions">SECI</a>, for Socialization, Externalization, Combination, Internalization) which considers a spiraling interaction between <a href="/wiki/Explicit_knowledge" title="Explicit knowledge">explicit knowledge</a> and tacit knowledge.<sup id="cite_ref-38Nonaka_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38Nonaka-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> In this model, knowledge follows a cycle in which implicit knowledge is \'extracted\' to become explicit knowledge, and explicit knowledge is \'re-internalised\' into implicit knowledge.<sup id="cite_ref-38Nonaka_36-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38Nonaka-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Hayes and Walsham (2003) describe knowledge and knowledge management as two different perspectives.<sup id="cite_ref-11Hayes_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11Hayes-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> The content perspective suggests that knowledge is easily stored; because it may be codified, while the relational perspective recognises the contextual and relational aspects of knowledge which can make knowledge difficult to share outside the specific context in which it is developed.<sup id="cite_ref-11Hayes_37-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11Hayes-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Early research suggested that KM needs to convert internalised tacit knowledge into explicit knowledge to share it, and the same effort must permit individuals to internalise and make personally meaningful any codified knowledge retrieved from the KM effort.<sup id="cite_ref-18Addicot_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18Addicot-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-55RST_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55RST-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Subsequent research suggested that a distinction between tacit knowledge and explicit knowledge represented an oversimplification and that the notion of explicit knowledge is self-contradictory.<sup id="cite_ref-48Wright_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48Wright-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Specifically, for knowledge to be made explicit, it must be translated into information (i.e., <a href="/wiki/Symbol" title="Symbol">symbols</a> outside our heads).<sup id="cite_ref-48Wright_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48Wright-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>  More recently, together with <a href="/wiki/Georg_von_Krogh" title="Georg von Krogh">Georg von Krogh</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sven_Voelpel" title="Sven Voelpel">Sven Voelpel</a>, Nonaka returned to his earlier work in an attempt to move the debate about knowledge conversion forward.<sup id="cite_ref-39Nonaka_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39Nonaka-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>A second proposed framework for categorizing knowledge dimensions distinguishes embedded knowledge of a <a href="/wiki/System" title="System">system</a> outside a human individual (e.g., an information system may have knowledge embedded into its design) from <a href="/wiki/Embodied_knowledge" class="mw-redirect" title="Embodied knowledge">embodied knowledge</a> representing a learned capability of a human body\'s <a href="/wiki/Nervous_system" title="Nervous system">nervous</a> and <a href="/wiki/Endocrine_system" title="Endocrine system">endocrine systems</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-40Sensky_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40Sensky-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>A third proposed framework distinguishes between the exploratory creation of "new knowledge" (i.e., innovation) vs. the <a href="/wiki/Knowledge_transfer" title="Knowledge transfer">transfer</a> or exploitation of "established knowledge" within a group, organisation, or community.<sup id="cite_ref-11Hayes_37-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11Hayes-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12Bray_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12Bray-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> Collaborative environments such as communities of practice or the use of <a href="/wiki/Social_computing" title="Social computing">social computing</a> tools can be used for both knowledge creation and transfer.<sup id="cite_ref-12Bray_42-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12Bray-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Strategies">Strategies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Knowledge_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Strategies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Knowledge may be accessed at three stages: before, during, or after KM-related activities.<sup id="cite_ref-24Bontis_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24Bontis-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> Organisations have tried knowledge capture <a href="/wiki/Incentive" title="Incentive">incentives</a>, including making content submission mandatory and incorporating rewards into <a href="/wiki/Performance_measurement" title="Performance measurement">performance measurement</a> plans.<sup id="cite_ref-23MIS_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23MIS-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> Considerable controversy exists over whether such incentives work and no consensus has emerged.<sup id="cite_ref-16Gupta_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16Gupta-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>One strategy to KM involves actively managing knowledge (push strategy).<sup id="cite_ref-16Gupta_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16Gupta-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13Rathau_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13Rathau-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> In such an instance, individuals strive to explicitly encode their knowledge into a shared knowledge repository, such as a <a href="/wiki/Database" title="Database">database</a>, as well as retrieving knowledge they need that other individuals have provided (codification).<sup id="cite_ref-13Rathau_44-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13Rathau-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Another strategy involves individuals making knowledge requests of experts associated with a particular subject on an ad hoc basis (pull strategy).<sup id="cite_ref-16Gupta_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16Gupta-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13Rathau_44-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13Rathau-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> In such an instance, expert individual(s) provide <a href="/wiki/Insights" class="mw-redirect" title="Insights">insights</a> to requestor (personalisation).<sup id="cite_ref-7Snowden_30-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7Snowden-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Hansen et al. defined the two strategies.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> Codification focuses on collecting and storing codified knowledge in electronic databases to make it accessible.<sup id="cite_ref-Smith_2004,_p._7_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Smith_2004,_p._7-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> Codification can therefore refer to both tacit and explicit knowledge.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> In contrast, personalization encourages individuals to share their knowledge directly.<sup id="cite_ref-Smith_2004,_p._7_46-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Smith_2004,_p._7-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> Information technology plays a less important role, as it is only facilitates communication and knowledge sharing.\n</p><p>Other knowledge management strategies and instruments for companies include:<sup id="cite_ref-16Gupta_7-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16Gupta-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8Bray_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8Bray-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7Snowden_30-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7Snowden-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Knowledge_sharing" title="Knowledge sharing">Knowledge sharing</a> (fostering a culture that encourages the sharing of information, based on the concept that knowledge is not irrevocable and should be shared and updated to remain relevant)\n<ul><li>Make knowledge-sharing a key role in employees\' job description</li>\n<li>Inter-project knowledge transfer</li>\n<li>Intra-organizational knowledge sharing</li>\n<li>Inter-organizational knowledge sharing</li></ul></li>\n<li>Proximity &amp; architecture (the physical situation of employees can be either conducive or obstructive to knowledge sharing)</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Storytelling" title="Storytelling">Storytelling</a> (as a means of transferring tacit knowledge)</li>\n<li>Cross-project learning</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/After_action_review" class="mw-redirect" title="After action review">After-action reviews</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Knowledge_mapping" class="mw-redirect" title="Knowledge mapping">Knowledge mapping</a> (a map of knowledge repositories within a company accessible by all)</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Communities_of_practice" class="mw-redirect" title="Communities of practice">Communities of practice</a></li>\n<li>Expert directories (to enable knowledge seeker to reach to the experts)</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Expert_Systems" class="mw-redirect" title="Expert Systems">Expert systems</a> (knowledge seeker responds to one or more specific questions to reach knowledge in a repository)</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Best_practice" title="Best practice">Best practice</a> transfer</li>\n<li>Knowledge fairs</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Competence_management" class="mw-redirect" title="Competence management">Competence management</a> (systematic evaluation and planning of competences of individual organisation members)</li>\n<li>Master\xe2\x80\x93apprentice relationship, Mentor-mentee relationship, <a href="/wiki/Job_shadow" title="Job shadow">job shadowing</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Collaborative_software" title="Collaborative software">Collaborative software</a> technologies (<a href="/wiki/Wiki" title="Wiki">wikis</a>, shared bookmarking, blogs, <a href="/wiki/Social_software" title="Social software">social software</a>, etc.)</li>\n<li>Knowledge repositories (<a href="/wiki/Database" title="Database">databases</a>, <a href="/wiki/Enterprise_bookmarking" title="Enterprise bookmarking">bookmarking engines</a>, etc.)</li>\n<li>Measuring and reporting <a href="/wiki/Intellectual_capital" title="Intellectual capital">intellectual capital</a> (a way of making explicit knowledge for companies)</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Knowledge_broker" title="Knowledge broker">Knowledge brokers</a> (some organisational members take on responsibility for a specific "field" and act as first reference on a specific subject)</li></ul>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Motivations">Motivations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Knowledge_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Motivations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Multiple <a href="/wiki/Motivation" title="Motivation">motivation</a>s lead organisations to undertake KM.<sup id="cite_ref-20Alavi_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20Alavi-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> Typical considerations include:<sup id="cite_ref-7Snowden_30-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7Snowden-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<ul><li>Making available increased knowledge content in the <a href="/wiki/New_product_development" title="New product development">development</a> and provision of <a href="/wiki/Product_(business)" title="Product (business)">products</a> and <a href="/wiki/Service_(economics)" title="Service (economics)">services</a></li>\n<li>Achieving shorter development cycles</li>\n<li>Facilitating and managing innovation and organisational learning</li>\n<li>Leveraging <a href="/wiki/Expert" title="Expert">expertise</a>s across the organisation</li>\n<li>Increasing <a href="/wiki/Social_network" title="Social network">network</a> <a href="/wiki/Interconnectivity" title="Interconnectivity">connectivity</a> between internal and external individuals</li>\n<li>Managing business environments and allowing employees to obtain relevant insights and <a href="/wiki/Idea" title="Idea">ideas</a> appropriate to their work</li>\n<li>Solving intractable or <a href="/wiki/Wicked_problem" title="Wicked problem">wicked problems</a></li>\n<li>Managing intellectual capital and assets in the workforce (such as the expertise and <a href="/wiki/Procedural_knowledge" title="Procedural knowledge">know-how</a> possessed by key individuals or stored in repositories)</li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="KM_technologies">KM technologies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Knowledge_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: KM technologies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Knowledge management (KM) technology can be categorised:\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Groupware" class="mw-redirect" title="Groupware">Groupware</a>\xe2\x80\x94Software that facilitates collaboration and sharing of organisational information. One of the earliest successful products in this category was <a href="/wiki/Lotus_Notes" class="mw-redirect" title="Lotus Notes">Lotus Notes</a>: it provided tools for <a href="/wiki/Threaded_discussion" class="mw-redirect" title="Threaded discussion">threaded discussions</a>, <a href="/wiki/Document_sharing" class="mw-redirect" title="Document sharing">document sharing</a>, organisation-wide uniform email, etc.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Workflow_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Workflow system">Workflow systems</a>\xe2\x80\x94Systems that allow the representation of processes associated with the creation, use and maintenance of organisational knowledge. For example, the process to create and utilise forms and documents.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Content_management" title="Content management">Content management</a> and <a href="/wiki/Document_management" class="mw-redirect" title="Document management">document management</a> systems\xe2\x80\x94Software systems that automate the process of creating web content and/or documents. Roles such as editors, graphic designers, writers and producers can be explicitly modeled along with the tasks in the process and validation criteria. Commercial vendors started either to support documents (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Documentum" title="Documentum">Documentum</a>) or to support web content (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Interwoven" class="mw-redirect" title="Interwoven">Interwoven</a>) but as the Internet grew these functions merged and vendors now perform both functions.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Enterprise_portal" title="Enterprise portal">Enterprise portals</a>\xe2\x80\x94Software that aggregates information across the entire organisation or for groups such as project teams (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_SharePoint" class="mw-redirect" title="Microsoft SharePoint">Microsoft SharePoint</a>).</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/ELearning" class="mw-redirect" title="ELearning">eLearning</a>\xe2\x80\x94Software that enables organisations to create customised training and education. This can include lesson plans, monitoring progress and online classes.</li>\n<li>Planning and <a href="/wiki/Appointment_scheduling_software" title="Appointment scheduling software">scheduling software</a>\xe2\x80\x94Software that automates schedule creation and maintenance (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Outlook" title="Microsoft Outlook">Microsoft Outlook</a>). The planning aspect can integrate with <a href="/wiki/Project_management_software" title="Project management software">project management software</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Project" title="Microsoft Project">Microsoft Project</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-19Harv_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19Harv-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Telepresence" title="Telepresence">Telepresence</a>\xe2\x80\x94Software that enables individuals to have virtual "face-to-face" meetings without assembling at one location. Videoconferencing is the most obvious example.</li></ul>\n<p>These categories overlap. Workflow, for example, is a significant aspect of a content or document management systems, most of which have tools for developing enterprise portals.<sup id="cite_ref-16Gupta_7-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16Gupta-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> \n</p><p>Proprietary KM technology products such as Lotus Notes defined proprietary formats for email, documents, forms, etc. The Internet drove most vendors to adopt Internet formats. <a href="/wiki/Open-source" class="mw-redirect" title="Open-source">Open-source</a> and <a href="/wiki/Freeware" title="Freeware">freeware</a> tools for the creation of <a href="/wiki/Blog" title="Blog">blogs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wiki" title="Wiki">wikis</a> now enable capabilities that used to require expensive commercial tools.<sup id="cite_ref-21TLO_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21TLO-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22Andrus_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22Andrus-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>KM is driving the adoption of tools that enable organisations to work at the semantic level,<sup id="cite_ref-26Cap_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26Cap-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> as part of the <a href="/wiki/Semantic_Web" title="Semantic Web">Semantic Web</a>:<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> for example, the <a href="/wiki/Stanford_University" title="Stanford University">Stanford</a> <a href="/wiki/Prot%C3%A9g%C3%A9_(software)" title="Prot\xc3\xa9g\xc3\xa9 (software)">Prot\xc3\xa9g\xc3\xa9 Ontology Editor</a>. Some commentators have argued that after many years the Semantic Web has failed to see widespread adoption,<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> while other commentators have argued that it has been a success.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_knowledge_management">Legal knowledge management</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Knowledge_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Legal knowledge management">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Knowledge management in law firms has evolved through three phases.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup>&#160; Phase one focused primarily on the development of taxonomies to systematize attorney work product and related research.&#160; The second phase focused on enterprise search to mine the growing volume of information managed by law firms.&#160; Legal industry specific search engines were deployed by many AmLaw 100 law firms during this phase.  Phase 3 was driven by changes in the legal market place and growing competition that led to price pressure and increased demands for efficiency from clients.&#160;A major focus of law firm KM today is in using historical billing information to generate alternative fee arrangements and more generally in the area of legal project management to more efficiently deliver legal services to clients.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
