<b>reserve currency</b> (or <b>anchor currency</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Currency" title="Currency">currency</a> that is held in significant quantities by governments and institutions as part of their <a href="/wiki/Foreign-exchange_reserves" title="Foreign-exchange reserves">foreign exchange reserves</a>.  The reserve currency is commonly used in international transactions, international investments and all aspects of the global economy. It is often considered a <a href="/wiki/Hard_currency" title="Hard currency">hard currency</a> or <a href="/wiki/Safe-haven_currency" class="mw-redirect" title="Safe-haven currency">safe-haven currency</a>. People who live in a country that issues a reserve currency can purchase imports and borrow across borders more cheaply than people in other nations because they do not need to exchange their currency to do so.\n</p><p>By the end of the 20th century, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">United States dollar</a> was considered the world\'s most dominant reserve currency.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The world\'s need for dollars has allowed the United States government as well as Americans to borrow at lower costs, granting them an advantage in excess of $100 billion per year.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reserve_currency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Reserve currencies come and go.  International currencies in the past have included the Greek <a href="/wiki/Drachma" class="mw-redirect" title="Drachma">drachma</a>, coined in the fifth century B.C., the Roman <a href="/wiki/Denarius" title="Denarius">denari</a>, the Byzantine <a href="/wiki/Solidus_(coin)" title="Solidus (coin)">solidus</a> and Arab <a href="/wiki/Gold_Dinar" class="mw-redirect" title="Gold Dinar">dinar</a> of the middle-ages, the Venetian <a href="/wiki/Ducato_(coin)" class="mw-redirect" title="Ducato (coin)">ducato</a> and the Florentine <a href="/wiki/Florin" title="Florin">florin</a> of the Renaissance, the seventeenth century <a href="/wiki/Dutch_guilder" title="Dutch guilder">Dutch guilder</a> and the <a href="/wiki/French_franc" title="French franc">French franc</a>.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Gold_Bars.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Gold_Bars.jpg/220px-Gold_Bars.jpg" width="220" height="140" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Gold_Bars.jpg/330px-Gold_Bars.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Gold_Bars.jpg/440px-Gold_Bars.jpg 2x" data-file-width="500" data-file-height="319" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Gold_Bars.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Under the <a href="/wiki/Gold_standard" title="Gold standard">gold standard</a>, the predominant <a href="/wiki/Global_financial_system" title="Global financial system">global financial system</a> from 1870 to 1914, <a href="/wiki/Banknote" title="Banknote">paper notes</a> were convertible into preset, fixed quantities of <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">gold</a>.</div></div></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Dutch_guilder" title="Dutch guilder">Dutch guilder</a> emerged as a <i>de facto</i> world currency in the 18th century due to unprecedented domination of trade by the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_East_India_Company" title="Dutch East India Company">Dutch East India Company</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-The_Euro_at_Ten_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Euro_at_Ten-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> However, the development of the modern concept of a reserve currency took place in the mid nineteenth century, with the introduction of national <a href="/wiki/Central_bank" title="Central bank">central banks</a> and treasuries and an increasingly <a href="/wiki/Globalisation" class="mw-redirect" title="Globalisation">integrated global economy</a>. By the 1860s, most industrialised countries had followed the lead of the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> and put their <a href="/wiki/Currency" title="Currency">currency</a> on to the <a href="/wiki/Gold_standard" title="Gold standard">gold standard</a>. At that point the UK was the primary exporter of manufactured goods and services and over 60% of world trade was invoiced in <a href="/wiki/Pound_sterling" title="Pound sterling">pound sterling</a>. British banks were also expanding overseas, London was the world centre for <a href="/wiki/Insurance" title="Insurance">insurance</a> and <a href="/wiki/Commodity_market" title="Commodity market">commodity markets</a> and British capital was the leading source of foreign investment around the world; sterling soon became the standard currency used for international commercial transactions.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:WhiteandKeynes.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/WhiteandKeynes.jpg/170px-WhiteandKeynes.jpg" width="170" height="214" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/WhiteandKeynes.jpg/255px-WhiteandKeynes.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/WhiteandKeynes.jpg/340px-WhiteandKeynes.jpg 2x" data-file-width="500" data-file-height="630" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:WhiteandKeynes.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/John_Maynard_Keynes" title="John Maynard Keynes">John Maynard Keynes</a> (right) and <a href="/wiki/Harry_Dexter_White" title="Harry Dexter White">Harry Dexter White</a> helped to draft the provisions of the post-war financial system. Here, they meet at the inaugural meeting of the <a href="/wiki/International_Monetary_Fund" title="International Monetary Fund">International Monetary Fund</a>, 1946.</div></div></div>\n<p>Attempts were made in the <a href="/wiki/Interwar_period" title="Interwar period">interwar period</a> to restore the gold standard. The British <a href="/wiki/Gold_Standard_Act_1925" class="mw-redirect" title="Gold Standard Act 1925">Gold Standard Act</a> reintroduced the gold bullion standard in 1925,<sup id="cite_ref-winnie_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-winnie-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> followed by many other countries. This led to relative stability, followed by <a href="/wiki/Deflation" title="Deflation">deflation</a>, but because the onset of the <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">Great Depression</a> and other factors, global trade greatly declined and the gold standard fell. Speculative attacks on the pound forced Britain entirely off the gold standard in 1931.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> \n</p><p>After <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, the international financial system was governed by a formal agreement, the <a href="/wiki/Bretton_Woods_System" class="mw-redirect" title="Bretton Woods System">Bretton Woods System</a>. Under this system the <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">United States dollar</a> was placed deliberately as the anchor of the system, with the US government guaranteeing other central banks that they could sell their US dollar reserves at a fixed rate for gold.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the late 1960s and early 1970s, the system suffered setbacks ostensibly due to problems pointed out by the <a href="/wiki/Triffin_dilemma" title="Triffin dilemma">Triffin dilemma</a>\xe2\x80\x94the conflict of economic interests that arises between short-term domestic objectives and long-term international objectives when a national currency also serves as a world reserve currency.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Global_currency_reserves">Global currency reserves</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reserve_currency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Global currency reserves">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The IMF regularly publishes the aggregated <i>Currency Composition of Foreign Exchange Reserves</i> (COFER).<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The reserves of the individual reporting countries and institutions are confidential.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Thus the following table is a limited view about the global currency reserves that only deals with allocated reserves:\n</p>\n<center><b>Currency composition of official foreign <a class="mw-selflink selflink">exchange reserves</a> (1965\xe2\x80\x932017)</b></center>\n<div style="overflow: auto;">\n<table class="wikitable sortable nowraplinks" style="">\n<tbody><tr>\n<td style="border-top:2px solid white; The argument is that, in the absence of sufficiently large shocks, a currency that dominates the marketplace will not lose much ground to challengers.\n</p><p>However, some economists, such as <a href="/wiki/Barry_Eichengreen" title="Barry Eichengreen">Barry Eichengreen</a>, argue that this is not as true when it comes to the denomination of official reserves because the network externalities are not strong. As long as the currency\'s market is sufficiently liquid, the benefits of reserve diversification are strong, as it insures against large capital losses. The implication is that the world may well soon begin to move away from a financial system dominated uniquely by the US dollar. In the first half of the 20th century multiple currencies did share the status as primary reserve currencies. Although the British Sterling was the largest currency, both the French franc and the German mark shared large portions of the market until the First World War, after which the mark was replaced by the dollar. Since the Second World War, the dollar has dominated official reserves, but this is likely a reflection of the unusual domination of the American economy during this period, as well as official discouragement of reserve status from the potential rivals, Germany and Japan.\n</p><p>The top reserve currency is generally selected by the banking community for the strength and stability of the economy in which it is used. Thus, as a currency becomes less stable, or its economy becomes less dominant, bankers may over time abandon it for a currency issued by a larger or more stable economy. This can take a relatively long time, as recognition is important in determining a reserve currency. For example, it took many years after the United States overtook the United Kingdom as the world\'s largest economy before the dollar overtook the pound sterling as the dominant global reserve currency.<sup id="cite_ref-Schenk_2009_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schenk_2009-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> In 1944, when the US dollar was chosen as the world reference currency at Bretton Woods, it was only the second currency in global reserves.<sup id="cite_ref-Schenk_2009_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schenk_2009-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/G8" class="mw-redirect" title="G8">G8</a> also frequently issues public statements as to <a href="/wiki/Exchange_rates" class="mw-redirect" title="Exchange rates">exchange rates</a>. In the past due to the <a href="/wiki/Plaza_Accord" title="Plaza Accord">Plaza Accord</a>, its predecessor bodies could directly manipulate rates to reverse large <a href="/wiki/Trade_deficit" class="mw-redirect" title="Trade deficit">trade deficits</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Major_reserve_currencies">Major reserve currencies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Global_Reserve_Currencies.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Distribution of global reserve currencies</div></div></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States_dollar">United States dollar</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reserve_currency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: United States dollar">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">United States dollar</a></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">United States dollar</a> is the most widely held currency in the Allocated Reserves today. Throughout the last decade, an average of two thirds of the total Allocated foreign exchange reserves of countries have been in US dollars. For this reason, the US dollar is said to have "reserve-currency status", making it somewhat easier for the United States to run higher trade deficits with greatly postponed economic impact or even postponing a <a href="/wiki/Currency_crisis" title="Currency crisis">currency crisis</a>. Central bank reserves held in dollar-denominated debt, however, are small compared to private holdings of such debt. In the event that non-United States holders of dollar-denominated assets decided to shift holdings to assets denominated in other currencies, there could be serious consequences for the US economy. Changes of this kind are rare, and typically change takes place gradually over time; the markets involved adjust accordingly.<sup id="cite_ref-Schenk_2009_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schenk_2009-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>However, the dollar remains the favorite reserve currency because it has stability along with assets such as <a href="/wiki/United_States_Treasury_security" title="United States Treasury security">United States Treasury security</a> that have both scale and liquidity.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The US dollar\'s dominant position in global reserves is challenged occasionally, because of the growing share of unallocated reserves, and because of the doubt regarding dollar stability in the long term.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> However, in the aftermath of the financial crisis, the dollar\xe2\x80\x99s share in the world\xe2\x80\x99s foreign-exchange trades rose slightly from 85% in 2010 to 87% in 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The dollar\'s role as the undisputed reserve currency of the world allows the United States to impose unilateral sanctions against actions performed between other countries, for example the American fine against <a href="/wiki/BNP_Paribas" title="BNP Paribas">BNP Paribas</a> for violations of U.S. sanctions that were not laws of France or the other countries involved in the transactions.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> In 2014 Beijing and Moscow signed a 150 billion yuan <a href="/wiki/Central_bank_liquidity_swap" title="Central bank liquidity swap">central bank liquidity swap</a> line agreement to get around American sanctions on their behaviors.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Euro">Euro</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reserve_currency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Euro">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/International_status_and_usage_of_the_euro" title="International status and usage of the euro">International status and usage of the euro</a></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Euro" title="Euro">euro</a> is currently the second most commonly held reserve currency, comprising about a quarter of allocated holdings. After World War II and the <a href="/wiki/Wirtschaftswunder" title="Wirtschaftswunder">rebuilding of the German economy</a>, the German <a href="/wiki/Deutsche_Mark" title="Deutsche Mark">Deutsche Mark</a> gained the status of the second most important reserve currency after the US dollar. When the euro was <a href="/wiki/Introduction_of_the_euro" class="mw-redirect" title="Introduction of the euro">introduced</a> on 1 January 1999, replacing the Mark, <a href="/wiki/French_franc" title="French franc">French franc</a> and ten other European currencies, it inherited the status of a major reserve currency from the Mark. Since then, its contribution to official reserves has risen continually as banks seek to diversify their reserves, and trade in the <a href="/wiki/Eurozone" title="Eurozone">eurozone</a> continues to expand.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Former U.S. <a href="/wiki/Federal_Reserve" class="mw-redirect" title="Federal Reserve">Federal Reserve</a> Chairman <a href="/wiki/Alan_Greenspan" title="Alan Greenspan">Alan Greenspan</a> said in September 2007 that the euro could replace the U.S. dollar as the world\'s primary reserve currency. It was "absolutely conceivable that the euro will replace the US dollar as reserve currency, or will be traded as an equally important reserve currency."<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Econometric analysis by Jeffrey Frankel and Menzie Chinn in 2006 suggested that the euro could replace the U.S. dollar as the major reserve currency by 2020 if either the remaining EU members, including the UK and Denmark, adopted the euro by 2020, or the recent depreciation trend of the dollar persisted into the future.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> In recent years, the euro\'s share of the worldwide currency reserve basket has continued to increase\xe2\x80\x94albeit at a slower rate than before the beginning of the worldwide <a href="/wiki/Credit_crunch" title="Credit crunch">credit crunch</a> related recession and <a href="/wiki/Eurozone_crisis" class="mw-redirect" title="Eurozone crisis">Eurozone crisis</a>, which harmed the euro and slowed its adoption.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> Since 2009, the reserve currency use of the euro has continued to drop, down to 23.9 percent in 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_reserve_currencies">Other reserve currencies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reserve_currency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Other reserve currencies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dutch_guilder">Dutch guilder</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reserve_currency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Dutch guilder">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Dutch_guilder" title="Dutch guilder">Dutch guilder</a> was the <i>de facto</i> reserve currency in Europe in 17th and 18th centuries.<sup id="cite_ref-The_Euro_at_Ten_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Euro_at_Ten-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pound_sterling">Pound sterling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reserve_currency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Pound sterling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The United Kingdom\'s <a href="/wiki/Pound_sterling" title="Pound sterling">pound sterling</a> was the primary reserve currency of much of the world in the 19th century and first half of the 20th century.<sup id="cite_ref-Schenk_2009_15-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schenk_2009-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The emergence of the USA as an economic superpower, and the establishment of the U.S. <a href="/wiki/Federal_Reserve_System" title="Federal Reserve System">Federal Reserve System</a> in 1913,<sup id="cite_ref-FDS-H-05_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FDS-H-05-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> and U.S. economic dominance from the second half of the 20th century onward, as well as economic weakness in the UK at various times during the second half of the 20th century, resulted in Sterling losing its status as the world\'s most important reserve currency: in the 1950s 55% of global reserves were still held in sterling; but the share was 10% lower within 20 years.<sup id="cite_ref-Schenk_2009_15-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schenk_2009-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Since 2006, with the exception of 2012, Sterling is the third most widely held reserve currency, having seen a resurgence in popularity in recent years, growing from about 2.5% to around 4% of all currency reserves.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> Analysts said this resurgence was caused by <a href="/wiki/Carry_trade#Currency" class="mw-redirect" title="Carry trade">carry-trade</a> investors considering the pound as a stable high-yield proxy to the euro, and by the position of \n<a href="/wiki/City_of_London" title="City of London">London</a> in world financial affairs.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Japanese_yen">Japanese yen</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reserve_currency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Japanese yen">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Japan\'s <a href="/wiki/Yen" class="mw-redirect" title="Yen">yen</a> is part of the <a href="/wiki/International_Monetary_Fund" title="International Monetary Fund">International Monetary Fund</a>\'s (IMF) <a href="/wiki/Special_drawing_rights" title="Special drawing rights">special drawing rights</a> (SDR) valuation. The SDR currency value is determined daily by the IMF, based on the exchange rates of the currencies making up the basket, as quoted at noon at the London market. The valuation basket is reviewed and adjusted every five years.<sup id="cite_ref-imff_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-imff-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The SDR Values and yen conversion for government procurement are used by the <a href="/wiki/Japan_External_Trade_Organization" title="Japan External Trade Organization">Japan External Trade Organization</a> for Japan\'s official procurement in <a href="/wiki/International_trade" title="International trade">international trade</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-jetro_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jetro-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Swiss_franc">Swiss franc</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reserve_currency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Swiss franc">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Swiss_franc" title="Swiss franc">Swiss franc</a>, despite gaining ground among the world\'s foreign-currency reserves<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> and being often used in denominating foreign loans,<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> cannot be considered as a world reserve currency, since the share of all foreign exchange reserves held in Swiss francs has historically been well below 0.5%. The daily trading market turnover of the franc, however, ranked fifth, or about 3.4%, among all currencies in a 2007 survey by the <a href="/wiki/Bank_for_International_Settlements" title="Bank for International Settlements">Bank for International Settlements</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Triennial-2007_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Triennial-2007-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Canadian_dollar">Canadian dollar</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reserve_currency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Canadian dollar">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>A number of central banks (and commercial banks) keep <a href="/wiki/Canadian_dollars" class="mw-redirect" title="Canadian dollars">Canadian dollars</a> as a reserve currency. In the economy of the Americas, the Canadian dollar plays a similar role to that played by the <a href="/wiki/Australian_dollar" title="Australian dollar">Australian dollar</a> (AUD) in the <a href="/wiki/Asia-Pacific" title="Asia-Pacific">Asia-Pacific</a> region. The Canadian dollar (as a regional reserve currency for banking) has been an important part of the British, French and Dutch Caribbean states\' economies and finance systems since the 1950s.<sup id="cite_ref-bankofcanada_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bankofcanada-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> The Canadian dollar is also held by many central banks in Central America and South America. It is held in Latin America because of remittances and international trade in the region.<sup id="cite_ref-bankofcanada_41-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bankofcanada-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Because Canada\xe2\x80\x99s primary foreign-trade relationship is with the United States, Canadian consumers, economists, and many businesses primarily define and value the Canadian dollar in terms of the United States dollar. Thus, by observing how the Canadian dollar floats in terms of the US dollar, foreign-exchange economists can indirectly observe internal behaviours and patterns in the US economy that could not be seen by direct observation. Also, because it is considered a <a href="/wiki/Petrocurrency" title="Petrocurrency">petrodollar</a>, the Canadian dollar has only fully evolved into a global reserve currency since the 1970s, when it was floated against all other world currencies.\n</p><p>The Canadian dollar, since 2013, is ranked 5th among foreign currency reserves in the world.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chinese_yuan">Chinese yuan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reserve_currency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Chinese yuan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Internationalization_of_the_renminbi" title="Internationalization of the renminbi">Internationalization of the renminbi</a></div>\n<p>Chinese yuan officially became a world reserve currency on November 30, 2015.  It represents 10.92% of the IMF\'s <a href="/wiki/Special_Drawing_Rights" class="mw-redirect" title="Special Drawing Rights">Special Drawing Rights</a> currency basket.<sup id="cite_ref-IMF_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IMF-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> This makes Chinese yuan the third reserve currency after the US dollar and Euro.<sup id="cite_ref-IMF_43-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IMF-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Calls_for_an_alternative_reserve_currency">Calls for an alternative reserve currency</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reserve_currency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Calls for an alternative reserve currency">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>A report released by the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Conference_on_Trade_and_Development" title="United Nations Conference on Trade and Development">United Nations Conference on Trade and Development</a> in 2010, called for abandoning the U.S. dollar as the single major reserve currency. The report states that the new reserve system should not be based on a single currency or even multiple national currencies but instead permit the emission of international liquidity to create a more stable global financial system.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Countries such as <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a> and the <a href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China" class="mw-redirect" title="People&#39;s Republic of China">People\'s Republic of China</a>, central banks, and economic analysts and groups, such as the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_Cooperation_Council" title="Gulf Cooperation Council">Gulf Cooperation Council</a>, have expressed a desire to see an independent new currency replace the dollar as the reserve currency.\n</p><p>On 10 July 2009, Russian President Medvedev proposed a new \'world currency\' at the G8 meeting in London as an alternative reserve currency to replace the dollar.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>China, Russia, India, Turkey, Brazil, Venezuela and other oil-producing countries have recently agreed "to transact all of their mutual trade and investment in their own currencies" effectively minimizing the need, at least in the short term, for a global reserve currency.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> Nevertheless, at the beginning of the 21st century, gold and crude oil were still priced in dollars, which helps export inflation and has brought complaints about OPEC\'s policies of managing oil quotas to maintain dollar price stability.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reserve_Currency_Backed_Tokens">Reserve Currency Backed Tokens</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span
