template">e</abbr></a></li></ul></div></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>The <b>Cabinet of the United Kingdom</b> is the collective decision-making body of Her Majesty\'s <a href="/wiki/Government_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Government of the United Kingdom">Government of the United Kingdom</a>, composed of the <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Prime Minister of the United Kingdom">Prime Minister</a> and 21 cabinet ministers, the most senior of the <a href="/wiki/Her_Majesty%27s_Government" class="mw-redirect" title="Her Majesty&#39;s Government">government</a> <a href="/wiki/Minister_(government)" title="Minister (government)">ministers</a>.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Ministers_of_the_Crown" class="mw-redirect" title="Ministers of the Crown">Ministers of the Crown</a>, and especially Cabinet ministers, are selected primarily from the elected members of <a href="/wiki/House_of_Commons_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="House of Commons of the United Kingdom">House of Commons</a>, and from the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Lords" title="House of Lords">House of Lords</a>, by the <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Prime Minister of the United Kingdom">Prime Minister</a>.  Cabinet ministers are heads of <a href="/wiki/Departments_of_the_United_Kingdom_Government" class="mw-redirect" title="Departments of the United Kingdom Government">government departments</a>, mostly with the office of "<a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_(United_Kingdom)" title="Secretary of State (United Kingdom)">Secretary of State</a> for [<i>function; e.g., Defence</i>]". The collective co-ordinating function of the Cabinet is reinforced by the statutory position that all the Secretaries of State jointly hold the same office, and can exercise the same powers.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Cabinet is the ultimate decision-making body of the <a href="/wiki/Executive_(government)" title="Executive (government)">executive</a> within the <a href="/wiki/Westminster_system" title="Westminster system">Westminster system</a> of government in traditional constitutional theory. This interpretation was originally put across in the work of nineteenth century constitutionalists such as <a href="/wiki/Walter_Bagehot" title="Walter Bagehot">Walter Bagehot</a>, who described the Cabinet as the "efficient secret" of the British political system in his book <i><a href="/wiki/The_English_Constitution" title="The English Constitution">The English Constitution</a></i>. The political and decision-making authority of the cabinet has been gradually reduced over the last several decades, with some claiming its role has been usurped by a "prime ministerial" government. In the modern political era, the Prime Minister releases information concerning <a href="/wiki/Cabinet_rank" title="Cabinet rank">Cabinet rank</a>; a list detailing the seniority of all Cabinet ministers.\n</p><p>The Cabinet is the executive committee of Her Majesty\'s <a href="/wiki/Privy_Council_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Privy Council of the United Kingdom">Privy Council</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> a body which has legislative, judicial and executive functions, and whose large membership includes members of the <a href="/wiki/Loyal_opposition" title="Loyal opposition">Opposition</a>. Its decisions are generally implemented either under the existing powers of individual government departments, or by <a href="/wiki/Order_in_Council" title="Order in Council">Orders in Council</a>.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" style="display:none" /><div class="toctitle" lang="en" class="toclevel-1 tocsection-11"><a href="#External_links"><span class="tocnumber">10</span> <span class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cabinet_of_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Until at least the 16th century, individual Officers of State had separate property, powers and responsibilities granted with their separate offices by Royal Command, and the Crown and the <a href="/wiki/Privy_Council" class="mw-redirect" title="Privy Council">Privy Council</a> constituted the only co-ordinating authorities. In England, phrases such as "cabinet counsel", meaning advice given in private, in a <a href="/wiki/Cabinet_(room)" title="Cabinet (room)">cabinet</a> in the sense of a small room, to the monarch, occur from the late 16th century, and, given the non-standardised spelling of the day, it is often hard to distinguish whether "council" or "counsel" is meant.<sup id="cite_ref-oedCabinet_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oedCabinet-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The <i><a href="/wiki/OED" class="mw-redirect" title="OED">OED</a></i> credits <a href="/wiki/Francis_Bacon" title="Francis Bacon">Francis Bacon</a> in his <i><a href="/wiki/Essays_(Francis_Bacon)" title="Essays (Francis Bacon)">Essays</a></i> (1605) with the first use of "Cabinet council", where it is described as a foreign habit, of which he disapproves: "For which inconveniences, the doctrine of Italy, and practice of France, in some kings\xe2\x80\x99 times, hath introduced cabinet counsels; a remedy worse than the disease".<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Charles_I_of_England" title="Charles I of England">Charles I</a> began a formal "Cabinet Council" from his accession in 1625, as his Privy Council, or "private council", and the first recorded use of "cabinet" by itself for such a body comes from 1644, and is again hostile and associates the term with dubious foreign practices.<sup id="cite_ref-oedCabinet_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oedCabinet-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Since the reign of <a href="/wiki/George_I_of_Great_Britain" title="George I of Great Britain">King George I</a> the Cabinet has been the principal executive group of British government.  Both he and <a href="/wiki/George_II_of_Great_Britain" title="George II of Great Britain">George II</a> made use of the system, as both were not native <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> speakers, unfamiliar with <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">British</a> politics, and thus relied heavily on selected groups of advisers. The term "minister" came into being since the royal officers "ministered" to the sovereign. The name and institution have been adopted by most English-speaking countries, and the <a href="/wiki/Cabinet_(government)" title="Cabinet (government)">Council of Ministers</a> or similar bodies of other countries are often informally referred to as cabinets.\n</p><p>The modern Cabinet system was set up by Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/David_Lloyd_George" title="David Lloyd George">David Lloyd George</a> during his premiership, 1916\xe2\x80\x931922, with a <a href="/wiki/Cabinet_Office" title="Cabinet Office">Cabinet Office</a> and Secretariat, committee structures, unpublished <a href="/wiki/Minutes" title="Minutes">minutes</a>, and a clearer relationship with departmental Cabinet ministers. The formal procedures, practice and proceedings of the Cabinet remain largely unpublished.\n</p><p>This development grew out of the exigencies of the <a href="/wiki/First_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="First World War">First World War</a>, where faster and better co-ordinated decisions across Government were seen as a crucial part of the war effort. Decisions on mass <a href="/wiki/Conscription" title="Conscription">conscription</a>, co-ordination worldwide with other governments across international theatres, and armament production tied into a general war strategy that could be developed and overseen from an inner "<a href="/wiki/War_Cabinet" class="mw-redirect" title="War Cabinet">War Cabinet</a>". The country went through successive crises after the war: the <a href="/wiki/1926_United_Kingdom_general_strike" title="1926 United Kingdom general strike">1926 United Kingdom general strike</a>; the <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">Great Depression</a> of 1929\xe2\x80\x9332; the rise of <a href="/wiki/Bolshevism" class="mw-redirect" title="Bolshevism">Bolshevism</a> after 1917 and <a href="/wiki/Fascism" title="Fascism">Fascism</a> after 1922; the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Civil_War" title="Spanish Civil War">Spanish Civil War</a> 1936 onwards; the invasion of <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Abyssinia</a> 1936; the League of Nations Crisis which followed; and the re-armament and resurgence of Germany from 1933, leading into <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">another World War</a>. All these demanded a highly organised and centralised Government centred on the Cabinet.\n</p><p>This centralisation enhanced the power of the Prime Minister, who moved from being the <i><a href="/wiki/Primus_inter_pares" title="Primus inter pares">primus inter pares</a></i> of the <a href="/wiki/H._H._Asquith" title="H. H. Asquith">Asquith Cabinets</a> of 1906 onwards, to the dominating figures of <a href="/wiki/David_Lloyd_George" title="David Lloyd George">David Lloyd George</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stanley_Baldwin" title="Stanley Baldwin">Stanley Baldwin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Winston_Churchill" title="Winston Churchill">Winston Churchill</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Composition">Composition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cabinet_of_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Composition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Cabinet ministers, like all ministers, are appointed and may be dismissed by the <a href="/wiki/British_monarch" class="mw-redirect" title="British monarch">monarch</a> without notice or reason, on the advice of the <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Prime Minister of the United Kingdom">Prime Minister</a>. The allocation and transfer of responsibilities between ministers and departments is also generally at the Prime Minister\'s discretion. The Cabinet has always been led by the Prime Minister, whose originally unpaid office as such was traditionally described as merely <i><a href="/wiki/Primus_inter_pares" title="Primus inter pares">primus inter pares</a></i> (first among equals), but today the Prime Minister is the preeminent <a href="/wiki/Head_of_government" title="Head of government">head of government</a>, with the effective power to appoint and dismiss Cabinet ministers and to control the Cabinet\'s agenda. The extent to which the Government is collegial varies with political conditions and individual personalities.\n</p><p>Any change to the composition of the Cabinet involving more than one appointment is customarily referred to as a <a href="/wiki/Cabinet_shuffle" class="mw-redirect" title="Cabinet shuffle">reshuffle</a>; a routine reshuffle normally occurs every summer. The total number of ministers allowed to be paid as "Cabinet ministers" is limited by the <a href="/wiki/Ministerial_and_Other_Salaries_Act_1975" title="Ministerial and Other Salaries Act 1975">Ministerial and Other Salaries Act 1975</a>, but there may be regular attendees at Cabinet who are not paid as Cabinet ministers. The number in addition to the Prime Minister is currently 21,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> total cabinet composition fluctuating between 21 and 23.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Cabinet_Secretary_(United_Kingdom)" title="Cabinet Secretary (United Kingdom)">Cabinet Secretary</a> does not have a political appointment such as Secretary of State and is not a member of the Cabinet, but is the professional Head of <a href="/wiki/Her_Majesty%27s_Civil_Service" class="mw-redirect" title="Her Majesty&#39;s Civil Service">Her Majesty\'s Civil Service</a>. (The Cabinet Secretaries of the devolved <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Government" title="Scottish Government">Scottish Government</a> are <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Ministers" class="mw-redirect" title="Scottish Ministers">Scottish Ministers</a>, unrelated to the U.K. Cabinet).\n</p><p>In formal <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Constitution of the United Kingdom">constitutional</a> terms, the Cabinet is a committee of <a href="/wiki/Her_Majesty%27s_Most_Honourable_Privy_Council" class="mw-redirect" title="Her Majesty&#39;s Most Honourable Privy Council">Her Majesty\'s Most Honourable Privy Council</a>. All Cabinet members are made Privy Counsellors shortly after appointment if not already Privy Counsellors, but only selected Privy Counsellors are appointed to the Cabinet or invited to attend. MPs and peers in the Cabinet use the style "<a href="/wiki/The_Right_Honourable" title="The Right Honourable">the Right Honourable</a>" (abbr. the Rt Hon.)<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Cabinet has come to be made up almost entirely of members of the House of Commons. The <a href="/wiki/Leader_of_the_House_of_Lords" title="Leader of the House of Lords">Leader of the House of Lords</a> is a member of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Lords" title="House of Lords">House of Lords</a>. Otherwise it is rare for a peer to sit in the Cabinet. The <a href="/wiki/Lord_Chancellor" title="Lord Chancellor">Lord Chancellor</a> was formerly the presiding officer of the House of Lords,  but since 2007 need not be a member of the Lords, and <a href="/wiki/Member_of_Parliament" class="mw-redirect" title="Member of Parliament">members of the House of Commons</a> have been appointed. Until the re-appointment to the cabinet of <a href="/wiki/Peter_Mandelson" title="Peter Mandelson">Lord Mandelson</a> on 3 October 2008, the old Leader of the Lords, <a href="/wiki/Valerie_Amos,_Baroness_Amos" title="Valerie Amos, Baroness Amos">Lady Amos</a>, was the last peer to sit in any other Cabinet post, as <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_International_Development" title="Secretary of State for International Development">Secretary of State for International Development</a> from May to October 2003. Before then, the last <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_(United_Kingdom)" title="Secretary of State (United Kingdom)">Secretary of State</a> for a major department drawn from the Lords was <a href="/wiki/David_Young,_Baron_Young_of_Graffham" title="David Young, Baron Young of Graffham">Lord Young of Graffham</a>, serving between 1985 and 1989 as <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_Employment" title="Secretary of State for Employment">Secretary of State for Employment</a> until 1987 and <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_Trade_and_Industry" class="mw-redirect" title="Secretary of State for Trade and Industry">Secretary of State for Trade and Industry</a> until 1989. The number of junior ministers who are peers has increased since 1997.\n</p><p>Occasionally cabinet members are selected from outside the Houses of Parliament and if necessary granted a peerage. <a href="/wiki/Harold_Wilson" title="Harold Wilson">Harold Wilson</a> appointed <a href="/wiki/Frank_Cousins" title="Frank Cousins">Frank Cousins</a> and <a href="/wiki/Patrick_Gordon_Walker" title="Patrick Gordon Walker">Patrick Gordon Walker</a> to the 1964 cabinet despite their not being MPs at the time. On 3 October 2008 Peter Mandelson, at the time of appointment not a member of either House, became <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_Business,_Innovation_and_Skills" class="mw-redirect" title="Secretary of State for Business, Innovation and Skills">Secretary of State for Business, Enterprise and Regulatory Reform</a> and was immediately made a <a href="/wiki/Life_peer" title="Life peer">life peer</a>. During the First World War, the South African politician <a href="/wiki/Jan_Smuts" title="Jan Smuts">Jan Smuts</a> served in <a href="/wiki/Lloyd_George" class="mw-redirect" title="Lloyd George">Lloyd George</a>\'s War Cabinet without ever becoming a member of either house of the British parliament.\n</p><p>There are some 100 junior members of the Government who are not members of the Cabinet, including <a href="/wiki/Ministers_of_State" class="mw-redirect" title="Ministers of State">Ministers of State</a> and <a href="/wiki/Parliamentary_Under-Secretary_of_State" title="Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State">Parliamentary Under-Secretaries of State</a>; and unpaid <a href="/wiki/Parliamentary_Private_Secretary" title="Parliamentary Private Secretary">Parliamentary Private Secretaries</a> are in practice apprentice ministers on the <a href="/wiki/Payroll_vote" title="Payroll vote">payroll vote</a>. Some of them may be Privy Counsellors, or may be appointed to the Privy Council as a mark of distinction, without becoming Cabinet ministers. Equally, some junior ministers below Cabinet level may be invited to all Cabinet meetings as a matter of course. The <a href="/wiki/Attorney_General_for_England_and_Wales" title="Attorney General for England and Wales">Attorney General for England and Wales</a> together with the chair of the governing political party, are customarily included, and other members of the Government can be invited at the Prime Minister\'s discretion, either regularly or ad hoc.\n</p><p>Since the end of the twentieth century more non-members of <a href="/wiki/Her_Majesty%27s_Government" class="mw-redirect" title="Her Majesty&#39;s Government">Her Majesty\'s Government</a> were permitted by the Prime Minister to attend Cabinet meetings on a regular basis, notably <a href="/wiki/Alastair_Campbell" title="Alastair Campbell">Alastair Campbell</a> in his capacity as Director of Communications and Strategy between 1997 and 2003, and <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Powell_(chief_of_staff_to_Tony_Blair)" class="mw-redirect" title="Jonathan Powell (chief of staff to Tony Blair)">Jonathan Powell</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Downing_Street_Chief_of_Staff" title="Downing Street Chief of Staff">Chief of Staff to the Prime Minister</a>, under <a href="/wiki/Tony_Blair" title="Tony Blair">Tony Blair</a>, with a distinctly separate role from the <a href="/wiki/Cabinet_Secretary" title="Cabinet Secretary">Cabinet Secretary</a>/Head of the Civil Service.\n</p><p>A photograph is taken of each new Cabinet in the garden or drawing room at 10 Downing Street.<sup id="cite_ref-Precedent_Book_1992_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Precedent_Book_1992-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Meetings_of_the_cabinet">Meetings of the cabinet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cabinet_of_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Meetings of the cabinet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Cabinet_table.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/The_Cabinet_table.jpg/220px-The_Cabinet_table.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/The_Cabinet_table.jpg/330px-The_Cabinet_table.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/The_Cabinet_table.jpg/440px-The_Cabinet_table.jpg 2x" data-file-width="450" data-file-height="300" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Cabinet_table.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Cabinet table</div></div></div>\n<p>The Cabinet meets on a regular basis, usually weekly on a Thursday morning notionally to discuss the most important issues of government policy, and to make decisions. Despite the custom of meeting on a Thursday, after the appointment of <a href="/wiki/Gordon_Brown" title="Gordon Brown">Gordon Brown</a> as Prime Minister the meeting day was switched to Tuesday.<sup id="cite_ref-Telegraph1_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Telegraph1-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> However, when David Cameron became prime minister, he held his cabinet meetings on Thursdays again.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The length of meetings varies according to the style of the Prime Minister and political conditions, but today meetings can be as short as 30 minutes, which suggests announcement or ratification of decisions taken in committee, by informal groups, or in bi-lateral discussions between the Prime Minister and individual colleagues, with discussion in Cabinet itself very limited. The Prime Minister normally has a weekly audience with the Queen thereafter.\n</p><p>The Cabinet has numerous <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_cabinet_committee" title="United Kingdom cabinet committee">sub-committees</a> which focus on particular policy areas, particularly ones which cut across several ministerial responsibilities, and therefore need coordination. These may be permanent committees or set up for a short duration to look at particular issues ("<a href="/wiki/Ad_hoc" title="Ad hoc">ad hoc</a> committees"). <a href="/wiki/Junior_minister" class="mw-redirect" title="Junior minister">Junior ministers</a> are also often members of these committees, in addition to Secretaries of State. The transaction of government business through meetings of the Cabinet and its many committees is administered by a small secretariat within the <a href="/wiki/Cabinet_Office" title="Cabinet Office">Cabinet Office</a>. Consequent <a href="/wiki/Order_in_Council" title="Order in Council">Orders in Council</a> are normally made by the <a href="/wiki/Queen-in-Council" class="mw-redirect" title="Queen-in-Council">Queen-in-Council</a> with a quorum of the Privy Council, which meets monthly or <i>ad hoc</i>.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Institute_for_Government" title="Institute for Government">Institute for Government</a> claims that the reduced number of full Cabinet meetings signify "that the role of Cabinet as a formal decision-making body has been in decline since the war."<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>. This view has been contradicted by <a href="/wiki/Vernon_Bogdanor" title="Vernon Bogdanor">Vernon Bogdanor</a>, a British constitutional expert, who claims that "the Cabinet has, in fact, been strengthened by the decline in full meetings, as it allows more matters to be transferred to cabinet committees. Thus, business is done more efficiently."<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Most Prime Ministers have had a so-called "kitchen cabinet" consisting of their own trusted advisers who may be Cabinet members but are often non-cabinet trusted personal advisers on their own staff. In recent governments, generally from <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Thatcher" title="Margaret Thatcher">Margaret Thatcher</a>, and especially in that of <a href="/wiki/Tony_Blair" title="Tony Blair">Tony Blair</a>, it has been reported that many or even all major decisions have been made before cabinet meetings. This suggestion has been made by former ministers including <a href="/wiki/Clare_Short" title="Clare Short">Clare Short</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chris_Smith,_Baron_Smith_of_Finsbury" title="Chris Smith, Baron Smith of Finsbury">Chris Smith</a>, in the media, and was made clear in the <a href="/wiki/Butler_Review" title="Butler Review">Butler Review</a>, where Blair\'s style of "sofa government" was censured.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Location_of_Cabinet_meetings">Location of Cabinet meetings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cabinet_of_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Location of Cabinet meetings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Cabinet meetings are usually held in the <a href="/wiki/10_Downing_Street#Cabinet_Room" title="10 Downing Street">Cabinet Room</a> of <a href="/wiki/10_Downing_Street" title="10 Downing Street">10 Downing Street</a>, the Prime Minister\'s official residence.\n</p><p>Cabinet meetings have also been held at <a href="/wiki/Chequers" title="Chequers">Chequers</a>, and in one case at the Grand Hotel, Brighton.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> In September 1921, under <a href="/wiki/David_Lloyd_George" title="David Lloyd George">David Lloyd George</a>, a Cabinet meeting was held at <a href="/wiki/Inverness" title="Inverness">Inverness</a> Town House in the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Highlands" title="Scottish Highlands">Scottish Highlands</a> to discuss <a href="/wiki/The_Irish_Problem" class="mw-redirect" title="The Irish Problem">the Irish Problem</a>, the only meeting outside England.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Parliamentary_accountability">Parliamentary accountability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cabinet_of_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Parliamentary accountability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>There are two key constitutional conventions regarding the accountability of cabinet ministers to the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Parliament of the United Kingdom">Parliament of the United Kingdom</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cabinet_collective_responsibility" title="Cabinet collective responsibility">cabinet collective responsibility</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Individual_ministerial_responsibility" title="Individual ministerial responsibility">individual ministerial responsibility</a>.\n</p><p>These are derived from the fact the members of the cabinet are <a href="/wiki/Members_of_Parliament" class="mw-redirect" title="Members of Parliament">Members of Parliament</a>, and therefore accountable to the House of which they are a member. The Queen will only appoint a Prime Minister whose Government can command the support of the House of Commons, which alone can grant <a href="/wiki/Confidence_and_supply" title="Confidence and supply">supply</a> to a Government by authorising taxes; and the House of Commons expects all ministers to be personally accountable to Parliament. In practice, Cabinet ministers will usually have a junior minister to represent their department in the House of Lords.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Cabinet_collective_responsibility" title="Cabinet collective responsibility">Cabinet collective responsibility</a> means that members of the cabinet make major decisions collectively, and are therefore collectively responsible for the consequences of these decisions. Therefore, no minister may speak against government decisions, and if a <a href="/wiki/Vote_of_no_confidence" class="mw-redirect" title="Vote of no confidence">vote of no confidence</a> is passed in Parliament, every minister and government official drawn from Parliament is expected to resign from the executive. Cabinet ministers who disagree with major decisions are expected to resign, <a href="/wiki/Robin_Cook" title="Robin Cook">Robin Cook</a> being a recent example over the decision to go to war with <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a> in 2003. The principle of collective responsibility is not impaired by the fact that decisions may be made in a <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_cabinet_committee" title="United Kingdom cabinet committee">cabinet committee</a> rather than by the full cabinet.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Individual_ministerial_responsibility" title="Individual ministerial responsibility">Individual ministerial responsibility</a> is the convention that in their capacity as head of department, a minister is personally responsible for the actions and failings of their department. Under circumstances of gross failure in their department, a minister is expected to resign (and may be forced to do so by the Prime Minister), while their <a href="/wiki/Civil_service" title="Civil service">civil servants</a> remain permanent and anonymous. This is relatively rare in practice, perhaps because administrative failure is of less interest to the media than personal scandal, and less susceptible to unequivocal proof. The closest example in recent years is perhaps <a href="/wiki/Estelle_Morris" title="Estelle Morris">Estelle Morris</a>, who resigned as <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_Education_and_Skills" class="mw-redirect" title="Secretary of State for Education and Skills">Secretary of State for Education and Skills</a> in 2002 following severe problems and inaccuracies in the marking of <a href="/wiki/Advanced_Level_(UK)" class="mw-redirect" title="Advanced Level (UK)">A-level</a> exams. The circumstances under which this convention is followed are not possible to define strictly, and depend on many other factors. If a minister\'s reputation is seen to be tarnished by a personal scandal (for example when it was luridly revealed that <a href="/wiki/David_Mellor" title="David Mellor">David Mellor</a> had an extramarital affair) they very often resign. This often follows a short period of intense media and opposition pressure for them to do so. In general, despite numerous scandals, in Britain known cases of serious corruption (e.g. acceptance of bribes) are relatively rare in comparison with many other democracies.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>Parliamentary Questions can be tabled for ministers in either house of Parliament for either written or oral reply. These may be "planted" questions for the advantage of the Government, or antagonistic questions from the Opposition, or may genuinely seek information. Cabinet ministers must respond, either themselves or through a deputy, although the answers do not always fully answer the question. Written answers, which are usually more specific and detailed than oral questions are usually written by a <a href="/wiki/Civil_servant" class="mw-redirect" title="Civil servant">civil servant</a>. Answers to written and oral questions are published in <a href="/wiki/Hansard" title="Hansard">Hansard</a>.\n</p><p>Parliament cannot dismiss individual ministers (though members or a House may call for their resignation, or formally resolve to reduce their salary by a nominal amount), but the House of Commons is able to determine the fate of the entire Government. If a <a href="/wiki/Vote_of_no_confidence" class="mw-redirect" title="Vote of no confidence">vote of no confidence</a> in the Government passes, then the Queen will seek to restore confidence either by a dissolution of Parliament and the election of a new one, or by the acceptance of the resignation of her entire government.\n</p><p>In the United Kingdom\'s parliamentary system, the <a href="/wiki/Executive_(government)" title="Executive (government)">executive</a> is not separate from the <a href="/wiki/Legislature" title="Legislature">legislature</a>, since Cabinet members are drawn from Parliament. Moreover, the executive tends to dominate the legislature for several reasons:\n</p>\n<ul><li>the <a href="/wiki/First-past-the-post" class="mw-redirect" title="First-past-the-post">first-past-the-post</a> <a href="/wiki/Voting_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Voting system">voting system</a> (which tends to give a large majority to the governing party)</li>\n<li>the power of the <a href="/wiki/Whip_(politics)" title="Whip (politics)">Government Whips</a> (whose role is to ensure party members vote in accordance with the party line)</li>\n<li>the "<a href="/wiki/Payroll_vote" title="Payroll vote">payroll vote</a>" (a term which refers to the fact that members of parliament of the majority party will wish to be promoted to an executive position, and then be on the government\'s payroll).</li>\n<li>collective ministerial responsibility requires members of the government to vote with the government on whipped votes, or else resign their position.</li></ul>\n<p>The combined effect of the Prime Minister\'s ability to control Cabinet by circumventing effective discussion in Cabinet and the executive\'s ability to dominate parliamentary proceedings places the British Prime Minister in a position of great power, that has been likened to an <a href="/wiki/Elective_dictatorship" title="Elective dictatorship">elective dictatorship</a> (a phrase coined by <a href="/wiki/Quintin_Hogg,_Baron_Hailsham_of_St_Marylebone" title="Quintin Hogg, Baron Hailsham of St Marylebone">Lord Hailsham</a> in 1976). The relative inability of Parliament to hold the Government of the day to account is often cited by the UK media as a justification for the vigour with which they question and challenge the Government. This is often controversial as some people believe that <a href="/wiki/It%27s_the_Sun_Wot_Won_It" class="mw-redirect" title="It&#39;s the Sun Wot Won It">Individual Media Corporations have had key influence upon certain components of British elections</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The classic view of Cabinet Government was laid out by <a href="/wiki/Walter_Bagehot" title="Walter Bagehot">Walter Bagehot</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/The_English_Constitution" title="The English Constitution">The English Constitution</a></i> (1867) in which he described the prime minister as the <i>primus\xe2\x80\x90inter\xe2\x80\x90pares</i> ("<a href="/wiki/Primus_inter_pares#Prime_minister_or_premier" title="Primus inter pares">first among equals</a>").<sup id="cite_ref-Fairclough_2002,_p._~23_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fairclough_2002,_p._~23-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The view was questioned by <a href="/wiki/Richard_Crossman" title="Richard Crossman">Richard Crossman</a> in <i>The Myths of Cabinet Government</i> (1972) and by <a href="/wiki/Tony_Benn" title="Tony Benn">Tony Benn</a>. They were both members of the Labour governments of the 1960s and thought that the position of the Prime Minister had acquired more power so that Prime Ministerial Government was a more apt description.<sup id="cite_ref-Fairclough_2002,_p._~23_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fairclough_2002,_p._~23-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Crossman stated that the increase the power of the prime minister resulted from power of centralised political parties, the development of a unified civil service, and the growth of the Prime Minister\'s private office and <a href="/wiki/Cabinet_Office_(UK)" class="mw-redirect" title="Cabinet Office (UK)">Cabinet secretariat</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Williams_1998,_pp._113\xe2\x80\x93114_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Williams_1998,_pp._113\xe2\x80\x93114-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Graham_Allen_(politician)" title="Graham Allen (politician)">Graham Allen</a> (a Government Whip during <a href="/wiki/Tony_Blair" title="Tony Blair">Tony Blair\'s</a> first government) makes the case in <i>The Last Prime Minister: Being Honest About the UK Presidency</i> (2003) that in fact the office of prime minister has presidential powers,<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> as did <a href="/wiki/Michael_Foley_(academic)" title="Michael Foley (academic)">Michael Foley</a> in <i>The British Presidency</i> (2000).<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> However the power that a prime minister has over his or her cabinet colleagues is directly proportional to the amount of support that they have with their political parties and this is often related to whether the party considers them to be an electoral asset or liability. Also when a party is divided into factions a Prime Minister may be forced to include other powerful party members in the Cabinet for party political cohesion. The Prime Minister\'s personal power is also curtailed if their party is in a power-sharing arrangement, or a formal coalition with another party (as happened in the <a href="/wiki/Cameron%E2%80%93Clegg_coalition" title="Cameron\xe2\x80\x93Clegg coalition">coalition government</a> of 2010 to 2015).<sup id="cite_ref-Williams_1998,_pp._113\xe2\x80\x93114_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Williams_1998,_pp._113\xe2\x80\x93114-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Fairclough_2002,_p._~23_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fairclough_2002,_p._~23-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline"
