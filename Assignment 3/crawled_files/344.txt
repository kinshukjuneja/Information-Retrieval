href="/wiki/Commander_of_the_Order_of_the_British_Empire" class="mw-redirect" title="Commander of the Order of the British Empire">CBE</a> (25 March 1908&#160;&#8211;&#32;16 April 1991) was an English film director, producer, screenwriter and editor, responsible for large-scale <a href="/wiki/Epic_film" title="Epic film">epics</a><sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> such as <i><a href="/wiki/The_Bridge_on_the_River_Kwai" title="The Bridge on the River Kwai">The Bridge on the River Kwai</a></i> (1957), <i><a href="/wiki/Lawrence_of_Arabia_(film)" title="Lawrence of Arabia (film)">Lawrence of Arabia</a></i> (1962), <i><a href="/wiki/Doctor_Zhivago_(film)" title="Doctor Zhivago (film)">Doctor Zhivago</a></i> (1965) and <i><a href="/wiki/A_Passage_to_India_(film)" title="A Passage to India (film)">A Passage to India</a></i> (1984). He also directed adaptations of  <a href="/wiki/Charles_Dickens" title="Charles Dickens">Charles Dickens</a> novels <i><a href="/wiki/Great_Expectations_(1946_film)" title="Great Expectations (1946 film)">Great Expectations</a></i> (1946) and <i><a href="/wiki/Oliver_Twist_(1948_film)" title="Oliver Twist (1948 film)">Oliver Twist</a></i> (1948), as well as the <a href="/wiki/Romance_film" title="Romance film">romantic drama</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Brief_Encounter" title="Brief Encounter">Brief Encounter</a></i> (1945).\n</p><p>Originally starting out as a film editor in the early 1930s, Lean made his directorial debut with 1942\'s <i><a href="/wiki/In_Which_We_Serve" title="In Which We Serve">In Which We Serve</a></i>, which was the first of four collaborations with <a href="/wiki/No%C3%ABl_Coward" title="No\xc3\xabl Coward">No\xc3\xabl Coward</a>. Beginning with <i><a href="/wiki/Summertime_(1955_film)" title="Summertime (1955 film)">Summertime</a></i> in 1955, Lean began to make internationally co-produced films financed by the big Hollywood studios; in 1970, however, the critical failure of his film <i><a href="/wiki/Ryan%27s_Daughter" title="Ryan&#39;s Daughter">Ryan\'s Daughter</a></i> led him to take a fourteen-year break from filmmaking, during which he planned a number of film projects which never came to fruition. In 1984 he had a career revival with <i><a href="/wiki/A_Passage_to_India_(film)" title="A Passage to India (film)">A Passage to India</a></i>, adapted from <a href="/wiki/E._M._Forster" title="E. M. Forster">E. M. Forster</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/A_Passage_to_India" title="A Passage to India">novel</a>; it was an instant hit with critics but proved to be the last film Lean would direct.\n</p><p>Lean\'s affinity for striking visuals and inventive editing techniques has led him to be lauded by directors such as <a href="/wiki/Steven_Spielberg" title="Steven Spielberg">Steven Spielberg</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Stanley_Kubrick" title="Stanley Kubrick">Stanley Kubrick</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Martin_Scorsese" title="Martin Scorsese">Martin Scorsese</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Collins_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Collins-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Ridley_Scott" title="Ridley Scott">Ridley Scott</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Lean was voted 9th greatest film director of all time in the <a href="/wiki/British_Film_Institute" title="British Film Institute">British Film Institute</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Sight_%26_Sound" title="Sight &amp; Sound">Sight &amp; Sound</a></i> "Directors\' Top Directors" poll in 2002.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Nominated seven times for the <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Director" title="Academy Award for Best Director">Academy Award for Best Director</a>, which he won twice for <i>The Bridge on the River Kwai</i> and <i>Lawrence of Arabia</i>, he has seven films in the <a href="/wiki/British_Film_Institute" title="British Film Institute">British Film Institute</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/BFI_Top_100_British_films" title="BFI Top 100 British films">Top 100 British Films</a> (with three of them being in the top five)<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> and was awarded the <a href="/wiki/AFI_Life_Achievement_Award" title="AFI Life Achievement Award">AFI Life Achievement Award</a> in 1990.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Lean&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Lean was born at 38 Blenheim Crescent, South Croydon, Surrey (now part of <a href="/wiki/Greater_London" title="Greater London">Greater London</a>), to Francis William le Blount Lean and the former Helena Tangye (niece of <a href="/wiki/Richard_Tangye" title="Richard Tangye">Sir Richard Trevithick Tangye</a>). His parents were <a href="/wiki/Quaker" class="mw-redirect" title="Quaker">Quakers</a> and he was a pupil at the Quaker-founded <a href="/wiki/Leighton_Park_School" title="Leighton Park School">Leighton Park School</a> in <a href="/wiki/Reading,_Berkshire" title="Reading, Berkshire">Reading</a>. His younger brother, <a href="/wiki/Edward_Tangye_Lean" title="Edward Tangye Lean">Edward Tangye Lean</a> (1911\xe2\x80\x931974), founded the original <a href="/wiki/Inklings" title="Inklings">Inklings</a> literary club when a student at <a href="/wiki/Oxford_University" class="mw-redirect" title="Oxford University">Oxford University</a>. Lean was a half-hearted schoolboy with a dreamy nature who was labeled a "dud"<sup id="cite_ref-Smith_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Smith-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> of a student; he left school in the Christmas Term of 1926, at the age of 18<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> and entered his father\'s chartered accountancy firm as an apprentice. A more formative event for his career than his formal education was an uncle\'s gift, when Lean was aged ten, of a <a href="/wiki/Brownie_(camera)" title="Brownie (camera)">Brownie box camera</a>. "You usually didn\'t give a boy a camera until he was 16 or 17 in those days. It was a huge compliment and I succeeded at it.\' Lean  printed and developed his films, and it was his \'great hobby\'.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>  In 1923,<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> his father deserted the family when he ran off with another woman, and Lean would later follow a similar path after his own first marriage and child.<sup id="cite_ref-Smith_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Smith-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Lean&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Period_as_film_editor">Period as film editor</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Lean&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Period as film editor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Bored by his work, Lean spent every evening in the cinema, and in 1927, after an aunt had advised him to find a job he enjoyed, he visited <a href="/wiki/Gaumont_British" class="mw-redirect" title="Gaumont British">Gaumont Studios</a> where his obvious enthusiasm earned him a month\'s trial without pay. He was taken on as a teaboy, promoted to clapperboy, and soon rose to the position of <a href="/wiki/Assistant_director" title="Assistant director">third assistant director</a>. By 1930 he was working as an editor on <a href="/wiki/Newsreels" class="mw-redirect" title="Newsreels">newsreels</a>, including those of <a href="/wiki/Gaumont_Film_Company" title="Gaumont Film Company">Gaumont Pictures</a> and <a href="/wiki/Movietone_News" title="Movietone News">Movietone</a>, while his move to feature films began with <i><a href="/wiki/Freedom_of_the_Seas_(film)" title="Freedom of the Seas (film)">Freedom of the Seas</a></i> (1934) and <i><a href="/wiki/Escape_Me_Never_(1935_film)" title="Escape Me Never (1935 film)">Escape Me Never</a></i> (1935).\n</p><p>He edited <a href="/wiki/Gabriel_Pascal" title="Gabriel Pascal">Gabriel Pascal</a>\'s film productions of two <a href="/wiki/George_Bernard_Shaw" title="George Bernard Shaw">George Bernard Shaw</a> plays, <i><a href="/wiki/Pygmalion_(1938_film)" title="Pygmalion (1938 film)">Pygmalion</a></i> (1938) and <i><a href="/wiki/Major_Barbara_(film)" title="Major Barbara (film)">Major Barbara</a></i> (1941). He edited <a href="/wiki/Powell_and_Pressburger" title="Powell and Pressburger">Powell &amp; Pressburger</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/49th_Parallel_(film)" title="49th Parallel (film)">49th Parallel</a></i> (1941) and <i><a href="/wiki/One_of_Our_Aircraft_Is_Missing" title="One of Our Aircraft Is Missing">One of Our Aircraft Is Missing</a></i> (1942). After this last film, Lean began his directing career, after editing more than two dozen features by 1942. As <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Sloman" title="Anthony Sloman">Tony Sloman</a> wrote in 1999, "As the varied likes of David Lean, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Wise" title="Robert Wise">Robert Wise</a>, <a href="/wiki/Terence_Fisher" title="Terence Fisher">Terence Fisher</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dorothy_Arzner" title="Dorothy Arzner">Dorothy Arzner</a> have proved, the cutting rooms are easily the finest grounding for film direction."<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> David Lean was given honorary membership of the <a href="/wiki/GBFTE" class="mw-redirect" title="GBFTE">Guild of British Film Editors</a> in 1968.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="British_films">British films</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Lean&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: British films">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>His first work as a director was in collaboration with <a href="/wiki/No%C3%ABl_Coward" title="No\xc3\xabl Coward">No\xc3\xabl Coward</a> on <i><a href="/wiki/In_Which_We_Serve" title="In Which We Serve">In Which We Serve</a></i> (1942), and he later adapted several of Coward\'s plays into successful films. These films are <i><a href="/wiki/This_Happy_Breed_(film)" title="This Happy Breed (film)">This Happy Breed</a></i> (1944), <i><a href="/wiki/Blithe_Spirit_(film)" title="Blithe Spirit (film)">Blithe Spirit</a></i> (1945) and <i><a href="/wiki/Brief_Encounter" title="Brief Encounter">Brief Encounter</a></i> (1945) with <a href="/wiki/Celia_Johnson" title="Celia Johnson">Celia Johnson</a> and <a href="/wiki/Trevor_Howard" title="Trevor Howard">Trevor Howard</a> as quietly understated clandestine lovers, torn between their unpredictable passion and their respective orderly middle-class marriages in suburban England. The film shared Grand Prix honors at the 1946 Cannes film festival and garnered Lean his first Academy nominations for directing and screen adaptation, and Celia Johnson a nomination for Best Actress. It has since become a classic, one of the most highly regarded British films.\n</p><p>Two celebrated <a href="/wiki/Charles_Dickens" title="Charles Dickens">Charles Dickens</a> adaptations followed \xe2\x80\x93 <i><a href="/wiki/Great_Expectations_(1946_film)" title="Great Expectations (1946 film)">Great Expectations</a></i> (1946) and <i><a href="/wiki/Oliver_Twist_(1948_film)" title="Oliver Twist (1948 film)">Oliver Twist</a></i> (1948). <a href="/wiki/David_Shipman_(writer)" title="David Shipman (writer)">David Shipman</a> wrote in <i>The Story of Cinema: Volume Two</i> (1984): "Of the other Dickens films, only Cukor\'s <i>David Copperfield</i> approaches the excellence of this pair, partly because his casting, too, was near perfect".<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> These two films were the first directed by Lean to star <a href="/wiki/Alec_Guinness" title="Alec Guinness">Alec Guinness</a>, whom Lean considered his "good luck charm". The actor\'s portrayal of Fagin was controversial at the time. The first screening in Berlin during February 1949 offended the surviving Jewish community and led to a riot. It caused problems too in New York, and after private screenings, was condemned by the <a href="/wiki/Anti-Defamation_League" title="Anti-Defamation League">Anti-Defamation League</a> and the American Board of Rabbis. "To our surprise it was accused of being anti-Semitic", Lean wrote. "We made Fagin an outsize and, we hoped, an amusing Jewish villain."<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The terms of the <a href="/wiki/Motion_Picture_Production_Code" title="Motion Picture Production Code">production code</a> meant that the film\'s release in the United States was delayed until July 1951 after cuts amounting to eight minutes.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The next film directed by Lean was <i><a href="/wiki/The_Passionate_Friends" title="The Passionate Friends">The Passionate Friends</a></i> (1949), an atypical Lean film, but one which marked his first occasion to work with <a href="/wiki/Claude_Rains" title="Claude Rains">Claude Rains</a>, who played the husband of a woman (Todd) torn between him and an old flame (Howard). <i>The Passionate Friends</i> was the first of three films to feature the actress <a href="/wiki/Ann_Todd" title="Ann Todd">Ann Todd</a>, who became his third wife. <i><a href="/wiki/Madeleine_(1950_film)" title="Madeleine (1950 film)">Madeleine</a></i> (1950), set in Victorian-era Glasgow is about an 1857 <i><a href="/wiki/Cause_c%C3%A9l%C3%A8bre" title="Cause c\xc3\xa9l\xc3\xa8bre">cause c\xc3\xa9l\xc3\xa8bre</a></i> with Todd\'s lead character accused of murdering a former lover. "Once more", writes film critic <a href="/wiki/David_Thomson_(film_critic)" title="David Thomson (film critic)">David Thomson</a> "Lean settles on the pressing need for propriety, but not before the film has put its characters and the audience through a wringer of contradictory feelings."<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> The last of the films with Todd, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Sound_Barrier" title="The Sound Barrier">The Sound Barrier</a></i> (1952), has a screenplay by the playwright <a href="/wiki/Terence_Rattigan" title="Terence Rattigan">Terence Rattigan</a> and was the first of his three films for <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Korda" title="Alexander Korda">Sir Alexander Korda</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/London_Films" title="London Films">London Films</a>. <i><a href="/wiki/Hobson%27s_Choice_(1954_film)" title="Hobson&#39;s Choice (1954 film)">Hobson\'s Choice</a></i> (1954), with <a href="/wiki/Charles_Laughton" title="Charles Laughton">Charles Laughton</a> in the lead, was based on the play by <a href="/wiki/Harold_Brighouse" title="Harold Brighouse">Harold Brighouse</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="International_films">International films</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Lean&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: International films">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:DavidLean1965.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/DavidLean1965.jpg/200px-DavidLean1965.jpg" width="200" height="275" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/DavidLean1965.jpg/300px-DavidLean1965.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/DavidLean1965.jpg/400px-DavidLean1965.jpg 2x" data-file-width="824" data-file-height="1133" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:DavidLean1965.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Lean in Northern <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a> in 1965 while shooting <i>Doctor Zhivago</i>.</div></div></div>\n<p><i><a href="/wiki/Summertime_(1955_film)" title="Summertime (1955 film)">Summertime</a></i> (1955) marked a new departure for Lean. It was partly American financed, although again made for Korda\'s London Films. The film features <a href="/wiki/Katharine_Hepburn" title="Katharine Hepburn">Katharine Hepburn</a> in the lead role as a middle-aged American woman who has a romance while on holiday in <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">Venice</a>. It was shot entirely on location there.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="For_Columbia_and_Sam_Spiegel">For Columbia and Sam Spiegel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Lean&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: For Columbia and Sam Spiegel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Lean\'s films now began to become infrequent but much larger in scale and more extensively released internationally. <i><a href="/wiki/The_Bridge_on_the_River_Kwai" title="The Bridge on the River Kwai">The Bridge on the River Kwai</a></i> (1957) was based on a novel by <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Boulle" title="Pierre Boulle">Pierre Boulle</a> recounting the story of British and American prisoners of war trying to survive in a Japanese prison camp during the <a href="/wiki/Second_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Second World War">Second World War</a>. The film stars <a href="/wiki/William_Holden" title="William Holden">William Holden</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alec_Guinness" title="Alec Guinness">Alec Guinness</a> and became the highest-grossing film of 1957 in the United States. It won seven <a href="/wiki/Academy_Awards" title="Academy Awards">Academy Awards</a>, including  <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Picture" title="Academy Award for Best Picture">Best Picture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Director" title="Academy Award for Best Director">Best Director</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Actor" title="Academy Award for Best Actor">Best Actor</a> for <a href="/wiki/Alec_Guinness" title="Alec Guinness">Alec Guinness</a>, who had battled with Lean to give more depth to his role as an obsessively correct British commander who is determined to build the best possible bridge for his Japanese captors in Burma.\n</p><p>After extensive location work in the Middle East, North Africa, Spain, and elsewhere, Lean\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Lawrence_of_Arabia_(film)" title="Lawrence of Arabia (film)">Lawrence of Arabia</a></i> was released in 1962. This was the first project of Lean\'s with a screenplay by playwright <a href="/wiki/Robert_Bolt" title="Robert Bolt">Robert Bolt</a>, rewriting an original script by <a href="/wiki/Michael_Wilson_(writer)" title="Michael Wilson (writer)">Michael Wilson</a> (one of the two blacklisted writers of <i>Bridge on the River Kwai</i>). It recounts the life of <a href="/wiki/T._E._Lawrence" title="T. E. Lawrence">T. E. Lawrence</a>, the British officer who is depicted in the film as uniting the squabbling Bedouin peoples of the Arab peninsula to fight in <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> and then push on for independence.\n</p><p>After some hesitation, <a href="/wiki/Alec_Guinness" title="Alec Guinness">Alec Guinness</a> once again appeared, in his fourth David Lean film, as the Arab leader, Prince Faisal, despite his misgivings from their conflicts on <i>Bridge on the River Kwai</i>.  French composer <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Jarre" title="Maurice Jarre">Maurice Jarre</a>, on his first Lean film, created a soaring film score with a famous theme and won his first Oscar for Best Original Score. The film turned actor <a href="/wiki/Peter_O%27Toole" title="Peter O&#39;Toole">Peter O\'Toole</a>, playing Lawrence, into an international star, was nominated for ten Oscars and won seven, including Best Picture and Lean\'s second win for Best Director. He remains the only British director to win more than one Oscar for directing.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="For_MGM">For MGM</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Lean&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: For MGM">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Lean had his greatest box-office success with <i><a href="/wiki/Doctor_Zhivago_(film)" title="Doctor Zhivago (film)">Doctor Zhivago</a></i> (1965), a romance set during the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Revolution" title="Russian Revolution">Russian Revolution</a>. The film, based on the banned novel by Nobel Prize-winning Russian poet <a href="/wiki/Boris_Pasternak" title="Boris Pasternak">Boris Pasternak</a>, tells the story of a brilliant and warm-hearted physician and poet (<a href="/wiki/Omar_Sharif" title="Omar Sharif">Omar Sharif</a>) who, while seemingly happily married into the Russian aristocracy, and a father,  falls in love with a beautiful abandoned young mother named Lara (<a href="/wiki/Julie_Christie" title="Julie Christie">Julie Christie</a>) and struggles to be with her in the chaos of the Bolshevik revolution and subsequent <a href="/wiki/Russian_Civil_War" title="Russian Civil War">Russian Civil War</a>.\n</p><p>Initially, reviews for <i>Doctor Zhivago</i> were lukewarm, but critics have since come to see it as one of Lean\'s best films, with film director <a href="/wiki/Paul_Greengrass" title="Paul Greengrass">Paul Greengrass</a> calling it "one of the great masterpieces of cinema".<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> As of  2015<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=David_Lean&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup>, it is the 8th highest-grossing film of all time, adjusted for inflation. Producer <a href="/wiki/Carlo_Ponti" title="Carlo Ponti">Carlo Ponti</a> used <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Jarre" title="Maurice Jarre">Maurice Jarre\'s</a> lush romantic score to create a pop tune called "<a href="/wiki/Lara%27s_Theme" title="Lara&#39;s Theme">Lara\'s Theme</a>", which became an international hit song with lyrics under the title "Somewhere My Love", one of cinema\'s most successful theme songs. The British director of photography, <a href="/wiki/Freddie_Young" title="Freddie Young">Freddie Young</a>, won an Academy Award for his color cinematography. Around the same time, Lean also directed some scenes of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Greatest_Story_Ever_Told" title="The Greatest Story Ever Told">The Greatest Story Ever Told</a></i> (1965) while <a href="/wiki/George_Stevens" title="George Stevens">George Stevens</a> was committed to location work in Nevada.\n</p><p>Lean\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Ryan%27s_Daughter" title="Ryan&#39;s Daughter">Ryan\'s Daughter</a></i> (1970) was released after an extended period on location in Ireland. A doomed romance set against the backdrop of 1916 Ireland\'s struggles against the British, it is loosely based on <a href="/wiki/Gustave_Flaubert" title="Gustave Flaubert">Gustave Flaubert</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Madame_Bovary" title="Madame Bovary">Madame Bovary</a></i>. Starring the aging Hollywood \'bad boy\' <a href="/wiki/Robert_Mitchum" title="Robert Mitchum">Robert Mitchum</a> in an uncharacteristic role as a long-suffering Irish husband and British actress <a href="/wiki/Sarah_Miles" title="Sarah Miles">Sarah Miles</a> as his faithless young wife, the film received far fewer positive reviews than the director\'s previous work, being particularly savaged by the New York critics.  Some critics felt the film\'s massive visual scale on gorgeous Irish beaches and extended running time did not suit its small-scale romantic narrative. Nonetheless, the film was a box office success, earning $31 million and making it the 8th highest-grossing film of that year. It won two Academy Awards the following year, another for cinematographer <a href="/wiki/Freddie_Young" title="Freddie Young">Freddie Young</a> and for supporting actor <a href="/wiki/John_Mills" title="John Mills">John Mills</a> in his role as a village halfwit.\n</p><p>The poor critical reception of the film prompted Lean to meet with the National Society of Film Critics,  gathered at the <a href="/wiki/Algonquin_Hotel" title="Algonquin Hotel">Algonquin Hotel</a> in New York, including <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_Yorker" title="The New Yorker">The New Yorker</a>\'</i>s <a href="/wiki/Pauline_Kael" title="Pauline Kael">Pauline Kael</a>, and ask them why they objected to the movie. "I sensed trouble from the moment I sat down," Lean says of the now famous luncheon. <a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)"><i>TIME</i></a> critic Richard Shickel asked Lean pointblank how he, the director of <i>Brief Encounter</i>, could have made "a piece of bullshit" like <i>Ryan\'s Daughter</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> These critics so lacerated the film for two hours to David Lean\'s face that the devastated Lean did not make another film for fourteen years. "They just took the film to bits," said Lean in a later television interview. "It really had such an awful effect on me for several years... you begin to think that maybe they\'re right. Why on earth am I making films if I don\'t have to? It shakes one\'s confidence terribly.""<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Last_years_and_unfulfilled_projects">Last years and unfulfilled projects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Lean&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Last years and unfulfilled projects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>From 1977 until 1980, Lean and Robert Bolt worked on a film adaptation of <i>Captain Bligh and Mr. Christian</i>, a dramatized account by <a href="/wiki/Richard_Hough" title="Richard Hough">Richard Hough</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Mutiny_on_the_Bounty" title="Mutiny on the Bounty">Mutiny on the <i>Bounty</i></a>. It was originally to be released as a two-part film, one named <i>The Lawbreakers</i> that dealt with the voyage out to Tahiti and the subsequent mutiny, and the second named <i>The Long Arm</i> that studied the journey of the mutineers after the mutiny as well as the admiralty\'s response in sending out the frigate <a href="/wiki/HMS_Pandora_(1779)" title="HMS Pandora (1779)">HMS <i>Pandora</i></a>, in which some of the mutineers were imprisoned. Lean could not find financial backing for both films after <a href="/wiki/Warner_Bros." title="Warner Bros.">Warner Bros.</a> withdrew from the project; he decided to combine it into one and looked at a seven-part TV series before getting backing from Italian mogul <a href="/wiki/Dino_De_Laurentiis" title="Dino De Laurentiis">Dino De Laurentiis</a>. The project then suffered a further setback when Bolt suffered a serious <a href="/wiki/Stroke" title="Stroke">stroke</a> and was unable to continue writing; the director felt that Bolt\'s involvement would be crucial to the film\'s success. <a href="/wiki/Melvyn_Bragg" title="Melvyn Bragg">Melvyn Bragg</a> ended up writing a considerable portion of the script.\n</p><p>Lean was forced to abandon the project after overseeing casting and the construction of the $4 million <i>Bounty</i> replica; at the last possible moment, actor <a href="/wiki/Mel_Gibson" title="Mel Gibson">Mel Gibson</a> brought in his friend <a href="/wiki/Roger_Donaldson" title="Roger Donaldson">Roger Donaldson</a> to direct the film, as producer De Laurentiis did not want to lose the millions he had already put into the project over what he thought was as insignificant a person as the director dropping out.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> The film was eventually released as <i><a href="/wiki/The_Bounty_(1984_film)" title="The Bounty (1984 film)">The Bounty</a></i>.\n</p><p>Lean then embarked on a project he had pursued since 1960, a film adaptation of <i><a href="/wiki/A_Passage_to_India_(film)" title="A Passage to India (film)">A Passage to India</a></i> (1984), from <a href="/wiki/E._M._Forster" title="E. M. Forster">E. M. Forster</a>\'s  <a href="/wiki/A_Passage_to_India" title="A Passage to India">1924 novel</a> of colonial conflicts in British-occupied India. Entirely shot on location in the sub-continent, this became his last completed film. He rejected a draft by <a href="/wiki/Santha_Rama_Rau" title="Santha Rama Rau">Santha Rama Rau</a>, responsible for the stage adaptation and Forster\'s preferred screenwriter, and wrote the script himself.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> In addition, Lean also edited the film with the result that his three roles in the production (writer, editor, director) were given equal status in the credits.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Lean recruited long-time collaborators for the cast and crew, including Maurice Jarre (who won another Academy-Award for the score), Alec Guinness in his sixth and final role for Lean, as an eccentric Hindu Brahmin, and <a href="/wiki/John_Box" title="John Box">John Box</a>, the production designer for <i>Dr. Zhivago</i>. Reversing the critical response to <i>Ryan\'s Daughter</i>, the film opened to universally enthusiastic reviews; the film was nominated for eleven Academy Awards and Lean himself nominated for three Academy Awards in <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Director" title="Academy Award for Best Director">directing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Film_Editing" class="mw-redirect" title="Academy Award for Film Editing">editing</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Writing_Adapted_Screenplay" class="mw-redirect" title="Academy Award for Writing Adapted Screenplay">writing</a>. His female star, in the complex role of a confused young British woman who falsely accuses an Indian man of rape, gained Australian actress  <a href="/wiki/Judy_Davis" title="Judy Davis">Judy Davis</a> her first Academy nomination.  <a href="/wiki/Peggy_Ashcroft" title="Peggy Ashcroft">Peggy Ashcroft</a>, as the sensitive Mrs. Moore, won the Oscar for best supporting actress, making her, at 77, the oldest actress to win that award. According to Roger Ebert, it is "one of the greatest screen adaptations I have ever seen".<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> But this was, sadly, to be his last.\n</p><p>He was signed on to direct a <a href="/wiki/Warner_Bros." title="Warner Bros.">Warner Bros.</a>-backed adaptation of <a href="/wiki/J._G._Ballard" title="J. G. Ballard">J. G. Ballard</a>\'s autobiographical novel <i><a href="/wiki/Empire_of_the_Sun" title="Empire of the Sun">Empire of the Sun</a></i> after director <a href="/wiki/Harold_Becker" title="Harold Becker">Harold Becker</a> left the project. <a href="/wiki/Steven_Spielberg" title="Steven Spielberg">Steven Spielberg</a> was brought on board as a producer for Lean, but later assumed the role of director when Lean dropped out of the project; Spielberg was drawn to the idea of making the film due to his long-time admiration for Lean and his films. <i><a href="/wiki/Empire_of_the_Sun_(film)" title="Empire of the Sun (film)">Empire of the Sun</a></i> was released in 1987.\n</p><p>During the last years of his life, Lean was in pre-production of a film version of <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Conrad" title="Joseph Conrad">Joseph Conrad</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Nostromo" title="Nostromo">Nostromo</a></i>. He assembled an all-star cast, including <a href="/wiki/Marlon_Brando" title="Marlon Brando">Marlon Brando</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paul_Scofield" title="Paul Scofield">Paul Scofield</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Quinn" title="Anthony Quinn">Anthony Quinn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peter_O%27Toole" title="Peter O&#39;Toole">Peter O\'Toole</a>, <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Lambert" title="Christopher Lambert">Christopher Lambert</a>, <a href="/wiki/Isabella_Rossellini" title="Isabella Rossellini">Isabella Rossellini</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dennis_Quaid" title="Dennis Quaid">Dennis Quaid</a>, with <a href="/wiki/Georges_Corraface" title="Georges Corraface">Georges Corraface</a> as the title character. Lean also wanted <a href="/wiki/Alec_Guinness" title="Alec Guinness">Alec Guinness</a> to play Doctor Monyghan, but the aged actor turned him down in a letter from 1989: "I believe I would be disastrous casting. The only thing in the part I might have done well is the crippled crab-like walk." As with <i>Empire of the Sun</i>, Steven Spielberg came on board as producer with the backing of Warner Bros., but after several rewrites and disagreements on the script, he left the project and was replaced by <a href="/wiki/Serge_Silberman" title="Serge Silberman">Serge Silberman</a>, a respected producer at Greenwich Film Productions.\n</p><p>The <i>Nostromo</i> project involved several writers, including <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Hampton" title="Christopher Hampton">Christopher Hampton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Robert_Bolt" title="Robert Bolt">Robert Bolt</a>, but their work was abandoned. In the end, Lean decided to write the film himself with the assistance of Maggie Unsworth (wife of renowned cinematographer <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_Unsworth" title="Geoffrey Unsworth">Geoffrey Unsworth</a>), with whom he had worked on the scripts for <i>Brief Encounter</i>, <i>Great Expectations</i>, <i>Oliver Twist</i>, and <i>The Passionate Friends</i>. Originally Lean considered filming in <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a> but later decided to film in London and <a href="/wiki/Madrid" title="Madrid">Madrid</a>, partly to secure O\'Toole, who had insisted he would take part only if the film was shot close to home. <i>Nostromo</i> had a total budget of $46 million and was six weeks away from filming at the time of Lean\'s death from <a href="/wiki/Head_and_neck_cancer" title="Head and neck cancer">throat cancer</a>. It was rumoured that fellow film director <a href="/wiki/John_Boorman" title="John Boorman">John Boorman</a> would take over direction, but the production collapsed. <i>Nostromo</i> was finally adapted for the small screen with an unrelated <a href="/wiki/BBC_television" class="mw-redirect" title="BBC television">BBC television</a> <a href="/wiki/Nostromo_(TV_serial)" title="Nostromo (TV serial)">mini-series</a> in 1997.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life_and_honours">Personal life and honours</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Lean&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Personal life and honours">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Lean was a long-term resident of <a href="/wiki/Limehouse" title="Limehouse">Limehouse</a>, east London. His home on Narrow Street is still owned by his family. His co-writer and producer <a href="/w/index.php?title=Norman_Spencer_(producer)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Norman Spencer (producer) (page does not exist)">Norman Spencer</a> has said that Lean was a "huge womaniser" and "to my knowledge, he had almost 1,000 women".<sup id="cite_ref-How_we_made_Hobson&#39;s_Choice_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-How_we_made_Hobson&#39;s_Choice-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> He was married six times, had one son, and at least two grandchildren\xe2\x80\x94from all of whom he was completely estranged<sup id="cite_ref-Collins_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Collins-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\xe2\x80\x94and was divorced five times. He was survived by his last wife, art dealer Sandra Cooke, the co-author (with Barry Chattington) of\n<i>David Lean: An Intimate Portrait</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Smith_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Smith-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> as well as Peter Lean, his son from his first marriage.\n</p><p>His six wives were:\n</p>\n<ul><li>Isabel Lean (28 June 1930 \xe2\x80\x93 1936) (his first cousin); one son, Peter</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Kay_Walsh" title="Kay Walsh">Kay Walsh</a> (23 November 1940 \xe2\x80\x93 1949)</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Ann_Todd" title="Ann Todd">Ann Todd</a> (21 May 1949 \xe2\x80\x93 1957)</li>\n<li>Leila Matkar (4 July 1960 \xe2\x80\x93 1978) (from <a href="/wiki/Hyderabad,_India" class="mw-redirect" title="Hyderabad, India">Hyderabad, India</a>); Lean\'s longest-lasting marriage<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>Sandra Hotz (28 October 1981 \xe2\x80\x93 1984)</li>\n<li>Sandra Cooke (15 December 1990 \xe2\x80\x93 16 April 1991, Lean\'s death)</li></ul>\n<p>Lean was appointed Commander of the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_British_Empire" title="Order of the British Empire">Order of the British Empire</a> (CBE) in 1973, and was knighted for his contributions and services to the arts in 1984.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> Lean received the <a href="/wiki/AFI_Life_Achievement_Award" title="AFI Life Achievement Award">AFI Life Achievement Award</a> in 1990. In 2012, Lean was among the <a href="/wiki/Culture_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Culture of the United Kingdom">British cultural icons</a> selected by artist Sir <a href="/wiki/Peter_Blake_(artist)" title="Peter Blake (artist)">Peter Blake</a> to appear in a new version of his most famous artwork \xe2\x80\x93 the Beatles\' <i><a href="/wiki/Sgt._Pepper%27s_Lonely_Hearts_Club_Band" title="Sgt. Pepper&#39;s Lonely Hearts Club Band">Sgt. Pepper\'s Lonely Hearts Club Band</a></i> album cover \xe2\x80\x93 to celebrate the British cultural figures of his life that he most admires.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1999, the <a href="/wiki/British_Film_Institute" title="British Film Institute">British Film Institute</a> compiled its list of the <a href="/wiki/BFI_Top_100_British_films" title="BFI Top 100 British films">Top 100 British films</a>; seven of Lean\'s films appeared on the list:\n</p>\n<ul><li><i><a href="/wiki/Brief_Encounter" title="Brief Encounter">Brief Encounter</a></i> (#2)</li>\n<li><i><a href="/wiki/Lawrence_of_Arabia_(film)" title="Lawrence of Arabia (film)">Lawrence of Arabia</a></i> (#3)</li>\n<li><i><a href="/wiki/Great_Expectations_(1946_film)" title="Great Expectations (1946 film)">Great Expectations</a></i> (#5)</li>\n<li><i><a href="/wiki/The_Bridge_on_the_River_Kwai" title="The Bridge on the River Kwai">The Bridge on the River Kwai</a></i> (#11)</li>\n<li><i><a href="/wiki/Doctor_Zhivago_(film)" title="Doctor Zhivago (film)">Doctor Zhivago</a></i> (#27)</li>\n<li><i><a href="/wiki/Oliver_Twist_(1948_film)" title="Oliver Twist (1948 film)">Oliver Twist</a></i> (#46)</li>\n<li><i><a href="/wiki/In_Which_We_Serve" title="In Which We Serve">In Which We Serve</a></i> (#92)</li></ul>\n<p>In addition to this, the <a href="/wiki/American_Film_Institute" title="American Film Institute">American Film Institute</a>\'s 1998 <a href="/wiki/AFI%27s_100_Years...100_Movies" title="AFI&#39;s 100 Years...100 Movies">100 Years...100 Movies</a> list placed <i>Lawrence of Arabia</i> 5th, <i>The Bridge on the River Kwai</i> 13th and <i>Doctor Zhivago</i> 39th; in the 2007 <a href="/wiki/AFI%27s_100_Years...100_Movies_(10th_Anniversary_Edition)" title="AFI&#39;s 100 Years...100 Movies (10th Anniversary Edition)">revised edition</a>, <i>Lawrence of Arabia</i> placed 7th and <i>The Bridge on the River Kwai</i> placed 36th.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Style_and_influence">Style and influence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Lean&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Style and influence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>As Lean himself pointed out,<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> his films are often admired by fellow directors as a showcase of the filmmaker\'s art. They are characterised by his highly creative and sometimes jarring combination of editing, cinematography and sound mixing, particularly a range of different scene transitions and broad natural landscape shots, to propel the film\'s narrative. As well as his proclivity for arresting visuals, Lean\'s films are largely character- and plot-driven, often featuring flawed protagonists who are largely blind to their shortcomings until it is too late, most notably in <i>The Bridge on the River Kwai</i>. Lean\'s films are also noted for their use of extended flashbacks and narrative framing devices, most notably in <i>Brief Encounter</i>, <i>Lawrence of Arabia</i> and <i>Doctor Zhivago</i>, as well as for stories of illicit romances, as in <i>Brief Encounter</i>, <i>The Passionate Friends</i>, <i>Doctor Zhivago</i> and <i>Ryan\'s Daughter</i>.\n</p><p>Lean was also notorious for his perfectionist approach to filmmaking; director <a href="/wiki/Claude_Chabrol" title="Claude Chabrol">Claude Chabrol</a> stated that he and Lean were the only directors working at the time who were prepared to wait "forever" for the perfect sunset, but whereas Chabrol measured "forever" in terms of days, Lean did so in terms of months.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Steven_Spielberg" title="Steven Spielberg">Steven Spielberg</a> and <a href="/wiki/Martin_Scorsese" title="Martin Scorsese">Martin Scorsese</a> in particular are fans of Lean\'s epic films, and claim him as one of their primary influences. Spielberg and Scorsese also helped in the 1989 restoration of <i>Lawrence of Arabia</i> which had been substantially altered both by the studio in theatrical release and in particular in its televised versions; the theatrical re-release greatly revived Lean\'s reputation.\n</p><p>Several of the many other later twentieth century directors who have acknowledged significant influence by Lean include <a href="/wiki/Stanley_Kubrick" title="Stanley Kubrick">Stanley Kubrick</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/George_Lucas" title="George Lucas">George Lucas</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Spike_Lee" title="Spike Lee">Spike Lee</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Sergio_Leone" title="Sergio Leone">Sergio Leone</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/John_Woo" title="John Woo">John Woo</a> once named <i>Lawrence of Arabia</i> among his top three films.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> More recently, <a href="/wiki/Joe_Wright" title="Joe Wright">Joe Wright</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Pride_%26_Prejudice_(2005_film)" title="Pride &amp; Prejudice (2005 film)">Pride &amp; Prejudice</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Atonement_(film)" title="Atonement (film)">Atonement</a></i>) has cited Lean\'s works, particularly <i>Doctor Zhivago</i>, as an important influence on his work,<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> as has director <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Nolan" title="Christopher Nolan">Christopher Nolan</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/The_Dark_Knight_Rises" title="The Dark Knight Rises">The Dark Knight Rises</a>)</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The critical verdict was not unanimous, however. For example, David Thomson, writing about Lean in his <i>New Biographical Dictionary of Film</i>, comments:\n</p>\n<table class="cquote pullquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">\n<tbody><tr>\n<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: &#39;Times New Roman&#39;, Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">\xe2\x80\x9c\n</td>\n<td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">From 1952 to 1991, he made eight films\xe2\x80\x94and in only one of them, I suggest \xe2\x80\x94<i>Lawrence</i>\xe2\x80\x94is the spectacle sufficient to mask the hollow rhetoric of the scripts. But Lean before 1952 made eight films in ten years that are lively, stirring, and an inspiration\xe2\x80\x94they make you want to go out and make movies, they are so in love with the screen\'s power and the combustion in editing."<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>\n</td>\n<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: bottom; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: &#39;Times New Roman&#39;, Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: right; padding: 10px 10px;">\xe2\x80\x9d\n</td></tr>\n\n</tbody></table> <p><i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> film critic <a href="/wiki/Bosley_Crowther" title="Bosley Crowther">Bosley Crowther</a> dismissed <i>Lawrence of Arabia</i> as "a huge, thundering camel-opera that tends to run down rather badly as it rolls on into its third hour and gets involved with sullen disillusion and political deceit",.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> Writing about the same film in <i><a another expensive mirage, dull, overlong, and coldly impersonal... on the whole I find it hatefully calculating and condescending..."<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a
