Sussex UK location map.svg"> </a></div>Site of the Battle of Hastings in East Sussex</div></div></div></div></div>\n<p>The <b>Battle of Hastings</b><sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;a&#93;</a></sup> was fought on 14 October 1066 between the Norman-French army of <a href="/wiki/William_the_Conqueror" title="William the Conqueror">William, the Duke of Normandy</a>, and an English army under the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxons" title="Anglo-Saxons">Anglo-Saxon</a> King <a href="/wiki/Harold_Godwinson" title="Harold Godwinson">Harold Godwinson</a>, beginning the <a href="/wiki/Norman_conquest_of_England" title="Norman conquest of England">Norman conquest of England</a>. It took place approximately 7 miles (11 kilometres) northwest of <a href="/wiki/Hastings" title="Hastings">Hastings</a>, close to the present-day town of <a href="/wiki/Battle,_East_Sussex" title="Battle, East Sussex">Battle, East Sussex</a>, and was a decisive <a href="/wiki/Normans" title="Normans">Norman</a> victory.\n</p><p>The background to the battle was the death of the childless King <a href="/wiki/Edward_the_Confessor" title="Edward the Confessor">Edward the Confessor</a> in January 1066, which set up a succession struggle between several claimants to his throne. Harold was crowned king shortly after Edward\'s death, but faced invasions by William, his own brother <a href="/wiki/Tostig_Godwinson" title="Tostig Godwinson">Tostig</a>, and the Norwegian King <a href="/wiki/Harald_Hardrada" title="Harald Hardrada">Harald Hardrada</a> (Harold III of Norway). Hardrada and Tostig defeated a hastily gathered army of Englishmen at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Fulford" title="Battle of Fulford">Battle of Fulford</a> on 20 September 1066, and were in turn defeated by Harold at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Stamford_Bridge" title="Battle of Stamford Bridge">Battle of Stamford Bridge</a> five days later. The deaths of Tostig and Hardrada at Stamford Bridge left William as Harold\'s only serious opponent. While Harold and his forces were recovering, William landed his invasion forces in the south of England at <a href="/wiki/Pevensey" title="Pevensey">Pevensey</a> on 28 September 1066 and established a beachhead for his conquest of the kingdom. Harold was forced to march south swiftly, gathering forces as he went.\n</p><p>The exact numbers present at the battle are unknown; modern estimates are around 10,000 for William and about 7,000 for Harold. The composition of the forces is clearer; the English army was composed almost entirely of <a href="/wiki/Infantry" title="Infantry">infantry</a> and had few <a href="/wiki/Archery" title="Archery">archers</a>, whereas only about half of the invading force was infantry, the rest split equally between <a href="/wiki/Cavalry" title="Cavalry">cavalry</a> and archers. Harold appears to have tried to surprise William, but scouts found his army and reported its arrival to William, who marched from Hastings to the battlefield to confront Harold. The battle lasted from about 9&#160;am to dusk. Early efforts of the invaders to break the English battle lines had little effect; therefore, the Normans adopted the tactic of pretending to flee in panic and then turning on their pursuers. Harold\'s death, probably near the end of the battle, led to the retreat and defeat of most of his army. After further marching and some skirmishes, William was crowned as king on Christmas Day 1066.\n</p><p>There continued to be rebellions and resistance to William\'s rule, but Hastings effectively marked the culmination of William\'s conquest of England. Casualty figures are hard to come by, but some historians estimate that 2,000 invaders died along with about twice that number of Englishmen. William founded <a href="/wiki/Battle_Abbey" title="Battle Abbey">a monastery</a> at the site of the battle, the high altar of the abbey church supposedly placed at the spot where Harold died.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" style="display:none" /><div class="toctitle" lang="en" id="Background">Background</span></h2>\n<p>In 911, French <a href="/wiki/Carolingian" class="mw-redirect" title="Carolingian">Carolingian</a> ruler <a href="/wiki/Charles_the_Simple" title="Charles the Simple">Charles the Simple</a> allowed a group of Vikings to settle in <a href="/wiki/Normandy" title="Normandy">Normandy</a> under their leader <a href="/wiki/Rollo_of_Normandy" class="mw-redirect" title="Rollo of Normandy">Rollo</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Bates8_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bates8-2">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Their settlement proved successful,<sup id="cite_ref-Normans15_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Normans15-3">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;b&#93;</a></sup> and they quickly adapted to the indigenous culture, renouncing <a href="/wiki/Paganism" title="Paganism">paganism</a>, converting to <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Bates12_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bates12-5">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> and intermarrying with the local population.<sup id="cite_ref-Bates20_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bates20-6">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Over time, the frontiers of the duchy expanded to the west.<sup id="cite_ref-Capet53_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Capet53-7">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> In 1002, King <a href="/wiki/%C3%86thelred_the_Unready" title="\xc3\x86thelred the Unready">\xc3\x86thelred II of England</a> married <a href="/wiki/Emma_of_Normandy" title="Emma of Normandy">Emma</a>, the sister of <a href="/wiki/Richard_II,_Duke_of_Normandy" title="Richard II, Duke of Normandy">Richard II, Duke of Normandy</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Unready54_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Unready54-8">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Their son <a href="/wiki/Edward_the_Confessor" title="Edward the Confessor">Edward the Confessor</a> spent many years in exile in Normandy, and succeeded to the English throne in 1042.<sup id="cite_ref-Ruling3_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ruling3-9">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> This led to the establishment of a powerful Norman interest in English politics, as Edward drew heavily on his former hosts for support, bringing in Norman courtiers, soldiers, and clerics and appointing them to positions of power, particularly in the Church. Edward was childless and embroiled in conflict with the formidable <a href="/wiki/Godwin,_Earl_of_Wessex" title="Godwin, Earl of Wessex">Godwin, Earl of Wessex</a>, and his sons, and he may also have encouraged Duke William of Normandy\'s ambitions for the English throne.<sup id="cite_ref-Unification86_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Unification86-10">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Succession_crisis_in_England">Succession crisis in England</span></h3>\n<p>King Edward\'s death on 5 January 1066<sup id="cite_ref-Handbook29_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Handbook29-11">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;c&#93;</a></sup> left no clear heir, and several contenders laid claim to the throne of England.<sup id="cite_ref-Death167_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Death167-14">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Edward\'s immediate successor was the <a href="/wiki/Earl_of_Wessex" title="Earl of Wessex">Earl of Wessex</a>, Harold Godwinson, the richest and most powerful of the English aristocrats and son of Godwin, Edward\'s earlier opponent. Harold was elected king by the <a href="/wiki/Witenagemot" title="Witenagemot">Witenagemot</a> of England and crowned by <a href="/wiki/Archbishop_of_York" title="Archbishop of York">Archbishop of York</a> <a href="/wiki/Ealdred_(archbishop_of_York)" title="Ealdred (archbishop of York)">Ealdred</a>, although Norman propaganda claimed that the ceremony was performed by <a href="/wiki/Stigand" title="Stigand">Stigand</a>, the uncanonically elected <a href="/wiki/Archbishop_of_Canterbury" title="Archbishop of Canterbury">Archbishop of Canterbury</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Death167_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Death167-14">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Walker136_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Walker136-15">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Harold was at once challenged by two powerful neighbouring rulers. Duke William claimed that he had been promised the throne by King Edward and that Harold had sworn agreement to this.<sup id="cite_ref-BatesWilliam73_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BatesWilliam73-16">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Harald III of Norway, commonly known as <a href="/wiki/Harald_Hardrada" title="Harald Hardrada">Harald Hardrada</a>, also contested the succession. His claim to the throne was based on an agreement between his predecessor <a href="/wiki/Magnus_I_of_Norway" class="mw-redirect" title="Magnus I of Norway">Magnus I of Norway</a> and the earlier King of England <a href="/wiki/Harthacanute" class="mw-redirect" title="Harthacanute">Harthacanute</a>, whereby, if either died without heir, the other would inherit both England and Norway.<sup id="cite_ref-Death188_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Death188-17">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> William and Harald Hardrada immediately set about assembling troops and ships for separate invasions.<sup id="cite_ref-Ruling12_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ruling12-18">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;d&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Tostig_and_Hardrada.27s_invasions"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Tostig_and_Hardrada\'s_invasions">Tostig and Hardrada\'s invasions</span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Fulford" title="Battle of Fulford">Battle of Fulford</a></div>\n<p>In early 1066, Harold\'s exiled brother <a href="/wiki/Tostig_Godwinson" title="Tostig Godwinson">Tostig Godwinson</a> raided south-eastern England with a fleet he had recruited in <a href="/wiki/Flanders" title="Flanders">Flanders</a>, later joined by other ships from <a href="/wiki/Orkney" title="Orkney">Orkney</a>. Threatened by Harold\'s fleet, Tostig moved north and raided in <a href="/wiki/East_Anglia" title="East Anglia">East Anglia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lincolnshire" title="Lincolnshire">Lincolnshire</a>. He was driven back to his ships by the brothers <a href="/wiki/Edwin,_Earl_of_Mercia" title="Edwin, Earl of Mercia">Edwin, Earl of Mercia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Morcar_of_Northumbria" class="mw-redirect" title="Morcar of Northumbria">Morcar, Earl of Northumbria</a>. Deserted by most of his followers, he withdrew to Scotland, where he spent the middle of the year recruiting fresh forces.<sup id="cite_ref-Harold144_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harold144-25">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Hardrada invaded northern England in early September, leading a fleet of more than 300 ships carrying perhaps 15,000 men. Hardrada\'s army was further augmented by the forces of Tostig, who supported the Norwegian king\'s bid for the throne. Advancing on York, the Norwegians occupied the city after defeating a northern English army under Edwin and Morcar on 20 September at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Fulford" title="Battle of Fulford">Battle of Fulford</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Harold154_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harold154-26">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span id="English_army_and_Harold.27s_preparations"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="English_army_and_Harold\'s_preparations">English army and Harold\'s preparations</span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Stamford_Bridge" title="Battle of Stamford Bridge">Battle of Stamford Bridge</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Battle_Flat_-_geograph.org.uk_-_285158.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Battle_Flat_-_geograph.org.uk_-_285158.jpg/220px-Battle_Flat_-_geograph.org.uk_-_285158.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Battle_Flat_-_geograph.org.uk_-_285158.jpg/440px-Battle_Flat_-_geograph.org.uk_-_285158.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="479" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Battle_Flat_-_geograph.org.uk_-_285158.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The location of the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Stamford_Bridge" title="Battle of Stamford Bridge">Battle of Stamford Bridge</a></div></div></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon_military_organization" title="Anglo-Saxon military organization">English army</a> was organised along regional lines, with the <i><a href="/wiki/Fyrd" title="Fyrd">fyrd</a></i>, or local levy, serving under a local magnate&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 whether an <a href="/wiki/Earl" title="Earl">earl</a>, bishop, or <a href="/wiki/Sheriff" title="Sheriff">sheriff</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Source69_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Source69-27">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> The <i>fyrd</i> was composed of men who owned their own land, and were equipped by their community to fulfil the king\'s demands for military forces.  For every five <a href="/wiki/Hide_(unit)" title="Hide (unit)">hides</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Marren55_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marren55-28">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> or units of land nominally capable of supporting one household,<sup id="cite_ref-Dict154_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dict154-29">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> one man was supposed to serve.<sup id="cite_ref-Marren55_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marren55-28">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> It appears that the <a href="/wiki/Hundred_(country_subdivision)" class="mw-redirect" title="Hundred (country subdivision)">hundred</a> was the main organising unit for the <i>fyrd</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Gravett28_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gravett28-30">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> As a whole, England could furnish about 14,000 men for the <i>fyrd</i>, when it was called out. The <i>fyrd</i> usually served for two months, except in emergencies. It was rare for the whole national <i>fyrd</i> to be called out; between 1046 and 1065 it was only done three times, in 1051, 1052, and 1065.<sup id="cite_ref-Marren55_28-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marren55-28">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> The king also had a group of personal armsmen, known as <a href="/wiki/Housecarl" title="Housecarl">housecarls</a>, who formed the backbone of the royal forces. Some earls also had their own forces of housecarls. <a href="/wiki/Thegn" title="Thegn">Thegns</a>, the local landowning elites, either fought with the royal housecarls or attached themselves to the forces of an earl or other magnate.<sup id="cite_ref-Source69_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Source69-27">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> The <i>fyrd</i> and the housecarls both fought on foot, with the major difference between them being the housecarls\' superior armour. The English army does not appear to have had a significant number of archers.<sup id="cite_ref-Gravett28_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gravett28-30">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Harold had spent mid-1066 on the south coast with a large army and fleet waiting for William to invade. The bulk of his forces were militia who needed to harvest their crops, so on 8 September Harold dismissed the militia and the fleet.<sup id="cite_ref-Harold145_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harold145-31">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> Learning of the Norwegian invasion he rushed north, gathering forces as he went, and took the Norwegians by surprise, defeating them at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Stamford_Bridge" title="Battle of Stamford Bridge">Battle of Stamford Bridge</a> on 25 September. Harald Hardrada and Tostig were killed, and the Norwegians suffered such great losses that only 24 of the original 300 ships were required to carry away the survivors. The English victory came at great cost, as Harold\'s army was left in a battered and weakened state.<sup id="cite_ref-Harold158_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harold158-32">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span id="William.27s_preparations_and_landing"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="William\'s_preparations_and_landing">William\'s preparations and landing</span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Pevensey_castle.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Pevensey_castle.jpg/220px-Pevensey_castle.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Pevensey_castle.jpg/330px-Pevensey_castle.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Pevensey_castle.jpg/440px-Pevensey_castle.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="427" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Pevensey_castle.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Interior ruins at <a href="/wiki/Pevensey_Castle" title="Pevensey Castle">Pevensey Castle</a>, some of which date to shortly after the Battle of Hastings<sup id="cite_ref-Castles252_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Castles252-33">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>\n<p>William assembled a large invasion fleet and an army gathered from Normandy and the rest of France, including large contingents from <a href="/wiki/Brittany" title="Brittany">Brittany</a> and Flanders.<sup id="cite_ref-Bates79_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bates79-34">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> He spent almost nine months on his preparations, as he had to construct a fleet from nothing.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;e&#93;</a></sup> According to some Norman chronicles, he also secured diplomatic support, although the accuracy of the reports has been a matter of historical debate. The most famous claim is that Pope <a href="/wiki/Pope_Alexander_II" title="Pope Alexander II">Alexander II</a> gave a papal banner as a token of support, which only appears in <a href="/wiki/William_of_Poitiers" title="William of Poitiers">William of Poitiers</a>\'s account, and not in more contemporary narratives.<sup id="cite_ref-Huscroft120_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Huscroft120-38">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> In April 1066 <a href="/wiki/Halley%27s_Comet" title="Halley&#39;s Comet">Halley\'s Comet</a> appeared in the sky, and was widely reported throughout Europe. Contemporary accounts connected the comet\'s appearance with the succession crisis in England.<sup id="cite_ref-Douglas181_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Douglas181-39">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;f&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>William mustered his forces at <a href="/wiki/Saint-Valery-sur-Somme" title="Saint-Valery-sur-Somme">Saint-Valery-sur-Somme</a>, and was ready to cross the English Channel by about 12 August.<sup id="cite_ref-Douglas192_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Douglas192-42">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> But the crossing was delayed, either because of unfavourable weather or to avoid being intercepted by the powerful English fleet. The Normans crossed to England a few days after Harold\'s victory over the Norwegians, following the dispersal of Harold\'s naval force, and landed at <a href="/wiki/Pevensey" title="Pevensey">Pevensey</a> in Sussex on 28 September.<sup id="cite_ref-Bates79_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bates79-34">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;g&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;h&#93;</a></sup> A few ships were blown off course and landed at <a href="/wiki/Romney_Marsh" title="Romney Marsh">Romney</a>, where the Normans fought the local <i>fyrd</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Marren91_36-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marren91-36">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> After landing, William\'s forces built a wooden castle at Hastings, from which they raided the surrounding area.<sup id="cite_ref-Bates79_34-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bates79-34">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> More fortifications were erected at Pevensey.<sup id="cite_ref-Lawson179_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lawson179-59">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Norman_forces_at_Hastings">Norman forces at Hastings</span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:292px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bayeux_Tapestry_scene51_Battle_of_Hastings_Norman_knights_and_archers.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/Bayeux_Tapestry_scene51_Battle_of_Hastings_Norman_knights_and_archers.jpg/290px-Bayeux_Tapestry_scene51_Battle_of_Hastings_Norman_knights_and_archers.jpg" width="290" height="105" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/Bayeux_Tapestry_scene51_Battle_of_Hastings_Norman_knights_and_archers.jpg/435px-Bayeux_Tapestry_scene51_Battle_of_Hastings_Norman_knights_and_archers.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/Bayeux_Tapestry_scene51_Battle_of_Hastings_Norman_knights_and_archers.jpg/580px-Bayeux_Tapestry_scene51_Battle_of_Hastings_Norman_knights_and_archers.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4212" data-file-height="1526" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bayeux_Tapestry_scene51_Battle_of_Hastings_Norman_knights_and_archers.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Norman knights and archers at the Battle of Hastings depicted in the <a href="/wiki/Bayeux_Tapestry" title="Bayeux Tapestry">Bayeux Tapestry</a></div></div></div>\n<p>The exact numbers and composition of William\'s force are unknown.<sup id="cite_ref-Gravett20_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gravett20-35">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> A contemporary document claims that William had 776 ships, but this may be an inflated figure.<sup id="cite_ref-Bennett25_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bennett25-60">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> Figures given by contemporary writers for the size of the army are highly exaggerated, varying from 14,000 to 150,000.<sup id="cite_ref-Lawson163_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lawson163-61">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> Modern historians have offered a range of estimates for the size of William\'s forces: 7,000\xe2\x80\x938,000 men, 1,000\xe2\x80\x932,000 of them cavalry;<sup id="cite_ref-Bennet26_62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bennet26-62">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> 10,000\xe2\x80\x9312,000 men;<sup id="cite_ref-Lawson163_61-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lawson163-61">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> 10,000 men, 3,000 of them cavalry;<sup id="cite_ref-Marren89_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marren89-63">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> or 7500 men.<sup id="cite_ref-Gravett20_35-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gravett20-35">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> The army consisted of cavalry, infantry, and archers or crossbowmen, with about equal numbers of cavalry and archers and the foot soldiers equal in number to the other two types combined.<sup id="cite_ref-Gravett27_64-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gravett27-64">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> Later lists of <a href="/wiki/Companions_of_William_the_Conqueror" title="Companions of William the Conqueror">companions of William the Conqueror</a> are extant, but most are padded with extra names; only about 35 named individuals can be reliably identified as having been with William at Hastings.<sup id="cite_ref-Gravett20_35-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gravett20-35">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Marren108_65-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marren108-65">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">&#91;i&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The main armour used was <a href="/wiki/Chainmail" class="mw-redirect" title="Chainmail">chainmail</a> hauberks, usually knee-length, with slits to allow riding, some with sleeves to the elbows. Some hauberks may have been made of scales attached to a tunic, with the scales made of metal, horn or hardened leather. Headgear was usually a conical metal helmet with a band of metal extending down to protect the nose.<sup id="cite_ref-Gravett15_68-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gravett15-68">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup> Horsemen and infantry carried shields. The infantryman\'s shield was usually round and made of wood, with reinforcement of metal. Horsemen had changed to a kite-shaped shield and were usually armed with a lance. The couched lance, carried tucked against the body under the right arm, was a relatively new refinement and was probably not used at Hastings; the terrain was unfavourable for long cavalry charges. Both the infantry and cavalry usually fought with a straight sword, long and double-edged. The infantry could also use javelins and long spears.<sup id="cite_ref-Gravett22_69-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gravett22-69">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup> Some of the cavalry may have used a mace instead of a sword. Archers would have used a <a href="/wiki/Self_bow" title="Self bow">self bow</a> or a crossbow, and most would not have had armour.<sup id="cite_ref-Gravett24_70-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gravett24-70">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Harold_moves_south">Harold moves south</span></h2>\n<p>After defeating his brother Tostig and Harald Hardrada in the north, Harold left much of his forces in the north, including Morcar and Edwin, and marched the rest of his army south to deal with the threatened Norman invasion.<sup id="cite_ref-Carpenter72_71-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carpenter72-71">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> It is unclear when Harold learned of William\'s landing, but it was probably while he was travelling south. Harold stopped in London, and was there for about a week before Hastings, so it is likely that he spent about a week on his march south, averaging about 27 miles (43 kilometres) per day,<sup id="cite_ref-Marren93_72-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marren93-72">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup> for the approximately 200 miles (320 kilometres).<sup id="cite_ref-Huscroft124_73-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Huscroft124-73">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup> Harold camped at Caldbec Hill on the night of 13 October, near what was described as a "hoar-apple tree". This location was about 8 miles (13 kilometres) from William\'s castle at Hastings.<sup id="cite_ref-Marren94_74-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marren94-74">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">&#91;j&#93;</a></sup> Some of the early contemporary French accounts mention an emissary or emissaries sent by Harold to William, which is likely. Nothing came of these efforts.<sup id="cite_ref-Lawson180_76-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lawson180-76">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Although Harold attempted to surprise the Normans, William\'s scouts reported the English arrival to the duke. The exact events preceding the battle are obscure, with contradictory accounts in the sources, but all agree that William led his army from his castle and advanced towards the enemy.<sup id="cite_ref-Lawson180_76-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lawson180-76">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup> Harold had taken a defensive position at the top of <a href="/wiki/Senlac_Hill" title="Senlac Hill">Senlac Hill</a> (present-day Battle, East Sussex), about 6 miles (9.7 kilometres) from William\'s castle at Hastings.<sup id="cite_ref-Marren99_77-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marren99-77">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="English_forces_at_Hastings">English forces at Hastings</span></h3>\n<p>The exact number of soldiers in Harold\'s army is unknown. The contemporary records do not give reliable figures; some Norman sources give 400,000 to 1,200,000 men on Harold\'s side.<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">&#91;k&#93;</a></sup> The English sources generally give very low figures for Harold\'s army, perhaps to make the English defeat seem less devastating.<sup id="cite_ref-Lawson128_80-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lawson128-80">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup> Recent historians have suggested figures of between 5,000 and 13,000 for Harold\'s army at Hastings,<sup id="cite_ref-Lawson130_81-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lawson130-81">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup> and most modern historians argue for a figure of 7,000\xe2\x80\x938,000 English troops.<sup id="cite_ref-Gravett28_30-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gravett28-30">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Marren105_82-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marren105-82">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup> These men would have been a mix of the <i>fyrd</i> and housecarls. Few individual Englishmen are known to have been at Hastings;<sup id="cite_ref-Gravett20_35-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gravett20-35">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> about 20 named individuals can reasonably be assumed to have fought with Harold at Hastings, including Harold\'s brothers <a href="/wiki/Gyrth" class="mw-redirect" title="Gyrth">Gyrth</a> and <a href="/wiki/Leofwine_Godwinson" title="Leofwine Godwinson">Leofwine</a> and two other relatives.<sup id="cite_ref-Marren107_66-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marren107-66">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-83" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83">&#91;l&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bayeuxtapestryscene52.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Bayeuxtapestryscene52.jpg/220px-Bayeuxtapestryscene52.jpg" width="220" height="170" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Bayeuxtapestryscene52.jpg/330px-Bayeuxtapestryscene52.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Bayeuxtapestryscene52.jpg/440px-Bayeuxtapestryscene52.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1800" data-file-height="1389" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bayeuxtapestryscene52.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Scene from the Bayeux Tapestry depicting mounted Norman soldiers attacking Anglo-Saxons who are fighting on foot in a shield wall</div></div></div>\n<p>The English army consisted entirely of infantry. It is possible that some of the higher class members of the army rode to battle, but when battle was joined they dismounted to fight on foot.<sup id="cite_ref-85" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-85">&#91;m&#93;</a></sup> The core of the army was made up of housecarls, full-time professional soldiers. Their armour consisted of a conical helmet, a <a href="/wiki/Mail_(armour)" title="Mail (armour)">mail</a> <a href="/wiki/Hauberk" title="Hauberk">hauberk</a>, and a shield, which might be either kite-shaped or round.<sup id="cite_ref-Gravett29_84-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gravett29-84">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup> Most housecarls fought with the two-handed <a href="/wiki/Dane_Axe" class="mw-redirect" title="Dane Axe">Danish battleaxe</a>, but they could also carry a sword.<sup id="cite_ref-Marren52_86-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marren52-86">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup> The rest of the army was made up of levies from the <i>fyrd</i>, also infantry but more lightly armoured and not professionals. Most of the infantry would have formed part of the <a href="/wiki/Shield_wall" title="Shield wall">shield wall</a>, in which all the men in the front ranks locked their shields together. Behind them would have been axemen and men with javelins as well as archers.<sup id="cite_ref-Fighting21_87-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fighting21-87">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Battle">Battle</span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Background_and_location">Background and location</span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Schlachtfeld_Schlacht_bei_Hastings_juni09.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Schlachtfeld_Schlacht_bei_Hastings_juni09.JPG/220px-Schlachtfeld_Schlacht_bei_Hastings_juni09.JPG" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Schlachtfeld_Schlacht_bei_Hastings_juni09.JPG/330px-Schlachtfeld_Schlacht_bei_Hastings_juni09.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Schlachtfeld_Schlacht_bei_Hastings_juni09.JPG/440px-Schlachtfeld_Schlacht_bei_Hastings_juni09.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1600" data-file-height="1200" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Schlachtfeld_Schlacht_bei_Hastings_juni09.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The battlefield from the north side</div></div></div>\n<p>Because many of the primary accounts contradict each other at times, it is impossible to provide a description of the battle that is beyond dispute.<sup id="cite_ref-Lawson183_88-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lawson183-88">&#91;75&#93;</a></sup> The only undisputed facts are that the fighting began at 9&#160;am on Saturday 14 October 1066 and that the battle lasted until dusk.<sup id="cite_ref-Marren114_89-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marren114-89">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup> Sunset on the day of the battle was at 4:54&#160;pm, with the battlefield mostly dark by 5:54&#160;pm and in full darkness by 6:24&#160;pm. Moonrise that night was not until 11:12&#160;pm, so once the sun set, there was little light on the battlefield.<sup id="cite_ref-Lawson212_90-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lawson212-90">&#91;77&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/William_of_Jumi%C3%A8ges" title="William of Jumi\xc3\xa8ges">William of Jumi\xc3\xa8ges</a> reports that Duke William kept his army armed and ready against a surprise night attack for the entire night before.<sup id="cite_ref-Lawson183_88-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lawson183-88">&#91;75&#93;</a></sup> The battle took place 7 miles (11&#160;km) north of Hastings at the present-day town of <a href="/wiki/Battle,_East_Sussex" title="Battle, East Sussex">Battle</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Gravett91_91-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gravett91-91">&#91;78&#93;</a></sup> between two hills&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 Caldbec Hill to the north and Telham Hill to the south. The area was heavily wooded, with a marsh nearby.<sup id="cite_ref-Marren101_92-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marren101-92">&#91;79&#93;</a></sup> The name traditionally given to the battle is unusual&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 there were several settlements much closer to the battlefield than Hastings. The <i><a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon_Chronicle" title="Anglo-Saxon Chronicle">Anglo-Saxon Chronicle</a></i> called it the battle "at the hoary apple tree". Within 40 years, the battle was described by the Anglo-Norman chronicler <a href="/wiki/Orderic_Vitalis" title="Orderic Vitalis">Orderic Vitalis</a> as "Senlac",<sup id="cite_ref-95" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-95">&#91;n&#93;</a></sup> a Norman-French adaptation of the Old English word "Sandlacu", which means "sandy water".<sup id="cite_ref-96" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-96">&#91;o&#93;</a></sup> This may have been the name of the stream that crosses the battlefield.<sup id="cite_ref-98" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-98">&#91;p&#93;</a></sup> The battle was already being referred to as "bellum Hasestingas" or "Battle of Hastings" by 1087, in the <i><a href="/wiki/Domesday_Book" title="Domesday Book">Domesday Book</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Marren157_99-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marren157-99">&#91;83&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Sunrise was at 6:48&#160;am that morning, and reports of the day record that it was unusually bright.<sup id="cite_ref-Gravett59_100-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gravett59-100">&#91;84&#93;</a></sup> The weather conditions are not recorded.<sup id="cite_ref-Marren116_101-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marren116-101">&#91;85&#93;</a></sup> The route that the English army took to the battlefield is not known precisely. Several roads are possible: one, an old Roman road that ran from Rochester to Hastings has long been favoured because of a large coin hoard found nearby in 1876. Another possibility is a Roman road between London and Lewes and then over local tracks to the battlefield.<sup id="cite_ref-Lawson183_88-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lawson183-88">&#91;75&#93;</a></sup> Some accounts of the battle indicate that the Normans advanced from Hastings to the battlefield, but the contemporary account of William of Jumi\xc3\xa8ges places the Normans at the site of the battle the night before.<sup id="cite_ref-Lawson186_102-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lawson186-102">&#91;86&#93;</a></sup> Most historians incline towards the former view,<sup id="cite_ref-Marren99_77-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marren99-77">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Gravett59_100-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gravett59-100">&#91;84&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Huscroft125_103-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Huscroft125-103">&#91;87&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Bennett40_104-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bennett40-104">&#91;88&#93;</a></sup> but M. K. Lawson argues that William of Jumi\xc3\xa8ges\'s account is correct.<sup id="cite_ref-Lawson186_102-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lawson186-102">&#91;86&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dispositions_of_forces_and_tactics">Dispositions of forces and tactics</span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Battle_of_Hastings,_1066.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Battle_of_Hastings%2C_1066.png/220px-Battle_of_Hastings%2C_1066.png" width="220" height="149" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Battle_of_Hastings%2C_1066.png/330px-Battle_of_Hastings%2C_1066.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Battle_of_Hastings%2C_1066.png/440px-Battle_of_Hastings%2C_1066.png 2x" data-file-width="752" data-file-height="508" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Battle_of_Hastings,_1066.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Battle dispositions</div></div></div>\n<p>Harold\'s forces deployed in a small, dense formation at the top of steep slope,<sup id="cite_ref-Gravett59_100-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gravett59-100">&#91;84&#93;</a></sup> with their flanks protected by woods and marshy ground in front of them.<sup id="cite_ref-Bennett40_104-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bennett40-104">&#91;88&#93;</a></sup> The line may have extended far enough to be anchored on a nearby stream.<sup id="cite_ref-Lawson190_105-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lawson190-105">&#91;89&#93;</a></sup> The English formed a shield wall, with the front ranks holding their shields close together or even overlapping to provide protection from attack.<sup id="cite_ref-Gravett64_106-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gravett64-106">&#91;90&#93;</a></sup> Sources differ on the exact site that the English fought on: some sources state the site of the abbey,<sup id="cite_ref-Hare84_107-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hare84-107">&#91;91&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-EngHer05Oct2016_108-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EngHer05Oct2016-108">&#91;92&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-BattlefieldsTrust05Oct2016_109-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BattlefieldsTrust05Oct2016-109">&#91;93&#93;</a></sup> but some newer sources suggest it was Caldbec Hill.<sup id="cite_ref-Lawson190_105-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lawson190-105">&#91;89&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Gravett59_100-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gravett59-100">&#91;84&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>More is known about the Norman deployment.<sup id="cite_ref-Lawson192_110-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lawson192-110">&#91;94&#93;</a></sup> Duke William appears to have arranged his forces in three groups, or "battles", which roughly corresponded to their origins. The left units were the <a href="/wiki/Breton_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Breton people">Bretons</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Bennett41_111-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bennett41-111">&#91;95&#93;</a></sup> along with those from <a href="/wiki/Anjou" title="Anjou">Anjou</a>, <a href="/wiki/Poitou" title="Poitou">Poitou</a> and <a href="/wiki/Maine_(province)" title="Maine (province)">Maine</a>. This division was led by <a href="/wiki/Alan_Rufus" title="Alan Rufus">Alan the Red</a>, a relative of the Breton count.<sup id="cite_ref-Gravett64_106-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gravett64-106">&#91;90&#93;</a></sup> The centre was held by the Normans,<sup id="cite_ref-Bennett41_111-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bennett41-111">&#91;95&#93;</a></sup> under the direct command of the duke and with many of his relatives and kinsmen grouped around the ducal party.<sup id="cite_ref-Gravett64_106-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gravett64-106">&#91;90&#93;</a></sup> The final division, on the right, consisted of the Frenchmen,<sup id="cite_ref-Bennett41_111-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bennett41-111">&#91;95&#93;</a></sup> along with some men from <a href="/wiki/Picardy" title="Picardy">Picardy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Boulogne-sur-Mer" title="Boulogne-sur-Mer">Boulogne</a>, and <a href="/wiki/County_of_Flanders" title="County of Flanders">Flanders</a>. The right was commanded by <a href="/wiki/William_fitzOsbern" class="mw-redirect" title="William fitzOsbern">William fitzOsbern</a> and Count <a href="/wiki/Eustace_II,_Count_of_Boulogne" title="Eustace II, Count of Boulogne">Eustace II of Boulogne</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Gravett64_106-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gravett64-106">&#91;90&#93;</a></sup> The front lines were made up of archers, with a line of foot soldiers armed with spears behind.<sup id="cite_ref-Bennett41_111-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bennett41-111">&#91;95&#93;</a></sup> There were probably a few crossbowmen and slingers in with the archers.<sup id="cite_ref-Gravett64_106-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gravett64-106">&#91;90&#93;</a></sup> The cavalry was held in reserve,<sup id="cite_ref-Bennett41_111-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bennett41-111">&#91;95&#93;</a></sup> and a small group of clergymen and servants situated at the base of Telham Hill was not expected to take part in the fighting.<sup id="cite_ref-Gravett64_106-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gravett64-106">&#91;90&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>William\'s disposition of his forces implies that he planned to open the battle with archers in the front rank weakening the enemy with arrows, followed by infantry who would engage in close combat. The infantry would create openings in the English lines that could be exploited by a cavalry charge to break through the English forces and pursue the fleeing soldiers.<sup id="cite_ref-Gravett64_106-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gravett64-106">&#91;90&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Beginning_of_the_battle">Beginning of the battle</span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Hastingsbattlefieldandbattleabbeyruinsbeta.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Hastingsbattlefieldandbattleabbeyruinsbeta.jpg/220px-Hastingsbattlefieldandbattleabbeyruinsbeta.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Hastingsbattlefieldandbattleabbeyruinsbeta.jpg/330px-Hastingsbattlefieldandbattleabbeyruinsbeta.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Hastingsbattlefieldandbattleabbeyruinsbeta.jpg/440px-Hastingsbattlefieldandbattleabbeyruinsbeta.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3888" data-file-height="2592" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Hastingsbattlefieldandbattleabbeyruinsbeta.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>View of the battlefield looking towards Senlac Hill</div></div></div>\n<p>The battle opened with the Norman archers shooting uphill at the English shield wall, to little effect. The uphill angle meant that the arrows either bounced off the shields of the English or overshot their targets and flew over the top of the hill.<sup id="cite_ref-Bennett41_111-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bennett41-111">&#91;95&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-112" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-112">&#91;q&#93;</a></sup>  The lack of English archers hampered the Norman archers, as there were few English arrows to be gathered up and reused.<sup id="cite_ref-Gravett65_113-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gravett65-113">&#91;96&#93;</a></sup> After the attack from the archers, William sent the spearmen forward to attack the English. They were met with a barrage of missiles, not arrows but spears, axes and stones.<sup id="cite_ref-Bennett41_111-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bennett41-111">&#91;95&#93;</a></sup> The infantry was unable to force openings in the shield wall, and the cavalry advanced in support.<sup id="cite_ref-Gravett65_113-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gravett65-113">&#91;96&#93;</a></sup> The cavalry also failed to make headway, and a general retreat began, blamed on the Breton division on William\'s left.<sup id="cite_ref-Bennett42_114-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bennett42-114">&#91;97&#93;</a></sup> A rumour started that the duke had been killed, which added to the confusion. The English forces began to pursue the fleeing invaders, but William rode through his forces, showing his face and yelling that he was still alive.<sup id="cite_ref-Gravett68_115-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gravett68-115">&#91;98&#93;</a></sup> The duke then led a counter-attack against the pursuing English forces; some of the English rallied on a hillock before being overwhelmed.<sup id="cite_ref-Bennett42_114-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bennett42-114">&#91;97&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>It is not known whether the English pursuit was ordered by Harold or if it was spontaneous. Wace relates that Harold ordered his men to stay in their formations but no other account gives this detail. The Bayeux Tapestry depicts the death of Harold\'s brothers Gyrth and Leofwine occurring just before the fight around the hillock. This may mean that the two brothers led the pursuit.<sup id="cite_ref-Gravett72_116-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gravett72-116">&#91;99&#93;</a></sup> The <i><a href="/wiki/Carmen_de_Hastingae_Proelio" title="Carmen de Hastingae Proelio">Carmen de Hastingae Proelio</a></i> relates a different story for the death of Gyrth, stating that the duke slew Harold\'s brother in combat, perhaps thinking that Gyrth was Harold. William of Poitiers states that the bodies of Gyrth and Leofwine were found near Harold\'s, implying that they died late in the battle. It is possible that if the two brothers died early in the fighting their bodies were taken to Harold, thus accounting for their being found near his body after the battle. The military historian Peter Marren speculates that if Gyrth and Leofwine died early in the battle, that may have influenced Harold to stand and fight to the end.<sup id="cite_ref-Marren127_117-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marren127-117">&#91;100&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Feigned_flights">Feigned flights</span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bayeuxtapestryscene53.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Bayeuxtapestryscene53.jpg/220px-Bayeuxtapestryscene53.jpg" width="220" height="227" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Bayeuxtapestryscene53.jpg/330px-Bayeuxtapestryscene53.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Bayeuxtapestryscene53.jpg/440px-Bayeuxtapestryscene53.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1792" data-file-height="1851" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bayeuxtapestryscene53.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Scene from the Bayeux Tapestry showing mounted Norman cavalrymen fighting Anglo-Saxon infantry</div></div></div>\n<p>A lull probably occurred early in the afternoon, and a break for rest and food would probably have been needed.<sup id="cite_ref-Gravett72_116-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gravett72-116">&#91;99&#93;</a></sup> William may have also needed time to implement a new strategy, which may have been inspired by the English pursuit and subsequent rout by the Normans. If the Normans could send their cavalry against the shield wall and then draw the English into more pursuits, breaks in the English line might form.<sup id="cite_ref-Bennett43_118-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bennett43-118">&#91;101&#93;</a></sup> William of Poitiers says the tactic was used twice. Although arguments have been made that the chroniclers\' accounts of this tactic were meant to excuse the flight of the Norman troops from battle, this is unlikely as the earlier flight was not glossed over. It was a tactic used by other Norman armies during the period.<sup id="cite_ref-Gravett72_116-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gravett72-116">&#91;99&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-119" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-119">&#91;r&#93;</a></sup> Some historians have argued that the story of the use of feigned flight as a deliberate tactic was invented after the battle; most historians agree that it was used by the Normans at Hastings.<sup id="cite_ref-Marren130_120-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marren130-120">&#91;102&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Although the feigned flights did not break the lines, they probably thinned out the housecarls in the English shield wall. The housecarls were replaced with members of the <i>fyrd</i>, and the shield wall held.<sup id="cite_ref-Gravett72_116-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gravett72-116">&#91;99&#93;</a></sup> Archers appear to have been used again before and during an assault by the cavalry and infantry led by the duke. Although 12th-century sources state that the archers were ordered to shoot at a high angle to shoot over the front of the shield wall, there is no trace of such an action in the more contemporary accounts.<sup id="cite_ref-Gravett76_121-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gravett76-121">&#91;103&#93;</a></sup> It is not known how many assaults were launched against the English lines, but some sources record various actions by both Normans and Englishmen that took place during the afternoon\'s fighting.<sup id="cite_ref-Marren131_122-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marren131-122">&#91;104&#93;</a></sup> The <i>Carmen</i> claims that Duke William had two horses killed under him during the fighting, but William of Poitiers\'s account states that it was three.<sup id="cite_ref-Marren135_123-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marren135-123">&#91;105&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Death_of_Harold">Death of Harold</span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Harold_stone.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Harold_stone.JPG/220px-Harold_stone.JPG" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Harold_stone.JPG/330px-Harold_stone.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Harold_stone.JPG/440px-Harold_stone.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="768" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Harold_stone.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Stone marking the spot of the high altar at Battle Abbey, where Harold died<sup id="cite_ref-Gravett79_124-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gravett79-124">&#91;106&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>\n<p>Harold appears to have died late in the battle, although accounts in the various sources are contradictory. William of Poitiers only mentions his death, without giving any details on how it occurred. The Tapestry is not helpful, as it shows a figure holding an arrow sticking out of his eye next to a falling fighter being hit with a sword. Over both figures is a statement "Here King Harold has been killed".<sup id="cite_ref-Gravett76_121-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gravett76-121">&#91;103&#93;</a></sup> It is not clear which figure is meant to be Harold, or if both are meant.<sup id="cite_ref-Lawson207_125-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lawson207-125">&#91;107&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-126" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-126">&#91;s&#93;</a></sup> The earliest written mention of the traditional account of Harold dying from an arrow to the eye dates to the 1080s from a history of the Normans written by an Italian monk, <a href="/wiki/Amatus_of_Montecassino" title="Amatus of Montecassino">Amatus of Montecassino</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Marren138_127-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marren138-127">&#91;108&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-129" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-129">&#91;t&#93;</a></sup> William of Malmesbury stated that Harold died from an arrow to the eye that went into the brain, and that a knight wounded Harold at the same time. Wace repeats the arrow-to-the-eye account. The <i>Carmen</i> states that Duke William killed Harold, but this is unlikely, as such a feat would have been recorded elsewhere.<sup id="cite_ref-Gravett76_121-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gravett76-121">&#91;103&#93;</a></sup> The account of William of Jumi\xc3\xa8ges is even more unlikely, as it has Harold dying in the morning, during the first fighting. The <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chronicle_of_Battle_Abbey&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Chronicle of Battle Abbey (page does not exist)">Chronicle of Battle Abbey</a></i> states that no one knew who killed Harold, as it happened in the press of battle.<sup id="cite_ref-Marren137_130-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marren137-130">&#91;110&#93;</a></sup> A modern biographer of Harold, Ian Walker, states that Harold probably died from an arrow in the eye, although he also says it is possible that Harold was struck down by a Norman knight while mortally wounded in the eye.<sup id="cite_ref-Walker179_131-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Walker179-131">&#91;111&#93;</a></sup> Another biographer of Harold, Peter Rex, after discussing the various accounts, concludes that it is not possible to declare how Harold died.<sup id="cite_ref-Rex256_128-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rex256-128">&#91;109&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Harold\'s death left the English forces leaderless, and they began to collapse.<sup id="cite_ref-Bennett43_118-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bennett43-118">&#91;101&#93;</a></sup> Many of them fled, but the soldiers of the royal household gathered around Harold\'s body and fought to the end.<sup id="cite_ref-Gravett76_121-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gravett76-121">&#91;103&#93;</a></sup> The Normans began to pursue the fleeing troops, and except for a rearguard action at a site known as the "Malfosse", the battle was over.<sup id="cite_ref-Bennett43_118-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bennett43-118">&#91;101&#93;</a></sup> Exactly what happened at the Malfosse, or "Evil Ditch", and where it took place, is unclear. It occurred at a small fortification or set of trenches where some Englishmen rallied and seriously wounded Eustace of Boulogne before being defeated by the Normans.<sup id="cite_ref-Gravett80_132-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gravett80-132">&#91;112&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reasons_for_the_outcome">Reasons for the outcome</span></h3>\n<p>Harold\'s defeat was probably due to several circumstances. One was the need to defend against two almost simultaneous invasions. The fact that Harold had dismissed his forces in southern England on 8 September also contributed to the defeat. Many historians fault Harold for hurrying south and not gathering more forces before confronting William at Hastings, although it is not clear that the English forces were insufficient to deal with William\'s forces.<sup id="cite_ref-Lawson217_133-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lawson217-133">&#91;113&#93;</a></sup> Against these arguments for an exhausted English army, the length of the battle, which lasted an entire day, shows that the English forces were not tired by their long march.<sup id="cite_ref-Huscroft130_134-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Huscroft130-134">&#91;114&#93;</a></sup> Tied in with the speed of Harold\'s advance to Hastings is the possibility Harold may not have trusted Earls Edwin of Mercia and Morcar of Northumbria once their enemy Tostig had been defeated, and declined to bring them and their forces south.<sup id="cite_ref-Lawson217_133-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lawson217-133">&#91;113&#93;</a></sup> Modern historians have pointed out that one reason for Harold\'s rush to battle was to contain William\'s depredations and keep him from breaking free of his beachhead.<sup id="cite_ref-Marren152_135-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marren152-135">&#91;115&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Most of the blame for the defeat probably lies in the events of the battle.<sup id="cite_ref-Lawson217_133-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lawson217-133">&#91;113&#93;</a></sup> William was the more experienced military leader,<sup id="cite_ref-Lawson219_136-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lawson219-136">&#91;116&#93;</a></sup> and in addition the lack of cavalry on the English side allowed Harold fewer tactical options.<sup id="cite_ref-Huscroft130_134-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Huscroft130-134">&#91;114&#93;</a></sup> Some writers have criticised Harold for not exploiting the opportunity offered by the rumoured death of William early in the battle.<sup id="cite_ref-Walker180_137-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Walker180-137">&#91;117&#93;</a></sup> The English appear to have erred in not staying strictly on the defensive, for when they pursued the retreating Normans they exposed their flanks to attack. Whether this was due to the inexperience of the English commanders or the indiscipline of the English soldiers is unclear.<sup id="cite_ref-Lawson219_136-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lawson219-136">&#91;116&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-139" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-139">&#91;u&#93;</a></sup> In the end, Harold\'s death appears to have been decisive, as it signalled the break-up of the English forces in disarray.<sup id="cite_ref-Huscroft130_134-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Huscroft130-134">&#91;114&#93;</a></sup> The historian <a href="/wiki/David_Nicolle" title="David Nicolle">David Nicolle</a> said of the battle that William\'s army "demonstrated&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 not without difficulty&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 the superiority of Norman-French mixed cavalry and infantry tactics over the Germanic-Scandinavian infantry traditions of the Anglo-Saxons."<sup id="cite_ref-Normans20_140-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Normans20-140">&#91;119&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Norman_conquest_of_England#Consequences" title="Norman conquest of England">Norman conquest of England \xc2\xa7&#160;Consequences</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Battleabbey-wyrdlight-0190.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Battleabbey-wyrdlight-0190.jpg/220px-Battleabbey-wyrdlight-0190.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Battleabbey-wyrdlight-0190.jpg/330px-Battleabbey-wyrdlight-0190.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Battleabbey-wyrdlight-0190.jpg/440px-Battleabbey-wyrdlight-0190.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1000" data-file-height="667" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Battleabbey-wyrdlight-0190.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Ruins of the monks\' dormitory at Battle Abbey</div></div></div>\n<p>The day after the battle, Harold\'s body was identified, either by his armour or marks on his body.<sup id="cite_ref-141" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-141">&#91;v&#93;</a></sup>  His personal standard was presented to William,<sup id="cite_ref-Rex253_142-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rex253-142">&#91;120&#93;</a></sup> and later sent to the papacy.<sup id="cite_ref-Gravett76_121-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gravett76-121">&#91;103&#93;</a></sup> The bodies of the English dead, including some of Harold\'s brothers and housecarls, were left on the battlefield,<sup id="cite_ref-Huscroft131_143-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Huscroft131-143">&#91;121&#93;</a></sup> although some were removed by relatives later.<sup id="cite_ref-Gravett81_144-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gravett81-144">&#91;122&#93;</a></sup> The Norman dead were buried in a large communal grave, which has not been found.<sup id="cite_ref-Marren146_145-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marren146-145">&#91;123&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-146" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-146">&#91;w&#93;</a></sup> Exact casualty figures are unknown. Of the Englishmen known to be at the battle, the number of dead implies that the death rate was about 50 per cent of those engaged, although this may be too high. Of the named Normans who fought at Hastings, one in seven is stated to have died, but these were all noblemen, and it is probable that the death rate among the common soldiers was higher. Although Orderic Vitalis\'s figures are highly exaggerated,<sup id="cite_ref-148" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-148">&#91;x&#93;</a></sup> his ratio of one in four casualties may be accurate. Marren speculates that perhaps 2,000 Normans and 4,000 Englishmen were killed at Hastings.<sup id="cite_ref-Marren147_147-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marren147-147">&#91;124&#93;</a></sup> Reports stated that some of the English dead were still being found on the hillside years later. Although scholars thought for a long time that remains would not be recoverable, due to the acidic soil, recent finds have changed this view.<sup id="cite_ref-past6_149-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-past6-149">&#91;125&#93;</a></sup> One skeleton that was found in a medieval cemetery, and originally was thought to be associated with the 13th century <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Lewes" title="Battle of Lewes">Battle of Lewes</a> now is thought to be associated with Hastings instead.<sup id="cite_ref-sac148_150-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sac148-150">&#91;126&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-151" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-151">&#91;y&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>One story relates that <a href="/wiki/Gytha_Thorkelsd%C3%B3ttir" title="Gytha Thorkelsd\xc3\xb3ttir">Gytha</a>, Harold\'s mother, offered the victorious duke the weight of her son\'s body in gold for its custody, but was refused. William ordered that Harold\'s body be thrown into the sea, but whether that took place is unclear.<sup id="cite_ref-Huscroft131_143-1" class="reference"><a later claimed that his body had been secretly buried there.<sup id="cite_ref-Huscroft131_143-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Huscroft131-143">&#91;121&#93;</a></sup> Other legends claimed He then travelled north-east along the <a href="/wiki/Chilterns" class="mw-redirect" title="Chilterns">Chilterns</a>, before advancing towards London from the north-west,<sup id="cite_ref-157" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-157">&#91;aa&#93;</a></sup> fighting further engagements against forces from the city. The English leaders surrendered to William at <a href="/wiki/Berkhamsted" title="Berkhamsted">Berkhamsted</a>, Hertfordshire.
