The 2011 UK census showed 42.8% Roman Catholic, 21.1% Presbyterian Church, with the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_Ireland" title="Church of Ireland">Church of Ireland</a> having 16.7% and the Methodist Church 5.0%. Members of other Christian churches comprised 5.8%, 7.9% stated they have no religion or did not state a religion, and members of non-Christian religions were 0.8%.<sup id="cite_ref-2011ksdata_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2011ksdata-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2011ks_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2011ks-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholicism_in_Ireland" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholicism in Ireland">Roman Catholic Church in Ireland</a> is the largest single church, though there is a greater number of Protestants overall. The Church is organised into four provinces though these are not coterminous with the modern political division of Ireland. The seat of the <a href="/wiki/Archbishop_of_Armagh" title="Archbishop of Armagh">Archbishop of Armagh</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Primacy_of_Ireland" title="Primacy of Ireland">Primacy of Ireland</a>, is St. Patrick\'s Cathedral, <a href="/wiki/Armagh" title="Armagh">Armagh</a>.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Presbyterian_Church_in_Ireland" title="Presbyterian Church in Ireland">Presbyterian Church in Ireland</a>, closely linked to the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_Scotland" title="Church of Scotland">Church of Scotland</a> in terms of theology and history, is the second-largest church and largest Protestant denomination. It is followed by the Anglican <a href="/wiki/Church_of_Ireland" title="Church of Ireland">Church of Ireland</a>, which was the state church of Ireland until it was disestablished by the <i><a href="/wiki/Irish_Church_Act_1869" title="Irish Church Act 1869">Irish Church Act 1869</a></i>. In 2002, the much smaller <a href="/wiki/Methodist_Church_in_Ireland" title="Methodist Church in Ireland">Methodist Church in Ireland</a> signed a covenant for greater co-operation and potential ultimate unity with the Church of Ireland.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The Church of Ireland is part of the <a href="/wiki/Anglican_Communion" title="Anglican Communion">Anglican Communion</a>.\n</p><p>Smaller, but growing, Protestant denominations such as the <a href="/wiki/Free_Presbyterian_Church_of_Ulster" title="Free Presbyterian Church of Ulster">Free Presbyterian Church of Ulster</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reformed_Presbyterian_Church_of_Ireland" title="Reformed Presbyterian Church of Ireland">Reformed Presbyterian Church of Ireland</a> amongst Presbyterians and the <a href="/wiki/Open_Brethren" title="Open Brethren">Open Brethren</a> are located in many places. The <a href="/wiki/Association_of_Baptist_Churches_in_Ireland" title="Association of Baptist Churches in Ireland">Association of Baptist Churches in Ireland</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Assemblies_of_God_Ireland" class="mw-redirect" title="Assemblies of God Ireland">Assemblies of God Ireland</a> are also organised on an all-Ireland basis, though in the case of the Assemblies of God this was the result of a recent reorganisation.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Comparison_between_Northern_Ireland_and_Great_Britain">Comparison between Northern Ireland and Great Britain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Religion_in_Northern_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Comparison between Northern Ireland and Great Britain">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In the 2011 census Northern Ireland had substantially more people stating that they were Christian (91.4%) than did England (59.4%), Scotland (53.8%) or Wales (57.6%).<sup id="cite_ref-2011_NI_Census_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2011_NI_Census-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Scotland_2011_census_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Scotland_2011_census-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-England_2011_census_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-England_2011_census-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Wales_2011_census_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wales_2011_census-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The proportion who stated that they had any religion was also higher in Northern Ireland (92.2%) than in England (68.1%), Scotland (56.3%) or Wales (60.3%).<sup id="cite_ref-2011_NI_Census_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2011_NI_Census-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Scotland_2011_census_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Scotland_2011_census-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-England_2011_census_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-England_2011_census-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Wales_2011_census_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wales_2011_census-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> In Northern Ireland those who did not state any religion in the 2011 census amounted to 7.9% of the population, lower than in England (31.9%), Scotland (43.7%) or Wales (39.7%).<sup id="cite_ref-2011_NI_Census_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2011_NI_Census-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Scotland_2011_census_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Scotland_2011_census-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-England_2011_census_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-England_2011_census-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Wales_2011_census_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wales_2011_census-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> This represented an increase from the 2001 census in those stating no religion of 10.6% in Northern Ireland, lower than the increases in England (54.5%), Scotland (38.1%) or Wales (57.6%).<sup id="cite_ref-2011_NI_Census_12-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2011_NI_Census-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Scotland_2011_census_13-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Scotland_2011_census-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-England_2011_census_14-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-England_2011_census-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Wales_2011_census_15-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wales_2011_census-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Secularization" title="Secularization">Secularisation</a> in Northern Ireland has followed different paths within each of the two main communities, being at a more advanced stage within the mainly Protestant community in which it is reflected more often with a formal move away from the churches and by expressing no formal religious attachment, mirroring the pattern in Great Britain, whereas in the mainly Catholic community it is reflected by declining mass attendance but often with retaining a formal Catholic identification, mirroring the pattern in the Republic of Ireland.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Those stating that they had no religion in the 2011 census were concentrated in largely Protestant areas, suggesting that they were mostly from a Protestant background.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Comparison_between_Northern_Ireland_and_the_Republic_of_Ireland">Comparison between Northern Ireland and the Republic of Ireland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Religion_in_Northern_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Comparison between Northern Ireland and the Republic of Ireland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In their respective 2011 censuses Northern Ireland had a lower proportion of people stating that they were Christian (82.3%) than the Republic of Ireland (90.4%) and had a higher proportion of people stating that they had no religion or not indicating a religious belief (16.9%) than the Republic of Ireland (7.6%). While in the 2011 census 84.2% of people in the Republic of Ireland identified themselves as Catholic in the 2011 census in Northern Ireland only 40.8% identified themselves as Catholic.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Minor_religions">Minor religions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Religion_in_Northern_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Minor religions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Islam">Islam</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Religion_in_Northern_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Islam">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Islam_in_Northern_Ireland" title="Islam in Northern Ireland">Islam in Northern Ireland</a></div>\n<p>While there were a small number of <a href="/wiki/Muslim" title="Muslim">Muslims</a> already living in what became Northern Ireland in 1921, the bulk of Muslims in Northern Ireland today come from families who immigrated during the late 20th century.  At the time of the 2001 Census there were 1,943 living in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> though the Belfast Islamic Centre claims that by January 2009, this number had increased to over 4,000.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> The Muslims in Northern Ireland come from over 40 countries of origin, from Western Europe all the way through to the Far East.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> This situation is reflected in comparably complex institutional arrangements.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Judaism">Judaism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Religion_in_Northern_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Judaism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Jews_in_Northern_Ireland" class="mw-redirect" title="History of the Jews in Northern Ireland">History of the Jews in Northern Ireland</a></div>\n<p>The earliest recorded Jew living in Northern Ireland was a tailor by the name of Manuel Lightfoot in 1652. The first Jewish congregation in Northern Ireland, <a href="/wiki/Belfast_Hebrew_Congregation" title="Belfast Hebrew Congregation">Belfast Hebrew Congregation</a>, was founded in 1870. In 2006, there are about 300 Jews living in Northern Ireland.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span contact between Irishmen and the <a href="/wiki/Bah%C3%A1%27%C3%AD_Faith" title="Bah\xc3\xa1&#39;\xc3\xad Faith">Bah\xc3\xa1\'\xc3\xad Faith</a> beyond the island and on the island.<sup elected its first Bah\xc3\xa1\'\xc3\xad <a href="/wiki/Local_Spiritual_Assembly" class="mw-redirect" title="Local Spiritual Assembly">Local Spiritual Assembly</a> in 1949 in Belfast.<sup id="cite_ref-Comm106_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Comm106-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> The Bah\xc3\xa1\'\xc3\xads held an international conference in Dublin in 1982 which was described as class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BCNI-RE-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Neo-paganism">Neo-paganism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hinduism_in_Northern_Ireland" title="Hinduism in Northern Ireland">Hinduism in Northern Ireland</a></div>\n<p>Hinduism is a relatively minor religion in Northern Ireland with only around 200 <a href="/wiki/Hinduism" title="Hinduism">Hindu</a> families in the region.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Religion_in_Northern_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Troubles">The Troubles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Religion_in_Northern_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: The Troubles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/The_Troubles" title="The Troubles">The Troubles</a></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/The_Troubles" title="The Troubles">The Troubles</a> was a period of ethno-political conflict in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a> which spilled over at various times into Great Britain and the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Republic of Ireland</a>. The duration of the Troubles is conventionally dated from approximately 1968 to the signing of the <a href="/wiki/Belfast_Agreement" class="mw-redirect" title="Belfast Agreement">Belfast Agreement</a> in 1998. Violence nonetheless continued beyond this period and still manifests on a small-scale basis.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The principal issues at stake in the Troubles were the <a href="/wiki/Partition_of_Ireland" title="Partition of Ireland">constitutional status of Northern Ireland</a> and the relationship between the mainly-Protestant Unionist and mainly-Catholic Nationalist communities in Northern Ireland. The Troubles had both political and military (or paramilitary) dimensions.  Its participants included politicians and political activists on both sides, <a href="/wiki/Irish_republicanism" title="Irish republicanism">republican</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ulster_loyalism" title="Ulster loyalism">loyalist</a> paramilitary organisations, the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Ulster_Constabulary" title="Royal Ulster Constabulary">Royal Ulster Constabulary</a> (RUC), the <a href="/wiki/British_Army" title="British Army">British Army</a> and the security forces of the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Republic of Ireland</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Religion_in_Northern_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: See also">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Demography_of_Northern_Ireland" title="Demography of Northern Ireland">Demography of Northern Ireland</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Religion_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Religion in the United Kingdom">Religion in the United Kingdom</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Religion_in_the_Republic_of_Ireland" title="Religion in the Republic of Ireland">Religion in the Republic of Ireland</a></li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Religion_in_Northern_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist columns references-column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 30em;
