style="text-align:right;font-size:115%;padding-top: 0.6em;"><div class="plainlinks hlist navbar mini"><ul><li class="nv-view"><a href="/wiki/Template:Politics_of_Northern_Ireland_1921-72" template">e</abbr></a></li></ul></div></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>The <b>Parliament of Northern Ireland</b> was the <a href="/wiki/Home_Rule" class="mw-redirect" title="Home Rule">Home Rule</a> legislature of <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>, created under the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Ireland_Act_1920" title="Government of Ireland Act 1920">Government of Ireland Act 1920</a>, which sat from 7 June 1921<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> to 30 March 1972, when it was suspended with the introduction of <a href="/wiki/Direct_rule_over_Northern_Ireland" class="mw-redirect" title="Direct rule over Northern Ireland">Direct Rule</a>. It was abolished under the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Constitution_Act_1973" title="Northern Ireland Constitution Act 1973">Northern Ireland Constitution Act 1973</a>.\n</p><p>The Parliament of Northern Ireland was <a href="/wiki/Bicameral" class="mw-redirect" title="Bicameral">bicameral</a>, consisting of a <a href="/wiki/House_of_Commons_of_Northern_Ireland" title="House of Commons of Northern Ireland">House of Commons</a> with 52 seats, and an indirectly elected <a href="/wiki/Senate_of_Northern_Ireland" title="Senate of Northern Ireland">Senate</a> with 26 seats. The <a href="/wiki/British_monarch" class="mw-redirect" title="British monarch">Sovereign</a> was represented by the <a href="/wiki/Governor_of_Northern_Ireland" title="Governor of Northern Ireland">Governor</a> (initially by the <a href="/wiki/Lord_Lieutenant_of_Ireland" title="Lord Lieutenant of Ireland">Lord Lieutenant</a>), who granted <a href="/wiki/Royal_assent" title="Royal assent">royal assent</a> to Acts of Parliament in Northern Ireland, but executive power rested with the <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Northern_Ireland" title="Prime Minister of Northern Ireland">Prime Minister</a>, the leader of the largest party in the House of Commons.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" style="display:none" /><div class="toctitle" lang="en" dir="ltr"><h2>Contents</h2><span class="toctogglespan"><label class="toctogglelabel" links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="House_of_Commons">House of Commons</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parliament_of_Northern_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: House of Commons">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/House_of_Commons_of_Northern_Ireland" title="House of Commons of Northern Ireland">House of Commons of Northern Ireland</a></div>\n<p>The House of Commons had 52 members, of which 48 were for territorial seats and four were for graduates of <a href="/wiki/Queen%27s_University_of_Belfast_(Northern_Ireland_Parliament_constituency)" title="Queen&#39;s University of Belfast (Northern Ireland Parliament constituency)">Queen\'s University, Belfast</a> (until 1969, when the four university seats were replaced by an additional four territorial seats). The Government of Ireland Act prescribed that elections to the House of Commons should be by <a href="/wiki/Single_transferable_vote" title="Single transferable vote">single transferable vote</a> (STV), though the Parliament was given power to alter the electoral system from three years after its first meeting. The STV system was the subject of criticism from grassroots <a href="/wiki/Unionists_(Ireland)" class="mw-redirect" title="Unionists (Ireland)">Unionists</a> but because the three-year period ended during the <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">Labour</a> government of 1924, the <a href="/wiki/Executive_Committee_of_the_Privy_Council_of_Northern_Ireland" class="mw-redirect" title="Executive Committee of the Privy Council of Northern Ireland">Stormont</a> government decided not to provoke the known <a href="/wiki/Egalitarian" class="mw-redirect" title="Egalitarian">egalitarian</a> sympathies of many Labour backbenchers and held the second election on the same basis. The loss of eight Unionist seats in that election caused great acrimony and in 1929 the system was changed to <a href="/wiki/First-past-the-post" class="mw-redirect" title="First-past-the-post">first-past-the-post</a> for all territorial constituencies, though STV was retained for the university seats.\n</p><p>In the 1925 election however, Republicans also lost four seats and a substantial proportion of votes. Nationalists gained the same number of seats that Republicans had lost, but had only gained a small percentage of votes. It had been felt by some that Northern Ireland should use the same first-past-the-post system that was in place in the rest of the <a href="/wiki/UK" class="mw-redirect" title="UK">UK</a>.\n</p><p>By the time the first-past-the-post system was implemented for the 1929 election, the Republicans had few or no candidates and pro-separatist electors were represented almost solely by the <a href="/wiki/Nationalist_Party_(Northern_Ireland)" title="Nationalist Party (Northern Ireland)">Nationalist Party</a>. Despite the change in the electoral system and accusations of gerrymander, the Nationalist Party lost 9.5% share of the vote, but still gained a seat. The more moderate <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Labour_Party" title="Northern Ireland Labour Party">Northern Ireland Labour Party</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ulster_Liberal_Party" title="Ulster Liberal Party">Ulster Liberal Party</a> both gained in vote share but lost seats.\n</p><p>The boundary changes for 1929 were not made by an impartial boundary commission but by the Unionist government, for which it was accused of <a href="/wiki/Gerrymandering" title="Gerrymandering">gerrymandering</a>. The charges that the Stormont seats (as opposed to local council wards) were gerrymandered against Nationalists is disputed by historians<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> (since the number of Nationalists elected under the two systems barely changed), though it is agreed that losses under the change to single-member constituency boundaries were suffered by independent unionists, the <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Party_(UK)" title="Liberal Party (UK)">Liberals</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Labour_Party" title="Northern Ireland Labour Party">Northern Ireland Labour Party</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Population movements were so small that these boundaries were used almost everywhere until the Parliament was dissolved in 1972. In 1968 the government abolished the Queen\'s University constituency (long after <a href="/wiki/University_constituency" title="University constituency">university constituencies</a> had been abolished at <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Parliament of the United Kingdom">Westminster</a>) and created four new constituencies in the outskirts of Belfast where populations had grown. This change helped the Unionists, as they held only two of the University seats but won all four of the newly created seats. There had, however, long been calls from outside Unionism to abolish the graduate franchise (and other anomalies) and to have "<a href="/wiki/One_man,_one_vote" title="One man, one vote">one person one vote</a>".\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Senate">Senate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parliament_of_Northern_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Senate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Senate_of_Northern_Ireland" title="Senate of Northern Ireland">Senate of Northern Ireland</a></div>\n<p>The Senate was a last-minute addition to the Parliament, after the original plans for a single Senate covering both the Stormont and <a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Dublin</a> Parliaments were overtaken by events.\n</p><p>Twenty-four senators were elected by the House of Commons using the single transferable vote. The elections were carried out after each general election, with 12 members elected for two parliaments each time. The other two seats were held <i>ex officio</i> by the <a href="/wiki/Lord_Mayor_of_Belfast" class="mw-redirect" title="Lord Mayor of Belfast">Lord Mayor of Belfast</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Mayor_of_Londonderry" class="mw-redirect" title="Mayor of Londonderry">Mayor of Londonderry</a>. The Senate generally had the same party balance as the House of Commons, though abstaining parties and very small parties were not represented. Because of this, and its dependence on the House of Commons for election, it had virtually no political impact.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Crown">The Crown</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parliament_of_Northern_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The Crown">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The British monarch was originally to have been represented in both Northern Ireland and <a href="/wiki/Southern_Ireland_(1921%E2%80%9322)" title="Southern Ireland (1921\xe2\x80\x9322)">Southern Ireland</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Lord_Lieutenant_of_Ireland" title="Lord Lieutenant of Ireland">Lord Lieutenant of Ireland</a>. However, the replacement of <i>Southern Ireland</i> by the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Free_State" title="Irish Free State">Irish Free State</a> led to the abolition of the post of Lord Lieutenant. Instead, a new office \xe2\x80\x93 <a href="/wiki/Governor_of_Northern_Ireland" title="Governor of Northern Ireland">Governor of Northern Ireland</a> \xe2\x80\x93 was created on 12 December 1922.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Location">Location</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parliament_of_Northern_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Location">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Initially the Parliament met in Belfast\'s <a href="/wiki/Belfast_City_Hall" title="Belfast City Hall">City Hall</a> but moved immediately to the Presbyterian Church\'s Assembly\'s College (later <a href="/wiki/Union_Theological_College" title="Union Theological College">Union Theological College</a>), where it remained during the period 1921\xe2\x80\x931932. The Commons met in the College\'s Gamble Library and the Senate in the Chapel. In 1932, Parliament moved to the new purpose-built <a href="/wiki/Parliament_Buildings_(Northern_Ireland)" title="Parliament Buildings (Northern Ireland)">Parliament Buildings</a>, designed by <a href="/wiki/Arnold_Thornely" title="Arnold Thornely">Sir Arnold Thornely</a>, at <a href="/wiki/Stormont,_Belfast" class="mw-redirect" title="Stormont, Belfast">Stormont</a>, on the eastern outskirts of the city. The city boundaries were extended slightly to include Stormont within the capital city. "Stormont" came to be a <a href="/wiki/Synecdoche" title="Synecdoche">synecdoche</a> referring both to the Parliament itself and to the Northern Ireland government.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legislation">Legislation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parliament_of_Northern_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Legislation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Stormont was given power to legislate over almost all aspects of Northern Ireland life, with only a few matters excluded from its remit: succession to the Crown, making of peace or war, armed forces, honors, naturalization, some central taxes and postal services were the most important (a full list is in section 4 of the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Ireland_Act_1920" title="Government of Ireland Act 1920">Government of Ireland Act 1920</a>). The Parliament did not try to infringe the terms of the Government of Ireland Act; on only one occasion did the United Kingdom government advise the King to withhold royal assent. This was the Local Government Act (Northern Ireland) which abolished proportional representation in local government elections; the issue was referred to London and royal assent was eventually given. The output of legislation was high for a devolved Parliament, though some of the Acts were adaptations of recently passed acts by the United Kingdom parliament.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> It was nominally prohibited by section 16 of the Schedule to the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Free_State_(Agreement)_Act_1922" title="Irish Free State (Agreement) Act 1922">Irish Free State (Agreement) Act 1922</a> from making any law which directly or indirectly discriminated against a religion, although this provision had little effect.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Brief_history">Brief history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parliament_of_Northern_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Brief history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ulster_Welcomes_Her_King_%26_Queen_(10990906846).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Ulster_Welcomes_Her_King_%26_Queen_%2810990906846%29.jpg/220px-Ulster_Welcomes_Her_King_%26_Queen_%2810990906846%29.jpg" width="220" height="160" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Ulster_Welcomes_Her_King_%26_Queen_%2810990906846%29.jpg/330px-Ulster_Welcomes_Her_King_%26_Queen_%2810990906846%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Ulster_Welcomes_Her_King_%26_Queen_%2810990906846%29.jpg/440px-Ulster_Welcomes_Her_King_%26_Queen_%2810990906846%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2500" data-file-height="1814" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ulster_Welcomes_Her_King_%26_Queen_(10990906846).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Robinson and Cleaver Department Store in Belfast, decorated for the State Opening of the first Northern Ireland parliament. June 22, 1921.</div></div></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_general_election,_1921" title="Northern Ireland general election, 1921">1921 general election</a> was explicitly fought on the issue of <a href="/wiki/Partition_of_Ireland" title="Partition of Ireland">partition</a>, being in effect a referendum on approval of the concept of a Northern Ireland administration. Thereafter, general election timing was up to the Prime Minister. Elections almost always took place at a time when the issue of partition had been raised in a new crisis.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> This generally guaranteed the loyalty of <a href="/wiki/Protestant" class="mw-redirect" title="Protestant">Protestant</a> voters to the <a href="/wiki/Ulster_Unionist_Party" title="Ulster Unionist Party">Unionist Party</a>. Independent Unionist candidates and the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Labour_Party" title="Northern Ireland Labour Party">Northern Ireland Labour Party</a> were usually accused of being splitters or dupes of the <a href="/wiki/Irish_nationalists" class="mw-redirect" title="Irish nationalists">Nationalists</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_general_election,_1925" title="Northern Ireland general election, 1925">1925 general election</a> was called to tie in with the expected report of the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Boundary_Commission" title="Irish Boundary Commission">Boundary Commission</a> required by the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Irish_Treaty" title="Anglo-Irish Treaty">Anglo-Irish Treaty</a> of 1922. The Boundary Commission was expected to recommend the transfer of many border areas to the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Free_State" title="Irish Free State">Irish Free State</a>, and the Unionist election slogan was "Not an Inch!". They lost eight seats in Belfast and <a href="/wiki/County_Antrim" title="County Antrim">County Antrim</a>, where the issue of the border had far less resonance. <a href="/wiki/History_of_Sinn_F%C3%A9in" title="History of Sinn F\xc3\xa9in">Sinn F\xc3\xa9in</a> had fought in 1921, but by 1925 was suffering the effects of its split over the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Irish_Treaty" title="Anglo-Irish Treaty">Anglo-Irish Treaty</a>. <a href="/wiki/%C3%89amon_de_Valera" title="\xc3\x89amon de Valera">\xc3\x89amon de Valera</a>\'s Sinn F\xc3\xa9in fought as <a href="/wiki/Irish_republicanism" title="Irish republicanism">Republicans</a> but won only two seats. The border was never changed.\n</p><p>A minor row erupted in 1925 when the elections to the Senate took place. Eleven Unionists and one Labour Senator were elected, despite there being a block of three composed of two non-abstaining Nationalists and a dissident Unionist. The latter three had mailed their votes, but due to a public holiday and the practices of the postal service, they arrived an hour after the election. Requests for a recount were denied. (It is doubtful whether the three votes would have been sufficient to elect a Senator under the election system, since they would not have achieved a complete <a href="/wiki/Single_transferable_vote" title="Single transferable vote">single transferable vote</a> quota alone and the Unionist votes were likely to transfer so heavily to each other that the Nationalist candidate would not reach quota throughout the rounds of counting.)<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> From later in 1925 to 1927, the <a href="/wiki/Nationalist_Party_(Northern_Ireland)" title="Nationalist Party (Northern Ireland)">Nationalist Party</a> members took their seats for the first time.\n</p><p>For the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_general_election,_1929" title="Northern Ireland general election, 1929">1929 general election</a> the Unionists replaced the <a href="/wiki/Proportional_representation" title="Proportional representation">proportional representation</a> system blamed for their bad performance in 1925.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The new boundaries set the pattern for politics until Stormont was abolished; the Unionists never fell below 33 seats. In the 1930s, the phrase a <a href="/wiki/A_Protestant_Parliament_for_a_Protestant_People" title="A Protestant Parliament for a Protestant People">Protestant parliament for a Protestant people</a> was a debated term.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy_dispute#Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute"><span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (May 2010)">dubious</span></a>&#32;<span class="metadata"> &#8211; <a href="/wiki/Talk:Parliament_of_Northern_Ireland#Dubious" title="Talk:Parliament of Northern Ireland">discuss</a></span></i>&#93;</sup> The <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_general_election,_1938" title="Northern Ireland general election, 1938">1938 general election</a> was called when the <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Prime Minister of the United Kingdom">Prime Minister of the United Kingdom</a> <a href="/wiki/Neville_Chamberlain" title="Neville Chamberlain">Neville Chamberlain</a> was negotiating a settlement of outstanding disputes with \xc3\x89amon de Valera, whose new <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_Ireland" title="Constitution of Ireland">constitution</a> laid claim to Northern Ireland, and the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_general_election,_1949" title="Northern Ireland general election, 1949">1949 election</a> was called when the <a href="/wiki/Irish_government" class="mw-redirect" title="Irish government">Irish government</a> declared itself a <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">republic</a>.\n</p><p>During the <a href="/wiki/Second_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Second World War">Second World War</a>, the Stormont government called on Westminster to introduce <a href="/wiki/Conscription" title="Conscription">conscription</a> several times, as this was already the case in Great Britain. The <a href="/wiki/British_government" class="mw-redirect" title="British government">British government</a> consistently refused, remembering how a similar attempt in 1918 had backfired dramatically, as <a href="/wiki/Irish_nationalism" title="Irish nationalism">nationalist</a> opposition made it unworkable. Much of the population of serving age were either in essential jobs or had already joined up voluntarily, making the potential yield of conscription low.\n</p><p>1965 saw a significant change, in that the Nationalists accepted office as the <a href="/wiki/Official_Opposition" class="mw-redirect" title="Official Opposition">Official Opposition</a>. This was intended as a reward for the attempts made by <a href="/wiki/Terence_O%27Neill" title="Terence O&#39;Neill">Terence O\'Neill</a> to end discrimination against <a href="/wiki/Catholic" class="mw-redirect" title="Catholic">Catholics</a> and normalise relations with the Republic. However, the Unionists split over O\'Neill\'s tentative reforms at the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_general_election,_1969" title="Northern Ireland general election, 1969">1969 general election</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ian_Paisley" title="Ian Paisley">Ian Paisley</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Unionist_Party" title="Protestant Unionist Party">Protestant Unionist Party</a> began to win by-elections. The new nationalist party, the <a href="/wiki/Social_Democratic_and_Labour_Party" title="Social Democratic and Labour Party">Social Democratic and Labour Party</a>, withdrew from Stormont in July 1971 over the refusal of an inquiry into <a href="/wiki/Royal_Ulster_Constabulary" title="Royal Ulster Constabulary">Royal Ulster Constabulary</a> actions in <a href="/wiki/Derry" title="Derry">Derry</a>.\n</p><p>Stormont was abolished and <a href="/wiki/Direct_rule_over_Northern_Ireland" class="mw-redirect" title="Direct rule over Northern Ireland">Direct Rule</a> from Westminster was introduced in March 1972, just six weeks after <a href="/wiki/Bloody_Sunday_(1972)" title="Bloody Sunday (1972)">Bloody Sunday</a>, when the Unionist government refused to hand over responsibility for law and order to <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Parliament of the United Kingdom">Westminster</a>. In its 50-year history, only one piece of legislation was passed that was introduced by a Nationalist member, The Wild Birds Protection Act.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> In October 1971, as the <a href="/wiki/Troubles" class="mw-redirect" title="Troubles">Troubles</a> worsened, <a href="/wiki/Gerard_Newe" title="Gerard Newe">Gerard Newe</a> had been appointed as a junior minister at Stormont, in an attempt to improve community relations. Fifty years after it came into existence, Newe was the first Catholic to serve in a Northern Ireland government, but because he was neither an MP nor a Senator, his appointment could last only six months.\n</p><p>The influence of the <a href="/wiki/Orange_Institution" class="mw-redirect" title="Orange Institution">Orange Order</a> in the governance of Northern Ireland was far-reaching. All of the six prime ministers of Northern Ireland were members of the Order, as were all but three cabinet ministers until 1969. Three of the ministers later left the Order, one because his daughter married a Catholic, one to become Minister of Community Relations in 1970, and the third was expelled for attending a Catholic religious ceremony. Of the 95 Stormont MPs who did not become cabinet ministers, 87 were Orangemen. Every unionist senator, with one exception, between 1921 and 1969 was an Orangeman. One of these senators, James Gyle, was suspended from the Order for seven years for visiting nationalist MP <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Devlin" title="Joseph Devlin">Joe Devlin</a> on his deathbed.\n</p><p>A fully digitised copy of the Commons\' debates (187,000 printed pages of Parliamentary Debates) is available online.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a
