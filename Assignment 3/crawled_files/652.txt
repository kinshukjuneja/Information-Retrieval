Lawrence</b> (11 September 1885&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 2 March 1930) was an English writer and <a href="/wiki/Poet" title="Poet">poet</a>. His collected works represent, among other things, an extended reflection upon the dehumanising effects of modernity and industrialisation. Some of the issues Lawrence explores are sexuality, emotional health, vitality, spontaneity, and instinct.\n</p><p>Lawrence\'s opinions earned him many enemies and he endured official persecution, censorship, and misrepresentation of his creative work throughout the second half of his life, much of which he spent in a voluntary exile he called his "savage pilgrimage".<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> At the time of his death, his public reputation was that of a pornographer who had wasted his considerable talents. <a href="/wiki/E._M._Forster" title="E. M. Forster">E. M. Forster</a>, in an obituary notice, challenged this widely held view, describing him as "the greatest imaginative novelist of our generation."<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The philosopher <a href="/wiki/Bertrand_Russell" title="Bertrand Russell">Bertrand Russell</a> characterised Lawrence as a "proto-German Fascist". Later, the literary critic <a href="/wiki/F._R._Leavis" title="F. R. Leavis">F. R. Leavis</a> championed both his artistic integrity and his moral seriousness.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" H. Lawrence at age 21 in 1906</div></div></div>\n<p>The fourth child of Arthur John Lawrence, a barely literate miner at <a href="/wiki/Brinsley_Colliery" title="Brinsley Colliery">Brinsley Colliery</a>, and Lydia Beardsall, a former <a href="/wiki/Pupil_teacher" class="mw-redirect" title="Pupil teacher">pupil teacher</a> who had been forced to perform manual work in a lace factory due to her family\'s financial difficulties,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Lawrence spent his formative years in the coal mining town of <a href="/wiki/Eastwood,_Nottinghamshire" title="Eastwood, Nottinghamshire">Eastwood</a>, Nottinghamshire. The house in which he was born, 8a Victoria Street, is now the <a href="/wiki/D._H._Lawrence_Birthplace_Museum" title="D. H. Lawrence Birthplace Museum">D. H. Lawrence Birthplace Museum</a>. His working-class background and the tensions between his parents provided the raw material for a number of his early works. Lawrence roamed out from an early age in the patches of open, hilly country and remaining fragments of Sherwood Forest in Felley woods to the north of Eastwood, beginning a lifelong appreciation of the natural world, and he often wrote about "the country of my heart"<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> as a setting for much of his fiction.\n</p><p>The young Lawrence attended Beauvale Board School<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> (now renamed Greasley Beauvale D. H. Lawrence Primary School in his honour) from 1891 until 1898, becoming the first local pupil to win a <a href="/wiki/County_council" title="County council">county council</a> scholarship to <a href="/wiki/Nottingham_High_School" title="Nottingham High School">Nottingham High School</a> in nearby Nottingham. He left in 1901,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> working for three months as a junior clerk at Haywood\'s surgical appliances factory, but a severe bout of <a href="/wiki/Pneumonia" title="Pneumonia">pneumonia</a> ended this career. During his convalescence he often visited Hagg\'s Farm, the home of the Chambers family, and began a friendship with Jessie Chambers. An important aspect of this relationship with Chambers and other adolescent acquaintances was a shared love of books,<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> an interest that lasted throughout Lawrence\'s life. In the years 1902 to 1906 Lawrence served as a <a href="/wiki/Pupil_teacher" class="mw-redirect" title="Pupil teacher">pupil teacher</a> at the British School, Eastwood. He went on to become a full-time student and received a <a href="/wiki/Qualified_Teacher_Status" title="Qualified Teacher Status">teaching certificate</a> from <a href="/wiki/University_of_Nottingham" title="University of Nottingham">University College, Nottingham</a>, in 1908. During these early years he was working on his first poems, some short stories, and a draft of a novel, <i>Laetitia</i>, which was eventually to become <i><a href="/wiki/The_White_Peacock" title="The White Peacock">The White Peacock</a>.</i> At the end of 1907 he won a short story competition in the <i>Nottinghamshire Guardian</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> the first time that he had gained any wider recognition for his literary talents.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_career">Early career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=D._H._Lawrence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Early career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In the autumn of 1908 the newly qualified Lawrence left his childhood home for London.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> While teaching in Davidson Road School, <a href="/wiki/Croydon" title="Croydon">Croydon</a>, he continued writing.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Jessie Chambers submitted some of Lawrence\'s early poetry to <a href="/wiki/Ford_Madox_Ford" title="Ford Madox Ford">Ford Madox Ford</a> (then known as Ford Hermann Hueffer), editor of the influential <i><a href="/wiki/The_English_Review" title="The English Review">The English Review</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Hueffer then commissioned the story <i><a href="/wiki/Odour_of_Chrysanthemums" title="Odour of Chrysanthemums">Odour of Chrysanthemums</a></i> which, when published in that magazine, encouraged <a href="/wiki/Heinemann_(book_publisher)" class="mw-redirect" title="Heinemann (book publisher)">Heinemann</a>, a London publisher, to ask Lawrence for more work. His career as a professional author now began in earnest, although he taught for another year. Shortly after the final proofs of his first published novel, <i><a href="/wiki/The_White_Peacock" title="The White Peacock">The White Peacock</a></i>, appeared in 1910, Lawrence\'s mother died of cancer. The young man was devastated, and he was to describe the next few months as his "sick year". It is clear that Lawrence had an extremely close relationship with his mother, and his grief became a major turning point in his life, just as the death of Mrs. Morel is a major turning point in his autobiographical novel <i><a href="/wiki/Sons_and_Lovers" title="Sons and Lovers">Sons and Lovers</a></i>, a work that draws upon much of the writer\'s provincial upbringing. Essentially concerned with the emotional battle for Lawrence\xe2\x80\x99s love between his mother and "Miriam" (in reality Jessie Chambers), the novel also documents Paul\'s (Lawrence\'s) brief intimate relationship with Miriam (Jessie) that Lawrence had finally initiated in the Christmas of 1909, ending it in August 1910.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The hurt caused to Jessie by this and finally by her portrayal in the novel caused the end of their friendship<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> and after it was published they never spoke to each other again.\n</p><p>In 1911 Lawrence was introduced to <a href="/wiki/Edward_Garnett" title="Edward Garnett">Edward Garnett</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Publisher%27s_reader" title="Publisher&#39;s reader">publisher\'s reader</a>, who acted as a mentor, provided further encouragement, and became a valued friend, as did his son <a href="/wiki/David_Garnett" title="David Garnett">David</a>. Throughout these months the young author revised <i>Paul Morel</i>, the first draft of what became <i><a href="/wiki/Sons_and_Lovers" title="Sons and Lovers">Sons and Lovers</a></i>. In addition, a teaching colleague, <a href="/wiki/Helen_Corke" title="Helen Corke">Helen Corke</a>, gave him access to her intimate diaries about an unhappy love affair, which formed the basis of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Trespasser_(novel)" title="The Trespasser (novel)">The Trespasser</a></i>, his second novel. In November 1911, he came down with a pneumonia again; once he recovered, Lawrence decided to abandon teaching in order to become a full-time writer. In February 1912 he broke off an engagement to Louie Burrows, an old friend from his days in Nottingham and Eastwood.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In March 1912 Lawrence met <a href="/wiki/Frieda_von_Richthofen" class="mw-redirect" title="Frieda von Richthofen">Frieda Weekley (<i>n\xc3\xa9e</i> von Richthofen)</a>, with whom he was to share the rest of his life. Six years older than her new lover, she was married to <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Weekley" title="Ernest Weekley">Ernest Weekley</a>, his former modern languages professor at University College, Nottingham, and had three young children. She <a href="/wiki/Elopement" title="Elopement">eloped</a> with Lawrence to her parents\' home in <a href="/wiki/Metz" title="Metz">Metz</a>, a garrison town then in Germany near the disputed border with France. Their stay there included Lawrence\'s first encounter with tensions between Germany and France, when he was arrested and accused of being a British spy, before being released following an intervention from Frieda\'s father. After this incident, Lawrence left for a small hamlet to the south of <a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">Munich</a>, where he was joined by Frieda for their "honeymoon", later memorialised in the series of love poems titled <i>Look! We Have Come Through</i> (1917). During 1912 Lawrence wrote the first of his so-called "mining plays", <i><a href="/wiki/The_Daughter-in-Law" title="The Daughter-in-Law">The Daughter-in-Law</a></i>, written in Nottingham dialect. The play was never to be performed, or even published, in Lawrence\'s lifetime.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:D.H._Lawrence,_29_November_1915.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/D.H._Lawrence%2C_29_November_1915.jpg/220px-D.H._Lawrence%2C_29_November_1915.jpg" width="220" height="369" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/D.H._Lawrence%2C_29_November_1915.jpg/330px-D.H._Lawrence%2C_29_November_1915.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/D.H._Lawrence%2C_29_November_1915.jpg/440px-D.H._Lawrence%2C_29_November_1915.jpg 2x" data-file-width="477" data-file-height="800" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:D.H._Lawrence,_29_November_1915.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Lady Ottoline Morrell (1873\xe2\x80\x931938), vintage snapshot print/NPG x140423. D. H. Lawrence, 29 November 1915</div></div></div>\n<p>From Germany they walked southwards across the <a href="/wiki/Alps" title="Alps">Alps</a> to Italy, a journey that was recorded in the first of his travel books, a collection of linked essays titled <i>Twilight in Italy</i> and the unfinished novel, <i>Mr Noon</i>. During his stay in Italy, Lawrence completed the final version of <i>Sons and Lovers</i> that, when published in 1913, was acknowledged to be a vivid portrait of the realities of working class provincial life. Lawrence, though, had become so tired of the work that he allowed Edward Garnett to cut about a hundred pages from the text.\n</p><p>Lawrence and Frieda returned to Britain in 1913 for a short visit, during which they encountered and befriended critic <a href="/wiki/John_Middleton_Murry" title="John Middleton Murry">John Middleton Murry</a> and New Zealand-born short story writer <a href="/wiki/Katherine_Mansfield" title="Katherine Mansfield">Katherine Mansfield</a>. Lawrence was able to meet Welsh tramp poet <a href="/wiki/W._H._Davies" title="W. H. Davies">W. H. Davies</a>, whose work, much of which was inspired by nature, he greatly admired. Davies collected autographs, and was particularly keen to obtain Lawrence\'s. <a href="/wiki/Georgian_poetry" class="mw-redirect" title="Georgian poetry">Georgian poetry</a> publisher <a href="/wiki/Edward_Marsh_(polymath)" title="Edward Marsh (polymath)">Edward Marsh</a> was able to secure an autograph (probably as part of a signed poem), and invited Lawrence and Frieda to meet Davies in London on 28 July, under his supervision. Lawrence was immediately captivated by the poet and later invited Davies to join Frieda and him in Germany. Despite his early enthusiasm for Davies\' work, however, Lawrence\'s opinion changed after reading <i>Foliage</i> and he commented after reading <i>Nature Poems</i> in Italy that they seemed "so thin, one can hardly feel them".<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Lawrence and Frieda soon went back to Italy, staying in a cottage in Fiascherino on the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Spezia" class="mw-redirect" title="Gulf of Spezia">Gulf of Spezia</a>. Here he started writing the first draft of a work of fiction that was to be transformed into two of his best-known novels, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Rainbow" title="The Rainbow">The Rainbow</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Women_in_Love" title="Women in Love">Women in Love</a></i>, in which unconventional female characters take centre stage. Both novels were highly controversial, and both were banned on publication in the UK for obscenity (<i><a href="/wiki/Women_in_Love" title="Women in Love">Women in Love</a></i> only temporarily). Both novels cover grand themes and ideas.\n</p><p><i><a href="/wiki/The_Rainbow" title="The Rainbow">The Rainbow</a></i> follows three generations of a Nottinghamshire farming family from the pre-industrial to the industrial age, focusing particularly on a daughter, Ursula, and her aspiration for a more fulfilling life than that of becoming a housebound wife.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Women_in_Love" title="Women in Love">Women in Love</a></i> delves into the complex relationships between four major characters, including the sisters Ursula and Gudrun. Both novels challenged conventional ideas about the arts, politics, economic growth, gender, sexual experience, friendship and marriage and can be seen as far ahead of their time. The frank and relatively straightforward manner in which Lawrence dealt with sexual attraction was ostensibly what got the books banned, perhaps in particular the mention of same-sex attraction \xe2\x80\x93 Ursula has an affair with a woman in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Rainbow" title="The Rainbow">The Rainbow</a></i> and in <i><a href="/wiki/Women_in_Love" title="Women in Love">Women in Love</a></i> there is an undercurrent of attraction between the two principal male characters.\n</p><p>While writing <i><a href="/wiki/Women_in_Love" title="Women in Love">Women in Love</a></i> in Cornwall during 1916\xe2\x80\x9317, Lawrence developed a strong and possibly romantic relationship with a Cornish farmer named William Henry Hocking.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Although it is not clear if their relationship was sexual, Frieda said she believed it was. Lawrence\'s fascination with the theme of homosexuality, which is overtly manifested in <i><a href="/wiki/Women_in_Love" title="Women in Love">Women in Love</a></i>, could be related to his own sexual orientation.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> In a letter written during 1913, he writes, "I should like to know why nearly every man that approaches greatness tends to homosexuality, whether he admits it or not ..."<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> He is also quoted as saying, "I believe the nearest I\'ve come to perfect love was with a young coal-miner when I was about 16."<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> However, given his enduring and robust relationship with Frieda it is likely that he was primarily "bi-curious" in the terminology of today, and whether he actually ever had homosexual relations remains an open question.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Eventually, Frieda obtained her divorce. The couple returned to Britain shortly before the outbreak of World War I and were married on 13 July 1914. At this time, Lawrence worked with London intellectuals and writers such as <a href="/wiki/Dora_Marsden" title="Dora Marsden">Dora Marsden</a> and the people involved with <a href="/wiki/The_Egoist_(periodical)" title="The Egoist (periodical)"><i>The Egoist</i></a> (<a href="/wiki/T._S._Eliot" title="T. S. Eliot">T. S. Eliot</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ezra_Pound" title="Ezra Pound">Ezra Pound</a>, and others). <i>The Egoist</i>, an important Modernist literary magazine, published some of his work. He was also reading and adapting <a href="/wiki/Filippo_Tommaso_Marinetti" title="Filippo Tommaso Marinetti">Marinetti</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Manifesto_of_Futurism" title="Manifesto of Futurism">Manifesto of Futurism</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> He also met at this time the young Jewish artist <a href="/wiki/Mark_Gertler_(artist)" title="Mark Gertler (artist)">Mark Gertler</a>, and they became (for a time) good friends; Lawrence would describe Gertler\'s 1916 anti-war painting, <a href="/wiki/Merry-Go-Round_(Gertler_painting)" title="Merry-Go-Round (Gertler painting)"><i>Merry-Go-Round</i></a> as "the best <i>modern</i> picture I have seen: I think it is great and true."<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Gertler would inspire the character Loerke (a sculptor) in <i><a href="/wiki/Women_in_Love" title="Women in Love">Women in Love</a></i>. Frieda\'s German parentage and Lawrence\'s open contempt for militarism caused them to be viewed with suspicion in wartime Britain and to live in near destitution. <i><a href="/wiki/The_Rainbow" title="The Rainbow">The Rainbow</a></i> (1915) was suppressed after an investigation into its alleged <a href="/wiki/Obscenity" title="Obscenity">obscenity</a> in 1915.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> Later, they were accused of spying and signaling to German submarines off the coast of <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a> where they lived at <a href="/wiki/Zennor" title="Zennor">Zennor</a>. During this period he finished writing <i><a href="/wiki/Women_in_Love" title="Women in Love">Women in Love</a></i>. Not published until 1920, it is now widely recognised as an English novel of great dramatic force and intellectual subtlety.\n</p><p>In late 1917, after constant harassment by the armed forces authorities, Lawrence was forced to leave Cornwall at three days\' notice under the terms of the <a href="/wiki/Defence_of_the_Realm_Act" class="mw-redirect" title="Defence of the Realm Act">Defence of the Realm Act</a>. This persecution was later described in an autobiographical chapter of his Australian novel <a href="/wiki/Kangaroo_(novel)" title="Kangaroo (novel)"><i>Kangaroo</i></a>, published in 1923. He spent some months in early 1918 in the small, rural village of <a href="/wiki/Hermitage,_Berkshire" title="Hermitage, Berkshire">Hermitage</a> near <a href="/wiki/Newbury,_Berkshire" title="Newbury, Berkshire">Newbury, Berkshire</a>. He then lived for just under a year (mid-1918 to early 1919) at Mountain Cottage, <a href="/wiki/Middleton-by-Wirksworth" title="Middleton-by-Wirksworth">Middleton-by-Wirksworth</a>, Derbyshire, where he wrote one of his most poetic short stories, <i>Wintry Peacock</i>. Until 1919 he was compelled by poverty to shift from address to address and barely survived a severe attack of <a href="/wiki/Influenza" title="Influenza">influenza</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Exile">Exile</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=D._H._Lawrence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Exile">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>After his experience of the war years, Lawrence began what he termed his "savage pilgrimage", a time of voluntary exile. He escaped from Britain at the earliest practical opportunity, to return only twice for brief visits, and with his wife spent the remainder of his life travelling. This <a href="/wiki/Wanderlust" title="Wanderlust">wanderlust</a> took him to Australia, Italy, <a href="/wiki/Ceylon" class="mw-redirect" title="Ceylon">Ceylon</a> (now called <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">Sri Lanka</a>), the United States, Mexico and the South of France.\n</p><p>Lawrence abandoned Britain in November 1919 and headed south, first to the <a href="/wiki/Abruzzo" title="Abruzzo">Abruzzo</a> region in central Italy and then onwards to <a href="/wiki/Capri" title="Capri">Capri</a> and the Fontana Vecchia in <a href="/wiki/Taormina" title="Taormina">Taormina</a>, Sicily. From Sicily he made brief excursions to <a href="/wiki/Sardinia" title="Sardinia">Sardinia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Monte_Cassino" title="Monte Cassino">Monte Cassino</a>, <a href="/wiki/Malta" title="Malta">Malta</a>, Northern Italy, Austria and Southern Germany. Many of these places appeared in his writings. New novels included <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lost_Girl" title="The Lost Girl">The Lost Girl</a></i> (for which he won the <a href="/wiki/James_Tait_Black_Memorial_Prize" title="James Tait Black Memorial Prize">James Tait Black Memorial Prize</a> for fiction), <a href="/wiki/Aaron%27s_Rod_(novel)" title="Aaron&#39;s Rod (novel)"><i>Aaron\'s Rod</i></a> and the fragment titled <i><a href="/wiki/Mr_Noon" title="Mr Noon">Mr Noon</a></i> (the first part of which was published in the Phoenix anthology of his works, and the entirety in 1984). He experimented with shorter novels or <a href="/wiki/Novellas" class="mw-redirect" title="Novellas">novellas</a>, such as <i><a href="/wiki/The_Captain%27s_Doll" title="The Captain&#39;s Doll">The Captain\'s Doll</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/The_Fox_(novella)" title="The Fox (novella)"><i>The Fox</i></a> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Ladybird" title="The Ladybird">The Ladybird</a></i>. In addition, some of his short stories were issued in the collection <i><a href="/wiki/England,_My_England_and_Other_Stories" title="England, My England and Other Stories">England, My England and Other Stories</a></i>. During these years he produced a number of poems about the natural world in <i><a href="/wiki/Birds,_Beasts_and_Flowers" title="Birds, Beasts and Flowers">Birds, Beasts and Flowers</a></i>. Lawrence is widely recognised as one of the finest travel writers in the English language. <i><a href="/wiki/Sea_and_Sardinia" title="Sea and Sardinia">Sea and Sardinia</a></i>, a book that describes a brief journey undertaken in January 1921, is a recreation of the life of the inhabitants of <a href="/wiki/Sardinia" title="Sardinia">Sardinia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Less well known is the memoir of <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Magnus" title="Maurice Magnus">Maurice Magnus</a>, <i>Memoirs of the Foreign Legion</i>, in which Lawrence recalls his visit to the monastery of <a href="/wiki/Monte_Cassino" title="Monte Cassino">Monte Cassino</a>. Other non-fiction books include two responses to <a href="/wiki/Freudian" class="mw-redirect" title="Freudian">Freudian</a> psychoanalysis, <i>Psychoanalysis and the Unconscious</i> and <i>Fantasia of the Unconscious</i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Movements_in_European_History" title="Movements in European History">Movements in European History</a></i>, a school textbook that was published under a pseudonym, a reflection of his blighted reputation in Britain.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_life_and_career">Later life and career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=D._H._Lawrence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Later life and career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In late February 1922 the Lawrences left Europe behind with the intention of migrating to the United States. They sailed in an easterly direction, first to Ceylon and then on to Australia. A short residence in <a href="/wiki/Darlington,_Western_Australia" title="Darlington, Western Australia">Darlington</a>, Western Australia, which included an encounter with local writer <a href="/wiki/Mollie_Skinner" title="Mollie Skinner">Mollie Skinner</a>, was followed by a brief stop in the small coastal town of <a href="/wiki/Thirroul,_New_South_Wales" title="Thirroul, New South Wales">Thirroul</a>, New South Wales, during which Lawrence completed <a href="/wiki/Kangaroo_(novel)" title="Kangaroo (novel)"><i>Kangaroo</i></a>, a novel about local fringe politics that also revealed a lot about his wartime experiences in Cornwall.\n</p><p>\nThe Lawrences finally arrived in the United States in September 1922. Lawrence had several times discussed the idea of setting up a <a href="/wiki/Utopian" class="mw-redirect" title="Utopian">utopian</a> community with several of his friends, having written to his old socialist friend in Eastwood, Willie Hopkin, in 1915,</p><blockquote><p>"I want to gather together about twenty souls and sail away from this world of war and squalor and found a little colony where there shall be no money but a sort of communism as far as necessaries of life go, and some real decency\xe2\x80\xa6 a place where one can live simply, apart from this civilisation\xe2\x80\xa6 [with] a few other people who are also at peace and happy and live, and understand and be free\xe2\x80\xa6"<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote><p>It was with this in mind that they made for the "bohemian" town of <a href="/wiki/Taos,_New_Mexico" title="Taos, New Mexico">Taos</a>, New Mexico, where <a href="/wiki/Mabel_Dodge_Luhan" title="Mabel Dodge Luhan">Mabel Dodge Luhan</a>, a prominent socialite, lived. Here they eventually acquired the 160-acre (0.65&#160;km<sup>2</sup>) Kiowa Ranch, now called the <a href="/wiki/D._H._Lawrence_Ranch" title="D. H. Lawrence Ranch">D. H. Lawrence Ranch</a>, in 1924 from Dodge Luhan in exchange for the manuscript of <i>Sons and Lovers</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> He stayed in New Mexico for two years, with extended visits to <a href="/wiki/Lake_Chapala" title="Lake Chapala">Lake Chapala</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oaxaca" title="Oaxaca">Oaxaca</a> in Mexico. While Lawrence was in New Mexico, he was visited by <a href="/wiki/Aldous_Huxley" title="Aldous Huxley">Aldous Huxley</a>.\n</p><p>Editor and book designer Merle Armitage wrote a book about D. H. Lawrence in New Mexico. <i>Taos Quartet in Three Movements</i> was originally to appear in Flair Magazine, but the magazine folded before its publication. This short work describes the tumultuous relationship of D. H. Lawrence, his wife Frieda, artist Dorothy Brett and Mabel Dodge Sterne. Armitage took it upon himself to print 16 hardcover copies of this work for his friends. <a href="/wiki/Richard_Pousette-Dart" title="Richard Pousette-Dart">Richard Pousette-Dart</a> executed the drawings for Taos Quartet, published in 1950.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>While in the US, Lawrence rewrote and published <i><a href="/wiki/Studies_in_Classic_American_Literature" title="Studies in Classic American Literature">Studies in Classic American Literature</a></i>, a set of critical essays begun in 1917, and later described by <a href="/wiki/Edmund_Wilson" title="Edmund Wilson">Edmund Wilson</a> as "one of the few first-rate books that have ever been written on the subject". These interpretations, with their insights into <a href="/wiki/Symbol" title="Symbol">symbolism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Transcendentalism" title="Transcendentalism">New England Transcendentalism</a> and the puritan sensibility, were a significant factor in the revival of the reputation of <a href="/wiki/Herman_Melville" title="Herman Melville">Herman Melville</a> during the early 1920s. In addition, Lawrence completed a number of new fictional works, including <i><a href="/wiki/The_Boy_in_the_Bush" title="The Boy in the Bush">The Boy in the Bush</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Plumed_Serpent" title="The Plumed Serpent">The Plumed Serpent</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/St_Mawr" title="St Mawr">St Mawr</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Woman_who_Rode_Away" title="The Woman who Rode Away">The Woman who Rode Away</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/The_Princess_(story)" class="mw-redirect" title="The Princess (story)"><i>The Princess</i></a> and assorted short stories. He also found time to produce some more travel writing, such as the collection of linked excursions that became <i><a href="/wiki/Mornings_in_Mexico" title="Mornings in Mexico">Mornings in Mexico</a></i>.\n</p><p>\nA brief voyage to England at the end of 1923 was a failure and he soon returned to Taos, convinced that his life as an author now lay in the United States. However, in March 1925 he suffered a near fatal attack of <a href="/wiki/Malaria" title="Malaria">malaria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tuberculosis" title="Tuberculosis">tuberculosis</a> while on a third visit to <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>. Although he eventually recovered, the diagnosis of his condition obliged him to return once again to Europe. He was dangerously ill and the poor health limited his ability to travel for the remainder of his life. The Lawrences made their home in a villa in Northern Italy, living near <a href="/wiki/Florence" title="Florence">Florence</a> while he wrote <a href="/wiki/The_Virgin_and_the_Gypsy" title="The Virgin and the Gypsy"><i>The Virgin and the Gipsy</i></a> and the various versions of <i><a href="/wiki/Lady_Chatterley%27s_Lover" title="Lady Chatterley&#39;s Lover">Lady Chatterley\'s Lover</a></i> (1928). The latter book, his last major novel, was initially published in private editions in Florence and Paris and reinforced his notoriety. A story set once more in Nottinghamshire about a cross-class relationship between a Lady and her gamekeeper, it broke new ground in describing their sexual relationship in explicit yet literary language. His intention in writing the novel was to challenge the British establishment\xe2\x80\x99s taboos around sex, to enable men and women "\xe2\x80\xa6to think sex, fully, completely, honestly, and cleanly."<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> Lawrence responded robustly to those who claimed to be offended, penning a large number of satirical poems, published under the title of "Pansies" and "Nettles", as well as a tract on <i>Pornography and Obscenity</i>.</p><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:DH_Lawrence_birthplace_museum_-_geograph-1814503.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/DH_Lawrence_birthplace_museum_-_geograph-1814503.jpg/170px-DH_Lawrence_birthplace_museum_-_geograph-1814503.jpg" width="170" height="245" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/DH_Lawrence_birthplace_museum_-_geograph-1814503.jpg/255px-DH_Lawrence_birthplace_museum_-_geograph-1814503.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/DH_Lawrence_birthplace_museum_-_geograph-1814503.jpg/340px-DH_Lawrence_birthplace_museum_-_geograph-1814503.jpg 2x" data-file-width="555" data-file-height="800" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:DH_Lawrence_birthplace_museum_-_geograph-1814503.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/D._H._Lawrence_Birthplace_Museum" title="D. H. Lawrence Birthplace Museum">D. H. Lawrence Birthplace Museum</a> in <a href="/wiki/Eastwood,_Nottinghamshire" title="Eastwood, Nottinghamshire">Eastwood, Nottinghamshire</a></div></div></div>\n<p>The return to Italy allowed Lawrence to renew old friendships; during these years he was particularly close to <a href="/wiki/Aldous_Huxley" title="Aldous Huxley">Aldous Huxley</a>, who was to edit the first collection of Lawrence\'s letters after his death, along with a memoir. With artist <a href="/wiki/Earl_Brewster" title="Earl Brewster">Earl Brewster</a>, Lawrence visited a number of local archaeological sites in April 1927. The resulting essays describing these visits to old tombs were written up and collected together as <i><a href="/wiki/Sketches_of_Etruscan_Places_and_other_Italian_essays" title="Sketches of Etruscan Places and other Italian essays">Sketches of Etruscan Places</a></i>, a book that contrasts the lively past with <a href="/wiki/Benito_Mussolini" title="Benito Mussolini">Benito Mussolini</a>\'s fascism. Lawrence continued to produce fiction, including short stories and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Escaped_Cock" title="The Escaped Cock">The Escaped Cock</a></i> (also published as <i>The Man Who Died</i>), an unorthodox reworking of the story of Jesus Christ\'s <a href="/wiki/Resurrection_of_Jesus" title="Resurrection of Jesus">Resurrection</a>. During these final years Lawrence renewed a serious interest in oil painting. Official harassment persisted and an exhibition of some of these pictures at the Warren Gallery in London was raided by the police in mid 1929 and a number of works were confiscated.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=D._H._Lawrence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Lawrence continued to write despite his failing health. In his last months he wrote numerous poems, reviews and essays, as well as a robust defence of his last novel against those who sought to suppress it. His last significant work was a reflection on the <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Revelation" title="Book of Revelation">Book of Revelation</a>, <i>Apocalypse</i>. After being discharged from a <a href="/wiki/Sanatorium" title="Sanatorium">sanatorium</a>, he died on 2 March 1930<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> at the Villa Robermond in <a href="/wiki/Vence" title="Vence">Vence</a>, France, from complications of tuberculosis. Frieda Weekley commissioned an elaborate headstone for his grave bearing a mosaic of his adopted emblem of the <a href="/wiki/Phoenix_(mythology)" title="Phoenix (mythology)">phoenix</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> After Lawrence\'s death, Frieda lived with <a href="/wiki/Angelo_Ravagli" title="Angelo Ravagli">Angelo Ravagli</a> on the ranch in <a href="/wiki/Taos,_New_Mexico" title="Taos, New Mexico">Taos</a> and eventually married him in 1950. In 1935 Ravagli arranged, on Frieda\'s behalf, to have Lawrence\xe2\x80\x99s body exhumed and cremated and his ashes brought back to the ranch to be interred there in a small chapel amid the mountains of <a href="/wiki/New_Mexico" title="New Mexico">New Mexico</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Philosophy_and_politics">Philosophy and politics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=D._H._Lawrence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Philosophy and politics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Critic <a href="/wiki/Terry_Eagleton" title="Terry Eagleton">Terry Eagleton</a> situates Lawrence on the radical right wing of politics, as hostile to democracy, liberalism, socialism, and egalitarianism, though never formally embracing fascism,<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> as he died before it reached its zenith. Lawrence\'s opinion of the masses is discussed in detail by Professor <a href="/wiki/John_Carey_(critic)" title="John Carey (critic)">John Carey</a> in <i>The Intellectuals and the Masses</i> (1992), and he quotes a 1908 letter from Lawrence to Blanche Jennings:\n</p>\n<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>If I had my way, I would build a lethal chamber as big as <a href="/wiki/The_Crystal_Palace" title="The Crystal Palace">the Crystal Palace</a>, with a military band playing softly, and a <a href="/wiki/Cinematograph" title="Cinematograph">Cinematograph</a> working brightly; then I\'d go out in the back streets and main streets and bring them in, all the sick, the halt, and the maimed; I would lead them gently, and they would smile me a weary thanks; and the band would softly bubble out the "<a href="/wiki/Hallelujah_Chorus" class="mw-redirect" title="Hallelujah Chorus">Hallelujah Chorus</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>\n</p></blockquote> \n<p>More of Lawrence\'s political ideas can be seen in his letters to <a href="/wiki/Bertrand_Russell" title="Bertrand Russell">Bertrand Russell</a> around the year 1915, where he voices his opposition to enfranchising the working class and his hostility to the burgeoning labour movements, and disparages the <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">French Revolution</a>, referring to "Liberty, Equality, and Fraternity" as the "three-fanged serpent". Rather than a republic, Lawrence called for an absolute dictator and equivalent dictatrix to lord over the lower peoples.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> Bertrand Russell rejected Lawrence\'s mystical notions of <i>blood consciousness</i> vehemently, and thought that they had led straight to <a href="/wiki/Auschwitz_concentration_camp" title="Auschwitz concentration camp">Auschwitz</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> In 1953 Russell, recalling his relationship with Lawrence in <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">the First World War</a>, characterised Lawrence as a "proto-German Fascist", saying "I was a firm believer in democracy, whereas he had developed the whole philosophy of Fascism before the politicians had thought of it."<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> Later, Harrison<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> drew attention to the veins of sadism and racism that run through Lawrence\'s writing.\n</p><p>Lawrence held seemingly contradictory views on feminism. The evidence of his written works, particularly his earlier novels, indicates a commitment to representing women as strong, independent and complex; as noted above he produced major works in which young, self-directing female characters were central. In his youth he supported extending the vote to women, and once wrote, \xe2\x80\x9cAll women in their natures are like giantesses. They will break through everything and go on with their own lives.\xe2\x80\x9d<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>  However, a number of feminist critics, notably <a href="/wiki/Kate_Millett" title="Kate Millett">Kate Millett</a>, have criticised, indeed ridiculed Lawrence\xe2\x80\x99s <a href="/wiki/Gender_politics" class="mw-redirect" title="Gender politics">sexual politics</a>, Millett claiming that he uses his female characters as mouthpieces to promote his creed of male supremacy, and that his story <i>The Woman Who Rode Away</i> showed Lawrence as a pornographic sadist with its portrayal of \xe2\x80\x9chuman sacrifice performed upon the woman to the greater glory and potency of the male.\xe2\x80\x9d<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Brenda_Maddox" title="Brenda Maddox">Brenda Maddox</a> further highlights this story and two others written around the same time, <i>St. Mawr</i> and <i>The Princess,</i> as \xe2\x80\x9cmasterworks of misogyny\xe2\x80\x9d.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Despite the inconsistency and at times inscrutability of his philosophical writings Lawrence continues to find an audience, and the ongoing publication of <a href="/wiki/The_Cambridge_Edition_of_the_Letters_and_Works_of_D._H._Lawrence" title="The Cambridge Edition of the Letters and Works of D. H. Lawrence">a new scholarly edition of his letters</a> and writings has demonstrated the range of his achievement. Philosophers like <a href="/wiki/Gilles_Deleuze" title="Gilles Deleuze">Gilles Deleuze</a> and <a href="/wiki/F%C3%A9lix_Guattari" title="F\xc3\xa9lix Guattari">F\xc3\xa9lix Guattari</a> found in Lawrence\xe2\x80\x99s critique of Freud an important precursor of anti-Oedipal accounts of the unconscious that has been much influential.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Written_works">Written works</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=D._H._Lawrence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Written works">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Novels">Novels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=D._H._Lawrence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Novels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Lawrence is best known for his novels <i><a href="/wiki/Sons_and_Lovers" title="Sons and Lovers">Sons and Lovers</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Rainbow" title="The Rainbow">The Rainbow</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Women_in_Love" title="Women in Love">Women in Love</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Lady_Chatterley%27s_Lover" title="Lady Chatterley&#39;s Lover">Lady Chatterley\'s Lover</a></i>. In these books, Lawrence explores the possibilities for life within an industrial setting. In particular Lawrence is concerned with the nature of relationships that can be had within such a setting. Though often classed as a <a href="/wiki/Literary_realism" title="Literary realism">realist</a>, Lawrence in fact uses his characters to give form to his personal philosophy. His depiction of sexuality, though seen as shocking when his work was first published in the early 20th century, has its roots in this highly personal way of thinking and being.\n</p><p>\nIt is worth noting that Lawrence was very interested in the <a href="/wiki/Haptic_communication" title="Haptic communication">sense of touch</a> and that his focus on physical intimacy has its roots in a desire to restore an emphasis on the body, and re-balance it with what he perceived to be Western civilisation\'s over-emphasis on the mind; writing in a 1929 essay "Men Must Work and Women As Well", he stated,</p><blockquote><p>"Now we see the trend of our civilization, in terms of human feeling and human relation. It is, and there is no denying it, towards a greater and greater abstraction from the physical, towards a further and further physical separateness between men and women, and between individual and individual\xe2\x80\xa6 It only remains for some men and women, individuals, to try to get back their bodies and preserve the flow of warmth, affection and physical unison." <i>Phoenix II: Uncollected Writings,</i> Ed. Warren Roberts and Harry T. Moore (New York) 1970</p></blockquote><p>In his later years Lawrence developed the potentialities of the short novel form in <i><a href="/wiki/St_Mawr" title="St Mawr">St Mawr</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Virgin_and_the_Gypsy" title="The Virgin and the Gypsy">The Virgin and the Gypsy</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Escaped_Cock" title="The Escaped Cock">The Escaped Cock</a></i>.\n</p><h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Short_stories">Short stories</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=D._H._Lawrence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Short stories">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Lawrence\'s best-known short stories include "<a href="/wiki/The_Captain%27s_Doll" title="The Captain&#39;s Doll">The Captain\'s Doll</a>", "<a href="/wiki/The_Fox_(short_story)" class="mw-redirect" title="The Fox (short story)">The Fox</a>", "<a href="/wiki/The_Ladybird" title="The Ladybird">The Ladybird</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Odour_of_Chrysanthemums" title="Odour of Chrysanthemums">Odour of Chrysanthemums</a>", "<a href="/wiki/The_Princess_(story)" class="mw-redirect" title="The Princess (story)">The Princess</a>", "<a href="/wiki/The_Rocking-Horse_Winner" title="The Rocking-Horse Winner">The Rocking-Horse Winner</a>", "<a href="/wiki/St_Mawr" title="St Mawr">St Mawr</a>", "<a href="/wiki/The_Virgin_and_the_Gypsy" title="The Virgin and the Gypsy">The Virgin and the Gypsy</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/The_Woman_who_Rode_Away" title="The Woman who Rode Away">The Woman who Rode Away</a>". (<i>The Virgin and the Gypsy</i> was published as a novella after he died.) Among his most praised collections is <i><a href="/wiki/The_Prussian_Officer_and_Other_Stories" title="The Prussian Officer and Other Stories">The Prussian Officer and Other Stories</a></i>, published in 1914. His collection <i>The Woman Who Rode Away and Other Stories</i>, published in 1928, develops the theme of leadership that Lawrence also explored in novels such as <i>Kangaroo, <a href="/wiki/The_Plumed_Serpent" title="The Plumed Serpent">The Plumed Serpent</a></i> and <i>Fanny and Annie</i>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Poetry">Poetry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=D._H._Lawrence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Poetry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Although best known for his novels, Lawrence wrote almost 800 poems, most of them relatively short. His first poems were written in 1904 and two of his poems, "Dreams Old" and "Dreams Nascent", were among his earliest published works in <i>The English Review</i>. It has been claimed that his early works clearly place him in the school of <a href="/wiki/Georgian_poets" class="mw-redirect" title="Georgian poets">Georgian poets</a>, and indeed some of his poems appear in the <i>Georgian Poetry</i> anthologies. However, <a href="/wiki/James_Reeves_(writer)" title="James Reeves (writer)">James Reeves</a> in his book on Georgian Poetry,<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> notes that Lawrence was never really a Georgian poet.  Indeed, later critics<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> contrast Lawrence\'s energy and dynamism with the complacency of Georgian poetry.\n</p><p>Just as the First World War dramatically changed the work of many of the poets who saw service in the trenches, Lawrence\'s own work saw a dramatic change, during his years in Cornwall. During this time, he wrote <a href="/wiki/Free_verse" title="Free verse">free verse</a> influenced by <a href="/wiki/Walt_Whitman" title="Walt Whitman">Walt Whitman</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> He set forth his manifesto for much of his later verse in the introduction to <i>New Poems</i>. "We can get rid of the stereotyped movements and the old hackneyed associations of sound or sense. We can break down those artificial conduits and canals through which we do so love to force our utterance. We can break the stiff neck of habit [\xe2\x80\xa6] But we cannot positively prescribe any motion, any rhythm."\n</p><p>\nLawrence rewrote many of his novels several times to perfect them and similarly he returned to some of his early poems when they were collected in 1928. This was in part to fictionalise them, but also to remove some of the artifice of his first works. As he put it himself: "A young man is afraid of his demon and puts his hand over the demon\'s mouth sometimes and speaks for him."<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> His best-known poems are probably those dealing with nature such as those in the collection <i>Birds, Beasts and Flowers</i>, including the Tortoise poems, and "Snake", one of his most frequently anthologised, displays some of his most frequent concerns: those of man\'s modern distance from nature and subtle hints at religious themes.</p><blockquote><div class="poem">\n<p>In the deep, strange-scented shade of the great dark carob tree<br />\nI came down the steps with my pitcher<br />\nAnd must wait, must stand and wait, for there he was at the trough before me.<br />\n(From "Snake")\n</p>\n</div></blockquote>\n<p><i>Look! We have come through!</i> is his other work from the period of the end of the war and it reveals another important element common to much of his writings; his inclination to lay himself bare in his writings. <a href="/wiki/Ezra_Pound" title="Ezra Pound">Ezra Pound</a> in his <i>Literary Essays</i> complained of Lawrence\'s interest in his own "disagreeable sensations" but praised him for his "low-life narrative." This is a reference to Lawrence\'s dialect poems akin to the Scots poems of <a href="/wiki/Robert_Burns" title="Robert Burns">Robert Burns</a>, in which he reproduced the language and concerns of the people of <a href="/wiki/Nottinghamshire" title="Nottinghamshire">Nottinghamshire</a> from his youth.\n</p>\n<blockquote><div class="poem">\n<p>Tha thought tha wanted ter be rid o\' me.<br />\n\'Appen tha did, an\' a\'.<br />\nTha thought tha wanted ter marry an\' se<br />\nIf ter couldna be master an\' th\' woman\'s boss,<br />\nTha\'d need a woman different from me,<br />\nAn\' tha knowed it; ay, yet tha comes across<br />\nTer say goodbye! an\' a\'.<br />\n(From "The Drained Cup")\n</p>\n</div></blockquote>\n<p>Although Lawrence\'s works after his Georgian period are clearly in the modernist tradition, they were often very different from those of many other <a href="/wiki/Modernist_poetry" title="Modernist poetry">modernist</a> writers, such as Pound. Pound\'s poems were often austere, with every word carefully worked on. Lawrence felt all poems had to be personal sentiments, and that a sense of spontaneity was vital. He called one collection of poems <i>Pansies</i>, partly for the simple ephemeral nature of the verse, but also as a pun on the French word <i>panser</i>, to dress or bandage a wound. "Pansies", as he made explicit in the introduction to <i>New Poems</i>, is also a pun on <a href="/wiki/Blaise_Pascal" title="Blaise Pascal">Blaise Pascal</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Pens%C3%A9es" title="Pens\xc3\xa9es">Pens\xc3\xa9es</a></i>. "The Noble Englishman" and "Don\'t Look at Me" were removed from the official edition of <i>Pansies</i> on the grounds of obscenity, which wounded him. Even though he lived most of the last ten years of his life abroad, his thoughts were often still on England. Published in 1930, just eleven days after his death, his last work <i>Nettles</i>  was a series of bitter, nettling but often wry attacks on the moral climate of England.\n</p>\n<blockquote><div class="poem">\n<p>O the stale old dogs who pretend to guard<br />\nthe morals of the masses,<br />\nhow smelly they make the great back-yard<br />\nwetting after everyone that passes.<br />\n(From "The Young and Their Moral Guardians")\n</p>\n</div></blockquote>\n<p>Two notebooks of Lawrence\'s unprinted verse were posthumously published as <i>Last Poems</i> and <i>More Pansies</i>. These contain two of Lawrence\'s most famous poems about death, "Bavarian Gentians" and "The Ship of Death".\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Literary_criticism">Literary criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=D._H._Lawrence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Literary criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Lawrence\'s criticism of other authors often provides insight into his own thinking and writing. Of particular note is his <i>Study of Thomas Hardy and Other Essays</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> In <i><a href="/wiki/Studies_in_Classic_American_Literature" title="Studies in Classic American Literature">Studies in Classic American Literature</a></i> Lawrence\xe2\x80\x99s responses to writers like <a href="/wiki/Walt_Whitman" title="Walt Whitman">Walt Whitman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Herman_Melville" title="Herman Melville">Herman Melville</a> and <a href="/wiki/Edgar_Allan_Poe" title="Edgar Allan Poe">Edgar Allan Poe</a> also shed light on his craft.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Painting">Painting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=D._H._Lawrence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Painting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>D. H. Lawrence had a lifelong interest in painting, which became one of his main forms of expression in his last years. His paintings were exhibited at the Warren Gallery in London\'s <a href="/wiki/Mayfair" title="Mayfair">Mayfair</a> in 1929. The exhibition was extremely controversial, with many of the 13,000 people visiting mainly to gawk. The <i><a href="/wiki/Daily_Express" title="Daily Express">Daily Express</a></i> claimed, "<i>Fight with an Amazon</i> represents a hideous, bearded man holding a fair-haired woman in his lascivious grip while wolves with dripping jaws look on expectantly, [this] is frankly indecent".<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> However, several artists and art experts praised the paintings. <a href="/wiki/Gwen_John" title="Gwen John">Gwen John</a>, reviewing the exhibition in <a href="/wiki/Everyman_(magazine)" title="Everyman (magazine)"><i>Everyman</i></a>, spoke of Lawrence\'s "stupendous gift of self-expression" and singled out <i>The Finding of Moses</i>, <i>Red Willow Trees</i> and <i>Boccaccio Story</i> as "pictures of real beauty and great vitality". Others singled out <i>Contadini</i> for special praise. After a complaint, the police seized thirteen of the twenty-five paintings (including <i>Boccaccio Story</i> and <i>Contadini</i>). Despite declarations of support from many writers, artists and <a href="/wiki/Member_of_Parliament#United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="Member of Parliament">Members of Parliament</a>, Lawrence was able to recover his paintings only by agreeing never to exhibit them in England again. The largest collection of the paintings is now at La Fonda de Taos<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> hotel in <a href="/wiki/Taos,_New_Mexico" title="Taos, New Mexico">Taos, New Mexico</a>. Several others, including <i>Boccaccio Story</i> and <i>Resurrection</i>, are at the Humanities Research Centre of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Texas_at_Austin" title="University of Texas at Austin">University of Texas at Austin</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Lady_Chatterley_trial"><i>Lady Chatterley</i> trial</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=D._H._Lawrence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Lady Chatterley trial">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/R_v_Penguin_Books_Ltd." class="mw-redirect" title="R v Penguin Books Ltd.">R v Penguin Books Ltd.</a></div>\n<p>A heavily censored abridgement of <i><a href="/wiki/Lady_Chatterley%27s_Lover" title="Lady Chatterley&#39;s Lover">Lady Chatterley\'s Lover</a></i> was published in the United States by <a href="/wiki/Alfred_A._Knopf" title="Alfred A. Knopf">Alfred A. Knopf</a> in 1928. This edition was posthumously re-issued in paperback there both by Signet Books and by <a href="/wiki/Penguin_Books" title="Penguin Books">Penguin Books</a> in 1946.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> When the full unexpurgated edition of <i>Lady Chatterley\'s Lover</i> was published by Penguin Books in Britain in 1960, the trial of Penguin under the <a href="/wiki/Obscene_Publications_Act_1959" title="Obscene Publications Act 1959">Obscene Publications Act</a> of 1959 became a major public event and a test of the new obscenity law. The 1959 act (introduced by <a href="/wiki/Roy_Jenkins" title="Roy Jenkins">Roy Jenkins</a>) had made it possible for publishers to escape conviction if they could show that a work was of literary merit. One of the objections was to the frequent use of the word "fuck" and its derivatives and the word "<a href="/wiki/Cunt" title="Cunt">cunt</a>".\n</p><p>Various academic critics and experts of diverse kinds, including <a href="/wiki/E._M._Forster" title="E. M. Forster">E. M. Forster</a>, <a href="/wiki/Helen_Gardner_(critic)" title="Helen Gardner (critic)">Helen Gardner</a>, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Hoggart" title="Richard Hoggart">Richard Hoggart</a>, <a href="/wiki/Raymond_Williams" title="Raymond Williams">Raymond Williams</a> and <a href="/wiki/Norman_St_John-Stevas" title="Norman St John-Stevas">Norman St John-Stevas</a>, were called as witnesses, and the verdict, delivered on 2 November 1960, was "not guilty". This resulted in a far greater degree of freedom for publishing explicit material in the UK. The prosecution was ridiculed for being out of touch with changing social norms when the chief prosecutor, <a href="/wiki/Mervyn_Griffith-Jones" title="Mervyn Griffith-Jones">Mervyn Griffith-Jones</a>, asked if it were the kind of book "you would wish your wife or servants to read".\n</p><p>The Penguin second edition, published in 1961, contains a publisher\'s dedication, which reads: "For having published this book, Penguin Books were prosecuted under the Obscene Publications Act, 1959 at the <a href="/wiki/Old_Bailey" title="Old Bailey">Old Bailey</a> in London from 20 October to 2 November 1960. This edition is therefore dedicated to the twelve jurors, three women and nine men, who returned a verdict of \'Not Guilty\' and thus made D. H. Lawrence\'s last novel available for the first time to the public in the United Kingdom."\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Posthumous_reputation">Posthumous reputation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=D._H._Lawrence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Posthumous reputation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2><p>\nThe obituaries shortly after Lawrence\'s death were, with the exception of the one by <a href="/wiki/E._M._Forster" title="E. M. Forster">E. M. Forster</a>, unsympathetic or hostile. However, there were those who articulated a more favourable recognition of the significance of this author\'s life and works. For example, his long-time friend <a href="/wiki/Catherine_Carswell" title="Catherine Carswell">Catherine Carswell</a> summed up his life in a letter to the periodical <i><a href="/wiki/Time_and_Tide_(magazine)" title="Time and Tide (magazine)">Time and Tide</a></i> published on 16 March 1930. In response to his critics, she wrote:</p><blockquote><p>In the face of formidable initial disadvantages and lifelong delicacy, poverty that lasted for three quarters of his life and hostility that survives his death, he did nothing that he did not really want to do, and all that he most wanted to do he did. He went all over the world, he owned a ranch, he lived in the most beautiful corners of Europe, and met whom he wanted to meet and told them that they were wrong and he was right. He painted and made things, and sang, and rode. He wrote something like three dozen books, of which even the worst page dances with life that could be mistaken for no other man\'s, while the best are admitted, even by those who hate him, to be unsurpassed. Without vices, with most human virtues, the husband of one wife, scrupulously honest, this estimable citizen yet managed to keep free from the shackles of civilization and the cant of literary cliques. He would have laughed lightly and cursed venomously in passing at the solemn owls\xe2\x80\x94each one secretly chained by the leg\xe2\x80\x94who now conduct his inquest. To do his work and lead his life in spite of them took some doing, but he did it, and long after they are forgotten, sensitive and innocent people\xe2\x80\x94if any are left\xe2\x80\x94will turn Lawrence\'s pages and will know from them what sort of a rare man Lawrence was.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote><p>Aldous Huxley also defended Lawrence in his introduction to a collection of letters published in 1932. However, the most influential advocate of Lawrence\'s literary reputation was <a href="/wiki/Cambridge" title="Cambridge">Cambridge</a> literary critic <a href="/wiki/F._R._Leavis" title="F. R. Leavis">F. R. Leavis</a>, who asserted that the author had made an important contribution to the tradition of English fiction. Leavis stressed that <i>The Rainbow</i>, <i>Women in Love</i>, and the short stories and tales were major works of art. Later, the obscenity trials over the unexpurgated edition of <i><a href="/wiki/Lady_Chatterley%27s_Lover" title="Lady Chatterley&#39;s Lover">Lady Chatterley\'s Lover</a></i> in America in 1959, and in Britain in 1960, and subsequent publication of the full text, ensured Lawrence\'s popularity (and notoriety) with a wider public.\n</p><p>Since 2008, an annual D. H. Lawrence Festival has been organised in Eastwood to celebrate Lawrence\'s life and works; in September 2016, events were held in Cornwall to celebrate the centenary of Lawrence\'s connection with <a href="/wiki/Zennor" title="Zennor">Zennor</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span id="Selected_depictions_of_Lawrence.27s_life"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Selected_depictions_of_Lawrence\'s_life">Selected depictions of Lawrence\'s life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=D._H._Lawrence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Selected depictions of Lawrence&#039;s life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul><li><i><a href="/wiki/Priest_of_Love" title="Priest of Love">Priest of Love</a></i>: a 1981 film based on the non-fiction biography of Lawrence of the same name.
