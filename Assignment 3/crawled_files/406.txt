id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>\t\t\t\t<div id="contentSub"></div>\n\t\t\t\t<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>\t\t\t\t<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:184px;"><a href="/wiki/File:LocationBermuda.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/ff/LocationBermuda.png" width="182" height="115" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="182" data-file-height="115" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:LocationBermuda.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Bermuda in respect to the <a href="/wiki/Southern_United_States" title="Southern United States">Southern United States</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Greater_Antilles" title="Greater Antilles">Greater Antilles</a>.</div></div></div>\n<p><b>Bermudian English</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Regional_accents_of_English" title="Regional accents of English">regional accent of English</a> found in <a href="/wiki/Bermuda" title="Bermuda">Bermuda</a>, a British <a href="/wiki/British_Overseas_Territories" title="British Overseas Territories">overseas territory</a> in the <a href="/wiki/North_Atlantic" class="mw-redirect" title="North Atlantic">North Atlantic</a>. <a href="/wiki/Standard_English" title="Standard English">Standard English</a> is used in professional settings and in writing, while <a href="/wiki/Vernacular" title="Vernacular">vernacular</a> Bermudian English is spoken on more casual occasions.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>  The Bermudian accent began to develop following settlement in the early 17th century, and retains traits of <a href="/wiki/Early_Modern_English" title="Early Modern English">Elizabethan English</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Casual observers tend to have difficulty in placing the Bermudian accent, as it differs from those that are clearly <a href="/wiki/British_English" title="British English">British</a>, <a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">American</a> or <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_English" title="Caribbean English">Caribbean</a>; they also note that the accent tends to vary between individuals.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>  To Americans, it sounds slightly British, while the British find it more American.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Categorisation">Categorisation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bermudian_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Categorisation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Bermudian English has been called "one of the most severely underresearched varieties of English".<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> It primarily shows a mixture of traits typical of British English and American English, and is generally classified as a form of American (rather than Caribbean) English.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The most detailed scholarly study of Bermudian English, in 1933, stated that this type of speech "would create least remark, if indeed any, between, say, <a href="/wiki/Norfolk,_Virginia" title="Norfolk, Virginia">Norfolk, Virginia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Charleston,_South_Carolina" title="Charleston, South Carolina">Charleston, South Carolina</a>";<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> British-Bermudian actor <a href="/wiki/Earl_Cameron_(actor)" title="Earl Cameron (actor)">Earl Cameron</a> noted that because the Bermudian accent sounded American, he was able to land a speaking role in 1942.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Large scale West Indian immigration to Bermuda, especially Sandys and Pembroke parishes, began with the expansion of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Naval_Dockyard,_Bermuda" title="Royal Naval Dockyard, Bermuda">Royal Naval Dockyard</a> (as a result of the lack of cheap, unskilled labour in Bermuda) at the turn of the twentieth century, and affected the dialect of certain demographic groups. Consequently, in certain aspects of vocalization, some Bermudian English dialects are close to some versions of Caribbean English,<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> and some would bracket all these varieties to the broad region of the "English-speaking West Indies".<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Azorean Portuguese has also impacted on Bermudian English as a result of immigration since the 1840s.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bermudian_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The accent\'s most evident characteristic is a variation in letter/sound assignment. The switching of <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[v]</span> and <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[w]</span>,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> characteristic of many dialects in Southern England during the 18th and 19th centuries,<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> and of <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[d]</span> and <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[d\xca\x92]</span> (similarly to the dialects of English speakers of Gaelic heritage), when combined with a <a href="/wiki/Front_vowel" title="Front vowel">front vowel</a>, can both be seen in the title of a humorous glossary, <i>Bermewjan Vurds</i> (Bermudian Words).<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>  The use of <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[\xc3\xa6]</span> and <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[\xc9\x9b]</span> is interchangeable and vowels are often elongated.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bermudian_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist" style="list-style-type: decimal;">\n<div class="mw-references-wrap mw-references-columns"><ol class="references">\n<li id="cite_note-1"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-1">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Ruth Thomas, "Notes on Bermudian Language", in "Bermuda connections", <i>Smithsonian Folklife Festival. 2001</i>.
