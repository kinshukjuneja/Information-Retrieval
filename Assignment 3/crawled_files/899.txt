Alliance</b> (<a href="/wiki/Scots_language" title="Scots language">Scots</a> for "Old Alliance"; <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <i lang="fr">Vieille Alliance</i>; <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Gaelic_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Scottish Gaelic language">Scottish Gaelic</a>: <i lang="gd">An Seann-chaidreachas</i><sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>) was an alliance made in 1295 between the kingdoms of <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Scotland" title="Kingdom of Scotland">Scotland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_France" title="Kingdom of France">France</a>. The alliance was formed for the purpose of controlling England\'s numerous  invasions. The <a href="/wiki/Scots_language" title="Scots language">Scots</a> word <i>auld</i>, meaning <i>old</i>, has become a partly affectionate term for the long-lasting alliance between the two countries. It remained until the signing of the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Edinburgh" title="Treaty of Edinburgh">Treaty of Edinburgh</a> in 1560.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> \n</p><p>The alliance played a significant role in the relations between Scotland, France and <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_England" title="Kingdom of England">England</a> from its beginning in 1295 to 1560. The alliance was renewed by all the <a href="/wiki/French_monarchy" class="mw-redirect" title="French monarchy">French</a> and <a href="/wiki/Scottish_monarchs" class="mw-redirect" title="Scottish monarchs">Scottish monarchs</a> of that period except <a href="/wiki/Louis_XI" class="mw-redirect" title="Louis XI">Louis XI</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-naturalisation_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-naturalisation-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> By the late 14th century, the renewal occurred regardless of whether either kingdom was in a war with England at the time.<sup id="cite_ref-wiley_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wiley-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The alliance dates from the treaty signed by <a href="/wiki/John_Balliol" title="John Balliol">John Balliol</a> and <a href="/wiki/Philip_IV_of_France" title="Philip IV of France">Philip IV of France</a> in 1295 against <a href="/wiki/Edward_I_of_England" title="Edward I of England">Edward Longshanks</a>. The terms of the treaty stipulated that if either country was attacked by <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_England" title="Kingdom of England">England</a>, the other country would invade English territory, as seen in the 1513 <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Flodden" title="Battle of Flodden">Battle of Flodden</a>, where the Scots invaded England in response to the English\'s campaign against France. The alliance played an important role in conflicts between both countries and England, such as the <a href="/wiki/Wars_of_Scottish_Independence" title="Wars of Scottish Independence">Wars of Scottish Independence</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Hundred_Years%27_War" title="Hundred Years&#39; War">Hundred Years\' War</a>, the <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_League_of_Cambrai" title="War of the League of Cambrai">War of the League of Cambrai</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Rough_Wooing" title="Rough Wooing">Rough Wooing</a>.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Auld_Alliance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The dynastic turmoil caused by the death of Scotland\'s seven-year-old queen, <a href="/wiki/Margaret,_Maid_of_Norway" title="Margaret, Maid of Norway">Margaret</a>, left the covetous king <a href="/wiki/Edward_I_of_England" title="Edward I of England">Edward I of England</a> with an irresistible opportunity to assert his authority over <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>. By 1295 it was clear that Edward was on a course for total subjugation of Scotland. In response the Council of Twelve who had taken over the government of Scotland temporarily, sought alliances wherever they could be found. With <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> and <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> close to war following <a href="/wiki/Philippe_IV" class="mw-redirect" title="Philippe IV">Philippe IV</a>\'s declaration of England\'s possession of <a href="/wiki/Gascony" title="Gascony">Gascony</a> forfeit in 1293, alliance with France was a clear course to take. In October 1295, a Scottish embassy to Philippe agreed to the Treaty of Paris.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>As with all subsequent renewals of what became the Auld Alliance, the treaty slightly favoured <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> more than <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>. The French were required to do no more than continue their struggle against the English in Gascony. However, the cost of any outright war between Scotland and <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> was to be borne entirely by the Scots. Nevertheless, Scotland, as remote and impoverished as it was, was now aligned to a major European power. Even if more symbolic than actual, the benefits of the alliance mattered greatly to Scotland.<sup id="cite_ref-Cussans_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cussans-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the short term however, the treaty proved to have no protection against Edward, whose swift and devastating invasion of Scotland in 1296 all but eradicated its independence. Furthermore, the cessation of hostilities between England and France in 1299, followed by the treaty of "perpetual peace and friendship," allowed Edward to devote all of his attention and forces to attack the Scots. Scotland, in the end, owed its eventual survival to the military acumen and inspiration of <a href="/wiki/Robert_the_Bruce" title="Robert the Bruce">Robert the Bruce</a> and the mistakes of <a href="/wiki/Edward_II" class="mw-redirect" title="Edward II">Edward II</a>, rather than its Auld Alliance with France.\n</p><p>In 1326, Robert the Bruce renewed the alliance, with the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Corbeil_(1326)" title="Treaty of Corbeil (1326)">Treaty of Corbeil</a>. The motive for this renewal was precautionary more than anything: neither realm seemed to have much to fear from England at the time. This, however, rapidly changed after 1330 when <a href="/wiki/Edward_III" class="mw-redirect" title="Edward III">Edward III</a> set out to complete his conquest of Scotland and to reassert his power in France. For the first time the Franco-Scottish alliance had been given a sense of emergency.\n</p><p>In 1346, Edward overwhelmed French forces at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Cr%C3%A9cy" title="Battle of Cr\xc3\xa9cy">Battle of Cr\xc3\xa9cy</a>. Two months later, <a href="/wiki/David_II_of_Scotland" title="David II of Scotland">David II of Scotland</a> was captured at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Neville%27s_Cross" title="Battle of Neville&#39;s Cross">Battle of Neville\'s Cross</a>, in a botched invasion of Northern England. His 11-year absence as Edward\'s prisoner only increased the internal turmoil and power struggles of Scotland. <a href="/wiki/David_II_of_Scotland" title="David II of Scotland">David II</a> was forced to reach a deal with <a href="/wiki/Edward_III" class="mw-redirect" title="Edward III">Edward III</a> to gain his freedom. Even after his release in 1357, David spent most of his remaining reign attempting to further English interests in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Cussans_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cussans-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The alliance was <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Vincennes-Edinburgh" title="Treaty of Vincennes-Edinburgh">renewed</a> between the two kingdoms in 1371, with the embassy of the <a href="/wiki/Walter_Wardlaw" title="Walter Wardlaw">Bishop of Glasgow</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Archibald_the_Grim" class="mw-redirect" title="Archibald the Grim">Lord of Galloway</a> to France. The treaty was signed by <a href="/wiki/Charles_V_of_France" title="Charles V of France">Charles V</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Ch%C3%A2teau_de_Vincennes" title="Ch\xc3\xa2teau de Vincennes">Ch\xc3\xa2teau de Vincennes</a> on 30 June, and at <a href="/wiki/Edinburgh_Castle" title="Edinburgh Castle">Edinburgh Castle</a> by <a href="/wiki/Robert_II_of_Scotland" title="Robert II of Scotland">Robert II</a> on 28 October.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The accession of pro-French <a href="/wiki/Robert_II_of_Scotland" title="Robert II of Scotland">Robert II</a> led to the immediate renewal of the alliance; however, the benefits to <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> were mixed. Plans were drawn up in 1385 for a Franco-Scottish invasion of <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>. This included the dispatch of a small French force to Scotland for the first time. These plans never came to any form of action after the French invasion failed to materialise. The deteriorating relations between France and Scotland were summed up by the French Chronicler <a href="/wiki/Jean_Froissart" title="Jean Froissart">Jean Froissart</a> who "wished the King of France would make a truce with the English for two or three years and then march to Scotland and utterly destroy it".<sup id="cite_ref-test_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-test-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Yet it was necessity that had driven the two kingdoms together and the need to resist aggressive new Lancastrian Kings of <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> that kept the alliance together in the 15th century. In 1418, with France on the brink of surrendering to the forces of <a href="/wiki/Henry_V_of_England" title="Henry V of England">Henry V</a>, the Dauphin, <a href="/wiki/Charles_VII_of_France" title="Charles VII of France">Charles VII</a>, called on his Scottish allies for help. Between 1419 and 1424 as many as 15,000 Scottish troops were sent to France.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>French and Scottish forces together won against the English at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Baug%C3%A9" title="Battle of Baug\xc3\xa9">Battle of Baug\xc3\xa9</a> in 1421. As it marked the turning point of the <a href="/wiki/Hundred_Years_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Hundred Years War">Hundred Years War</a>, the significance of this battle was great. However, their victory was a short-lived one: at <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Verneuil" title="Battle of Verneuil">Verneuil</a> in 1424, the Scots army was defeated. Despite this defeat, the Scots had given France a valuable breathing space, effectively saving the country from English domination.<sup id="cite_ref-Cussans_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cussans-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In addition, in 1429 Scots came to the aid of <a href="/wiki/Joan_of_Arc" title="Joan of Arc">Joan of Arc</a> in her famous <a href="/wiki/Relief_of_Orl%C3%A9ans" class="mw-redirect" title="Relief of Orl\xc3\xa9ans">relief of Orl\xc3\xa9ans</a>. Scottish soldiers also served in the <a href="/wiki/Garde_%C3%89cossaise" title="Garde \xc3\x89cossaise">Garde \xc3\x89cossaise</a>, the loyal bodyguard of the French monarchy. Many members of the Scottish expeditions to France chose to settle there. Some officers were granted lands and titles in France. In the 15th and 16th centuries, they became naturalised French subjects.<sup id="cite_ref-naturalisation_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-naturalisation-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Francois_Second_Mary_Stuart.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Francois_Second_Mary_Stuart.jpg/220px-Francois_Second_Mary_Stuart.jpg" width="220" height="288" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Francois_Second_Mary_Stuart.jpg/330px-Francois_Second_Mary_Stuart.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/3a/Francois_Second_Mary_Stuart.jpg 2x" data-file-width="394" data-file-height="515" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Francois_Second_Mary_Stuart.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The marriage of <a href="/wiki/Mary,_Queen_of_Scots" title="Mary, Queen of Scots">Mary, Queen of Scots</a> with <a href="/wiki/Francis_II_of_France" title="Francis II of France">Francis II of France</a> briefly revived the Auld Alliance</div></div></div>\n<p>Through the rest of the 15th century the alliance was formally renewed four times.<sup id="cite_ref-Cussans_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cussans-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The eventual victory of <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Hundred_Years_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Hundred Years War">Hundred Years War</a> combined with the turmoil in <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> following the <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_Roses" class="mw-redirect" title="War of the Roses">War of the Roses</a> meant that the English threat was greatly reduced, thus rendering the alliance almost obsolete. But it didn\'t stop the Auld Alliance from taking part in the war, and from it, they gained the advantage of taking many strongholds and a few possessions such as <a href="/wiki/Jersey" title="Jersey">Jersey</a> and <a href="/wiki/Berwick-On-Tweed" class="mw-redirect" title="Berwick-On-Tweed">Berwick-On-Tweed</a> from England, in exchange of helping to support the Lancastrian causes against the Yorkists during the war, and, despite the Yorkists winning and managing to regain the possessions given to them, they were still supportive to England\'s rebels against Yorkist rule, even supporting the last Lancastrian, <a href="/wiki/Henry_VII_of_England" title="Henry VII of England">Henry VII</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Bosworth" class="mw-redirect" title="Battle of Bosworth">Battle of Bosworth</a> in 1485. As the 16th Century began, the marriage of Henry VII\'s daughters <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Tudor" title="Margaret Tudor">Margaret Tudor</a> to <a href="/wiki/James_IV_of_Scotland" title="James IV of Scotland">James IV of Scotland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mary_Tudor,_Queen_of_France" title="Mary Tudor, Queen of France">Mary Tudor</a> to <a href="/wiki/Louis_XII_of_France" title="Louis XII of France">Louis XII of France</a>, as a sign of peace from England, appeared to have finally ended the Franco-Scottish alliance for good.\n</p><p>It underwent a dramatic revival in 1512 when it was formally reviewed (as it was again in 1517 and 1548). Both soon petered out but <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> still suffered badly following the death of <a href="/wiki/James_IV_of_Scotland" title="James IV of Scotland">James IV</a> and most of his nobles at <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Flodden" title="Battle of Flodden">Flodden</a> in 1513. Periodic Anglo-French and Anglo-Scottish conflict throughout the 15th century continued, but the certainties that had driven the Auld Alliance were disappearing. As <a href="/wiki/Protestantism" title="Protestantism">Protestantism</a> gained ground in Scotland opinion grew there that favoured closer links with England than with France.<sup id="cite_ref-Cussans_6-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cussans-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1558 the alliance between the two kingdoms was finally revived with the marriage of <a href="/wiki/Mary,_Queen_of_Scots" title="Mary, Queen of Scots">Mary, Queen of Scots</a> to the future <a href="/wiki/Francis_II_of_France" title="Francis II of France">Francis II of France</a>, but only until 1560.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> After Mary\'s exile in 1568 to England, Scotland was transformed by its new king <a href="/wiki/James_VI_and_I" title="James VI and I">James VI</a>, who was heir to the English throne as well as Scotland\'s, to a <a href="/wiki/Protestant" class="mw-redirect" title="Protestant">Protestant</a> nation. His desire to form close links with <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> meant that the alliance had outlived its usefulness. In the 1560s, after more than 250 years, formal treaties between Scotland and France were officially ended by the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Edinburgh" title="Treaty of Edinburgh">Treaty of Edinburgh</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> With the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Reformation" title="Scottish Reformation">Scottish Reformation</a>, Scotland was declared <a href="/wiki/Protestant" class="mw-redirect" title="Protestant">Protestant</a>, and allied itself with Protestant England instead. During the Reformation, the Protestant <a href="/wiki/Lords_of_the_Congregation" title="Lords of the Congregation">Lords of the Congregation</a> had rejected the Auld Alliance and brokered English military support with their <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Berwick_(1560)" title="Treaty of Berwick (1560)">treaty of Berwick</a> against the French Regent <a href="/wiki/Mary_of_Guise" title="Mary of Guise">Mary of Guise</a>. Two hundred Scottish soldiers were sent to Normandy in 1562 to aid the French <a href="/wiki/Huguenots" title="Huguenots">Huguenots</a> in their struggle against royal authority during the <a href="/wiki/French_Wars_of_Religion" title="French Wars of Religion">French Wars of Religion</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Garde_%C3%89cossaise" title="Garde \xc3\x89cossaise">Garde \xc3\x89cossaise</a>, however, continued until 1830 when <a href="/wiki/Charles_X" class="mw-redirect" title="Charles X">Charles X</a> of France abdicated.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Wider_influence">Wider influence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Auld_Alliance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Wider influence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The alliance extended into the lives of the Scottish population in a number of ways, including <a href="/wiki/Architecture" title="Architecture">architecture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scots_law" title="Scots law">law</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Scots_language" title="Scots language">Scots language</a> and <a href="/wiki/Scottish_cuisine" title="Scottish cuisine">cuisine</a>. Scottish Soldiery within the French army was one contributor, but so were reciprocal dual nationality agreements,<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> privileges France granted to Scottish <a href="/wiki/Vintners" class="mw-redirect" title="Vintners">vintners</a><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> and the number of Scots who studied at French universities, something which continued up until the <a href="/wiki/Napoleonic_Wars" title="Napoleonic Wars">Napoleonic Wars</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/David_de_Moravia" title="David de Moravia">David de Moravia</a>, Bishop of Moray, helped found the <a href="/wiki/Scots_College_(Paris)" title="Scots College (Paris)">Scots College</a> of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Paris" title="University of Paris">University of Paris</a> and amongst those who studied or taught at French Universities were the poets <a href="/wiki/John_Barbour_(poet)" title="John Barbour (poet)">John Barbour</a> and <a href="/wiki/George_Buchanan" title="George Buchanan">George Buchanan</a>; the historian <a href="/wiki/Hector_Boece" title="Hector Boece">Hector Boece</a>; the founder of <a href="/wiki/St_Andrews_University" class="mw-redirect" title="St Andrews University">St Andrews University</a>, <a href="/wiki/Henry_Wardlaw" title="Henry Wardlaw">Henry Wardlaw</a>; the founder of <a href="/wiki/Aberdeen_University" class="mw-redirect" title="Aberdeen University">Aberdeen University</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_Elphinstone" title="William Elphinstone">William Elphinstone</a>; the founder of the <a href="/wiki/Advocates_Library" title="Advocates Library">Advocates Library</a>, <a href="/wiki/George_Mackenzie_of_Rosehaugh" title="George Mackenzie of Rosehaugh">George Mackenzie</a> and the translator of <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Rabelais" title="Fran\xc3\xa7ois Rabelais"> Rabelais</a>, Sir <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Urquhart" title="Thomas Urquhart">Thomas Urquhart</a>. Examples of architectural influence include two Scottish castles built with French castle-building in mind: <a href="/wiki/Bothwell_Castle" title="Bothwell Castle">Bothwell</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kildrummy_Castle" title="Kildrummy Castle">Kildrummy</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Auld_Alliance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:122px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Free_French_House_plaque,_Regent_Terrace,_Edinburgh.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Free_French_House_plaque%2C_Regent_Terrace%2C_Edinburgh.jpg/120px-Free_French_House_plaque%2C_Regent_Terrace%2C_Edinburgh.jpg" width="120" height="92" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Free_French_House_plaque%2C_Regent_Terrace%2C_Edinburgh.jpg/180px-Free_French_House_plaque%2C_Regent_Terrace%2C_Edinburgh.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Free_French_House_plaque%2C_Regent_Terrace%2C_Edinburgh.jpg/240px-Free_French_House_plaque%2C_Regent_Terrace%2C_Edinburgh.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2510" data-file-height="1916" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Free_French_House_plaque,_Regent_Terrace,_Edinburgh.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>"La plus vieille alliance du monde"</div></div></div>\n<p>In a speech which he delivered in Edinburgh in June 1942, <a href="/wiki/Charles_de_Gaulle" title="Charles de Gaulle">Charles de Gaulle</a> described the alliance between Scotland and France as "the oldest alliance in the world". He also declared that:<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<blockquote><p>"In every combat where for five centuries the destiny of France was at stake, there were always men of Scotland to fight side by side with men of France, and what Frenchmen feel is that no people has ever been more generous than yours with its friendship."</p></blockquote>\n<p>In 1995, celebrations were held in both countries for the 700th anniversary of the beginning of the alliance.<sup id="cite_ref-wiley_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wiley-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> <br />\n</p><p>In 2011, British historian Dr Siobhan Talbott published the result of her research on this matter and concluded accordingly that the Auld Alliance has never been revoked.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span
