id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>\t\t\t\t<div id="contentSub"><span class="mw-redirectedfrom">\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(Redirected from <a /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Pipe_band_in_the_Canongate,_Edinburgh.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Lonach Pipe band, <a href="/wiki/Edinburgh" title="Edinburgh">Edinburgh</a> Scotland, 2009. Pipe bands are among the most recognizable forms of traditional Scottish music.</div></div></div>\n<p><b>Scottish folk music</b> (also <b>Scottish traditional music</b>) is music that uses forms that are identified as part of the Scottish musical tradition. There is evidence that there was a flourishing culture of popular music in Scotland during the late Middle Ages, but the only song with a melody to survive from this period is the "Pleugh Song". After the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Reformation" title="Scottish Reformation">Reformation</a>, the secular popular tradition of music continued, despite attempts by the Kirk, particularly in the Lowlands, to suppress dancing and events like <a href="/wiki/Penny_wedding" class="mw-redirect" title="Penny wedding">penny weddings</a>. The first clear reference to the use of the <a href="/wiki/Highland_bagpipes" class="mw-redirect" title="Highland bagpipes">Highland bagpipes</a> mentions their use at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Pinkie_Cleugh" title="Battle of Pinkie Cleugh">Battle of Pinkie Cleugh</a> in 1547. The Highlands in the early seventeenth century saw the development of piping families including the <a href="/wiki/MacCrimmon_(piping_family)" title="MacCrimmon (piping family)">MacCrimmons</a>, MacArthurs, <a href="/wiki/Clan_Gregor" title="Clan Gregor">MacGregors</a> and the Mackays of <a href="/wiki/Gairloch" title="Gairloch">Gairloch</a>. There is also evidence of adoption of the fiddle in the Highlands. Well-known musicians included the fiddler Pattie Birnie and the piper <a href="/wiki/Habbie_Simpson" title="Habbie Simpson">Habbie Simpson</a>. This tradition continued into the nineteenth century, with major figures such as the fiddlers <a href="/wiki/Niel_Gow" title="Niel Gow">Neil</a>  and his son <a href="/wiki/Nathaniel_Gow" title="Nathaniel Gow">Nathaniel Gow</a>. There is evidence of <a href="/wiki/Ballad" title="Ballad">ballads</a> from this period. Some may date back to the late Medieval era and deal with events and people that can be traced back as far as the thirteenth century. They remained an oral tradition until they were collected as folk songs in the eighteenth century.\n</p><p>The earliest printed collection of secular music comes from the seventeenth century. Collection began to gain momentum in the early eighteenth century and, as the kirk\'s opposition to music waned, there were a flood of publications including <a href="/wiki/Allan_Ramsay_(poet)" title="Allan Ramsay (poet)">Allan Ramsay</a>\'s verse compendium <i>The Tea Table Miscellany</i> (1723) and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Scots_Musical_Museum" class="mw-redirect" title="The Scots Musical Museum">The Scots Musical Museum</a></i> (1787 to 1803) by James Johnson and <a href="/wiki/Robert_Burns" title="Robert Burns">Robert Burns</a>. From the late nineteenth century there was renewed interest in traditional music, which was more academic and political in intent. In Scotland collectors included the Reverend James Duncan and <a href="/wiki/Gavin_Greig" title="Gavin Greig">Gavin Greig</a>. Major performers included <a href="/wiki/James_Scott_Skinner" title="James Scott Skinner">James Scott Skinner</a>. This revival began to have a major impact on classical music, with the development of what was in effect a national school of orchestral and operatic music in Scotland, with composers such as included <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Mackenzie_(composer)" title="Alexander Mackenzie (composer)">Alexander Mackenzie</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_Wallace_(Scottish_composer)" title="William Wallace (Scottish composer)">William Wallace</a>, Learmont Drysdale, <a href="/wiki/Hamish_MacCunn" title="Hamish MacCunn">Hamish MacCunn</a>  and <a href="/wiki/John_Blackwood_McEwen" title="John Blackwood McEwen">John McEwen</a>.\n</p><p>After World War II traditional music in Scotland was marginalised, but remained a living tradition. This was changed by individuals including <a href="/wiki/Alan_Lomax" title="Alan Lomax">Alan Lomax</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hamish_Henderson" title="Hamish Henderson">Hamish Henderson</a> and <a href="/wiki/Peter_Douglas_Kennedy" title="Peter Douglas Kennedy">Peter Kennedy</a>, through collecting, publications, recordings and radio programmes. Acts that were popularised included  <a href="/wiki/John_Strachan_(singer)" title="John Strachan (singer)">John Strachan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_MacBeath" title="Jimmy MacBeath">Jimmy MacBeath</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jeannie_Robertson" title="Jeannie Robertson">Jeannie Robertson</a> and <a href="/wiki/Flora_MacNeil" title="Flora MacNeil">Flora MacNeil</a>. In the 1960s there was a flourishing <a href="/wiki/Folk_club" title="Folk club">folk club</a> culture and <a href="/wiki/Ewan_MacColl" title="Ewan MacColl">Ewan MacColl</a> emerged as a leading figure in the revival in Britain. They hosted traditional performers, including Donald Higgins and the <a href="/wiki/Belle_Stewart" title="Belle Stewart">Stewarts of Blairgowrie</a>, beside English performers and new Scottish revivalists such as <a href="/wiki/Robin_Hall" title="Robin Hall">Robin Hall</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jimmie_Macgregor" title="Jimmie Macgregor">Jimmie Macgregor</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Corries" title="The Corries">The Corries</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Ian_Campbell_Folk_Group" title="Ian Campbell Folk Group">Ian Campbell Folk Group</a>. There was also a strand of popular Scottish music that benefited from the arrival of radio and television, which relied on images of Scottishness derived from <a href="/wiki/Tartanry" title="Tartanry">tartanry</a> and stereotypes employed in <a href="/wiki/Music_hall" title="Music hall">music hall</a> and <a href="/wiki/Variety_show" title="Variety show">variety</a>, exemplified by the TV programme <i><a href="/wiki/The_White_Heather_Club" title="The White Heather Club">The White Heather Club</a></i> which ran from 1958 to 1967, hosted by <a href="/wiki/Andy_Stewart_(musician)" title="Andy Stewart (musician)">Andy Stewart</a> and starring <a href="/wiki/Moira_Anderson" title="Moira Anderson">Moira Anderson</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kenneth_McKellar_(singer)" title="Kenneth McKellar (singer)">Kenneth McKeller</a>. The fusing of various styles of American music with British folk created a distinctive form of <a href="/wiki/Fingerstyle_guitar" title="Fingerstyle guitar">fingerstyle guitar</a> playing known as <a href="/wiki/Folk_baroque" title="Folk baroque">folk baroque</a>, pioneered by figures including <a href="/wiki/Davy_Graham" class="mw-redirect" title="Davy Graham">Davy Graham</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bert_Jansch" title="Bert Jansch">Bert Jansch</a>. Others totally abandoned the traditional element including <a href="/wiki/Donovan" title="Donovan">Donovan</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Incredible_String_Band" class="mw-redirect" title="Incredible String Band">Incredible String Band</a>, who have been seen as developing <a href="/wiki/Psychedelic_folk" title="Psychedelic folk">psychedelic folk</a>. Acoustic groups who continued to interpret traditional material through into the 1970s included <a href="/wiki/Ossian_(band)" title="Ossian (band)">Ossian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Silly_Wizard" title="Silly Wizard">Silly Wizard</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Boys_of_the_Lough" title="The Boys of the Lough">The Boys of the Lough</a>,  <a href="/wiki/Battlefield_Band" title="Battlefield Band">Battlefield Band</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Clutha" title="The Clutha">The Clutha</a> and the Whistlebinkies.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Celtic_rock" title="Celtic rock">Celtic rock</a> developed as a variant of <a href="/wiki/British_folk_rock" title="British folk rock">British folk rock</a> by Scottish groups including the <a href="/wiki/JSD_Band" title="JSD Band">JSD Band</a> and Spencer\'s Feat.\n<a href="/wiki/Five_Hand_Reel" title="Five Hand Reel">Five Hand Reel</a>, who combined Irish and Scottish personnel, emerged as the most successful exponents of the style. From the late 1970s the attendance at, and numbers of, folk clubs began to decrease, as new musical and social trends began to dominate. However, in Scotland the circuit of <a href="/wiki/Ceilidh" class="mw-redirect" title="Ceilidh">ceilidhs</a> and festivals helped prop up traditional music. Two of the most successful groups of the 1980s that emerged from this dance band circuit were <a href="/wiki/Runrig" title="Runrig">Runrig</a> and <a href="/wiki/Capercaillie_(band)" title="Capercaillie (band)">Capercaillie</a>. A by-product of the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_people#Scottish_people_abroad" title="Scottish people">Celtic Diaspora</a> was the existence of large communities across the world that looked for their cultural roots and identity to their origins in the Celtic nations. From the USA this includes Scottish bands <a href="/wiki/Seven_Nations_(band)" title="Seven Nations (band)">Seven Nations</a>, <a href="/wiki/Prydein_(band)" title="Prydein (band)">Prydein</a> and <a href="/wiki/Flatfoot_56" title="Flatfoot 56">Flatfoot 56</a>. From Canada are bands such as <a href="/wiki/Enter_the_Haggis" title="Enter the Haggis">Enter the Haggis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Great_Big_Sea" title="Great Big Sea">Great Big Sea</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Real_Mckenzies" class="mw-redirect" title="The Real Mckenzies">The Real Mckenzies</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spirit_of_the_West" title="Spirit of the West">Spirit of the West</a>.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" style="display:none" /><div class="toctitle" lang="en" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/c/cf/Allan-highlandwedding1780_detail2.JPG/170px-Allan-highlandwedding1780_detail2.JPG" width="170" height="250" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/c/cf/Allan-highlandwedding1780_detail2.JPG 1.5x" data-file-width="252" data-file-height="371" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Allan-highlandwedding1780_detail2.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A detail from <i>The Highland Wedding</i> by <a href="/wiki/David_Allan_(Scottish_painter_1744%E2%80%931796)" class="mw-redirect" title="David Allan (Scottish painter 1744\xe2\x80\x931796)">David Allan</a>, 1780</div></div></div>\n<p>There is evidence that there was a flourishing culture of popular music in <a href="/wiki/Scotland_in_the_Late_Middle_Ages" title="Scotland in the Late Middle Ages">Scotland in the Late Middle Ages</a>. This includes the long list of songs given in <i>The Complaynt of Scotland</i> (1549). Many of the poems of this period were also originally songs, but for none has a notation of their music survived. Melodies have survived separately in the post-Reformation publication of <i>The Gude and Godlie Ballatis</i> (1567),<sup id="cite_ref-Baxter2001app130-33_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Baxter2001app130-33-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> which were spiritual satires on popular songs, adapted and published by the brothers <a href="/wiki/James_Wedderburn_(poet)" title="James Wedderburn (poet)">James</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Wedderburn" title="John Wedderburn">John</a> and <a href="/wiki/Robert_Wedderburn_(poet)" title="Robert Wedderburn (poet)">Robert Wedderburn</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Wormald1991pp187-90_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wormald1991pp187-90-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The only song with a melody to survive from this period is the "Pleugh Song".<sup id="cite_ref-Baxter2001app130-33_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Baxter2001app130-33-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> After the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Reformation" title="Scottish Reformation">Reformation</a>, the secular popular tradition of music continued, despite attempts by the Kirk, particularly in the Lowlands, to suppress dancing and events like <a href="/wiki/Penny_wedding" class="mw-redirect" title="Penny wedding">penny weddings</a> at which tunes were played.<sup id="cite_ref-Porterp22_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Porterp22-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The first clear reference to the use of the <a href="/wiki/Highland_bagpipes" class="mw-redirect" title="Highland bagpipes">Highland bagpipes</a> is from a French history, which mentions their use at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Pinkie_Cleugh" title="Battle of Pinkie Cleugh">Battle of Pinkie Cleugh</a> in 1547. <a href="/wiki/George_Buchanan" title="George Buchanan">George Buchanan</a> claimed that they had replaced the trumpet on the battlefield. This period saw the creation of the ce\xc3\xb2l m\xc3\xb3r (the great music) of the bagpipe, which reflected its martial origins, with battle-tunes, marches, gatherings, salutes and laments.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The Highlands in the early seventeenth century saw the development of piping families including the <a href="/wiki/MacCrimmon_(piping_family)" title="MacCrimmon (piping family)">MacCrimmonds</a>, MacArthurs, <a href="/wiki/Clan_Gregor" title="Clan Gregor">MacGregors</a> and the Mackays of <a href="/wiki/Gairloch" title="Gairloch">Gairloch</a>. There is also evidence of adoption of the fiddle in the Highlands with <a href="/wiki/Martin_Martin" title="Martin Martin">Martin Martin</a> noting in his <i>A Description of the Western Isles of Scotland</i> (1703) that he knew of eighteen players in Lewis alone.<sup id="cite_ref-Porterp35_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Porterp35-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Well-known musicians included the fiddler Pattie Birnie (c. 1635\xe2\x80\x931721) and the piper <a href="/wiki/Habbie_Simpson" title="Habbie Simpson">Habbie Simpson</a> (1550\xe2\x80\x931620).<sup id="cite_ref-Porterp22_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Porterp22-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> This tradition continued into the nineteenth century, with major figures such as the fiddlers <a href="/wiki/Niel_Gow" title="Niel Gow">Neil</a> (1727\xe2\x80\x931807) and his son <a href="/wiki/Nathaniel_Gow" title="Nathaniel Gow">Nathaniel Gow</a> (1763\xe2\x80\x931831), who, along with a large number of anonymous musicians, composed hundreds of fiddle tunes and variations.<sup id="cite_ref-Baxter2001app140-1_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Baxter2001app140-1-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>There is evidence of <a href="/wiki/Ballad" title="Ballad">ballads</a> from this period. Some may date back to the late Medieval era and deal with events and people that can be traced back as far as the thirteenth century, including "<a href="/wiki/Sir_Patrick_Spens" title="Sir Patrick Spens">Sir Patrick Spens</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Thomas_the_Rhymer" title="Thomas the Rhymer">Thomas the Rhymer</a>", but for which there is no evidence until the eighteenth century.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Scottish ballads are distinct, showing some pre-Christian influences in the inclusion of supernatural elements such as the fairies in the Scottish ballad "<a href="/wiki/Tam_Lin" title="Tam Lin">Tam Lin</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-Broadview2006_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Broadview2006-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> They remained an oral tradition until the increased interest in folk songs in the eighteenth century led collectors such as Bishop <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Percy_(bishop_of_Dromore)" title="Thomas Percy (bishop of Dromore)">Thomas Percy</a> to publish volumes of popular ballads.<sup id="cite_ref-Broadview2006_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Broadview2006-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_song_collection">Early song collection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scottish_folk_music&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early song collection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Playfordtunescover.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/Playfordtunescover.jpg/220px-Playfordtunescover.jpg" width="220" height="181" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/Playfordtunescover.jpg/330px-Playfordtunescover.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/Playfordtunescover.jpg/440px-Playfordtunescover.jpg 2x" data-file-width="592" data-file-height="488" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Playfordtunescover.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Cover of John Playford\'s <i>Collection of original Scotch-tunes, (full of the highland humours) for the violin</i> (1700)</div></div></div>\n<p>In Scotland the earliest printed collection of secular music was by publisher John Forbes, produced in <a href="/wiki/Aberdeen" title="Aberdeen">Aberdeen</a> in 1662 as <i>Songs and Fancies: to Thre, Foure, or Five Partes, both Apt for Voices and Viols</i>. It was printed three times in the next twenty years, and contained seventy-seven songs, of which twenty-five were of Scottish origin.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> In the eighteenth century publications included <a href="/wiki/John_Playford" title="John Playford">John Playford</a>\'s <i>Collection of original Scotch-tunes, (full of the highland humours) for the violin</i> (1700), Margaret Sinkler\'s <i>Music Book</i> (1710), James Watson\'s <i>Choice Collection of Comic and Serious Scots Poems both Ancient and Modern</i> 1711. The oppression of secular music and dancing by the kirk began to ease between about 1715 and 1725 and the level of musical activity was reflected in a flood musical publications in broadsheets and compendiums of music such as the makar <a href="/wiki/Allan_Ramsay_(poet)" title="Allan Ramsay (poet)">Allan Ramsay</a>\'s verse compendium <i>The Tea Table Miscellany</i> (1723),  William Thomson\'s <i>Orpheus Caledonius: or, A collection of Scots songs</i> (1733), <a href="/wiki/James_Oswald_(composer)" title="James Oswald (composer)">James Oswald</a>\'s <i>The Caledonian Pocket Companion</i> (1751), and <a href="/wiki/David_Herd_(anthologist)" title="David Herd (anthologist)">David Herd</a>\'s <i>Ancient and modern Scottish songs, heroic ballads, etc.: collected from memory, tradition and ancient authors</i> (1776). These were drawn on for the most influential collection, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Scots_Musical_Museum" class="mw-redirect" title="The Scots Musical Museum">The Scots Musical Museum</a></i> published in six volumes from 1787 to 1803 by <a href="/wiki/James_Johnson_(engraver)" title="James Johnson (engraver)">James Johnson</a> and <a href="/wiki/Robert_Burns" title="Robert Burns">Robert Burns</a>, which also included new words by Burns. The <i>Select Scottish Airs</i> collected by <a href="/wiki/George_Thomson_(musician)" title="George Thomson (musician)">George Thomson</a> and published between 1799 and 1818 included contributions from Burns and <a href="/wiki/Walter_Scott" title="Walter Scott">Walter Scott</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Among Scott\'s early works was the influential collection of ballads <i>The Minstrelsy of the Scottish Border</i> (1802\xe2\x80\x9303).<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Revivals">Revivals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scottish_folk_music&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Revivals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/British_folk_revival" title="British folk revival">British folk revival</a></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="First_revival">First revival</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scottish_folk_music&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: First revival">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Francis_J._Child.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Francis_J._Child.jpg/170px-Francis_J._Child.jpg" width="170" height="241" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Francis_J._Child.jpg/255px-Francis_J._Child.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Francis_J._Child.jpg/340px-Francis_J._Child.jpg 2x" data-file-width="576" data-file-height="815" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Francis_J._Child.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Francis James Child, one of the key figures in beginning the first folk revival</div></div></div>\n<p>From the late nineteenth century there was renewed interest in traditional music, which was more academic and political in intent. <a href="/wiki/Harvard" class="mw-redirect" title="Harvard">Harvard</a> professor <a href="/wiki/Francis_James_Child" title="Francis James Child">Francis James Child</a>\'s (1825\xe2\x80\x9396) eight-volume collection <i><a href="/wiki/The_English_and_Scottish_Popular_Ballads" class="mw-redirect" title="The English and Scottish Popular Ballads">The English and Scottish Popular Ballads</a></i> (1882\xe2\x80\x9392) has been the most influential on defining the repertoire of subsequent performers and the English music teacher Cecil Sharp was probably the most important in understanding of the nature of folk song.<sup id="cite_ref-Sweers2005pp31-8_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sweers2005pp31-8-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> In Scotland collectors included the Reverend James Duncan (1848\xe2\x80\x931917) and <a href="/wiki/Gavin_Greig" title="Gavin Greig">Gavin Greig</a> (1856\xe2\x80\x931914), who collected over 1,000 songs, mainly from Aberdeenshire.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The tradition continued with figures including <a href="/wiki/James_Scott_Skinner" title="James Scott Skinner">James Scott Skinner</a> (1843\xe2\x80\x931927), known as the "Strathspey King", who played the fiddle in venues ranging from the local functions in his native <a href="/wiki/Banchory" title="Banchory">Banchory</a>, to urban centres of the south and at <a href="/wiki/Balmoral_Castle" title="Balmoral Castle">Balmoral</a>. In 1923 the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Scottish_Country_Dance_Society" title="Royal Scottish Country Dance Society">Royal Scottish Country Dance Society</a> was founded in an attempt to preserve traditional Scottish dance that were threatened by the introduction of the continental ballroom dances such as the <a href="/wiki/Waltz" title="Waltz">waltz</a> or <a href="/wiki/Quadrilles" class="mw-redirect" title="Quadrilles">quadrilles</a>. The accordion also began to be a central instrument at Highland balls and dances.<sup id="cite_ref-Baxter2001cpp434-5_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Baxter2001cpp434-5-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>This revival began to have a major impact on classical music, with the development of what was in effect a national school of orchestral and operatic music in Scotland. Major composers included <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Mackenzie_(composer)" title="Alexander Mackenzie (composer)">Alexander Mackenzie</a> (1847\xe2\x80\x931935), <a href="/wiki/William_Wallace_(Scottish_composer)" title="William Wallace (Scottish composer)">William Wallace</a> (1860\xe2\x80\x931940), <a href="/wiki/Learmont_Drysdale" title="Learmont Drysdale">Learmont Drysdale</a> (1866\xe2\x80\x931909), <a href="/wiki/Hamish_MacCunn" title="Hamish MacCunn">Hamish MacCunn</a> (1868\xe2\x80\x931916) and <a href="/wiki/John_Blackwood_McEwen" title="John Blackwood McEwen">John McEwen</a> (1868\xe2\x80\x931948).<sup id="cite_ref-Gardiner2005p195-6_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gardiner2005p195-6-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Mackenzie, who studied in Germany and Italy and mixed Scottish themes with German Romanticism,<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> is best known for his three <i>Scottish Rhapsodies</i> (1879\xe2\x80\x9380, 1911), <i>Pibroch</i> for violin and orchestra (1889) and the <i>Scottish Concerto</i> for piano (1897), all involving Scottish themes and folk melodies.<sup id="cite_ref-Gardiner2005p195-6_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gardiner2005p195-6-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Wallace\'s work included an overture, <i>In Praise of Scottish Poesie</i> (1894).<sup id="cite_ref-Stevenson_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stevenson-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Drysdale\'s work often dealt with Scottish themes, including the overture <i>Tam O\xe2\x80\x99 Shanter</i> (1890), the cantata <i>The Kelpie</i> (1891).<sup id="cite_ref-Wordpress2010_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wordpress2010-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> MacCunn\'s overture <i><a href="/wiki/The_Land_of_the_Mountain_and_the_Flood" title="The Land of the Mountain and the Flood">The Land of the Mountain and the Flood</a></i> (1887), his <i>Six Scotch Dances</i> (1896), his operas <i><a href="/wiki/Jeanie_Deans_(opera)" title="Jeanie Deans (opera)">Jeanie Deans</a></i> (1894) and <i>Dairmid</i> (1897) and choral works on Scottish subjects<sup id="cite_ref-Gardiner2005p195-6_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gardiner2005p195-6-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> have been described by I. G. C. Hutchison as the musical equivalent of the <a href="/wiki/Scots_Baronial" class="mw-redirect" title="Scots Baronial">Scots Baronial</a> castles of <a href="/wiki/Abbotsford_House" title="Abbotsford House">Abbotsford</a> and <a href="/wiki/Balmoral_Castle" title="Balmoral Castle">Balmoral</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Similarly, McEwen\'s <i>Pibroch</i> (1889), <i>Border Ballads</i> (1908) and <i>Solway Symphony</i> (1911) incorporated traditional Scottish folk melodies.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Second_revival">Second revival</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scottish_folk_music&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Second revival">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Hamishhenderson.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/6f/Hamishhenderson.jpg/170px-Hamishhenderson.jpg" width="170" height="272" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/6f/Hamishhenderson.jpg/255px-Hamishhenderson.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/6f/Hamishhenderson.jpg/340px-Hamishhenderson.jpg 2x" data-file-width="408" data-file-height="652" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Hamishhenderson.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The bust of <a href="/wiki/Hamish_Henderson" title="Hamish Henderson">Hamish Henderson</a> in <a href="/wiki/South_Gyle" title="South Gyle">South Gyle</a></div></div></div>\n<p>After World War II traditional music in Scotland was marginalised, but, unlike in England, it remained a much stronger force, with the C\xc3\xa9ilidh house still present in rural communities until the early 1950s and traditional material still performed by the older generation, even if the younger generation tended to prefer modern styles of music. This decline was changed by the actions of individuals such as American musicologist <a href="/wiki/Alan_Lomax" title="Alan Lomax">Alan Lomax</a>, who collected numerous songs in Scotland that were issued by <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Records" title="Columbia Records">Columbia Records</a> around 1955. Radio broadcasts by Lomax, <a href="/wiki/Hamish_Henderson" title="Hamish Henderson">Hamish Henderson</a> and <a href="/wiki/Peter_Douglas_Kennedy" title="Peter Douglas Kennedy">Peter Kennedy</a> (1922\xe2\x80\x932006) were also important in raising awareness of the tradition, particularly Kennedy\'s <i>As I Roved Out</i>, which was largely based around Scottish and Irish music. The <a href="/wiki/School_of_Scottish_Studies" title="School of Scottish Studies">School of Scottish Studies</a> was founded at University of Edinburgh in 1951, with Henderson as a research fellow and a collection of songs begun by <a href="/wiki/Calum_Maclean" title="Calum Maclean">Calum Maclean</a> (1915\xe2\x80\x9360).<sup id="cite_ref-Sweers2005pp256-7_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sweers2005pp256-7-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Acts that were popularised included  <a href="/wiki/John_Strachan_(singer)" title="John Strachan (singer)">John Strachan</a> (1875\xe2\x80\x931958), <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_MacBeath" title="Jimmy MacBeath">Jimmy MacBeath</a> (1894\xe2\x80\x931972), <a href="/wiki/Jeannie_Robertson" title="Jeannie Robertson">Jeannie Robertson</a> (1908\xe2\x80\x9375) and <a href="/wiki/Flora_MacNeil" title="Flora MacNeil">Flora MacNeil</a> (1928-2015).<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> A number festivals also popularised the music, such as <a href="/wiki/Edinburgh_People%27s_Festival" title="Edinburgh People&#39;s Festival">Edinburgh People\'s Festival</a> (1951\xe2\x80\x9353) and Aberdeen Folk Festival (1963\xe2\x80\x93).<sup id="cite_ref-Sweers2005pp256-7_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sweers2005pp256-7-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> In the 1960s there was a flourishing <a href="/wiki/Folk_club" title="Folk club">folk club</a> culture. The first folk club was founded in London by <a href="/wiki/Ewan_MacColl" title="Ewan MacColl">Ewan MacColl</a> (1915\xe2\x80\x9389), who emerged as a leading figure in the revival in Britain, recording influential records such as <i>Scottish Popular Ballads</i> (1956).<sup id="cite_ref-Broughtonetal1999p261-3_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Broughtonetal1999p261-3-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Scottish folk clubs were less dogmatic than their English counterparts, which rapidly moved to an all English folk song policy, and continued to encourage a mixture of Scottish, Irish, English and American material. Early on they hosted traditional performers, including Donald Higgins and the <a href="/wiki/Belle_Stewart" title="Belle Stewart">Stewarts of Blairgowrie</a>, beside English performers and new Scottish revivalists such as <a href="/wiki/Robin_Hall" title="Robin Hall">Robin Hall</a> (1936\xe2\x80\x9398), <a href="/wiki/Jimmie_Macgregor" title="Jimmie Macgregor">Jimmie Macgregor</a> (born 1930) and <a href="/wiki/The_Corries" title="The Corries">The Corries</a>. A number of these new performers, including the <a href="/wiki/Ian_Campbell_Folk_Group" title="Ian Campbell Folk Group">Ian Campbell Folk Group</a>, emerged from the <a href="/wiki/Skiffle" title="Skiffle">skiffle</a> movement.<sup id="cite_ref-Sweers2005pp256-7_21-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sweers2005pp256-7-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>There was also a strand of popular Scottish music that benefited from the arrival of radio and television, which relied on images of Scottishness derived from <a href="/wiki/Tartanry" title="Tartanry">tartanry</a> and stereotypes employed in <a href="/wiki/Music_hall" title="Music hall">music hall</a> and <a href="/wiki/Variety_show" title="Variety show">variety</a>. Proponents included <a href="/wiki/Andy_Stewart_(musician)" title="Andy Stewart (musician)">Andy Stewart</a> (1933\xe2\x80\x9393), whose weekly programme <i><a href="/wiki/The_White_Heather_Club" title="The White Heather Club">The White Heather Club</a></i> ran in Scotland from 1958 to 1967. Frequent guests included <a href="/wiki/Moira_Anderson" title="Moira Anderson">Moira Anderson</a> (born 1938) and <a href="/wiki/Kenneth_McKellar_(singer)" title="Kenneth McKellar (singer)">Kenneth McKeller</a> (1927\xe2\x80\x932010), who enjoyed their own programmes.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> The programmes and their music were immensely popular, although their version of Scottish music and identity was despised by many modernists.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The fusing of various styles of American music with British folk created a distinctive form of <a href="/wiki/Fingerstyle_guitar" title="Fingerstyle guitar">fingerstyle guitar</a> playing known as <a href="/wiki/Folk_baroque" title="Folk baroque">folk baroque</a>, pioneered by figures including <a href="/wiki/Davy_Graham" class="mw-redirect" title="Davy Graham">Davy Graham</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bert_Jansch" title="Bert Jansch">Bert Jansch</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Sweers2005pp31-8_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sweers2005pp31-8-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> This led in part to British <a href="/wiki/Progressive_folk" title="Progressive folk">progressive folk</a> music, which attempted to elevate folk music through greater musicianship, or compositional and arrangement skills.<sup id="cite_ref-Sweers2005pp31-8_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sweers2005pp31-8-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Many progressive folk performers continued to retain a traditional element in their music, including Jansch who became a member of the band <a href="/wiki/Pentangle" title="Pentangle">Pentangle</a> in 1967.<sup id="cite_ref-Sweers2005pp31-8_12-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sweers2005pp31-8-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Others largely abandoned the traditional element of their music. Particularly important were <a href="/wiki/Donovan" title="Donovan">Donovan</a> (who was most influenced by emerging progressive folk musicians in America such as <a href="/wiki/Bob_Dylan" title="Bob Dylan">Bob Dylan</a>) and the <a href="/wiki/Incredible_String_Band" class="mw-redirect" title="Incredible String Band">Incredible String Band</a>, who from 1967 incorporated a range of influences including <a href="/wiki/Medieval_music" title="Medieval music">medieval</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eastern_music" class="mw-redirect" title="Eastern music">Eastern music</a> into their compositions, leading to the development of <a href="/wiki/Psychedelic_folk" title="Psychedelic folk">psychedelic folk</a>, which had a considerable impact on <a href="/wiki/Progressive_rock" title="Progressive rock">progressive</a> and <a href="/wiki/Psychedelic_rock" title="Psychedelic rock">psychedelic rock</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Acoustic groups who continued to interpret traditional material through into the 1970s included <a href="/wiki/Ossian_(band)" title="Ossian (band)">Ossian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Silly_Wizard" title="Silly Wizard">Silly Wizard</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Broughtonetal1999p267_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Broughtonetal1999p267-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/The_Boys_of_the_Lough" title="The Boys of the Lough">The Boys of the Lough</a> and <a href="/wiki/Battlefield_Band" title="Battlefield Band">Battlefield Band</a>, emerged from the flourishing Glasgow folk scene.<sup id="cite_ref-Sweers2005pp256-7_21-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sweers2005pp256-7-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Also from this scene were the highly influential <a href="/wiki/The_Clutha" title="The Clutha">The Clutha</a>, whose line up, with two fiddlers, was later augmented by the piper Jimmy Anderson, and the Whistlebinkies, who pursued a strongly instrumental format, relying on traditional instruments, including a <a href="/wiki/Cl%C3%A0rsach" class="mw-redirect" title="Cl\xc3\xa0rsach">Cl\xc3\xa0rsach</a> (Celtic harp).<sup id="cite_ref-Broughtonetal1999p261-3_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Broughtonetal1999p261-3-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Many of these groups played largely music originating from the Lowlands, while later, more successful bands tended to favor the Gaelic sounds of the Highlands. While fairly popular within folk circles, none of these groups achieved the success of Irish groups such as <a href="/wiki/The_Chieftains" title="The Chieftains">The Chieftains</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Dubliners" title="The Dubliners">The Dubliners</a>. Some of these bands produced noted solo artists, including <a href="/wiki/Andy_M._Stewart" title="Andy M. Stewart">Andy M. Stewart</a> of Silly Wizard, <a href="/wiki/Brian_McNeill" title="Brian McNeill">Brian McNeill</a> of Battlefield Band, and <a href="/wiki/Dougie_MacLean" title="Dougie MacLean">Dougie MacLean</a> of the Tannahill Weavers. MacLean is perhaps the best known of these, having written "Caledonia," one of Scotland\'s most beloved songs.\n</p><p>Though perhaps not as popular as some of their Celtic fusion counterparts, traditional Scottish artists are still making music. These include Hebridean singer <a href="/wiki/Julie_Fowlis" title="Julie Fowlis">Julie Fowlis</a>, \'Gaelic supergroup\' <a href="/wiki/D%C3%A0imh" title="D\xc3\xa0imh">D\xc3\xa0imh</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lau_(band)" title="Lau (band)">Lau</a>. <a href="/wiki/Old_Blind_Dogs" title="Old Blind Dogs">Old Blind Dogs</a> have also found success singing in the <a href="/wiki/Doric_Scots" class="mw-redirect" title="Doric Scots">Doric Scots</a> dialect of their native <a href="/wiki/Aberdeenshire" title="Aberdeenshire">Aberdeenshire</a>. <a href="/wiki/Albannach_(band)" title="Albannach (band)">Albannach</a> has gained recognition for their distinctive combination of pipes and drums.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Celtic_rock">Celtic rock</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scottish_folk_music&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Celtic rock">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Celtic_rock" title="Celtic rock">Celtic rock</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Dick_Gaughan_1.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Dick_Gaughan_1.jpg/220px-Dick_Gaughan_1.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Dick_Gaughan_1.jpg/330px-Dick_Gaughan_1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Dick_Gaughan_1.jpg/440px-Dick_Gaughan_1.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2453" data-file-height="1635" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Dick_Gaughan_1.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Dick_Gaughan" title="Dick Gaughan">Dick Gaughan</a> live at the "R\xc3\xa4tsche" in Geislingen/Steige (Germany) in 2006</div></div></div>\n<p>Celtic rock developed as a variant of <a href="/wiki/British_folk_rock" title="British folk rock">British folk rock</a>, playing traditional English folk music with rock instrumentation, developed by <a href="/wiki/Fairport_Convention" title="Fairport Convention">Fairport Convention</a> and its members from 1969. <a href="/wiki/Donovan" title="Donovan">Donovan</a> used the term "Celtic rock" to describe the folk rock he created for his <i><a href="/wiki/Open_Road_(Donovan_album)" title="Open Road (Donovan album)">Open Road</a></i> album in 1970, featured a song with "Celtic rock" as its title.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> The adoption of British folk rock heavily influenced by Scottish traditional music produced groups including the <a href="/wiki/JSD_Band" title="JSD Band">JSD Band</a> The Natural Acoustic Band (1970)and Spencer\'s Feat. Out of the wreckage of the latter in 1974, guitarist <a href="/wiki/Dick_Gaughan" title="Dick Gaughan">Dick Gaughan</a> (born 1948) formed probably the most successful band in this genre <a href="/wiki/Five_Hand_Reel" title="Five Hand Reel">Five Hand Reel</a>, who combed Irish and Scottish personnel, before he embarked on an influential solo career.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>From the late 1970s the attendance at, and numbers of, folk clubs began to decrease, as new musical and social trends, including <a href="/wiki/Punk_rock" title="Punk rock">punk rock</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_wave_music" title="New wave music">new wave</a> and <a href="/wiki/Electronic_music" title="Electronic music">electronic music</a> began to dominate. However, in Scotland the circuit of <a href="/wiki/Ceilidh" class="mw-redirect" title="Ceilidh">ceilidhs</a> and festivals helped prop up traditional music.<sup id="cite_ref-Sweers2005pp31-8_12-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sweers2005pp31-8-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Two of the most successful groups of the 1980s emerged from this dance band circuit. From 1978, when they began to release original albums, <a href="/wiki/Runrig" title="Runrig">Runrig</a> produced highly polished Scottish folk rock, including the first commercially successful album with the all <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Gaelic" title="Scottish Gaelic">Gaelic</a> <i>Play Gaelic</i> in 1978.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> From the 1980s <a href="/wiki/Capercaillie_(band)" title="Capercaillie (band)">Capercaillie</a> combined Scottish folk music, electric instruments and haunting vocals to considerable success.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> While bagpipes had become an essential element in Scottish folk bands they were much rarer in folk rock outfits, but were successfully integrated into their sound by <a href="/wiki/Wolfstone" title="Wolfstone">Wolfstone</a> from 1989, who focused on a combination of highland music and rock.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> More recently, bands such as <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A0nran" title="M\xc3\xa0nran">M\xc3\xa0nran</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tide_Lines" title="Tide Lines">Tide Lines</a> have also focused on a combination of Celtic music and pop-rock. Additionally, groups such as <a href="/wiki/Shooglenifty" title="Shooglenifty">Shooglenifty</a> and <a href="/wiki/Peatbog_Faeries" title="Peatbog Faeries">Peatbog Faeries</a> mixed traditional highland music with more modern sounds, such as dubstep rhythms, creating a genre sometimes referred to as "Acid Croft". <a href="/wiki/Niteworks" title="Niteworks">Niteworks</a> inspired the two aforementioned bands and the electronic sampling of <a href="/wiki/Martyn_Bennett" title="Martyn Bennett">Martyn Bennett</a> have further developed Celtic electronic music which has been described as both Gaelictronica<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> and Celtictronica.\n</p><p>Successful Scottish stadium rock acts such as <a href="/wiki/Simple_Minds" title="Simple Minds">Simple Minds</a> from <a href="/wiki/Glasgow" title="Glasgow">Glasgow</a> and <a href="/wiki/Big_Country" title="Big Country">Big Country</a> from <a href="/wiki/Dunfermline" title="Dunfermline">Dunfermline</a> incorporated traditional Celtic sounds onto many of their songs. The former based their hit "<a href="/wiki/Belfast_Child" title="Belfast Child">Belfast Child</a>" around the traditional Irish song "<a href="/wiki/She_Moved_Through_the_Fair" title="She Moved Through the Fair">She Moved Through the Fair</a>" and incorporated accordion into their line-up, while the latter\'s guitar and drum sounds were heavily influenced by Scottish pipe bands, particularly on songs such as "<a href="/wiki/In_a_Big_Country" title="In a Big Country">In a Big Country</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Fields_of_Fire_(song)" title="Fields of Fire (song)">Fields of Fire</a>." Big Country also covered Robert Burns\' "<a href="/wiki/Killiecrankie" title="Killiecrankie">Killiecrankie</a>."\n</p><p>One by-product of the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_people#Scottish_people_abroad" title="Scottish people">Celtic Diaspora</a> was the existence of large communities across the world that looked for their cultural roots and identity to their origins in the Celtic nations. While it seems young musicians from these communities usually chose between their folk culture and mainstream forms of music such as rock or pop, after the advent of <a href="/wiki/Celtic_punk" title="Celtic punk">Celtic punk</a> relatively large numbers of bands began to emerge styling themselves as Celtic rock. This is particularly noticeable in the USA and Canada, where there are large communities descended from Irish and Scottish immigrants. From the USA this includes Scottish bands <a href="/wiki/Seven_Nations_(band)" title="Seven Nations (band)">Seven Nations</a>, <a href="/wiki/Prydein_(band)" title="Prydein (band)">Prydein</a> and <a href="/wiki/Flatfoot_56" title="Flatfoot 56">Flatfoot 56</a>. From Canada are bands such as <a href="/wiki/Enter_the_Haggis" title="Enter the Haggis">Enter the Haggis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Great_Big_Sea" title="Great Big Sea">Great Big Sea</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Real_Mckenzies" class="mw-redirect" title="The Real Mckenzies">The Real Mckenzies</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spirit_of_the_West" title="Spirit of the West">Spirit of the West</a>. These groups were naturally influenced by American forms of music, some containing members with no Celtic ancestry and commonly singing in English.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scottish_folk_music&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Notes">Notes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scottish_folk_music&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Notes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="reflist columns references-column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 30em; -webkit-column-width: 30em; column-width: 30em; list-style-type: decimal;">\n<ol class="references">\n<li id="cite_note-Baxter2001app130-33-1"><span class="mw-cite-backlink">^ <a href="#cite_ref-Baxter2001app130-33_1-0"><sup><i><b>a</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-Baxter2001app130-33_1-1"><sup><i><b>b</b></i></sup></a></span> <span class="reference-text">J.
