/></a></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><b>Benjamin Jonson</b> (c. 11 June 1572 \xe2\x80\x93 c. 16 August 1637<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>) was an English playwright, poet, actor, and literary critic, whose artistry exerted a lasting impact upon English poetry and stage comedy. He popularised the <a href="/wiki/Comedy_of_humours" title="Comedy of humours">comedy of humours</a>. He is best known for the <a href="/wiki/Satire" title="Satire">satirical</a> plays <i><a href="/wiki/Every_Man_in_His_Humour" title="Every Man in His Humour">Every Man in His Humour</a></i> (1598), <i><a href="/wiki/Volpone" title="Volpone">Volpone, or The Fox</a></i> (c. 1606), <i><a href="/wiki/The_Alchemist_(play)" title="The Alchemist (play)">The Alchemist</a></i> (1610) and <i><a href="/wiki/Bartholomew_Fair_(play)" title="Bartholomew Fair (play)">Bartholomew Fair</a></i> (1614) and for his <a href="/wiki/Lyric_poetry" title="Lyric poetry">lyric</a> and <a href="/wiki/Epigram" title="Epigram">epigrammatic</a> poetry; he is generally regarded as the second most important English playwright during the reign of <a href="/wiki/James_VI_and_I" title="James VI and I">James VI and I</a> after <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">William Shakespeare</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Jonson was a <a href="/wiki/Classics" title="Classics">classically educated</a>, well-read and cultured man of the <a href="/wiki/English_Renaissance" title="English Renaissance">English Renaissance</a> with an appetite for controversy (personal and political, artistic and intellectual) whose cultural influence was of unparalleled breadth upon the playwrights and the poets of the <a href="/wiki/Jacobean_era" title="Jacobean era">Jacobean era</a> (1603\xe2\x80\x931625) and of the <a href="/wiki/Caroline_era" title="Caroline era">Caroline era</a> (1625\xe2\x80\x931642).<sup Jonson\'s clergyman father died two months before his birth; his mother married a master <a href="/wiki/Bricklayer" title="Bricklayer">bricklayer</a> two years later.<sup id="cite_ref-Robert_Chambers,_Book_of_Days_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Robert_Chambers,_Book_of_Days-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Jonson attended school in <a href="/wiki/St_Martin%27s_Lane" title="St Martin&#39;s Lane">St Martin\'s Lane</a>. Later, a family friend paid for his studies at <a href="/wiki/Westminster_School" title="Westminster School">Westminster School</a>, where the <a href="/wiki/Antiquarian" title="Antiquarian">antiquarian</a>, historian, <a href="/wiki/Topographer" class="mw-redirect" title="Topographer">topographer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Officer_of_arms" title="Officer of arms">officer of arms</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_Camden" title="William Camden">William Camden</a> (1551\xe2\x80\x931623) was one of his masters. In the event, the pupil and the master became friends, and the intellectual influence of Camden\'s broad-ranging scholarship upon Jonson\'s art and <a href="/wiki/Style_(fiction)" class="mw-redirect" title="Style (fiction)">literary style</a> remained notable, until Camden\'s death in 1623.\n</p><p>On leaving Westminster School, Jonson was to have attended the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Cambridge" title="University of Cambridge">University of Cambridge</a>, to continue his book learning but did not, because of his unwilled apprenticeship to his bricklayer stepfather.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated388_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated388-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Robert_Chambers,_Book_of_Days_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Robert_Chambers,_Book_of_Days-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> According to the churchman and historian <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Fuller" title="Thomas Fuller">Thomas Fuller</a> (1608\xe2\x80\x9361), Jonson at this time built a garden wall in <a href="/wiki/Lincoln%27s_Inn" title="Lincoln&#39;s Inn">Lincoln\'s Inn</a>. After having been an apprentice bricklayer, Ben Jonson went to the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> and volunteered to soldier with the English regiments of <a href="/wiki/Francis_Vere" title="Francis Vere">Francis Vere</a> (1560\xe2\x80\x931609) in <a href="/wiki/Flanders" title="Flanders">Flanders</a>.\n</p><p>The <i>Hawthornden Manuscripts</i> (1619), of the conversations between Ben Jonson and the poet <a href="/wiki/William_Drummond_of_Hawthornden" title="William Drummond of Hawthornden">William Drummond of Hawthornden</a> (1585\xe2\x80\x931649), report that, when in Flanders, Jonson engaged, fought and killed an enemy soldier in <a href="/wiki/Single_combat" title="Single combat">single combat</a>, and took for trophies the weapons of the vanquished soldier.<sup id="cite_ref-Hawthornden_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hawthornden-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> After his military activity on the Continent, Jonson returned to England and worked as an actor and as a playwright. As an actor, Jonson was the protagonist \xe2\x80\x9cHieronimo\xe2\x80\x9d (Geronimo) in the play <i><a href="/wiki/The_Spanish_Tragedy" title="The Spanish Tragedy">The Spanish Tragedy</a></i> (ca. 1586), by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Kyd" title="Thomas Kyd">Thomas Kyd</a> (1558\xe2\x80\x9394), the first <a href="/wiki/Revenge_tragedy" title="Revenge tragedy">revenge tragedy</a> in English literature. Moreover, by 1597, he was a working playwright employed by <a href="/wiki/Philip_Henslowe" title="Philip Henslowe">Philip Henslowe</a>, the leading producer for the English public theatre; by the next year, the production of <i><a href="/wiki/Every_Man_in_His_Humour" title="Every Man in His Humour">Every Man in His Humour</a></i> (1598) had established Jonson\'s reputation as a dramatist.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Regarding his marriage Jonson described his wife to William Drummond as "a shrew, yet honest". The identity of Jonson\'s wife has always been obscure, yet she sometimes is identified as "Ann Lewis", the woman who married a Benjamin Jonson in 1594, at the church of <a href="/wiki/St_Magnus-the-Martyr" title="St Magnus-the-Martyr">St Magnus-the-Martyr</a>, near <a href="/wiki/London_Bridge" title="London Bridge">London Bridge</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated612_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated612-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Concerning the family of Anne Lewis and Ben Jonson, the St. Martin\'s Church registers indicate that Mary Jonson, their eldest daughter, died in November 1593, at six months of age. Then a decade later, in 1603, Benjamin Jonson, their eldest son, died of <a href="/wiki/Bubonic_plague" title="Bubonic plague">Bubonic plague</a> when he was seven years old; to lament and honour the dead boy, Benjamin Jonson <i>p\xc3\xa8re</i> wrote the elegiac <i><a href="/wiki/On_My_First_Sonne" title="On My First Sonne">On My First Sonne</a></i> (1603). Moreover, 32 years later, a second son, also named Benjamin Jonson, died in 1635. In that period, Ann Lewis and Ben Jonson lived separate lives for five years; their matrimonial arrangement cast Ann Lewis as the housewife Jonson, and Ben Jonson as the artist who enjoyed the residential hospitality of his patrons, Sir Robert Townshend and Lord Aubigny, <a href="/wiki/Esme_Stuart,_3rd_Duke_of_Lennox" class="mw-redirect" title="Esme Stuart, 3rd Duke of Lennox">Esme Stuart, 3rd Duke of Lennox</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated612_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated612-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ben_Jonson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>By summer 1597, Jonson had a fixed engagement in the <a href="/wiki/Admiral%27s_Men" title="Admiral&#39;s Men">Admiral\'s Men</a>, then performing under <a href="/wiki/Philip_Henslowe" title="Philip Henslowe">Philip Henslowe</a>\'s management at <a href="/wiki/The_Rose_(theatre)" title="The Rose (theatre)">The Rose</a>. <a href="/wiki/John_Aubrey" title="John Aubrey">John Aubrey</a> reports, on uncertain authority, that Jonson was not successful as an actor; whatever his skills as an actor, he was evidently more valuable to the company as a writer.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>By this time Jonson had begun to write original plays for the Admiral\'s Men; in 1598 he was mentioned by <a href="/wiki/Francis_Meres" title="Francis Meres">Francis Meres</a> in his <i>Palladis Tamia</i> as one of "the best for tragedy." None of his early tragedies survive, however. An undated comedy, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Case_is_Altered" title="The Case is Altered">The Case is Altered</a></i>, may be his earliest surviving play.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1597 a play which he co-wrote with <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Nashe" title="Thomas Nashe">Thomas Nashe</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Isle_of_Dogs_(play)" title="The Isle of Dogs (play)">The Isle of Dogs</a></i>, was suppressed after causing great offence. Arrest warrants for Jonson and Nashe were issued by Queen <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_I" class="mw-redirect" title="Elizabeth I">Elizabeth I</a>\'s so-called interrogator, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Topcliffe" title="Richard Topcliffe">Richard Topcliffe</a>. Jonson was jailed in <a href="/wiki/Marshalsea_Prison" class="mw-redirect" title="Marshalsea Prison">Marshalsea Prison</a> and charged with "Leude and mutynous behaviour", while Nashe managed to escape to <a href="/wiki/Great_Yarmouth" title="Great Yarmouth">Great Yarmouth</a>. Two of the actors, <a href="/wiki/Gabriel_Spenser" title="Gabriel Spenser">Gabriel Spenser</a> and Robert Shaw, were also imprisoned. A year later, Jonson was again briefly imprisoned, this time in <a href="/wiki/Newgate_Prison" title="Newgate Prison">Newgate Prison</a>, for killing Gabriel Spenser in a <a href="/wiki/Duel" title="Duel">duel</a> on 22 September 1598 in Hogsden Fields<sup id="cite_ref-Hawthornden_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hawthornden-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> (today part of <a href="/wiki/Hoxton" title="Hoxton">Hoxton</a>). Tried on a charge of <a href="/wiki/Manslaughter" title="Manslaughter">manslaughter</a>, Jonson pleaded guilty but was released by <a href="/wiki/Benefit_of_clergy" title="Benefit of clergy">benefit of clergy</a>, a legal ploy through which he gained leniency by reciting a brief bible verse (the <a href="/wiki/Neck-verse" class="mw-redirect" title="Neck-verse">neck-verse</a>), forfeiting his \'goods and chattels\' and being branded on his left thumb.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> While in jail Jonson converted to Catholicism, possibly through the influence of fellow-prisoner Father <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Wright_(writer)" title="Thomas Wright (writer)">Thomas Wright</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Jesuit" class="mw-redirect" title="Jesuit">Jesuit</a> priest.<sup id="cite_ref-ID2008_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ID2008-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1598 Jonson produced his first great success, <i><a href="/wiki/Every_Man_in_His_Humour" title="Every Man in His Humour">Every Man in His Humour</a></i>, capitalising on the vogue for humorous plays which <a href="/wiki/George_Chapman" title="George Chapman">George Chapman</a> had begun with <i><a href="/wiki/An_Humorous_Day%27s_Mirth" title="An Humorous Day&#39;s Mirth">An Humorous Day\'s Mirth</a></i>. <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">William Shakespeare</a> was among the first actors to be cast. Jonson followed this in 1599 with <i><a href="/wiki/Every_Man_out_of_His_Humour" title="Every Man out of His Humour">Every Man out of His Humour</a></i>, a pedantic attempt to imitate <a href="/wiki/Aristophanes" title="Aristophanes">Aristophanes</a>. It is not known whether this was a success on stage, but when published it proved popular and went through several editions.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>Jonson\'s other work for the theatre in the last years of <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_I" class="mw-redirect" title="Elizabeth I">Elizabeth I</a>\'s reign was marked by fighting and controversy. <i><a href="/wiki/Cynthia%27s_Revels" title="Cynthia&#39;s Revels">Cynthia\'s Revels</a></i> was produced by the <a href="/wiki/Children_of_the_Chapel" title="Children of the Chapel">Children of the Chapel</a> Royal at <a href="/wiki/Blackfriars_Theatre" title="Blackfriars Theatre">Blackfriars Theatre</a> in 1600. It satirised both <a href="/wiki/John_Marston_(poet)" title="John Marston (poet)">John Marston</a>, who Jonson believed had accused him of lustfulness in <i><a href="/wiki/Histriomastix_(play)" title="Histriomastix (play)">Histriomastix</a></i>, and <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Dekker_(poet)" class="mw-redirect" title="Thomas Dekker (poet)">Thomas Dekker</a>. Jonson attacked the two poets again in <i><a href="/wiki/Poetaster_(play)" title="Poetaster (play)">Poetaster</a></i> (1601). Dekker responded with <i><a href="/wiki/Satiromastix" title="Satiromastix">Satiromastix</a></i>, subtitled "the untrussing of the humorous poet". The final scene of this play, whilst certainly not to be taken at face value as a portrait of Jonson, offers a caricature that is recognisable from Drummond\'s report \xe2\x80\x93 boasting about himself and condemning other poets, criticising performances of his plays and calling attention to himself in any available way.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>This "<a href="/wiki/War_of_the_Theatres" title="War of the Theatres">War of the Theatres</a>" appears to have ended with reconciliation on all sides. Jonson collaborated with Dekker on a <a href="/wiki/Procession" title="Procession">pageant</a> welcoming <a href="/wiki/James_I_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="James I of England">James I</a> to England in 1603 although Drummond reports that Jonson called Dekker a rogue. Marston dedicated <i>The Malcontent</i> to Jonson and the two collaborated with Chapman on <i><a href="/wiki/Eastward_Ho" class="mw-redirect" title="Eastward Ho">Eastward Ho</a></i>, a 1605 play whose anti-Scottish sentiment briefly landed both Jonson and Chapman in jail.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Royal_patronage">Royal patronage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ben_Jonson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Royal patronage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>At the beginning of the English reign of <a href="/wiki/James_VI_and_I" title="James VI and I">James VI and I</a> in 1603 Jonson joined other poets and playwrights in welcoming the new king. Jonson quickly adapted himself to the additional demand for <a href="/wiki/Masque" title="Masque">masques</a> and entertainments introduced with the new reign and fostered by both the king and his consort <a href="/wiki/Anne_of_Denmark" title="Anne of Denmark">Anne of Denmark</a>. In addition to his popularity on the public stage and in the royal hall, he enjoyed the patronage of aristocrats such as <a href="/wiki/Roger_Manners,_5th_Earl_of_Rutland" title="Roger Manners, 5th Earl of Rutland">Elizabeth Sidney</a> (daughter of <a href="/wiki/Philip_Sidney" title="Philip Sidney">Sir Philip Sidney</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Lady_Mary_Wroth" title="Lady Mary Wroth">Lady Mary Wroth</a>. This connection with the Sidney family provided the impetus for one of Jonson\'s most famous lyrics, the <a href="/wiki/Country_house_poems" class="mw-redirect" title="Country house poems">country house poem</a> <i>To <a href="/wiki/Penshurst_Place" title="Penshurst Place">Penshurst</a></i>.\n</p><p>In February 1603 <a href="/wiki/John_Manningham" title="John Manningham">John Manningham</a> reported that Jonson was living on Robert Townsend, son of <a href="/wiki/Roger_Townshend_(died_1590)" title="Roger Townshend (died 1590)">Sir Roger Townshend</a>, and "scorns the world."<sup id="cite_ref-:0_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Perhaps this explains why his trouble with English authorities continued. That same year he was questioned by the <a href="/wiki/Privy_Council_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Privy Council of the United Kingdom">Privy Council</a> about <i><a href="/wiki/Sejanus_His_Fall" title="Sejanus His Fall">Sejanus</a></i>, a politically themed play about corruption in the Roman Empire. He was again in trouble for topical allusions in a play, now lost, in which he took part. Shortly after his release from a brief spell of imprisonment imposed to mark the authorities\' displeasure at the work, in the second week of October 1605, he was present at a supper party attended by most of the <a href="/wiki/Gunpowder_Plot" title="Gunpowder Plot">Gunpowder Plot</a> conspirators. After the plot\'s discovery he appears to have avoided further imprisonment; he volunteered what he knew of the affair to the investigator <a href="/wiki/Robert_Cecil,_1st_Earl_of_Salisbury" title="Robert Cecil, 1st Earl of Salisbury">Robert Cecil</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Privy_Council" class="mw-redirect" title="Privy Council">Privy Council</a>. Father Thomas Wright, who heard Fawkes\'s confession, was known to Jonson from prison in 1598 and Cecil may have directed him to bring the priest before the council, as a witness.<sup id="cite_ref-ID2008_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ID2008-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> (Teague, 249).\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Jonson_1616_folio_Workes_title_page.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Jonson_1616_folio_Workes_title_page.jpg/200px-Jonson_1616_folio_Workes_title_page.jpg" width="200" height="301" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Jonson_1616_folio_Workes_title_page.jpg/300px-Jonson_1616_folio_Workes_title_page.jpg 1.5x, href="/wiki/Folio_(printing)" class="mw-redirect" title="Folio (printing)">folio</a> publication that included stage plays</div></div></div>\n<p>At the same time, Jonson pursued a more prestigious career, writing masques for James\'s court. <i><a href="/wiki/The_Entertainment_at_Althorp" title="The Entertainment at Althorp">The Satyr</a></i> (1603) and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Masque_of_Blackness" title="The Masque of Blackness">The Masque of Blackness</a></i> (1605) are two of about two dozen masques which Jonson wrote for James or for Queen Anne, some of them performed at <a href="/wiki/Apethorpe_Palace" title="Apethorpe Palace">Apethorpe Palace</a> when the King was in residence. <i>The Masque of Blackness</i> was praised by <a href="/wiki/Algernon_Charles_Swinburne" title="Algernon Charles Swinburne">Algernon Charles Swinburne</a> as the consummate example of this now-extinct genre, which mingled speech, dancing and spectacle.\n</p><p>On many of these projects he collaborated, not always peacefully, with designer <a href="/wiki/Inigo_Jones" title="Inigo Jones">Inigo Jones</a>. For example, Jones designed the scenery for Jonson\'s masque <i><a href="/wiki/Oberon,_the_Faery_Prince" title="Oberon, the Faery Prince">Oberon, the Faery Prince</a></i> performed at <a href="/wiki/Whitehall" title="Whitehall">Whitehall</a> on 1 January 1611 in which <a href="/wiki/Henry_Frederick,_Prince_of_Wales" title="Henry Frederick, Prince of Wales">Prince Henry</a>, eldest son of James I, appeared in the title role. Perhaps partly as a result of this new career, Jonson gave up writing plays for the public theatres for a decade. He later told Drummond that he had made less than two hundred pounds on all his plays together.\n</p><p>In 1616 Jonson received a yearly pension of 100 <a href="/wiki/Mark_(money)" class="mw-redirect" title="Mark (money)">marks</a> (about \xc2\xa360), leading some to identify him as England\'s first <a href="/wiki/Poet_Laureate" class="mw-redirect" title="Poet Laureate">Poet Laureate</a>. This sign of royal favour may have encouraged him to publish the first volume of the folio collected edition of his works that year. Other volumes followed in 1640\xe2\x80\x9341 and 1692. (See: <a href="/wiki/Ben_Jonson_folios" title="Ben Jonson folios">Ben Jonson folios</a>)\n</p><p>On 8 July 1618 Jonson set out from Bishopsgate in London to walk to Edinburgh, arriving in Scotland\'s capital on 17 September. For the most part he followed the great north road, and was treated to lavish and enthusiastic welcomes in both towns and country houses.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> On his arrival he lodged initially with John Stuart, a cousin of King James, in Leith, and was made an honorary burgess of Edinburgh at a dinner laid on by the city on 26 September.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> He stayed in Scotland until late January 1619, and the best-remembered hospitality he enjoyed was that of the Scottish poet, <a href="/wiki/William_Drummond_of_Hawthornden" title="William Drummond of Hawthornden">William Drummond of Hawthornden</a>, sited on the <a href="/wiki/River_Esk,_Lothian" title="River Esk, Lothian">River Esk</a>. Drummond undertook to record as much of Jonson\'s conversation as he could in his diary, and thus recorded aspects of Jonson\'s personality that would otherwise have been less clearly seen. Jonson delivers his opinions, in Drummond\'s terse reporting, in an expansive and even magisterial mood. Drummond noted he was "a great lover and praiser of himself, a contemner and scorner of others".\n</p><p>On returning to England, he was awarded an <a href="/wiki/Honorary_degree" title="Honorary degree">honorary</a> <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Arts_(Oxbridge_and_Dublin)" title="Master of Arts (Oxbridge and Dublin)">Master of Arts</a> <a href="/wiki/Academic_degree" title="Academic degree">degree</a> from Oxford University.\n</p><p>The period between 1605 and 1620 may be viewed as Jonson\'s heyday. By 1616 he had produced all the plays on which his present reputation as a dramatist is based, including the tragedy <i><a href="/wiki/Catiline_His_Conspiracy" title="Catiline His Conspiracy">Catiline</a></i> (acted and printed 1611), which achieved limited success and the comedies <i><a href="/wiki/Volpone" title="Volpone">Volpone</a></i> (acted 1605 and printed in 1607), <i><a href="/wiki/Epicoene,_or_the_Silent_Woman" class="mw-redirect" title="Epicoene, or the Silent Woman">Epicoene, or the Silent Woman</a></i> (1609), <i><a href="/wiki/The_Alchemist_(play)" title="The Alchemist (play)">The Alchemist</a></i> (1610), <i><a href="/wiki/Bartholomew_Fayre:_A_Comedy" class="mw-redirect" title="Bartholomew Fayre: A Comedy">Bartholomew Fair</a></i> (1614) and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Devil_is_an_Ass" class="mw-redirect" title="The Devil is an Ass">The Devil is an Ass</a></i> (1616). <i>The Alchemist</i> and <i>Volpone</i> were immediately successful. Of <i>Epicoene</i>, Jonson told Drummond of a satirical verse which reported that the play\'s subtitle was appropriate, since its audience had refused to applaud the play (i.e., remained silent). Yet <i>Epicoene</i>, along with <i>Bartholomew Fair</i> and (to a lesser extent) <i>The Devil is an Ass</i> have in modern times achieved a certain degree of recognition. While his life during this period was apparently more settled than it had been in the 1590s, his financial security was still not assured.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Religion">Religion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ben_Jonson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Religion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Jonson recounted that his father had been a prosperous <a href="/wiki/Protestant" class="mw-redirect" title="Protestant">Protestant</a> landowner until the reign of "<a href="/wiki/Mary_I_of_England" title="Mary I of England">Bloody Mary</a>" and had suffered imprisonment and the forfeiture of his wealth during that monarch\'s attempt to restore England to Catholicism. On <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_I_of_England" title="Elizabeth I of England">Elizabeth</a>\'s accession he was freed and was able to travel to London to become a clergyman.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> (All that\'s known of Jonson\'s father, who died a month before his son was born, comes from the poet\'s own narrative.) Jonson\'s elementary education was in a small church school attached to <a href="/wiki/St_Martin-in-the-Fields#Medieval_and_Tudor" title="St Martin-in-the-Fields">St Martin-in-the-Fields</a> parish, and at the age of about seven he secured a place at <a href="/wiki/Westminster_School" title="Westminster School">Westminster School</a>, then part of <a href="/wiki/Westminster_Abbey" title="Westminster Abbey">Westminster Abbey</a>.\n</p><p>Notwithstanding this emphatically Protestant grounding, Jonson maintained an interest in Catholic doctrine throughout his adult life and, at a particularly perilous time while a religious war with Spain was widely expected and persecution of Catholics was intensifying, he converted to the faith.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-R51_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-R51-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> This took place in October 1598, while Jonson was on remand in <a href="/wiki/Newgate_Prison" title="Newgate Prison">Newgate Gaol</a> charged with manslaughter. Jonson\'s biographer Ian Donaldson is among those who suggest that the conversion was instigated by Father Thomas Wright, a <a href="/wiki/Jesuit" class="mw-redirect" title="Jesuit">Jesuit</a> priest who had resigned from the order over his acceptance of Queen Elizabeth\'s right to rule in England.<sup id="cite_ref-D134_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-D134-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> Wright, although placed under <a href="/wiki/House_arrest" title="House arrest">house arrest</a> on the orders of <a href="/wiki/William_Cecil,_1st_Baron_Burghley" title="William Cecil, 1st Baron Burghley">Lord Burghley</a>, was permitted to minister to the inmates of London prisons.<sup id="cite_ref-D134_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-D134-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> It may have been that Jonson, fearing that his trial would go against him, was seeking the unequivocal <a href="/wiki/Absolution#Roman_Catholic_Church" title="Absolution">\nabsolution</a> that Catholicism could offer if he were sentenced to death.<sup id="cite_ref-R51_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-R51-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Alternatively, he could have been looking to personal advantage from accepting conversion since Father Wright\'s protector, the <a href="/wiki/Robert_Devereux,_2nd_Earl_of_Essex" title="Robert Devereux, 2nd Earl of Essex">Earl of Essex</a>, was among those who might hope to rise to influence after the succession of a new monarch.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Jonson\'s conversion came at a weighty time in affairs of state; the royal succession, from the childless Elizabeth, had not been settled and Essex\'s Catholic allies were hopeful that a sympathetic ruler might attain the throne.\n</p><p>Conviction, and certainly not expedience alone, sustained Jonson\'s faith during the troublesome twelve years he remained a Catholic. His stance received attention beyond the low-level intolerance to which most followers of that faith were exposed. The first draft of his play <i><a href="/wiki/Sejanus_His_Fall" title="Sejanus His Fall">Sejanus</a></i> was banned for "<a href="/wiki/Papist" title="Papist">popery</a>", and did not re-appear until some offending passages were cut.<sup id="cite_ref-ID2008_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ID2008-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> In January 1606 he (with Anne, his wife) appeared before the <a href="/wiki/Consistory_Court" class="mw-redirect" title="Consistory Court">Consistory Court</a> in London to answer a charge of <a href="/wiki/Recusancy" title="Recusancy">recusancy</a>, with Jonson alone additionally accused of allowing his fame as a Catholic to "seduce" citizens to the cause.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> This was a serious matter (the <a href="/wiki/Gunpowder_Plot" title="Gunpowder Plot">Gunpowder Plot</a> was still fresh in mind) but he explained that his failure to take <a href="/wiki/Eucharist" title="Eucharist">communion</a> was only because he had not found sound theological endorsement for the practice, and by paying a fine of thirteen shillings (65p) he escaped the more serious penalties at the authorities\' disposal. His habit was to slip outside during the sacrament, a common routine at the time\xe2\x80\x94indeed it was one followed by the royal consort, <a href="/wiki/Anne_of_Denmark" title="Anne of Denmark">Queen Anne</a>, herself\xe2\x80\x94to show political loyalty while not offending the conscience.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> Leading church figures, including <a href="/wiki/John_Overall_(bishop)" title="John Overall (bishop)">John Overall</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dean_of_St_Paul%27s" title="Dean of St Paul&#39;s">Dean of St Paul\'s</a>, were tasked with winning Jonson back to Protestantism, but these overtures were resisted.<sup id="cite_ref-D228_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-D228-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In May 1610 <a href="/wiki/Henry_IV_of_France" title="Henry IV of France">Henry IV of France</a> was assassinated, purportedly in the name of the Pope; he had been a Catholic monarch respected in England for tolerance towards Protestants, and his murder seems to have been the immediate cause of Jonson\'s decision to rejoin the Church of England.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-D272_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-D272-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> He did this in flamboyant style, pointedly drinking a full chalice of <a href="/wiki/Communion_wine" class="mw-redirect" title="Communion wine">communion wine</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Eucharist" title="Eucharist">eucharist</a> to demonstrate his renunciation of the Catholic rite, in which the priest alone drinks the wine.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-R177_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-R177-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> The exact date of the ceremony is unknown.<sup id="cite_ref-D272_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-D272-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> However, his interest in Catholic belief and practice remained with him until his death.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Decline_and_death">Decline and death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ben_Jonson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Decline and death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Jonson\'s productivity began to decline in the 1620s, but he remained well known.  In that time, rose to the prominence the <a href="/wiki/Sons_of_Ben_(literary_group)" title="Sons of Ben (literary group)">Sons of Ben</a> or the "<a href="/wiki/Tribe_of_Ben" class="mw-redirect" title="Tribe of Ben">Tribe of Ben</a>" - those younger poets such as <a href="/wiki/Robert_Herrick_(poet)" title="Robert Herrick (poet)">Robert Herrick</a>, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Lovelace" title="Richard Lovelace">Richard Lovelace</a>, and <a href="/wiki/John_Suckling_(poet)" title="John Suckling (poet)">Sir John Suckling</a> who took their bearing in verse from Jonson. However, a series of setbacks drained his strength and damaged his reputation. He resumed writing regular plays in the 1620s, but these are not considered among his best. They are of significant interest, however, for their portrayal of <a href="/wiki/Charles_I_of_England" title="Charles I of England">Charles I</a>\'s England. <i><a href="/wiki/The_Staple_of_News" title="The Staple of News">The Staple of News</a></i>, for example, offers a remarkable look at the earliest stage of English journalism. The lukewarm reception given that play was, however, nothing compared to the dismal failure of <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_Inn" title="The New Inn">The New Inn</a></i>; the cold reception given this play prompted Jonson to write a poem condemning his audience (the <i>Ode to Myself</i>), which in turn prompted <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Carew" title="Thomas Carew">Thomas Carew</a>, one of the "Tribe of Ben," to respond in a poem that asks Jonson to recognise his own decline.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The principal factor in Jonson\'s partial eclipse was, however, the death of James and the accession of <a href="/wiki/Charles_I_of_England" title="Charles I of England">King Charles I</a> in 1625. Jonson felt neglected by the new court. A decisive quarrel with Jones harmed his career as a writer of court masques, although he continued to entertain the court on an irregular basis. For his part, Charles displayed a certain degree of care for the great poet of his father\'s day: he increased Jonson\'s annual pension to \xc2\xa3100 and included a <a href="/wiki/English_units_of_wine_casks#Tierce" class="mw-redirect" title="English units of wine casks">tierce</a> of wine and beer.\n</p><p>Despite the strokes that he suffered in the 1620s, Jonson continued to write. At his death in 1637 he seems to have been working on another play, <i>The Sad Shepherd</i>. Though only two acts are extant, this represents a remarkable new direction for Jonson: a move into <a href="/wiki/Pastoral" title="Pastoral">pastoral</a> drama. During the early 1630s he also conducted a correspondence with <a href="/wiki/James_Howell" title="James Howell">James Howell</a>, who warned him about disfavour at court in the wake of his dispute with Jones.\n</p><p>Jonson died on or around 16 August 1637, and his funeral was held the next day. It was attended by \'all or the greatest part of the nobility then in town\'.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> He is buried in the north aisle of the nave in <a href="/wiki/Westminster_Abbey" title="Westminster Abbey">Westminster Abbey</a>, with the inscription "O Rare Ben Johnson <i>[sic]</i>" set in the slab over his grave.<sup id="cite_ref-DCWA_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DCWA-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/John_Aubrey" title="John Aubrey">John Aubrey</a>, in a more meticulous record than usual, notes that a passer-by, John Young of <a href="/wiki/Great_Milton" title="Great Milton">Great Milton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oxfordshire" title="Oxfordshire">Oxfordshire</a>, saw the bare grave marker and on impulse paid a workman eighteen pence to make the inscription. Another theory suggests that the tribute came from <a href="/wiki/William_Davenant" title="William Davenant">William Davenant</a>, Jonson\'s successor as Poet Laureate (and card-playing companion of Young), as the same phrase appears on Davenant\'s nearby gravestone, but essayist <a href="/wiki/James_Henry_Leigh_Hunt" class="mw-redirect" title="James Henry Leigh Hunt">Leigh Hunt</a> contends that Davenant\'s wording represented no more than Young\'s coinage, cheaply re-used.<sup id="cite_ref-DCWA_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DCWA-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> The fact that Jonson was buried in an upright position was an indication of his reduced circumstances at the time of his death,<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> although it has also been written that he asked for a grave exactly 18&#160;inches square from the monarch and received an upright grave to fit in the requested space.<sup id="cite_ref-world_and_its_people_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-world_and_its_people-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>It has been claimed that the inscription could be read "Orare Ben Jonson" (pray for Ben Jonson), possibly in an allusion to Jonson\'s acceptance of <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_theology" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholic theology">Catholic doctrine</a> during his lifetime (although he had returned to the Church of England) but the carving shows a distinct space between  "O" and "rare".<sup id="cite_ref-ID2008_15-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ID2008-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>A monument to Jonson was erected in about 1723 by the Earl of Oxford and is in the eastern aisle of Westminster Abbey\'s <a href="/wiki/Poets%27_Corner" title="Poets&#39; Corner">Poets\' Corner</a>. It includes a portrait medallion and the same inscription as on the gravestone. It seems Jonson was to have had a monument erected by subscription soon after his death but the <a href="/wiki/English_Civil_War" title="English Civil War">English Civil War</a> intervened.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="His_work">His work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ben_Jonson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: His work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Refimprove" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" width="50" height="39" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="399" /></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>needs additional citations for <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">verification</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ben_Jonson&amp;action=edit">improve this article</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. Unsourced material may be challenged and removed.</span>  <small><i>(August 2017)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Drama">Drama</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ben_Jonson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Drama">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Apart from two tragedies, <i><a href="/wiki/Sejanus_(play)" class="mw-redirect" title="Sejanus (play)">Sejanus</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Catiline:_His_Conspiracy" class="mw-redirect" title="Catiline: His Conspiracy">Catiline</a></i>, that largely failed to impress Renaissance audiences, Jonson\'s work for the public theatres was in comedy. These plays vary in some respects. The minor early plays, particularly those written for <a href="/wiki/Boy_player" title="Boy player">boy players</a>, present somewhat looser plots and less-developed characters than those written later, for adult companies. Already in the plays which were his salvos in the Poet\'s War, he displays the keen eye for absurdity and hypocrisy that marks his best-known plays; in these early efforts, however, plot mostly takes second place to variety of incident and comic set-pieces. They are, also, notably ill-tempered. <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Davies_(bookseller)" title="Thomas Davies (bookseller)">Thomas Davies</a> called <i>Poetaster</i> "a contemptible mixture of the serio-comic, where the names of <a href="/wiki/Augustus_Caesar" class="mw-redirect" title="Augustus Caesar">Augustus Caesar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maecenas" class="mw-redirect" title="Maecenas">Maecenas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Virgil" title="Virgil">Virgil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Horace" title="Horace">Horace</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ovid" title="Ovid">Ovid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tibullus" title="Tibullus">Tibullus</a>, are all sacrificed upon the altar of private resentment." Another early comedy in a different vein, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Case_is_Altered" title="The Case is Altered">The Case is Altered</a></i>, is markedly similar to Shakespeare\'s romantic comedies in its foreign setting, emphasis on genial wit and love-plot. Henslowe\'s diary indicates that Jonson had a hand in numerous other plays, including many in genres such as English history with which he is not otherwise associated.\n</p><p>The comedies of his middle career, from <i><a href="/wiki/Eastward_Ho" class="mw-redirect" title="Eastward Ho">Eastward Ho</a></i> to <i><a href="/wiki/The_Devil_is_an_Ass" class="mw-redirect" title="The Devil is an Ass">The Devil is an Ass</a></i> are for the most part <a href="/wiki/City_comedy" title="City comedy">city comedy</a>, with a London setting, themes of trickery and money, and a distinct moral ambiguity, despite Jonson\'s professed aim in the Prologue to <i><a href="/wiki/Volpone" title="Volpone">Volpone</a></i> to "mix profit with your pleasure". His late plays or "<a href="/w/index.php?title=Dotages&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Dotages (page does not exist)">dotages</a>", particularly <i><a href="/wiki/The_Magnetic_Lady" title="The Magnetic Lady">The Magnetic Lady</a></i> and <i>The Sad Shepherd</i>, exhibit signs of an accommodation with the romantic tendencies of <a href="/wiki/English_Renaissance_theatre" title="English Renaissance theatre">Elizabethan comedy</a>.\n</p><p>Within this general progression, however, Jonson\'s comic style remained constant and easily recognisable. He announces his programme in the prologue to the <a href="/wiki/Folio_(printing)" class="mw-redirect" title="Folio (printing)">folio</a> version of <i><a href="/wiki/Every_Man_in_His_Humour" title="Every Man in His Humour">Every Man in His Humour</a></i>: he promises to represent "deeds, and language, such as men do use." He planned to write comedies that revived the classical premises of Elizabethan dramatic theory\xe2\x80\x94or rather, since all but the loosest English comedies could claim some descent from <a href="/wiki/Plautus" title="Plautus">Plautus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Terence" title="Terence">Terence</a>, he intended to apply those premises with rigour.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> This commitment entailed negations: after <i>The Case is Altered</i>, Jonson eschewed distant locations, noble characters, romantic plots and other staples of Elizabethan comedy, focusing instead on the satiric and realistic inheritance of <a href="/wiki/New_comedy" class="mw-redirect" title="New comedy">new comedy</a>. He set his plays in contemporary settings, peopled them with recognisable types, and set them to actions that, if not strictly realistic, involved everyday motives such as greed and <a href="/wiki/Jealousy" title="Jealousy">jealousy</a>. In accordance with the temper of his age, he was often so broad in his characterisation that many of his most famous scenes border on the <a href="/wiki/Farce" title="Farce">farcical</a> (as <a href="/wiki/William_Congreve" title="William Congreve">William Congreve</a>, for example, judged <i>Epicoene</i>.) He was more diligent in adhering to the <a href="/wiki/Classical_unities" title="Classical unities">classical unities</a> than many of his peers\xe2\x80\x94although as <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Cavendish" class="mw-redirect" title="Margaret Cavendish">Margaret Cavendish</a> noted, the unity of action in the major comedies was rather compromised by Jonson\'s abundance of incident. To this classical model Jonson applied the two features of his style which save his classical imitations from mere pedantry: the vividness with which he depicted the lives of his characters, and the intricacy of his plots. Coleridge, for instance, claimed that <i><a href="/wiki/The_Alchemist_(play)" title="The Alchemist (play)">The Alchemist</a></i> had one of the three most perfect plots in literature.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Poetry">Poetry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ben_Jonson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Poetry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Houghton_MS_Lowell_Autograph_File_185,_Jonson.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Houghton_MS_Lowell_Autograph_File_185%2C_Jonson.jpg/220px-Houghton_MS_Lowell_Autograph_File_185%2C_Jonson.jpg" width="220" height="331" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Houghton_MS_Lowell_Autograph_File_185%2C_Jonson.jpg/330px-Houghton_MS_Lowell_Autograph_File_185%2C_Jonson.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Houghton_MS_Lowell_Autograph_File_185%2C_Jonson.jpg/440px-Houghton_MS_Lowell_Autograph_File_185%2C_Jonson.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1101" data-file-height="1659" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Houghton_MS_Lowell_Autograph_File_185,_Jonson.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>"Epitaph for Cecilia Bulstrode" manuscript, 1609</div></div></div>\n<p>Jonson\'s poetry, like his drama, is informed by his classical learning. Some of his better-known poems are close translations of Greek or Roman models; all display the careful attention to form and style that often came naturally to those trained in classics in the <a href="/wiki/Renaissance_humanism" title="Renaissance humanism">humanist</a> manner. Jonson largely avoided the debates about rhyme and meter that had consumed Elizabethan classicists such as <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Campion" title="Thomas Campion">Thomas Campion</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gabriel_Harvey" title="Gabriel Harvey">Gabriel Harvey</a>. Accepting both rhyme and stress, Jonson used them to mimic the classical qualities of simplicity, restraint and precision.\n</p><p>"Epigrams" (published in the 1616 folio) is an entry in a genre that was popular among late-Elizabethan and Jacobean audiences, although Jonson was perhaps the only poet of his time to work in its full classical range. The epigrams explore various attitudes, most from the satiric stock of the day: complaints against women, courtiers and spies abound. The condemnatory poems are short and anonymous; Jonson\xe2\x80\x99s epigrams of praise, including a famous poem to Camden and lines to Lucy Harington, are longer and are mostly addressed to specific individuals. Although it is included among the epigrams, "<a href="/wiki/On_My_First_Sonne" title="On My First Sonne">On My First Sonne</a>" is neither satirical nor very short; the poem, intensely personal and deeply felt, typifies a genre that would come to be called "lyric poetry." It is possible that the spelling of \'son\' as \'Sonne\' is meant to allude to the <a href="/wiki/Sonnet" title="Sonnet">sonnet</a> form, with which it shares some features. A few other so-called epigrams share this quality. Jonson\'s poems of "The Forest" also appeared in the first folio. Most of the fifteen poems are addressed to Jonson\'s aristocratic supporters, but the most famous are his country-house poem \xe2\x80\x9c<a href="/wiki/To_Penshurst" class="mw-redirect" title="To Penshurst">To Penshurst</a>\xe2\x80\x9d and the poem \xe2\x80\x9c<a href="/wiki/To_Celia" title="To Celia">To Celia</a>\xe2\x80\x9d ("Come, my Celia, let us prove") that appears also in <i><a href="/wiki/Volpone" title="Volpone">Volpone</a></i>.\n</p><p><i>Underwood</i>, published in the expanded folio of 1640, is a larger and more heterogeneous group of poems. It contains <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=A_Celebration_of_Charis&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="A Celebration of Charis (page does not exist)">A Celebration of Charis</a></i>, Jonson\'s most extended effort at love poetry; various religious pieces; <a href="/wiki/Encomium" title="Encomium">encomiastic poems</a> including the poem to Shakespeare and a sonnet on <a href="/wiki/Mary_Wroth" class="mw-redirect" title="Mary Wroth">Mary Wroth</a>; the <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Execration_against_Vulcan&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Execration against Vulcan (page does not exist)">Execration against Vulcan</a></i> and others. The 1640 volume also contains three elegies which have often been ascribed to Donne (one of them appeared in Donne\'s posthumous collected poems).\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relationship_with_Shakespeare">Relationship with Shakespeare</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ben_Jonson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Relationship with Shakespeare">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div Jonson debating at the "<a href="/wiki/Mermaid_Tavern" title="Mermaid Tavern">Mermaid Tavern</a>".</div></div></div>\n<p>There are many legends about Jonson\'s rivalry with <a href="/wiki/Shakespeare" class="mw-redirect" title="Shakespeare">Shakespeare</a>, some of which may be true.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Drummond reports that during their conversation, Jonson scoffed at two apparent absurdities in Shakespeare\'s plays: a nonsensical line in <i><a href="/wiki/Julius_Caesar_(play)" title="Julius Caesar (play)">Julius Caesar</a></i>, and the setting of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Winter%27s_Tale" title="The Winter&#39;s Tale">The Winter\'s Tale</a></i> on the non-existent seacoast of Bohemia. Drummond also reported Jonson as saying that Shakespeare "wanted art" (i.e., lacked skill). Whether Drummond is viewed as accurate or not, the comments fit well with Jonson\'s well-known theories about literature.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>In <a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/On_Shakespeare" class="extiw" title="s:On Shakespeare">"De Shakespeare Nostrat"</a> in <i>Timber</i>, which was published posthumously and reflects his lifetime of practical experience, Jonson offers a fuller and more conciliatory comment. He recalls being told by certain actors that Shakespeare never blotted (i.e., crossed out) a line when he wrote. His own claimed response was "Would he had blotted a thousand!"<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;a&#93;</a></sup> However, Jonson explains, "He was, indeed, honest and of an open and free nature, had an excellent phantasy, brave notions and gentle expressions, wherein he flowed with that facility that sometimes it was necessary he should be stopped".<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> Jonson concludes that "there was ever more in him to be praised than to be pardoned." Also when Shakespeare died, he said, "He was not of an age, but for all time."<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Thomas_Fuller" title="Thomas Fuller">Thomas Fuller</a> relates stories of Jonson and Shakespeare engaging in debates in the <a href="/wiki/Mermaid_Tavern" title="Mermaid Tavern">Mermaid Tavern</a>; Fuller imagines conversations in which Shakespeare would run rings around the more learned but more ponderous Jonson. That the two men knew each other personally is beyond doubt, not only because of the tone of Jonson\'s references to him but because Shakespeare\'s company produced a number of Jonson\'s plays, at least two of which (<i><a href="/wiki/Every_Man_in_His_Humour" title="Every Man in His Humour">Every Man in His Humour</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Sejanus_His_Fall" title="Sejanus His Fall">Sejanus His Fall</a></i>) Shakespeare certainly acted in. However, it is now impossible to tell how much personal communication they had, and tales of their friendship cannot be substantiated.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>Jonson\'s most influential and revealing commentary on Shakespeare is the second of the two poems that he contributed to the prefatory verse that opens Shakespeare\'s <a href="/wiki/First_Folio" title="First Folio">First Folio</a>. This poem, <a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/To_the_Memory_of_My_Beloved_the_Author,_Mr._William_Shakespeare_and_What_He_Hath_Left_Us" class="extiw" title="s:To the Memory of My Beloved the Author, Mr. William Shakespeare and What He Hath Left Us">"To the Memory of My Beloved the Author, Mr. William Shakespeare and What He Hath Left Us"</a>, did a good deal to create the traditional view of Shakespeare as a poet who, despite "small Latine, and lesse Greeke",<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> had a natural genius. The poem has traditionally been thought to exemplify the contrast which Jonson perceived between himself, the disciplined and erudite classicist, scornful of ignorance and sceptical of the masses, and Shakespeare, represented in the poem as a kind of natural wonder whose genius was not subject to any rules except those of the audiences for which he wrote. But the poem itself qualifies this view:\n</p>\n<dl><dd>Yet must I not give Nature all: Thy Art,</dd>\n<dd>My gentle Shakespeare, must enjoy a part.</dd></dl>\n<p>Some view this elegy as a conventional exercise, but others see it as a heartfelt tribute to the "Sweet Swan of Avon", the "Soul of the Age!" It has been argued that Jonson helped to edit the <a href="/wiki/First_Folio" title="First Folio">First Folio</a>, and he may have been inspired to write this poem by reading his fellow playwright\'s works, a number of which had been previously either unpublished or available in less satisfactory versions, in a relatively complete form.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception_and_influence">Reception and influence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ben_Jonson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Reception and influence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Jonson was a towering literary figure, and his influence was enormous for he has been described as \'One of the most vigorous minds that ever added to the strength of English literature\'.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> Before the English Civil War, the "Tribe of Ben" touted his importance, and during the Restoration Jonson\'s satirical comedies and his theory and practice of "humour characters" (which are often misunderstood; see William Congreve\'s letters for clarification) was extremely influential, providing the blueprint for many Restoration comedies. <a href="/wiki/John_Aubrey" title="John Aubrey">John Aubrey</a> wrote of Jonson in "<a href="/wiki/Brief_Lives" title="Brief Lives">Brief Lives</a>." By 1700 Jonson\'s status began to decline. In the Romantic era, Jonson suffered the fate of being unfairly compared and contrasted to Shakespeare, as the taste for Jonson\'s type of satirical comedy decreased. Jonson was at times greatly appreciated by the Romantics, but overall he was denigrated for not writing in a Shakespearean vein.\n</p><p>In 2012, after more than two decades of research, <a href="/wiki/Cambridge_University_Press" title="Cambridge University Press">Cambridge University Press</a> published the first new edition of Jonson\'s complete works for 60 years.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Drama_2">Drama</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ben_Jonson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Drama">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>As G. E. Bentley notes in <i>Shakespeare and Jonson: Their Reputations in the Seventeenth Century Compared</i>, Jonson\'s reputation was in some respects equal to Shakespeare\'s in the 17th century. After the English theatres were reopened on the <a href="/wiki/English_Restoration" class="mw-redirect" title="English Restoration">Restoration</a> of <a href="/wiki/Charles_II_of_England" title="Charles II of England">Charles II</a>, Jonson\'s work, along with Shakespeare\'s and <a href="/wiki/John_Fletcher_(playwright)" title="John Fletcher (playwright)">Fletcher</a>\'s, formed the initial core of the Restoration repertory. It was not until after 1710 that Shakespeare\'s plays (ordinarily in heavily revised forms) were more frequently performed than those of his Renaissance contemporaries. Many critics since the 18th century have ranked Jonson below only Shakespeare among <a href="/wiki/English_Renaissance_theatre" title="English Renaissance theatre">English Renaissance dramatists</a>. Critical judgment has tended to emphasise the very qualities that Jonson himself lauds in his prefaces, in <i>Timber</i>, and in his scattered prefaces and dedications: the realism and propriety of his language, the bite of his satire, and the care with which he plotted his comedies.\n</p><p>For some critics, the temptation to contrast Jonson (representing art or craft) with Shakespeare (representing nature, or untutored genius) has seemed natural; Jonson himself may be said to have initiated this interpretation in the second folio, and <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Butler_(poet)" title="Samuel Butler (poet)">Samuel Butler</a> drew the same comparison in his <a href="/wiki/Commonplace_book" title="Commonplace book">commonplace book</a> later in the century.\n</p><p>At the Restoration, this sensed difference became a kind of critical dogma. <a href="/wiki/Charles_de_Saint-%C3%89vremond" title="Charles de Saint-\xc3\x89vremond">Charles de Saint-\xc3\x89vremond</a> placed Jonson\'s comedies above all else in English drama, and <a href="/wiki/Charles_Gildon" title="Charles Gildon">Charles Gildon</a> called Jonson the father of English comedy. <a href="/wiki/John_Dryden" title="John Dryden">John Dryden</a> offered a more common assessment in the "Essay of Dramatic Poesie," in which his <a href="/wiki/Avatar" title="Avatar">Avatar</a> Neander compares Shakespeare to <a href="/wiki/Homer" title="Homer">Homer</a> and Jonson to <a href="/wiki/Virgil" title="Virgil">Virgil</a>: the former represented profound creativity, the latter polished artifice. But "artifice" was in the 17th century almost synonymous with "art"; Jonson, for instance, used "artificer" as a synonym for "artist" (<i>Discoveries,</i> 33). For <a href="/wiki/Lewis_Theobald" title="Lewis Theobald">Lewis Theobald</a>, too, Jonson "ow[ed] all his Excellence to his Art,\xe2\x80\x9d in contrast to Shakespeare, the natural genius. <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_Rowe_(dramatist)" class="mw-redirect" title="Nicholas Rowe (dramatist)">Nicholas Rowe</a>, to whom may be traced the legend that Jonson owed the production of <i>Every Man in his Humour</i> to Shakespeare\'s intercession, likewise attributed Jonson\'s excellence to learning, which did not raise him quite to the level of genius. A consensus formed: Jonson was the first English poet to understand classical precepts with any accuracy, and he was the first to apply those precepts successfully to contemporary life. But there were also more negative spins on Jonson\'s learned art; for instance, in the 1750s, <a href="/wiki/Edward_Young" title="Edward Young">Edward Young</a> casually remarked on the way in which Jonson\'s learning worked, like Samson\'s strength, to his own detriment. Earlier, <a href="/wiki/Aphra_Behn" title="Aphra Behn">Aphra Behn</a>, writing in defence of female playwrights, had pointed to Jonson as a writer whose learning did not make him popular; unsurprisingly, she compares him unfavourably to Shakespeare. Particularly in the tragedies, with their lengthy speeches abstracted from <a href="/wiki/Sallust" title="Sallust">Sallust</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cicero" title="Cicero">Cicero</a>, <a href="/wiki/Augustan_literature" title="Augustan literature">Augustan</a> critics saw a writer whose learning had swamped his <a href="/wiki/Aesthetic" class="mw-redirect" title="Aesthetic">aesthetic</a> judgment.\n</p><p>In this period, <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Pope" title="Alexander Pope">Alexander Pope</a> is exceptional in that he noted the tendency to exaggeration in these competing critical portraits: "It is ever the nature of Parties to be in extremes; and nothing is so probable, as that because Ben Johnson had much the most learning, it was said on the one hand that Shakespear had none at all; and because Shakespear had much the most wit and fancy, it was retorted on the other, that Johnson wanted both."<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> For the most part, the 18th century consensus remained committed to the division that Pope doubted; as late as the 1750s, <a href="/wiki/Sarah_Fielding" title="Sarah Fielding">Sarah Fielding</a> could put a brief recapitulation of this analysis in the mouth of a "man of sense" encountered by David Simple.\n</p><p>Though his stature declined during the 18th century, Jonson was still read and commented on throughout the century, generally in the kind of comparative and dismissive terms just described. <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_Wilhelm_von_Gerstenberg" title="Heinrich Wilhelm von Gerstenberg">Heinrich Wilhelm von Gerstenberg</a> translated parts of <a href="/wiki/Peter_Whalley_(clergyman)" class="mw-redirect" title="Peter Whalley (clergyman)">Peter Whalley</a>\'s edition into German in 1765. Shortly before the Romantic revolution, <a href="/wiki/Edward_Capell" title="Edward Capell">Edward Capell</a> offered an almost unqualified rejection of Jonson as a dramatic poet, who (he writes) "has very poor pretensions to the high place he holds among the English Bards, as there is no original manner to distinguish him and the tedious sameness visible in his plots indicates a defect of Genius."<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> The disastrous failures of productions of <i>Volpone</i> and <i>Epicoene</i> in the early 1770s no doubt bolstered a widespread sense that Jonson had at last grown too antiquated for the contemporary public; if he still attracted enthusiasts such as <a href="/wiki/Charles_Pratt,_1st_Earl_Camden" title="Charles Pratt, 1st Earl Camden">Earl Camden</a> and <a href="/wiki/William_Gifford" title="William Gifford">William Gifford</a>, he all but disappeared from the stage in the last quarter of the century.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Romanticism" title="Romanticism">romantic</a> revolution in criticism brought about an overall decline in the critical estimation of Jonson. Hazlitt refers dismissively to Jonson\'s "laborious caution." Coleridge, while more respectful, describes Jonson as psychologically superficial: \xe2\x80\x9cHe was a very accurately observing man; but he cared only to observe what was open to, and likely to impress, the senses.\xe2\x80\x9d Coleridge placed Jonson second only to Shakespeare; other romantic critics were less approving. The early 19th century was the great age for recovering Renaissance drama. Jonson, whose reputation had survived, appears to have been less interesting to some readers than writers such as <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Middleton" title="Thomas Middleton">Thomas Middleton</a> or <a href="/wiki/John_Heywood" title="John Heywood">John Heywood</a>, who were in some senses "discoveries" of the 19th century. Moreover, the emphasis which the romantic writers placed on imagination, and their concomitant tendency to distrust studied art, lowered Jonson\'s status, if it also sharpened their awareness of the difference traditionally noted between Jonson and Shakespeare. This trend was by no means universal, however; <a href="/wiki/William_Gifford" title="William Gifford">William Gifford</a>, Jonson\'s first editor of the 19th century, did a great deal to defend Jonson\'s reputation during this period of general decline. In the next era, <a href="/wiki/Algernon_Charles_Swinburne" title="Algernon Charles Swinburne">Swinburne</a>, who was more interested in Jonson than most <a href="/wiki/Victorians" class="mw-redirect" title="Victorians">Victorians</a>, wrote, "The flowers of his growing have every quality but one which belongs to the rarest and finest among flowers: they have colour, form, variety, fertility, vigour: the one thing they want is fragrance\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x93 by \xe2\x80\x9cfragrance,\xe2\x80\x9d Swinburne means spontaneity.\n</p><p>In the 20th century, Jonson\xe2\x80\x99s body of work has been subject to a more varied set of analyses, broadly consistent with the interests and programmes of modern literary criticism. In an essay printed in <i>The Sacred Wood</i>, <a href="/wiki/T._S._Eliot" title="T. S. Eliot">T. S. Eliot</a> attempted to repudiate the charge that Jonson was an arid classicist by analysing the role of imagination in his dialogue. Eliot was appreciative of Jonson\'s overall conception and his "surface", a view consonant with the modernist reaction against Romantic criticism, which tended to denigrate playwrights who did not concentrate on representations of psychological depth. Around mid-century, a number of critics and scholars followed Eliot\'s lead, producing detailed studies of Jonson\'s verbal style. At the same time, study of Elizabethan themes and conventions, such as those by <a href="/w/index.php?title=E._E._Stoll&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="E. E. Stoll (page does not exist)">E. E. Stoll</a> and <a href="/wiki/M._C._Bradbrook" title="M. C. Bradbrook">M. C. Bradbrook</a>, provided a more vivid sense of how Jonson\'s work was shaped by the expectations of his time.\n</p><p>The proliferation of new critical perspectives after mid-century touched on Jonson inconsistently. Jonas Barish was the leading figure among critics who appreciated Jonson\'s artistry. On the other hand, Jonson received less attention from the new critics than did some other playwrights and his work was not of programmatic interest to psychoanalytic critics. But Jonson\'s career eventually made him a focal point for the revived <a href="/wiki/New_Historicism" title="New Historicism">sociopolitical criticism</a>. Jonson\'s works, particularly his masques and pageants, offer significant information regarding the relations of literary production and political power, as do his contacts with and poems for aristocratic patrons; moreover, his career at the centre of London\'s emerging literary world has been seen as exemplifying the development of a fully commodified literary culture. In this respect he is seen as a transitional figure, an author whose skills and ambition led him to a leading role both in the declining culture of <a href="/wiki/Patronage" title="Patronage">patronage</a> and in the rising culture of mass consumption.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Poetry_2">Poetry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ben_Jonson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Poetry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Jonson has been called \'the first poet laureate\'.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> If Jonson\'s reputation as a playwright has traditionally been linked to Shakespeare, his reputation as a poet has, since the early 20th century, been linked to that of <a href="/wiki/John_Donne" title="John Donne">John Donne</a>. In this comparison, Jonson represents the <a href="/wiki/Cavalier_poet" title="Cavalier poet">cavalier</a> strain of poetry, emphasising grace and clarity of expression; Donne, by contrast, epitomised the <a href="/wiki/Metaphysical_poets" title="Metaphysical poets">metaphysical</a> school of poetry, with its reliance on strained, <a href="/wiki/Baroque" title="Baroque">baroque</a> metaphors and often vague phrasing. Since the critics who made this comparison (<a href="/wiki/Herbert_Grierson" class="mw-redirect" title="Herbert Grierson">Herbert Grierson</a> for example), were to varying extents rediscovering Donne, this comparison often worked to the detriment of Jonson\'s reputation.\n</p><p>In his time Jonson was at least as influential as Donne. In 1623, historian <a href="/wiki/Edmund_Bolton" title="Edmund Bolton">Edmund Bolton</a> named him the best and most polished English poet. That this judgment was widely shared is indicated by the admitted influence he had on younger poets. The grounds for describing Jonson as the "father" of cavalier poets are clear: many of the cavalier poets described themselves as his "sons" or his "tribe". For some of this tribe, the connection was as much social as poetic; <a href="/wiki/Robert_Herrick_(poet)" title="Robert Herrick (poet)">Herrick</a> described meetings at "the Sun, the Dog, the Triple Tunne". All of them, including those like Herrick whose accomplishments in verse are generally regarded as superior to Jonson\'s, took inspiration from Jonson\'s revival of classical forms and themes, his subtle melodies, and his disciplined use of <a href="/wiki/Wit" title="Wit">wit</a>. In these respects Jonson may be regarded as among the most important figures in the prehistory of English <a href="/wiki/Augustan_literature" title="Augustan literature">neoclassicism</a>.\n</p><p>The best of Jonson\'s lyrics have remained current since his time; periodically, they experience a brief vogue, as after the publication of Peter Whalley\'s edition of 1756. Jonson\'s poetry continues to interest scholars for the light which it sheds on English literary history, such as politics, systems of patronage and intellectual attitudes. For the general reader, Jonson\'s reputation rests on a few lyrics that, though brief, are surpassed for grace and precision by very few Renaissance poems: "<a href="/wiki/On_My_First_Sonne" title="On My First Sonne">On My First Sonne</a>"; "<a href="/wiki/To_Celia" title="To Celia">To Celia</a>"; "<a href="/wiki/To_Penshurst" class="mw-redirect" title="To Penshurst">To Penshurst</a>"; and the <a href="/wiki/Epitaph" title="Epitaph">epitaph</a> on Salomon Pavy, a <a href="/wiki/Boy_player" title="Boy player">boy player</a>  abducted from his parents who acted in Jonson\'s plays.\n</p>\n<h2><span id="Jonson.27s_works"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Jonson\'s_works">Jonson\'s works</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a famously quipped on the manner in which language became a measure of the speaker or writer:\n</p>\n<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Language most shows a man: Speak, that I may see thee. It springs out of the most retired and inmost parts of us, and is the image of the parent of it, the mind. No glass renders a man\xe2\x80\x99s form or likeness so true as his speech. Nay, it is likened to a man; and as we consider feature and composition in a man, so words in language; in the greatness, aptness, sound structure, and harmony of it.</p><div class="templatequotecite">\xe2\x80\x94&#8201;<cite>Ben Jonson, 1640 (posthumous)<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup></cite></div>\n</blockquote>\n<p>As with other English Renaissance dramatists, a portion of Ben Jonson\'s literary output has not survived. In addition to <i><a href="/wiki/The_Isle_of_Dogs_(play)" title="The Isle of Dogs (play)">The Isle of Dogs</a></i> (1597), the records suggest these lost plays as wholly or partially Jonson\'s work: <i>Richard Crookback</i> (1602); <i>Hot Anger Soon Cold</i> (1598), with Porter and <a href="/wiki/Henry_Chettle" title="Henry Chettle">Henry Chettle</a>; <i>Page of Plymouth</i> (1599), with Dekker; and <i>Robert II, King of Scots</i> (1599), with Chettle and Dekker. Several of Jonson\'s masques and entertainments also are not extant: <i>The Entertainment at Merchant Taylors</i> (1607); <i>The Entertainment at Salisbury House for James I</i> (1608); and <i>The May Lord</i> (1613\xe2\x80\x9319).\n</p><p>Finally, there are questionable or borderline attributions. Jonson may have had a hand in <i><a href="/wiki/Rollo_Duke_of_Normandy" title="Rollo Duke of Normandy">Rollo, Duke of Normandy, or The Bloody Brother</a></i>, a play in the canon of John Fletcher and his collaborators. The comedy <i><a href="/wiki/The_Widow_(play)" title="The Widow (play)">The Widow</a></i> was printed in 1652 as the work of <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Middleton" title="Thomas Middleton">Thomas Middleton</a>, Fletcher and Jonson, though scholars have been intensely sceptical about Jonson\'s presence in the play. A few attributions of anonymous plays, such as <i><a href="/wiki/The_London_Prodigal" title="The London Prodigal">The London Prodigal</a></i>, have been ventured by individual researchers, but have met with cool responses.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biographies_of_Ben_Jonson">Biographies of Ben Jonson</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ben_Jonson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Biographies of Ben Jonson">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul><li><i>Ben Jonson: His Life and Work</i> by <a href="/wiki/Rosalind_Miles" title="Rosalind Miles">Rosalind Miles</a></li>\n<li><i>Ben Jonson: His Craft and Art</i> by Rosalind Miles</li>\n<li><i>Ben Jonson: A Literary Life</i> by <a href="/w/index.php?title=W._David_Kay&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="W.
