href="/w/index.php?title=Education_in_Northern_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Administration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Department_of_Education_(Northern_Ireland)" title="Department of Education (Northern Ireland)">Department of Education</a> (DE) is responsible for Northern Ireland\'s education policy, with the exception of the higher and further education sector which is the responsibility of the <a href="/wiki/Department_for_the_Economy" title="Department for the Economy">Department for the Economy</a> (DfE).\n</p><p>The Department of Education\'s main areas of responsibility cover pre-school, primary, post-primary and special education; the youth service; the promotion of community relations within and between schools; and teacher education and salaries. Its primary statutory duty is to promote the education of the <a href="/wiki/People_of_Northern_Ireland" title="People of Northern Ireland">people of Northern Ireland</a> and to ensure the effective implementation of education policy.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Education_Authority">Education Authority</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Education_in_Northern_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Education Authority">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Education_Authority" title="Education Authority">Education Authority</a></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Education_Authority" title="Education Authority">Education Authority</a> is responsible for ensuring that efficient and effective primary and secondary education services are available to meet the needs of children and young people, and support for the provision of efficient and effective youth services. These services were previously delivered by the five Education and Library Boards (ELBs) until the creation of the Education Authority, which assumed these roles in 2015. Classroom 2000 (<a href="/wiki/C2k" title="C2k">C2k</a>), on behalf of the authority, is responsible for the provision of information and communications technology managed services to all schools in Northern Ireland. id="Curriculum">Curriculum</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Education_in_Northern_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Curriculum">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The majority of examinations sat, and education plans followed, in Northern Irish schools are set by the <a href="/wiki/Council_for_the_Curriculum,_Examinations_%26_Assessment" title="Council for the Curriculum, Examinations &amp; Assessment">Council for the Curriculum, Examinations &amp; Assessment</a> (CCEA). All schools in Northern Ireland follow the Northern Ireland Curriculum which is based on the National Curriculum used in England and Wales. At age 11, on entering secondary education, all pupils study a broad base of subjects which include geography, English, mathematics, science, physical education, music and modern languages. Currently there are proposals to reform the curriculum to make its emphasis more skills-based under which, in addition to those mentioned, home economics, local and global citizenship and personal, social and health education would become compulsory subjects.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>At age 14, pupils select which subjects to continue to study for <a href="/wiki/General_Certificate_of_Secondary_Education" title="General Certificate of Secondary Education">General Certificate of Secondary Education</a> (GCSE) examinations. Currently it is compulsory to study English, mathematics and religious studies, although a full GCSE course does not have to be studied for the latter.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> In addition, pupils usually elect to continue with other subjects and many study for eight or nine GCSEs but possibly up to ten or eleven. GCSEs mark the end of compulsory education in Northern Ireland.\n</p><p>At age 16, some pupils stay at school and choose to study <a href="/wiki/Advanced_Level_(UK)" class="mw-redirect" title="Advanced Level (UK)">Advanced Level</a> AS and A2 level subjects or more vocational qualifications such as <a href="/w/index.php?title=Applied_Advanced_Levels&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Applied Advanced Levels (page does not exist)">Applied Advanced Levels</a>. Those choosing AS and A2 levels normally pick three or four subjects and success in these can determine acceptance into higher education courses at university.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Eleven_plus">Eleven plus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Education_in_Northern_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Eleven plus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Eleven_plus_exam" class="mw-redirect" title="Eleven plus exam">Eleven plus exam</a></div>\n<p>Northern Ireland formerly ran a transfer test at a governmental level to decide which primary school students qualified for a place at a Grammar School. This system was abolished by <a href="/wiki/Caitr%C3%ADona_Ruane" title="Caitr\xc3\xadona Ruane">Caitr\xc3\xadona Ruane</a> during her time as Minister of Education, a decision which was confirmed by <a href="/wiki/UK_Government" class="mw-redirect" title="UK Government">UK Government</a> direct rule ministers. This policy was continued by subsequent minister <a href="/wiki/John_O%27Dowd" title="John O&#39;Dowd">John O\'Dowd</a>. The majority of grammar schools did, however, decide to set their own entrance exams a situation which continues to this day. There are two types in Northern Ireland:\xe2\x80\x93 AQE and GL assessment.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Controlled_schools">Controlled schools</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Education_in_Northern_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Controlled schools">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Controlled schools in Northern Ireland (nursery, primary, special, secondary and grammar schools) are under the management of the school\'s board of governors and the employing authority is the Education Authority (EA). Although open to those of all faiths and none, many of these schools were originally <a href="/wiki/Ulster_Protestants" title="Ulster Protestants">Protestant</a> church schools, whose control was transferred to the state in the first half of the twentieth century. The three largest Protestant churches (<a href="/wiki/Presbyterian" class="mw-redirect" title="Presbyterian">Presbyterian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Church_of_Ireland" title="Church of Ireland">Church of Ireland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Methodist" class="mw-redirect" title="Methodist">Methodist</a>), known as the transferors, maintain a link with the schools through church representation on controlled school boards of governors.\n</p><p>The controlled sector is the largest education sector in Northern Ireland. According to figures from the <a href="/wiki/Department_of_Education_(Northern_Ireland)" title="Department of Education (Northern Ireland)">Department of Education</a> for 2016/2017,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> there are 560 controlled schools, 48% of the total number of schools registered in Northern Ireland. The number of pupils attending controlled schools is 140,632, approximately 42% of all pupils in Northern Ireland. In terms of religious breakdown, 66% of pupils in controlled schools are Protestant, 10% are Catholic, 18% have no religion and 6% are \xe2\x80\x98other\xe2\x80\x99. Controlled schools are managed by the Education Authority through Boards of Governors.\n</p><p>In October 2014 an Education Bill<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> was put before the assembly, which created the Education Authority. Alongside this, the Minister and the Northern Ireland Executive agreed to establish and fund a support body for schools in the controlled sector.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Controlled_Schools%27_Support_Council" title="Controlled Schools&#39; Support Council">The Controlled Schools\xe2\x80\x99 Support Council (CSSC)</a> became operational on 1 September 2016, and its headquarters are in Stranmillis University College, Belfast.  The CSSC seeks to support the interests of schools in the controlled sector through a focus on five key areas: advocacy, ethos, governance, raising standards and area planning.  Almost 90% of controlled schools are members of the <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.csscni.org.uk/about/controlled/">Controlled Schools\' Support Council</a>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Catholic_education">Catholic education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Education_in_Northern_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Catholic education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Lumen_Christi_College_SMC.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Lumen_Christi_College_SMC.jpg/220px-Lumen_Christi_College_SMC.jpg" width="220" height="159" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Lumen_Christi_College_SMC.jpg/330px-Lumen_Christi_College_SMC.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Lumen_Christi_College_SMC.jpg/440px-Lumen_Christi_College_SMC.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3064" data-file-height="2221" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Lumen_Christi_College_SMC.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Lumen_Christi_College_(Derry,_Northern_Ireland)" class="mw-redirect" title="Lumen Christi College (Derry, Northern Ireland)">Lumen Christi College</a>, <a href="/wiki/Derry" title="Derry">Derry</a></div></div></div>\n<p>There are 466 <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Roman Catholic</a>-managed schools in Northern Ireland. According to figures from the <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.education-ni.gov.uk/topics/statistics-and-research/statistics">Department of Education for 2016/2017</a>, the number of pupils registered at school in Northern Ireland is 332,986. The number of pupils attending Catholic-managed schools is 121,733, approximately 37%.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Council_for_Catholic_Maintained_Schools" title="Council for Catholic Maintained Schools">Council for Catholic Maintained Schools</a> (the CCMS)<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> is the advocate for the Catholic maintained schools sector in Northern Ireland. The CCMS represents trustees, schools and governors on issues such as raising and maintaining standards, the school estate and teacher employment. As the largest employer of teachers in Northern Ireland (8,500 teachers), the CCMS plays a central role in supporting teachers whether through its welfare service or, for example, in working parties such as the Independent Inquiry into Teacher Pay and Conditions of Service.  Exempt from fair employment legislation, it is permitted to discriminate against non-Catholic teachers.\n</p><p>The CCMS supports trustees in the provision of school buildings, and governors and principals in the management and control of schools.  The CCMS also has a wider role within the Northern Ireland education sector and contributes to policy on a wide range of issues such as curriculum review, selection, pre-school education, pastoral care and leadership.\n</p><p>There are 36 council members who oversee and authorise the strategic and operational policies and practices of the CCMS. Council members are appointed for the duration of each council period for four years. Membership to the council is by appointment and recommendation<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Council members receive payment for travelling and incurred costs only. There are four categories of Council members:\n</p>\n<ul><li>Department of Education representatives \xe2\x80\x93 advertised through the press for these positions.</li>\n<li>Trustee representatives \xe2\x80\x93 recommended by Northern Ireland <a href="/wiki/Bishop" title="Bishop">bishops</a>.</li>\n<li>Parents\' representatives \xe2\x80\x93 drawn from local community on a voluntary basis.</li>\n<li>Teachers\' representatives \xe2\x80\x93 drawn from the teaching profession on a voluntary basis.</li></ul>\n<p>Established under the auspices of 1989 Education Reform (Northern Ireland) Order, the Council\xe2\x80\x99s primary purpose is the provision of an upper tier of management for the Catholic Maintained Sector with the primary objective of raising standards in Catholic maintained schools.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Integrated_education">Integrated education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Education_in_Northern_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Integrated education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Although <a href="/wiki/Integrated_education" title="Integrated education">integrated education</a> is expanding, Northern Ireland has a highly segregated education system. Teaching a balanced view of some subjects (especially regional history) is difficult in these conditions. The churches in Northern Ireland have not been involved in the development of integrated schools.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The schools have been established by the voluntary efforts of parents. The <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.nicie.org/">Northern Ireland Council for Integrated Education</a> (NICIE), a voluntary organisation, promotes, develops and supports integrated education in Northern Ireland, <a href="/wiki/English_medium_education" class="mw-redirect" title="English medium education">through the medium of English only</a>.\n</p><p>The Integrated Education Fund (IEF) is a financial foundation for the development and growth of integrated education in Northern Ireland in response to parental demand. The IEF seeks to bridge the financial gap between starting integrated schools and securing full government funding and support.\n</p><p>It was established in 1998 with money from <a href="/wiki/Structural_Funds_and_Cohesion_Fund" title="Structural Funds and Cohesion Fund">EU Structural Funds</a>, the Department of Education NI, the <a href="/wiki/Nuffield_Foundation" title="Nuffield Foundation">Nuffield Foundation</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Rowntree_Charitable_Trust" title="Joseph Rowntree Charitable Trust">Joseph Rowntree Charitable Trust</a>, as a financial foundation for the development and growth of Integrated Education. The Fund financially supports the establishment of new schools, the growth of existing schools and those schools seeking to become integrated through the transformation process. Funding is generally seed corn and projects are \'pump primed\' with the objective of eventually securing full government funding and support.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Irish_language_medium_education">Irish language medium education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Education_in_Northern_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Irish language medium education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The Education (Northern Ireland) Order 1998 placed a duty on the Department of Education, similar to that already in existence in relation to integrated education through the 1989 Education Reform Order, "to encourage and facilitate the development of Irish-medium education\xe2\x80\x9d. Pupils are usually taught most subjects through the medium of <a href="/wiki/Irish_language" title="Irish language">Irish</a>, which is the <a href="/wiki/Second_language" title="Second language">second language</a> of most of the pupils, whilst English is taught through English. This form of education has been described as <a href="/wiki/Language_immersion" title="Language immersion">Immersion education</a>, and is now firmly established as a successful and effective form of\n<a href="/wiki/Bilingual_education" title="Bilingual education">bilingual education</a>. It aims to develop a high standard of language competence in the immersion language (Irish) across the curriculum, but must also, and can, ensure a similar level of achievement in the first language (in this case, usually English) as that reached by pupils attending monolingual <a href="/wiki/English-medium_education" title="English-medium education">English medium schools</a>.\n</p><p>Irish-medium schools, or <a href="/wiki/Gaelscoil" title="Gaelscoil">Gaelscoileanna</a>, are able to achieve grant-aided status, under the same procedures as other schools, by applying for voluntary maintained status. In addition to free-standing schools, Irish language medium education can be provided through units in existing schools. Unit arrangements permit Irish-language-medium education to be supported where a free-standing school would not be viable. A unit may operate as a self-contained provision under the management of a host English-medium school and usually on the same site.\n</p><p>There are currently two types of Irish-medium schools in Northern Ireland.  There are stand-alone schools, of which there are 27, and there are Irish-medium units attached to English-medium host schools.  There are 12 of these type of school.\n</p><p>In addition to this, there are two independent schools teaching through the medium of Irish. These are Gaelscoil Ghleann Darach in <a href="/wiki/Crumlin,_County_Antrim" title="Crumlin, County Antrim">Crumlin</a> and Gaelscoil na Dar\xc3\xb3ige in <a href="/wiki/Derry" title="Derry">Derry City</a>.\nComhairle na Gaelscola\xc3\xadochta (CnaG) is the representative body for Irish-medium Education.  It was set up in 2000 by the Department of Education to promote, facilitate and encourage Irish-medium Education. One of CnaG\'s central objectives is to seek to extend the availability of Irish-medium Education to parents who wish to avail of it for their children.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="School_years">School years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Education_in_Northern_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: School years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul><li>Primary education\n<ul><li>Primary school\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Foundation_Stage" title="Foundation Stage">Foundation Stage</a>\n<ul><li>Primary 1, age 4 to 5 (equivalent to Reception in England and Wales)</li>\n<li>Primary 2, age 5 to 6</li></ul></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Key_Stage_1" title="Key Stage 1">Key Stage 1</a>\n<ul><li>Primary 3, age 6 to 7</li>\n<li>Primary 4, age 7 to 8</li></ul></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Key_Stage_2" title="Key Stage 2">Key Stage 2</a>\n<ul><li>Primary 5, age 8 to 9</li>\n<li>Primary 6, age 9 to 10</li>\n<li>Primary 7, age 10 to 11</li></ul></li></ul></li></ul></li></ul>\n<p>(For entry to grammar schools, entrance exams take place to determine places.
