href="/wiki/Departments_of_the_United_Kingdom_Government" class="mw-redirect" title="Departments of the United Kingdom Government">department</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Government of the United Kingdom">Government of the United Kingdom</a>. It is responsible for protecting and promoting British interests worldwide. It was created in 1968 by merging the Foreign Office and the <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_Commonwealth_Affairs" title="Secretary of State for Commonwealth Affairs">Commonwealth Office</a>.\n</p><p>The head of the FCO is the <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_Foreign_and_Commonwealth_Affairs" title="Secretary of State for Foreign and Commonwealth Affairs">Secretary of State for Foreign and Commonwealth Affairs</a>, commonly abbreviated to "Foreign Secretary". This is regarded as one of the four most prestigious positions in the <a href="/wiki/Cabinet_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Cabinet of the United Kingdom">Cabinet</a> \xe2\x80\x93 the <a href="/wiki/Great_Offices_of_State" title="Great Offices of State">Great Offices of State</a> \xe2\x80\x93 alongside those of <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Prime Minister of the United Kingdom">Prime Minister</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chancellor_of_the_Exchequer" title="Chancellor of the Exchequer">Chancellor of the Exchequer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Home_Secretary" title="Home Secretary">Home Secretary</a>.\n</p><p>The FCO is managed from day to day by a <a href="/wiki/Civil_servant" class="mw-redirect" title="Civil servant">civil servant</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Permanent_Under-Secretary_of_State_for_Foreign_Affairs" title="Permanent Under-Secretary of State for Foreign Affairs">Permanent Under-Secretary of State for Foreign Affairs</a>, who also acts as the Head of <a href="/wiki/Her_Majesty%27s_Diplomatic_Service" title="Her Majesty&#39;s Diplomatic Service">Her Majesty\'s Diplomatic Service</a>. This position is held by <a href="/wiki/Simon_McDonald_(diplomat)" title="Simon McDonald (diplomat)">Sir Simon McDonald</a>, who took office on 1 September 2015.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="mw-headline" id="The_Foreign_Office">The Foreign Office</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Foreign_and_Commonwealth_Office&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The Foreign Office">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Foreign.office.london.arp.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Foreign.office.london.arp.jpg/220px-Foreign.office.london.arp.jpg" width="220" height="163" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Foreign.office.london.arp.jpg/330px-Foreign.office.london.arp.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Foreign.office.london.arp.jpg/440px-Foreign.office.london.arp.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1729" data-file-height="1282" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Foreign.office.london.arp.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Foreign Office building by Sir <a href="/wiki/George_Gilbert_Scott" title="George Gilbert Scott">George Gilbert Scott</a>, viewed from <a href="/wiki/Horse_Guards_Road" title="Horse Guards Road">Horse Guards Road</a></div></div></div>\n<dl><dt>Eighteenth century</dt></dl>\n<p>The Foreign Office was formed in March 1782 by combining the <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_the_Southern_Department" title="Secretary of State for the Southern Department">Southern</a> and <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_the_Northern_Department" title="Secretary of State for the Northern Department">Northern</a> Departments of the Secretary of State, each of which covered both foreign and domestic affairs in their parts of the Kingdom. The two departments\' foreign affairs responsibilities became the Foreign Office, whilst their domestic affairs responsibilities were assigned to the <a href="/wiki/Home_Office" title="Home Office">Home Office</a>. The Home Office is technically the senior.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<dl><dt>Nineteenth century</dt></dl>\n<p>During the 19th century, it was not infrequent for the Foreign Office to approach <i><a href="/wiki/The_Times" title="The Times">The Times</a></i> newspaper and ask for continental intelligence, which was often superior to that conveyed by official sources.<sup id="cite_ref-historyandpolicy_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-historyandpolicy-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Examples of journalists who specialized in foreign affairs and were well connected to politicians included: <a href="/wiki/Henry_Southern_(journalist)" title="Henry Southern (journalist)">Henry Southern</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ignatius_Valentine_Chirol" title="Ignatius Valentine Chirol">Valentine Chirol</a>, Harold Nicolson, and <a href="/wiki/R._H._Bruce_Lockhart" title="R. H. Bruce Lockhart">Robert Bruce Lockhart</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<dl><dt>Twentieth century</dt></dl>\n<p>During <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Arab_Bureau" title="Arab Bureau">Arab Bureau</a> was set up within the British Foreign Office as a section of the <i>Cairo Intelligence Department</i>. During the early <a href="/wiki/Cold_war" class="mw-redirect" title="Cold war">cold war</a> an important department was the <a href="/wiki/Information_Research_Department" title="Information Research Department">Information Research Department</a>, set up to counter <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet</a> propaganda and infiltration.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Foreign_and_Commonwealth_Office">The Foreign and Commonwealth Office</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Foreign_and_Commonwealth_Office&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: The Foreign and Commonwealth Office">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The FCO was formed in 1968, from the merger of the short-lived <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_Commonwealth_Affairs" title="Secretary of State for Commonwealth Affairs">Commonwealth Office</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_Foreign_Affairs" class="mw-redirect" title="Secretary of State for Foreign Affairs">Foreign Office</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The Commonwealth Office had been created only in 1966, by the merger of the <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_Commonwealth_Relations" title="Secretary of State for Commonwealth Relations">Commonwealth Relations Office</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_the_Colonies" title="Secretary of State for the Colonies">Colonial Office</a>, and the Commonwealth Relations Office had been formed by the merger of the <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_Dominion_Affairs" title="Secretary of State for Dominion Affairs">Dominions Office</a> and the <a href="/wiki/India_Office" title="India Office">India Office</a> in 1947\xe2\x80\x94with the Dominions Office having been split from the Colonial Office in 1925.\n</p><p>The Foreign and Commonwealth Office held responsibility for <a href="/wiki/International_development" title="International development">international development</a> issues between 1970 and 1974, and again between 1979 and 1997. From 1997, this became the responsibility of the separate <a href="/wiki/Department_for_International_Development" title="Department for International Development">Department for International Development</a>.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/The_National_Archives_(United_Kingdom)" title="The National Archives (United Kingdom)">The National Archives</a> website contains a Government timeline to show the departments responsible for Foreign Affairs from 1945.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Developments">Developments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Foreign_and_Commonwealth_Office&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Developments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>When <a href="/wiki/David_Miliband" title="David Miliband">David Miliband</a> took over as Foreign Secretary in June 2007, he set in hand a review of the FCO\xe2\x80\x99s strategic priorities.  One of the key messages of these discussions was the conclusion that the existing framework of ten international strategic priorities, dating from 2003, was no longer appropriate.  Although the framework had been useful in helping the FCO plan its work and allocate its resources, there was agreement that it needed a new framework to drive its work forward.\n</p><p>The new strategic framework consists of three core elements:\n</p>\n<ul><li>A flexible global network of staff and offices, serving the whole of the UK Government.</li>\n<li>Three essential services that support the British economy, British nationals abroad and managed migration for Britain.  These services are delivered through <a href="/wiki/UK_Trade_%26_Investment" title="UK Trade &amp; Investment">UK Trade &amp; Investment</a> (UKTI), consular teams in Britain and overseas, and <a href="/wiki/UK_Visas_and_Immigration" title="UK Visas and Immigration">UK Visas and Immigration</a>.</li>\n<li>Four policy goals:\n<ul><li>countering terrorism and weapons proliferation and their causes</li>\n<li>preventing and resolving conflict</li>\n<li>promoting a low carbon, high-growth, global economy</li>\n<li>developing effective international institutions, in particular the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> and the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>.</li></ul></li></ul>\n<p>In August 2005, a report by management consultant group <a href="/w/index.php?title=Collinson_Grant&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Collinson Grant (page does not exist)">Collinson Grant</a> was made public by <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Mackinlay" class="mw-redirect" title="Andrew Mackinlay">Andrew Mackinlay</a>. The report severely criticised the FCO\'s management structure, noting:\n</p>\n<ul><li>The Foreign Office could be "slow to act".</li>\n<li>Delegation is lacking within the management structure.</li>\n<li>Accountability was poor.</li>\n<li>The FCO could feasibly cut 1200 jobs.</li>\n<li>At least \xc2\xa348 million could be saved annually.</li></ul>\n<p>The Foreign Office commissioned the report to highlight areas which would help it achieve its pledge to reduce spending by \xc2\xa387 million over three years. In response to the report being made public, the Foreign Office stated it had already implemented the report\'s recommendations.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 2009, Gordon Brown created the position of chief scientific adviser (CSA) to the FCO. The first science adviser was David C. Clary.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>On 25 April 2010, the department apologised after <i><a href="/wiki/The_Sunday_Telegraph" title="The Sunday Telegraph">The Sunday Telegraph</a></i> obtained a "foolish" document calling for the upcoming September visit of <a href="/wiki/Pope_Benedict_XVI" title="Pope Benedict XVI">Pope Benedict XVI</a> to be marked by the launch of "Benedict-branded" <a href="/wiki/Condom" title="Condom">condoms</a>, the opening of an <a href="/wiki/Abortion" title="Abortion">abortion</a> clinic and the blessing of a <a href="/wiki/Same-sex_marriage" title="Same-sex marriage">same-sex marriage</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 2012, the Foreign Office was criticised by <a href="/wiki/Gerald_Steinberg" class="mw-redirect" title="Gerald Steinberg">Gerald Steinberg</a>, of the Jerusalem-based research institute, <a href="/wiki/NGO_Monitor" title="NGO Monitor">NGO Monitor</a>, saying that the Foreign Office and the Department for International Development to Palestinian NGOs provided more than \xc2\xa3500,000 in funding to Palestinian NGOs which he says "promote political attacks on Israel." In response, a spokesman for the Foreign Office said, \xe2\x80\x9cwe are very careful about who and what we fund. The objective of our funding is to support efforts to achieve a two-state solution. Funding a particular project for a limited period of time does not mean that we endorse every single action or public comment made by an NGO or by its employees.\xe2\x80\x9d<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In September 2012, the FCO and the Canadian Department of Foreign Affairs signed a Memorandum of Understanding on diplomatic cooperation, which promotes the co-location of embassies, the joint provision of consular services, and common crisis response. The project has been criticised for further diminishing the UK\'s influence in Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-Gaspers_FPC_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gaspers_FPC-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overseas_Territories_Directorate">Overseas Territories Directorate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Foreign_and_Commonwealth_Office&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Overseas Territories Directorate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The Overseas Territories Directorate is responsible for the <a href="/wiki/British_Overseas_Territories" title="British Overseas Territories">British Overseas Territories</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="FCO_Services">FCO Services</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Foreign_and_Commonwealth_Office&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: FCO Services">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In April 2006, a new <a href="/wiki/Executive_agency" title="Executive agency">executive agency</a> was established, FCO Services, to provide corporate service functions.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> In April 2008 it moved to <a href="/wiki/Trading_Fund" class="mw-redirect" title="Trading Fund">Trading Fund</a> status so it had the ability to provide similar services which it already offers to the FCO,<sup id="cite_ref-Fund_Order_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fund_Order-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> to other government departments and even outside businesses.\n</p><p>It is accountable to the Secretary of State for Foreign and Commonwealth Affairs, and provides secure support services to the FCO, other government departments and foreign governments and bodies with which the UK has close links.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Since 2011, FCO Services has been developing the Government Secure Application Environment (GSAE) on a secure <a href="/wiki/Cloud_computing" title="Cloud computing">cloud computing</a> platform to support UK government organisations.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>For over 10 years, FCO Services has been working globally, to keep customer assets and information safe. FCO Services is a public sector organisation, it is not funded by Vote and has to rely on the income it produces to meet its costs, by providing services on a commercial basis to customers both in the UK and throughout the world. Its Accounting Officer and Chief Executive is accountable to the Secretary of State for Foreign &amp; Commonwealth Affairs and to Parliament, for the organisation\'s performance and conduct.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Buildings">Buildings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Foreign_and_Commonwealth_Office&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Buildings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Foreign_and_India_Offices,_London,_1866_ILN.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Foreign_and_India_Offices%2C_London%2C_1866_ILN.jpg/220px-Foreign_and_India_Offices%2C_London%2C_1866_ILN.jpg" width="220" height="127" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Foreign_and_India_Offices%2C_London%2C_1866_ILN.jpg/330px-Foreign_and_India_Offices%2C_London%2C_1866_ILN.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Foreign_and_India_Offices%2C_London%2C_1866_ILN.jpg/440px-Foreign_and_India_Offices%2C_London%2C_1866_ILN.jpg 2x" data-file-width="600" data-file-height="347" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Foreign_and_India_Offices,_London,_1866_ILN.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The western end of the Foreign and Commonwealth Office\xe2\x80\x99s building in 1866, facing <a href="/wiki/St._James%27s_Park" class="mw-redirect" title="St. James&#39;s Park">St. James\'s Park</a>. A mansion in the St James\'s district in the West End of London which the Foreign Office holds on lease from the Crown. It is used primarily for hospitality, entertaining foreign dignitaries and housing <a href="/wiki/Government_Wine_Cellar" title="Government Wine Cellar">The Government Wine Cellar</a>.</li></ul>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Foreign_and_Commonwealth_Office_Main_Building">Foreign and Commonwealth Office Main Building</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Foreign_and_Commonwealth_Office&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Foreign and Commonwealth Office Main Building">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><span id="Main_Building"></span> \n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Cmglee_London_FCO_Grand_Staircase.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Cmglee_London_FCO_Grand_Staircase.jpg/170px-Cmglee_London_FCO_Grand_Staircase.jpg" width="170" height="425" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Cmglee_London_FCO_Grand_Staircase.jpg/255px-Cmglee_London_FCO_Grand_Staircase.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Cmglee_London_FCO_Grand_Staircase.jpg/340px-Cmglee_London_FCO_Grand_Staircase.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2048" data-file-height="5120" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Cmglee_London_FCO_Grand_Staircase.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Grand Staircase in September 2013</div></div></div>\n<p>The Foreign and Commonwealth Office occupies a building which originally provided premises for four separate government departments: the Foreign Office, the India Office, the Colonial Office, and the Home Office. Construction on the building began in 1861 and finished in 1868, and it was designed by the architect <a href="/wiki/George_Gilbert_Scott" title="George Gilbert Scott">George Gilbert Scott</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-history_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>  Its architecture is in the <a href="/wiki/Italianate_architecture" title="Italianate architecture">Italianate</a> style; Scott had initially envisaged a <a href="/wiki/Gothic_Revival_architecture" title="Gothic Revival architecture">Gothic</a> design, but <a href="/wiki/Henry_Temple,_3rd_Viscount_Palmerston" class="mw-redirect" title="Henry Temple, 3rd Viscount Palmerston">Lord Palmerston</a>, then Prime Minister, insisted on a classical style.<sup id="cite_ref-history_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> English sculptors <a href="/wiki/Henry_Hugh_Armstead" title="Henry Hugh Armstead">Henry Hugh Armstead</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Birnie_Philip" title="John Birnie Philip">John Birnie Philip</a> produced a number of allegorical figures (\'Art\', \'Law\', \'Commerce\', etc.) for the exterior.\n</p><p>In 1925 the Foreign Office played host to the signing of the <a href="/wiki/Locarno_Treaties" title="Locarno Treaties">Locarno Treaties</a>, aimed at reducing tension in Europe.  The ceremony took place in a suite of rooms that had been designed for banqueting, which subsequently became known as the Locarno Suite.<sup id="cite_ref-route_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-route-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> During the Second World War, the Locarno Suite\'s fine furnishings were removed or covered up, and it became home to a foreign office code-breaking department.<sup id="cite_ref-route_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-route-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Due to increasing numbers of staff, the offices became increasingly cramped and much of the fine <a href="/wiki/Victorian_architecture" title="Victorian architecture">Victorian</a> interior was covered over\xe2\x80\x94especially after <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. In the 1960s, demolition was proposed, as part of major redevelopment plan for the area drawn up by architect Sir <a href="/wiki/Leslie_Martin" title="Leslie Martin">Leslie Martin</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-history_19-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> A subsequent public outcry prevented these proposals from ever being implemented. Instead, the Foreign Office became a Grade 1 <a href="/wiki/Listed_building" title="Listed building">listed building</a> in 1970.<sup id="cite_ref-history_19-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> In 1978, the Home office moved to a new building, easing overcrowding.\n</p><p>With a new sense of the building\'s historical value, it underwent a 17-year, \xc2\xa3100 million restoration process, completed in 1997.<sup id="cite_ref-history_19-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> The Locarno Suite, used as offices and storage since the Second World War, was fully restored for use in international conferences. The building is now open to the public each year over <a href="/wiki/Open_House_Weekend" class="mw-redirect" title="Open House Weekend">Open House Weekend</a>.\n</p><p>In 2014 refurbishment to accommodate all Foreign and Commonwealth Office employees into one building was started by <a href="/wiki/Mace_(construction_company)" title="Mace (construction company)">Mace</a>.<sup FCO&#160;</div></div></div></div></div></div></div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Devolution">Devolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Foreign_and_Commonwealth_Office&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Devolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><a href="/wiki/International_relations" title="International relations">International relations</a> are handled centrally from Westminster on behalf of the whole of Britain and its dependencies.  However, the devolved administrations also maintain an overseas presence in the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">USA</a> and <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> alongside British diplomatic missions. These offices aim to promote their own economies and ensure that devolved interests are taken into account in <a href="/wiki/Foreign_relations_of_Britain" class="mw-redirect" title="Foreign relations of Britain">British foreign policy</a>. Ministers from devolved administrations can attend international negotiations when agreed with the British Government e.g. EU fisheries negotiations.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Similarly, ministers from the devolved administrations meet at approximately quarterly intervals through the Joint Ministerial Committee (Europe), chaired by the Foreign Secretary to "discuss matters bearing on devolved responsibilities that are under discussion within the European Union."<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
