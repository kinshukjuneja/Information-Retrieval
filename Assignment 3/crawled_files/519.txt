of Shakespeare">portraits that may depict</a> <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">William Shakespeare</a> (1564\xe2\x80\x931616). Painted between 1600 and 1610, it may have served as the basis for the engraved portrait of Shakespeare used in the <i><a href="/wiki/First_Folio" title="First Folio">First Folio</a></i> in 1623.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> It is named after the <a href="/wiki/James_Brydges,_3rd_Duke_of_Chandos" title="James Brydges, 3rd Duke of Chandos">Dukes of Chandos</a>, who formerly owned the painting. The portrait was given to the <a href="/wiki/National_Portrait_Gallery,_London" title="National Portrait Gallery, London">National Portrait Gallery, London</a> on its foundation in 1856, and it is listed as the first work in its collection.<sup id="cite_ref-NPG_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NPG-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>It has not been possible to determine with certainty who painted the portrait, nor whether it really depicts Shakespeare. However, the National Portrait Gallery believes that it probably does depict the writer.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Title_page_William_Shakespeare%27s_First_Folio_1623.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" data-file-width="950" data-file-height="1513" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Title_page_William_Shakespeare%27s_First_Folio_1623.jpg" class="internal" data-file-width="375" data-file-height="400" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Chesterfield_portrait.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The "Chesterfield Portrait" of Shakespeare.</div></div></div>\n<p>It has been claimed that Shakespeare\'s friend <a href="/wiki/Richard_Burbage" title="Richard Burbage">Richard Burbage</a> (1567\xe2\x80\x931619) painted the Chandos portrait,<sup id="cite_ref-mags_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mags-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>  but the first known reference to the painting is in a note written in 1719 by <a href="/wiki/George_Vertue" title="George Vertue">George Vertue</a>, who states that it was painted by <a href="/wiki/John_Taylor_(painter)" title="John Taylor (painter)">John Taylor</a>, a respected member of the <a href="/wiki/Worshipful_Company_of_Painter-Stainers" title="Worshipful Company of Painter-Stainers">Painter-Stainers\' company</a> who may also have been the same John Taylor who acted with the <a href="/wiki/Children_of_Paul%27s" title="Children of Paul&#39;s">Children of Paul\'s</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-cooper54_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cooper54-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Vertue refers to Taylor as an actor and painter and as Shakespeare\'s "intimate friend". <a href="/wiki/Katherine_Duncan-Jones" title="Katherine Duncan-Jones">Katherine Duncan-Jones</a> argues that \'John Taylor\' could have been a misreading of what had originally been "Jo: Taylor"; she suggests that this may refer to the actor <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Taylor_(actor)" title="Joseph Taylor (actor)">Joseph Taylor</a>, who was a prot\xc3\xa9g\xc3\xa9 of the older Shakespeare.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> \n</p><p>Vertue also states that before the Duke of Chandos acquired it, the portrait was owned by Shakespeare\'s possible godson, <a href="/wiki/William_Davenant" title="William Davenant">William Davenant</a> (1606\xe2\x80\x931668),<sup id="cite_ref-mags_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mags-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-cooper54_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cooper54-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> who, according to the gossip chronicler <a href="/wiki/John_Aubrey" title="John Aubrey">John Aubrey</a>, claimed to be the playwright\'s illegitimate son.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> He also states that it was left to Davenant in Taylor\'s will and that it was bought by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Betterton" title="Thomas Betterton">Thomas Betterton</a> from Davenant and then sold to the lawyer Robert Keck, a collector of Shakespeare memorabilia.<sup id="cite_ref-NPG_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NPG-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-mags_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mags-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>After Keck\'s death in 1719, it passed to his daughter, and was inherited by John Nichol, who married into the Keck family. Nichol\'s daughter Margaret married <a href="/wiki/James_Brydges,_3rd_Duke_of_Chandos" title="James Brydges, 3rd Duke of Chandos">James Brydges, 3rd Duke of Chandos</a>. The painting passed through descent within the Chandos title until <a href="/wiki/Richard_Temple-Grenville,_2nd_Duke_of_Buckingham_and_Chandos" class="mw-redirect" title="Richard Temple-Grenville, 2nd Duke of Buckingham and Chandos">Richard Temple-Grenville, 2nd Duke of Buckingham and Chandos</a> sold it to the <a href="/wiki/Francis_Egerton,_1st_Earl_of_Ellesmere" title="Francis Egerton, 1st Earl of Ellesmere">Earl of Ellesmere</a> in 1848. Ellesmere donated it to the National Portrait Gallery.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Scholarly_views">Scholarly views</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chandos_portrait&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Scholarly views">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>A contemporary image of the playwright is the <a href="/wiki/Engraving" title="Engraving">engraving</a> in the posthumously published <a href="/wiki/First_Folio" title="First Folio">First Folio</a> of 1623, which was created by <a href="/wiki/Martin_Droeshout" title="Martin Droeshout">Martin Droeshout</a> and was probably commissioned by Shakespeare\'s friends and family. It is considered likely that the Droeshout engraving is a reasonably accurate likeness because of the use by these close associates and that contemporaries such as <a href="/wiki/Ben_Jonson" title="Ben Jonson">Ben Jonson</a> praised it at the time of the publication.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Since the man in the Chandos portrait resembles the one in the Droeshout engraving, the similarity lends an indirect legitimacy to the oil painting. A further indication of legitimacy is the fact that the Chandos portrait was the inspiration for two posthumous portraits of Shakespeare, one by <a href="/wiki/Gerard_Soest" title="Gerard Soest">Gerard Soest</a> and another, grander one, known as the "<a href="/wiki/File:Chesterfield_portrait.jpg" title="File:Chesterfield portrait.jpg">Chesterfield portrait</a>" after a former owner of that painting.<sup id="cite_ref-NPG_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NPG-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> These were probably painted in the 1660s or 1670s, within living memory of Shakespeare. The Chesterfield portrait is held by the <a href="/wiki/Shakespeare_Birthplace_Trust" title="Shakespeare Birthplace Trust">Shakespeare Birthplace Trust</a> in <a href="/wiki/Stratford-upon-Avon" title="Stratford-upon-Avon">Stratford-upon-Avon</a>.\n</p><p>In 2006, <a href="/wiki/Tarnya_Cooper" title="Tarnya Cooper">Tarnya Cooper</a> of the National Portrait Gallery completed a three-and-a-half-year study of portraits purported to be of Shakespeare and concluded that the Chandos portrait was most likely a representation of Shakespeare. Cooper points to the earring and the loose shirt-ties of the sitter, which were emblematic of poets (the poet <a href="/wiki/John_Donne" title="John Donne">John Donne</a> and Shakespeare\'s patron the <a href="/wiki/Henry_Herbert,_2nd_Earl_of_Pembroke" title="Henry Herbert, 2nd Earl of Pembroke">Earl of Pembroke</a> sported similar fashions). However, she readily acknowledges that the painting\'s authenticity cannot be proven.<sup id="cite_ref-NPG_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NPG-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Cooper also notes that the painting has been badly damaged by over-cleaning and retouching. Parts are abraded and some parts have been slightly altered. The hair has been extended and the beard is longer and more pointed than when originally painted.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Copies">Copies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chandos_portrait&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Copies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Chandos_copy.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Chandos_copy.jpg/170px-Chandos_copy.jpg" width="170" height="225" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Chandos_copy.jpg/255px-Chandos_copy.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Chandos_copy.jpg/340px-Chandos_copy.jpg 2x" data-file-width="483" data-file-height="638" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Chandos_copy.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An early copy of the portrait from c.1670, which may give a clearer idea of the original appearance of the beard</div></div></div>\n<p>In addition to the Chesterfield portrait, a copy was made at least as early as 1689, by an unknown artist. Many 18th century images used it as a model for portrayals of Shakespeare.\n</p><p>The painting was engraved by <a href="/wiki/Gerard_Vandergucht" title="Gerard Vandergucht">Gerard Vandergucht</a> for <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_Rowe_(writer)" title="Nicholas Rowe (writer)">Nicholas Rowe</a>\'s 1709 edition of Shakespeare\'s works. Another print was made by <a href="/wiki/Jacobus_Houbraken" title="Jacobus Houbraken">Jacobus Houbraken</a> in 1747.<sup id="cite_ref-NPG_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NPG-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ethnic_interpretations">Ethnic interpretations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chandos_portrait&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Ethnic interpretations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Because the images of Shakespeare are either doubtful in provenance or lacking expression, no one image seems to reconcile well with readers\' imaginations. The relatively dusky features have caused repeated comment, often of a racist nature. <a href="/wiki/George_Steevens" title="George Steevens">George Steevens</a> said that the picture gave Shakespeare "the complexion of a Jew, or rather that of a chimney sweeper in the jaundice".<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> According to <a href="/wiki/Ben_Macintyre" title="Ben Macintyre">Ben Macintyre</a>, "Some Victorians recoiled at the idea that the Chandos portrait represented Shakespeare. One critic, J. Hain Friswell, insisted \'one cannot readily imagine our essentially English Shakespeare to have been a dark, heavy man, with a foreign expression\'."<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Friswell agreed with Steevens that the portrait had "a decidedly Jewish physiognomy" adding that it displayed "a somewhat lubricious mouth, red-edged eyes" and "wanton lips, with a coarse expression."<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> According to <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Jones" title="Ernest Jones">Ernest Jones</a>, the portrait convinced <a href="/wiki/Sigmund_Freud" title="Sigmund Freud">Sigmund Freud</a> that Shakespeare was French; "He insisted that his countenance could not be that of an Anglo-Saxon but must be French, and he suggested that the name was a corruption of Jacques Pierre."<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The Iraqi writer <a href="/wiki/Safa_Khulusi" title="Safa Khulusi">Safa Khulusi</a> argued that its "un-English" look and "Islamic beard" was evidence for his theory that Shakespeare was an Arab.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> However, assuming the portrait to be genuinely of Shakespeare, there is no real reason to accept the truth of any such claims. Sixteenth and seventeenth-century Englishmen came in a wide variety of shapes, sizes and complexions, reflecting the mixed ancestry of Anglo-Saxon, Celtic, Viking, Norman, Roman, French and many other ethnic sources.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chandos_portrait&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist columns references-column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 30em; -webkit-column-width: 30em; column-width: 30em; list-style-type: decimal;">\n<ol class="references">\n<li id="cite_note-1"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-1">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text"><cite class="citation web"><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.npg.org.uk/collections/search/portrait.php?search=ss&amp;sText=shakespeare&amp;LinkID=mp04051&amp;rNo=0&amp;role=sit">"National Portrait Gallery \xe2\x80\x93 Portrait NPG 1; William Shakespeare"</a>.
