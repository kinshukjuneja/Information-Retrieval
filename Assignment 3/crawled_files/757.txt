id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>\t\t\t\t<div id="contentSub"></div>\n\t\t\t\t<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>\t\t\t\t<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to For the Idlewild album, see <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Fiction:_Best_of_1997%E2%80%932007" title="Scottish Fiction: Best of 1997\xe2\x80\x932007">Scottish Fiction: Best of 1997\xe2\x80\x932007</a>.</div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Three_great_men_of_Scottish_literature.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/Three_great_men_of_Scottish_literature.JPG/300px-Three_great_men_of_Scottish_literature.JPG" width="300" height="225" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/Three_great_men_of_Scottish_literature.JPG/450px-Three_great_men_of_Scottish_literature.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/Three_great_men_of_Scottish_literature.JPG/600px-Three_great_men_of_Scottish_literature.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2560" data-file-height="1920" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Three_great_men_of_Scottish_literature.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Three great men of Scottish literature: busts of Burns, Scott and Stevenson.</div></div></div>\n<p><b>Scottish literature</b> is <a href="/wiki/Literature" title="Literature">literature</a> written in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> or by <a href="/wiki/List_of_Scottish_writers" title="List of Scottish writers">Scottish writers</a>. It includes works in <a href="/wiki/Scottish_English" title="Scottish English">English</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Gaelic_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Scottish Gaelic language">Scottish Gaelic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scots_language" title="Scots language">Scots</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brythonic_languages" class="mw-redirect" title="Brythonic languages">Brythonic</a>, <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>, <a href="/wiki/Latin_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Latin language">Latin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Norn_language" title="Norn language">Norn</a> or other languages written within the modern boundaries of Scotland.\n</p><p>The earliest extant literature written in what is now Scotland, was composed in Brythonic speech in the sixth century and has survived as part of <a href="/wiki/Welsh-language_literature" title="Welsh-language literature">Welsh literature</a>. In the following centuries there was literature in Latin, under the influence of the Catholic Church, and in <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a>, brought by <a href="/wiki/Angles" title="Angles">Anglian</a> settlers. As the state of <a href="/wiki/Alba" title="Alba">Alba</a> developed into the kingdom of Scotland from the eighth century, there was a flourishing literary elite who regularly produced texts in both Gaelic and Latin, sharing a common literary culture with Ireland and elsewhere. After the <a href="/wiki/Davidian_Revolution" title="Davidian Revolution">Davidian Revolution</a> of the thirteenth century a flourishing French language culture predominated, while Norse literature was produced from areas of Scandinavian settlement. The first surviving major text in <a href="/wiki/Early_Scots" title="Early Scots">Early Scots</a> literature is the fourteenth-century poet <a href="/wiki/John_Barbour_(poet)" title="John Barbour (poet)">John Barbour</a>\'s epic <i><a href="/wiki/The_Brus" title="The Brus">Brus</a></i>, which was followed by a series of vernacular versions of medieval romances. These were joined in the fifteenth century by Scots prose works.\n</p><p>In the early modern era royal patronage supported poetry, prose and drama. <a href="/wiki/James_V_of_Scotland" title="James V of Scotland">James V</a>\'s court saw works such as <a href="/wiki/David_Lyndsay" title="David Lyndsay">Sir David Lindsay of the Mount</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/A_Satire_of_the_Three_Estates" title="A Satire of the Three Estates">The Thrie Estaitis</a></i>. In the late sixteenth century <a href="/wiki/James_VI_of_Scotland" class="mw-redirect" title="James VI of Scotland">James VI</a> became patron and member of a circle of Scottish court poets and musicians known as the <a href="/wiki/Castalian_Band" title="Castalian Band">Castalian Band</a>. When he acceded to the English throne in 1603 many followed him to the new court, but without a centre of royal patronage the tradition of Scots poetry subsided. It was revived after union with England in 1707 by figures including <a href="/wiki/Allan_Ramsay_(poet)" title="Allan Ramsay (poet)">Allan Ramsay</a> and <a href="/wiki/James_Macpherson" title="James Macpherson">James Macpherson</a>. The latter\'s <a href="/wiki/Ossian" title="Ossian">Ossian Cycle</a> made him the first Scottish poet to gain an international reputation. He helped inspire <a href="/wiki/Robert_Burns" title="Robert Burns">Robert Burns</a>, considered by many to be the national poet, and <a href="/wiki/Walter_Scott" title="Walter Scott">Walter Scott</a>, whose <a href="/wiki/Waverley_Novels" title="Waverley Novels">Waverley Novels</a> did much to define Scottish identity in the nineteenth century. Towards the end of the Victorian era a number of Scottish-born authors achieved international reputations, including <a href="/wiki/Robert_Louis_Stevenson" title="Robert Louis Stevenson">Robert Louis Stevenson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Conan_Doyle" title="Arthur Conan Doyle">Arthur Conan Doyle</a>, <a href="/wiki/J._M._Barrie" title="J. M. Barrie">J. M. Barrie</a> and <a href="/wiki/George_MacDonald" title="George MacDonald">George MacDonald</a>.\n</p><p>In the twentieth century there was a surge of activity in Scottish literature, known as the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Renaissance" title="Scottish Renaissance">Scottish Renaissance</a>. The leading figure, <a href="/wiki/Hugh_MacDiarmid" title="Hugh MacDiarmid">Hugh MacDiarmid</a>, attempted to revive the Scots language as a medium for serious literature. Members of the movement were followed by a new generation of post-war poets including <a href="/wiki/Edwin_Morgan_(poet)" title="Edwin Morgan (poet)">Edwin Morgan</a>, who would be appointed the first <a href="/wiki/Scots_Makar" class="mw-redirect" title="Scots Makar">Scots Makar</a> by the inaugural Scottish government in 2004. From the 1980s Scottish literature enjoyed another major revival, particularly associated with writers including James Kelman and <a href="/wiki/Irvine_Welsh" title="Irvine Welsh">Irvine Welsh</a>. Scottish poets who emerged in the same period included <a href="/wiki/Carol_Ann_Duffy" title="Carol Ann Duffy">Carol Ann Duffy</a>, who was named as the first Scot to be UK <a href="/wiki/Poet_Laureate" class="mw-redirect" title="Poet Laureate">Poet Laureate</a> in May 2009.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" literature in the Middle Ages">Scottish literature in the Middle Ages</a></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_Middle_Ages">Early Middle Ages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scottish_literature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early Middle Ages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Scotland_in_the_Early_Middle_Ages" title="Scotland in the Early Middle Ages">Scotland in the Early Middle Ages</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Gododdin1.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Gododdin1.jpg/170px-Gododdin1.jpg" width="170" height="229" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Gododdin1.jpg/255px-Gododdin1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Gododdin1.jpg/340px-Gododdin1.jpg 2x" data-file-width="969" data-file-height="1305" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Gododdin1.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A page from the Book of Aneirin shows the first part of the text from the Gododdin c. sixth century.</div></div></div>\n<p>After the collapse of Roman authority in the early fifth century, four major circles of political and cultural influence emerged in Northern Britain. In the east were the <a href="/wiki/Picts" title="Picts">Picts</a>, whose kingdoms eventually stretched from the river Forth to Shetland. In the west were the Gaelic (<a href="/wiki/Goidelic" class="mw-redirect" title="Goidelic">Goidelic</a>)-speaking people of <a href="/wiki/D%C3%A1l_Riata" title="D\xc3\xa1l Riata">D\xc3\xa1l Riata</a>, who had close links with Ireland, from where they brought with them the name Scots. In the south were the British (<a href="/wiki/Britons_(historical)" class="mw-redirect" title="Britons (historical)">Brythonic</a>-speaking) descendants of the peoples of the Roman-influenced kingdoms of "<a href="/wiki/Y_Gogledd_Hen" class="mw-redirect" title="Y Gogledd Hen">The Old North</a>", the most powerful and longest surviving of which was the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Strathclyde" title="Kingdom of Strathclyde">Kingdom of Strathclyde</a>. Finally, there were the English or "Angles", Germanic invaders who had overrun much of southern Britain and held the Kingdom of <a href="/wiki/Bernicia" title="Bernicia">Bernicia</a> (later the northern part of <a href="/wiki/Northumbria" class="mw-redirect" title="Northumbria">Northumbria</a>), which reached into what are now the Borders of Scotland in the south-east.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> To these Christianisation, particularly from the sixth century, added <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> as an intellectual and written language. Modern scholarship, based on surviving placenames and historical evidence, indicates that the <a href="/wiki/Pictish_language" title="Pictish language">Picts</a> spoke a Brythonic language, but none of their literature seems to have survived into the modern era.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> However, there is surviving literature from what would become Scotland in Brythonic, Gaelic, Latin and <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a>.\n</p><p>Much of the earliest <a href="/wiki/Welsh-language_literature" title="Welsh-language literature">Welsh literature</a> was actually composed in or near the country we now call Scotland, in the Brythonic speech, from which <a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">Welsh</a> would be derived, which was not then confined to Wales and Cornwall, although it was only written down in Wales much later. These include <i>The <a href="/wiki/Y_Gododdin" title="Y Gododdin">Gododdin</a></i>, considered the earliest surviving verse from Scotland, which is attributed to the <a href="/wiki/Bard" title="Bard">bard</a> <a href="/wiki/Aneirin" title="Aneirin">Aneirin</a>, said to have been resident in Brythonic kingdom of Gododdin in the sixth century. It is a series of <a href="/wiki/Elegy" title="Elegy">elegies</a> to the men of the Gododdin killed fighting at the <i><a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Catraeth" title="Battle of Catraeth">Battle of Catraeth</a></i> around 600 AD. Similarly, the <i><a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Gwen_Ystrad" class="mw-redirect" title="Battle of Gwen Ystrad">Battle of Gwen Ystrad</a></i> is attributed to <a href="/wiki/Taliesin" title="Taliesin">Taliesin</a>, traditionally thought to be a bard at the court of <a href="/wiki/Rheged" title="Rheged">Rheged</a> in roughly the same period.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>There are religious works in <a href="/wiki/Middle_Irish" title="Middle Irish">Gaelic</a> including the <i>Elegy for <a href="/wiki/St_Columba" class="mw-redirect" title="St Columba">St Columba</a></i> by Dallan Forgaill, c. 597 and "In Praise of St Columba" by Beccan mac Luigdech of Rum, c. 677.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Latin_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Latin language">Latin</a> they include a "Prayer for Protection" (attributed to St Mugint), c. mid-sixth century and <i><a href="/wiki/Hiberno-Latin#Altus_Prosator" title="Hiberno-Latin">Altus Prosator</a></i> ("The High Creator", attributed to St Columba), c. 597.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> What is arguably the most important medieval work written in Scotland, the <i><a href="/wiki/Vita_Columbae" class="mw-redirect" title="Vita Columbae">Vita Columbae</a></i>, by <a href="/wiki/Adomn%C3%A1n" title="Adomn\xc3\xa1n">Adomn\xc3\xa1n</a>, abbot of Iona (627/8\xe2\x80\x93704), was also written in Latin.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The next most important piece of Scottish hagiography, the verse <i><a href="/wiki/Life_of_St._Ninian" class="mw-redirect" title="Life of St. Ninian">Life of St. Ninian</a></i>, was written in Latin in <a href="/wiki/Whithorn" title="Whithorn">Whithorn</a> in the eighth century.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>\nIn Old English there is <i>The <a href="/wiki/Dream_of_the_Rood" title="Dream of the Rood">Dream of the Rood</a></i>, from which lines are found on the <a href="/wiki/Ruthwell_Cross" title="Ruthwell Cross">Ruthwell Cross</a>, making it the only surviving fragment of <a href="/wiki/Northumbrian_(Anglo-Saxon)" class="mw-redirect" title="Northumbrian (Anglo-Saxon)">Northumbrian</a> Old English from early Medieval Scotland.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> It has also been suggested on the basis of ornithological references that the poem <i><a href="/wiki/The_Seafarer_(poem)" title="The Seafarer (poem)">The Seafarer</a></i> was composed somewhere near the <a href="/wiki/Bass_Rock" title="Bass Rock">Bass Rock</a> in East Lothian.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> </p><div style="clear:both;"></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="High_Middle_Ages">High Middle Ages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scottish_literature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: High Middle Ages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Scotland_in_the_High_Middle_Ages" title="Scotland in the High Middle Ages">Scotland in the High Middle Ages</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:BookDeerMattCh1vv18_21Fol05r.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/BookDeerMattCh1vv18_21Fol05r.jpg/170px-BookDeerMattCh1vv18_21Fol05r.jpg" width="170" height="238" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/BookDeerMattCh1vv18_21Fol05r.jpg/255px-BookDeerMattCh1vv18_21Fol05r.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/BookDeerMattCh1vv18_21Fol05r.jpg/340px-BookDeerMattCh1vv18_21Fol05r.jpg 2x" data-file-width="453" data-file-height="633" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:BookDeerMattCh1vv18_21Fol05r.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Book_of_Deer" title="Book of Deer">Book of Deer</a>, Folio 5r contains the text of the <a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_Matthew" title="Gospel of Matthew">Gospel of Matthew</a> from 1:18 through 1:21.</div></div></div>\n<p>Beginning in the later eighth century, <a href="/wiki/Viking" class="mw-redirect" title="Viking">Viking</a> raids and invasions may have forced a merger of the Gaelic and Pictish crowns that culminated in the rise of <a href="/wiki/Kenneth_I_of_Scotland" class="mw-redirect" title="Kenneth I of Scotland">C\xc3\xadnaed mac Ailp\xc3\xadn</a> (Kenneth MacAlpin) in the 840s, which brought to power the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Alpin" title="House of Alpin">House of Alpin</a> and the creation of the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Alba" title="Kingdom of Alba">Kingdom of Alba</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Yorke2006p54_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Yorke2006p54-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Historical sources, as well as place name evidence, indicate the ways in which the Pictish language in the north and Cumbric languages in the south were overlaid and replaced by Gaelic, Old English and later <a href="/wiki/Norse_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Norse language">Norse</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The Kingdom of Alba was overwhelmingly an oral society dominated by Gaelic culture. Our fuller sources for Ireland of the same period suggest that there would have been <a href="/wiki/Filidh" class="mw-redirect" title="Filidh">filidh</a>, who acted as poets, musicians and historians, often attached to the court of a lord or king, and passed on their knowledge and culture in Gaelic to the next generation.<sup id="cite_ref-Crawford2009_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Crawford2009-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Houston2002p76_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Houston2002p76-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>From the eleventh century <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>, <a href="/wiki/Flemish" title="Flemish">Flemish</a> and particularly English became the main languages of Scottish <a href="/wiki/Burgh" title="Burgh">burghs</a>, most of which were located in the south and east.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> At least from the accession of <a href="/wiki/David_I_of_Scotland" title="David I of Scotland">David I</a> (r. 1124\xe2\x80\x9353), as part of a <a href="/wiki/Davidian_Revolution" title="Davidian Revolution">Davidian Revolution</a> that introduced French culture and political systems, Gaelic ceased to be the main language of the royal court and was probably replaced by French. After this "de-gallicisation" of the Scottish court, a less highly regarded order of bards took over the functions of the filidh and they would continue to act in a similar role in the Highlands and Islands into the eighteenth century. They often trained in bardic schools, of which a few, like the one run by the <a href="/wiki/MacMhuirich_bardic_family" title="MacMhuirich bardic family">MacMhuirich</a> dynasty, who were bards to the <a href="/wiki/Lord_of_the_Isles" title="Lord of the Isles">Lord of the Isles</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> existed in Scotland and a larger number in Ireland, until they were suppressed from the seventeenth century.<sup id="cite_ref-Houston2002p76_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Houston2002p76-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Members of bardic schools were trained in the complex rules and forms of Gaelic poetry.<sup id="cite_ref-Wormald1991pp60-7_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wormald1991pp60-7-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Much of their work was never written down and what survives was only recorded from the sixteenth century.<sup id="cite_ref-Crawford2009_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Crawford2009-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>It is possible that more Middle Irish literature was written in medieval Scotland than is often thought, but has not survived because the Gaelic literary establishment of eastern Scotland died out before the fourteenth century. Thomas Owen Clancy has argued that the <i><a href="/wiki/Lebor_Bretnach" title="Lebor Bretnach">Lebor Bretnach</a></i>, the so-called "Irish Nennius", was written in Scotland, and probably at the monastery in Abernethy, but this text survives only from manuscripts preserved in Ireland.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Other literary work that has survived includes that of the prolific poet <a href="/wiki/Gille_Brighde_Albanach" class="mw-redirect" title="Gille Brighde Albanach">Gille Brighde Albanach</a>. About 1218, Gille Brighde wrote a poem\xe2\x80\x94<i>Heading for Damietta</i>\xe2\x80\x94on his experiences of the <a href="/wiki/Fifth_Crusade" title="Fifth Crusade">Fifth Crusade</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>\nIn the thirteenth century, <a href="/wiki/Old_French_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Old French language">French</a> flourished as a <a href="/wiki/Literary_language" title="Literary language">literary language</a>, and produced the <i><a href="/wiki/Roman_de_Fergus" title="Roman de Fergus">Roman de Fergus</a></i>, the earliest piece of non-Celtic <a href="/wiki/Vernacular" title="Vernacular">vernacular</a> literature to survive from Scotland.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Many other stories in the <a href="/wiki/Arthurian_Cycle" class="mw-redirect" title="Arthurian Cycle">Arthurian Cycle</a>, written in French and preserved only outside Scotland, are thought by some scholars including D. D. R. Owen, to have been written in Scotland. There is some Norse literature from areas of Scandinavian settlement, such as the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Isles" title="Northern Isles">Northern Isles</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Western_Isles" class="mw-redirect" title="Western Isles">Western Isles</a>. The famous <i><a href="/wiki/Orkneyinga_Saga" class="mw-redirect" title="Orkneyinga Saga">Orkneyinga Saga</a></i> however, although it pertains to the <a href="/wiki/Earldom_of_Orkney" title="Earldom of Orkney">Earldom of Orkney</a>, was written in <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> In addition to French, Latin too was a literary language, with works that include the "Carmen de morte Sumerledi", a poem which exults triumphantly the victory of the citizens of Glasgow over <a href="/wiki/Somerled" title="Somerled">Somairle mac Gilla Brigte</a><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> and the "Inchcolm Antiphoner", a hymn in praise of St. Columba.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup></p><div style="clear:both;"></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Late_Middle_Ages">Late Middle Ages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scottish_literature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Late Middle Ages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Scotland_in_the_Late_Middle_Ages" title="Scotland in the Late Middle Ages">Scotland in the Late Middle Ages</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:King_James_I_of_Scotland.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/King_James_I_of_Scotland.jpg/170px-King_James_I_of_Scotland.jpg" width="170" height="211" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/King_James_I_of_Scotland.jpg/255px-King_James_I_of_Scotland.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/King_James_I_of_Scotland.jpg/340px-King_James_I_of_Scotland.jpg 2x" data-file-width="540" data-file-height="669" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:King_James_I_of_Scotland.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/James_I_of_Scotland" title="James I of Scotland">James I</a>, who spent much of his life imprisoned in England, where he gained a reputation as a musician and poet.</div></div></div>\n<p>In the late Middle Ages, <a href="/wiki/Middle_Scots" title="Middle Scots">Middle Scots</a>, often simply called English, became the dominant language of the country. It was derived largely from Old English, with the addition of elements from Gaelic and French. Although resembling the language spoken in northern England, it became a distinct dialect from the late fourteenth century onwards.<sup id="cite_ref-Wormald1991pp60-7_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wormald1991pp60-7-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> It began to be adopted by the ruling elite as they gradually abandoned French. By the fifteenth century it was the language of government, with acts of parliament, council records and treasurer\'s accounts almost all using it from the reign of James I onwards. As a result, Gaelic, once dominant north of the <a href="/wiki/River_Tay" title="River Tay">River Tay</a>, began a steady decline.<sup id="cite_ref-Wormald1991pp60-7_16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wormald1991pp60-7-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Lowland writers began to treat Gaelic as a second class, rustic and even amusing language, helping to frame attitudes towards the highlands and to create a cultural gulf with the lowlands.<sup id="cite_ref-Wormald1991pp60-7_16-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wormald1991pp60-7-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The first surviving major text in Scots literature is <a href="/wiki/John_Barbour_(poet)" title="John Barbour (poet)">John Barbour</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Brus" title="The Brus">Brus</a></i> (1375), composed under the patronage of Robert II and telling the story in epic poetry of Robert I\'s actions before the English invasion till the end of the war of independence.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> The work was extremely popular among the Scots-speaking aristocracy and Barbour is referred to as the father of Scots poetry, holding a similar place to his contemporary <a href="/wiki/Chaucer" class="mw-redirect" title="Chaucer">Chaucer</a> in England.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> In the early fifteenth century these were followed by <a href="/wiki/Andrew_of_Wyntoun" title="Andrew of Wyntoun">Andrew of Wyntoun</a>\'s verse <i>Orygynale Cronykil of Scotland</i> and <a href="/wiki/Blind_Harry" title="Blind Harry">Blind Harry</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Actes_and_Deidis_of_the_Illustre_and_Vallyeant_Campioun_Schir_William_Wallace" class="mw-redirect" title="The Actes and Deidis of the Illustre and Vallyeant Campioun Schir William Wallace">The Wallace</a></i>, which blended <a href="/wiki/Romance_(heroic_literature)" class="mw-redirect" title="Romance (heroic literature)">historical romance</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Chronicle" title="Chronicle">verse chronicle</a>. They were probably influenced by Scots versions of popular French romances that were also produced in the period, including <i><a href="/wiki/The_Buik_of_Alexander" title="The Buik of Alexander">The Buik of Alexander</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Lancelot" title="Lancelot">Launcelot o the Laik</a></i> and <i>The Porteous of Noblenes</i> by <a href="/wiki/Gilbert_Hay_(poet)" title="Gilbert Hay (poet)">Gilbert Hay</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Wormald1991pp60-7_16-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wormald1991pp60-7-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Much Middle Scots literature was produced by <a href="/wiki/Makars" class="mw-redirect" title="Makars">makars</a>, poets with links to the royal court, which included <a href="/wiki/James_I_of_Scotland" title="James I of Scotland">James I</a> (who wrote <i><a href="/wiki/The_Kingis_Quair" title="The Kingis Quair">The Kingis Quair</a></i>). Many of the makars had university education and so were also connected with the <a href="/wiki/Ecclesia_(church)" class="mw-redirect" title="Ecclesia (church)">Kirk</a>. However, Dunbar\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Lament_for_the_Makaris" title="Lament for the Makaris">Lament for the Makaris</a></i> (c.1505) provides evidence of a wider tradition of secular writing outside of Court and Kirk now largely lost.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Before the advent of printing in Scotland, writers such as <a href="/wiki/Robert_Henryson" title="Robert Henryson">Robert Henryson</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_Dunbar" title="William Dunbar">William Dunbar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Walter_Kennedy_(poet)" title="Walter Kennedy (poet)">Walter Kennedy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gavin_Douglas" title="Gavin Douglas">Gavin Douglas</a> have been seen as leading a golden age in <a href="/wiki/Poetry_of_Scotland" title="Poetry of Scotland">Scottish poetry</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Wormald1991pp60-7_16-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wormald1991pp60-7-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the late fifteenth century, Scots prose also began to develop as a genre. Although there are earlier fragments of original Scots prose, such as the <i>Auchinleck Chronicle</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> the first complete surviving work includes <a href="/wiki/John_Ireland_(theologian)" title="John Ireland (theologian)">John Ireland</a>\'s <i>The Meroure of Wyssdome</i> (1490).<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> There were also prose translations of French books of chivalry that survive from the 1450s, including <i>The Book of the Law of Armys</i> and the <i>Order of Knychthode</i> and the treatise <i><a href="/wiki/Secretum_Secretorum" title="Secretum Secretorum">Secreta Secretorum</a></i>, an Arabic work believed to be Aristotle\'s advice to <a href="/wiki/Alexander_the_Great" title="Alexander the Great">Alexander the Great</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Wormald1991pp60-7_16-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wormald1991pp60-7-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> The establishment of a printing press under royal patent in 1507 would begin to make it easier to disseminate Scottish literature and was probably aimed at bolstering Scottish national identity.<sup id="cite_ref-Bawcutt&amp;Williams2006pp26-9_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bawcutt&amp;Williams2006pp26-9-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> The first Scottish press was established in <a href="/wiki/Cowgate" title="Cowgate">Southgait</a> in Edinburgh by the merchant <a href="/wiki/Walter_Chepman" title="Walter Chepman">Walter Chepman</a> (c. 1473\xe2\x80\x93c. 1528) and the bookseller <a href="/wiki/Androw_Myllar" title="Androw Myllar">Andrew Myllar</a> (f. 1505\xe2\x80\x9308). Although the first press was relatively short lived, beside law codes and religious works, the press also produced editions of the work of Scottish makars before its demise, probably about 1510. The next recorded press was that of <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Davidson_(printer)" title="Thomas Davidson (printer)">Thomas Davidson</a> (f. 1532\xe2\x80\x9342), the first in a long line of "king\'s printers", who also produced editions of works of the makars.<sup id="cite_ref-MacQuarrie2001pp491-3_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MacQuarrie2001pp491-3-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> The landmark work in the reign of <a href="/wiki/James_IV_of_Scotland" title="James IV of Scotland">James IV</a> was Gavin Douglas\'s version of <a href="/wiki/Virgil" title="Virgil">Virgil</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Aeneid" title="Aeneid">Aeneid</a></i>, the <i><a href="/wiki/Eneados" title="Eneados">Eneados</a></i>, which was the first complete translation of a major classical text in an <a href="/wiki/English_languages" class="mw-redirect" title="English languages">Anglic</a> language, finished in 1513, but overshadowed by the disaster at Flodden.<sup id="cite_ref-Wormald1991pp60-7_16-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wormald1991pp60-7-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_modern_era">Early modern era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scottish_literature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Early modern era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Literature_in_early_modern_Scotland" title="Literature in early modern Scotland">Literature in early modern Scotland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Renaissance_in_Scotland" title="Renaissance in Scotland">Renaissance in Scotland</a></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sixteenth_century">Sixteenth century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scottish_literature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Sixteenth century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:William_Alexander,_1st_Earl_of_Stirling_-_Project_Gutenberg_etext_20110.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/William_Alexander%2C_1st_Earl_of_Stirling_-_Project_Gutenberg_etext_20110.jpg/170px-William_Alexander%2C_1st_Earl_of_Stirling_-_Project_Gutenberg_etext_20110.jpg" width="170" height="225" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/68/William_Alexander%2C_1st_Earl_of_Stirling_-_Project_Gutenberg_etext_20110.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="230" data-file-height="305" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:William_Alexander,_1st_Earl_of_Stirling_-_Project_Gutenberg_etext_20110.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Sir_William_Alexander,_Earl_of_Stirling" class="mw-redirect" title="Sir William Alexander, Earl of Stirling">William Alexander</a>, statesman and author.</div></div></div>\n<p>As a patron of poets and authors <a href="/wiki/James_V_of_Scotland" title="James V of Scotland">James V</a> (r. 1513\xe2\x80\x9342) supported William Stewart and <a href="/wiki/John_Bellenden" title="John Bellenden">John Bellenden</a>, who translated the Latin <i>History of Scotland</i> compiled in 1527 by <a href="/wiki/Hector_Boece" title="Hector Boece">Hector Boece</a>, into verse and prose.<sup id="cite_ref-Brownetalpp256-7_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brownetalpp256-7-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/David_Lyndsay" title="David Lyndsay">David Lyndsay</a> (c. 1486\xe2\x80\x931555), diplomat and the head of the <a href="/wiki/Lyon_Court" class="mw-redirect" title="Lyon Court">Lyon Court</a>, was a prolific poet. He wrote elegiac narratives, romances and satires.<sup id="cite_ref-vanHeijnsbergen2001pp129-30_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vanHeijnsbergen2001pp129-30-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/George_Buchanan" title="George Buchanan">George Buchanan</a> (1506\xe2\x80\x9382) had a major influence as a Latin poet, founding a tradition of neo-Latin poetry that would continue in to the seventeenth century.<sup id="cite_ref-Mason2001pp120-3_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mason2001pp120-3-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> Contributors to this tradition included royal secretary <a href="/wiki/John_Maitland,_1st_Lord_Maitland_of_Thirlestane" title="John Maitland, 1st Lord Maitland of Thirlestane">John Maitland</a> (1537\xe2\x80\x9395), reformer <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Melville" title="Andrew Melville">Andrew Melville</a> (1545\xe2\x80\x931622), <a href="/wiki/John_Johnston_(poet)" title="John Johnston (poet)">John Johnston</a> (1570?\xe2\x80\x931611) and <a href="/wiki/David_Hume_of_Godscroft" title="David Hume of Godscroft">David Hume of Godscroft</a> (1558\xe2\x80\x931629).<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>From the 1550s, in the reign of <a href="/wiki/Mary,_Queen_of_Scots" title="Mary, Queen of Scots">Mary, Queen of Scots</a> (r. 1542\xe2\x80\x9367) and the minority of her son <a href="/wiki/James_VI_of_Scotland" class="mw-redirect" title="James VI of Scotland">James VI</a> (r. 1567\xe2\x80\x931625), cultural pursuits were limited by the lack of a royal court and by political turmoil. The Kirk, heavily influenced by <a href="/wiki/Calvinism" title="Calvinism">Calvinism</a>, also discouraged poetry that was not devotional in nature. Nevertheless, poets from this period included <a href="/wiki/Richard_Maitland" title="Richard Maitland">Richard Maitland</a> of Lethington (1496\xe2\x80\x931586), who produced meditative and satirical verses in the style of Dunbar; <a href="/wiki/John_Rolland" title="John Rolland">John Rolland</a> (fl. 1530\xe2\x80\x9375), who wrote allegorical satires in the tradition of Douglas and courtier and minister <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Hume" title="Alexander Hume">Alexander Hume</a> (c. 1556\xe2\x80\x931609), whose corpus of work includes nature poetry and <a href="/wiki/Epistolary_poem" title="Epistolary poem">epistolary verse</a>. <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Scott_(16th-century_poet)" title="Alexander Scott (16th-century poet)">Alexander Scott</a>\'s (?1520\xe2\x80\x9382/3) use of short verse designed to be sung to music, opened the way for the Castilan poets of James VI\'s adult reign.<sup id="cite_ref-vanHeijnsbergen2001pp129-30_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vanHeijnsbergen2001pp129-30-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the 1580s and 1590s James VI strongly promoted the literature of the country of his birth in Scots. His treatise, <i><a href="/wiki/Reulis_and_Cautelis" class="mw-redirect" title="Reulis and Cautelis">Some Rules and Cautions to be Observed and Eschewed in Scottish Prosody</a></i>, published in 1584 when he was aged 18, was both a poetic manual and a description of the poetic tradition in his mother tongue, to which he applied Renaissance principles.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> He became patron and member of a loose circle of Scottish <a href="/wiki/Jacobean_era" title="Jacobean era">Jacobean</a> court poets and musicians, later called the <a href="/wiki/Castalian_Band" title="Castalian Band">Castalian Band</a>, which included <a href="/wiki/William_Fowler_(makar)" title="William Fowler (makar)">William Fowler</a> (c. 1560\xe2\x80\x931612), <a href="/wiki/John_Stewart_of_Baldynneis" title="John Stewart of Baldynneis">John Stewart of Baldynneis</a> (c. 1545\xe2\x80\x93c. 1605), and <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Montgomerie" title="Alexander Montgomerie">Alexander Montgomerie</a> (c. 1550\xe2\x80\x9398).<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> They translated key Renaissance texts and produced poems using French forms, including <a href="/wiki/Sonnet" title="Sonnet">sonnets</a> and short sonnets, for narrative, nature description, satire and meditations on love. Later poets that followed in this vein included <a href="/wiki/William_Alexander,_1st_Earl_of_Stirling" title="William Alexander, 1st Earl of Stirling">William Alexander</a> (c. 1567\xe2\x80\x931640), Alexander Craig (c. 1567\xe2\x80\x931627) and <a href="/wiki/Robert_Aytoun" title="Robert Aytoun">Robert Ayton</a> (1570\xe2\x80\x931627).<sup id="cite_ref-vanHeijnsbergen2001pp129-30_31-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vanHeijnsbergen2001pp129-30-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> By the late 1590s the king\'s championing of his native Scottish tradition was to some extent diffused by the prospect of inheriting of the English throne.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Lyndsay produced an interlude at <a href="/wiki/Linlithgow_Palace" title="Linlithgow Palace">Linlithgow Palace</a> for the king and queen thought to be a version of his play <i><a href="/wiki/A_Satire_of_the_Three_Estates" title="A Satire of the Three Estates">The Thrie Estaitis</a></i> in 1540, which satirised the corruption of church and state, and which is the only complete play to survive from before the Reformation.<sup id="cite_ref-Brownetalpp256-7_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brownetalpp256-7-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> Buchanan was major influence on Continental theatre with plays such as <i>Jepheths</i> and <i>Baptistes</i>, which influenced <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Corneille" title="Pierre Corneille">Pierre Corneille</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jean_Racine" title="Jean Racine">Jean Racine</a> and through them the neo-classical tradition in French drama, but his impact in Scotland was limited by his choice of Latin as a medium.<sup id="cite_ref-Brown2011pp1-3_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown2011pp1-3-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> The anonymous <i>The Maner of the Cyring of ane Play</i> (before 1568)<sup id="cite_ref-vanHeijnsbergen2001pp127-8_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vanHeijnsbergen2001pp127-8-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> and <i>Philotus</i> (published in London in 1603), are isolated examples of surviving plays. The latter is a vernacular Scots comedy of errors, probably designed for court performance for Mary, Queen of Scots or James VI.<sup id="cite_ref-Carpenter2011p15_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carpenter2011p15-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> The same system of professional companies of players and theatres that developed in England in this period was absent in Scotland, but James VI signalled his interest in drama by arranging for a company of English players to erect a playhouse and perform in 1599.<sup id="cite_ref-Carpenter2011p21_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carpenter2011p21-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Seventeenth_century">Seventeenth century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scottish_literature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Seventeenth century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Assembly1766.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/Assembly1766.jpg/170px-Assembly1766.jpg" width="170" height="303" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/Assembly1766.jpg/255px-Assembly1766.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/Assembly1766.jpg/340px-Assembly1766.jpg 2x" data-file-width="346" data-file-height="617" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Assembly1766.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The first page of <i>The Assembly</i> by Archibald Pitcairne from the 1766 edition</div></div></div>\n<p>Having extolled the virtues of Scots "poesie", following his accession to the English throne, James VI increasingly favoured the language of southern England. In 1611 the Kirk adopted the English <a href="/wiki/Authorised_King_James_Version" class="mw-redirect" title="Authorised King James Version">Authorised King James Version</a> of the Bible. In 1617 interpreters were declared no longer necessary in the port of London because Scots and Englishmen were now "not so far different bot ane understandeth ane uther". Jenny Wormald, describes James as creating a "three-tier system, with Gaelic at the bottom and English at the top".<sup id="cite_ref-Wormald1991pp192-3_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wormald1991pp192-3-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> The loss of the court as a centre of patronage in 1603 was a major blow to Scottish literature. A number of Scottish poets, including William Alexander, John Murray and Robert Aytoun accompanied the king to London, where they continued to write,<sup id="cite_ref-Brown2003pp253-4_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown2003pp253-4-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> but they soon began to <a href="/wiki/Anglicise" class="mw-redirect" title="Anglicise">anglicise</a> their written language.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> James\'s characteristic role as active literary participant and patron in the English court made him a defining figure for English Renaissance poetry and drama, which would reach a pinnacle of achievement in his reign,<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> but his patronage for the <a href="/wiki/Stylistics_(linguistics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Stylistics (linguistics)">high style</a> in his own Scottish tradition largely became sidelined.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> The only significant court poet to continue to work in Scotland after the king\'s departure was <a href="/wiki/William_Drummond_of_Hawthornden" title="William Drummond of Hawthornden">William Drummond of Hawthornden</a> (1585\xe2\x80\x931649).<sup id="cite_ref-vanHeijnsbergen2001pp127-8_38-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vanHeijnsbergen2001pp127-8-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>As the tradition of classical Gaelic poetry declined, a new tradition of vernacular Gaelic poetry began to emerge. While Classical poetry used a language largely fixed in the twelfth century, the vernacular continued to develop. In contrast to the Classical tradition, which used <a href="/wiki/Syllabic_verse" title="Syllabic verse">syllabic metre</a>, vernacular poets tended to use <a href="/wiki/Accentual_verse" title="Accentual verse">stressed metre</a>. However, they shared with the Classic poets a set of complex metaphors and role, as the verse was still often panegyric. A number of these vernacular poets were women,<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> such as Mary MacLeod of Harris (c. 1615\xe2\x80\x931707).<sup id="cite_ref-MacDonald2001pp255-7_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MacDonald2001pp255-7-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The tradition of neo-Latin poetry reached its fruition with the publication of the anthology of the <i>Deliciae Poetarum Scotorum</i> (1637), published in Amsterdam by <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Johnston_(poet)" title="Arthur Johnston (poet)">Arthur Johnston</a> (c. 1579\xe2\x80\x931641) and <a href="/wiki/Sir_John_Scott_of_Scotstarvet" class="mw-redirect" title="Sir John Scott of Scotstarvet">Sir John Scott of Scotstarvet</a> (1585\xe2\x80\x931670) and containing work by the major Scottish practitioners since Buchanan.<sup id="cite_ref-Mason2001pp120-3_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mason2001pp120-3-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> This period was marked by the work of the first named female Scottish poets.<sup id="cite_ref-vanHeijnsbergen2001pp127-8_38-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vanHeijnsbergen2001pp127-8-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Melville" title="Elizabeth Melville">Elizabeth Melville</a>\'s (f. 1585\xe2\x80\x931630) <i>Ane Godlie Dream</i> (1603) was a popular religious allegory and the first book published by a woman in Scotland.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Anna_Hume" title="Anna Hume">Anna Hume</a>, daughter of David Hume of Godscroft, adapted <a href="/wiki/Petrarch" title="Petrarch">Petrarch</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Triumphs" title="Triumphs">Triumphs</a></i> as <i>Triumphs of Love: Chastitie: Death</i> (1644).<sup id="cite_ref-vanHeijnsbergen2001pp127-8_38-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vanHeijnsbergen2001pp127-8-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>This was the period when the <a href="/wiki/Ballad" title="Ballad">ballad</a> emerged as a significant written form in Scotland. Some ballads may date back to the late medieval era and deal with events and people that can be traced back as far as the thirteenth century, including "<a href="/wiki/Sir_Patrick_Spens" title="Sir Patrick Spens">Sir Patrick Spens</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Thomas_the_Rhymer" title="Thomas the Rhymer">Thomas the Rhymer</a>", but which are not known to have existed until the eighteenth century.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> They were probably composed and transmitted orally and only began to be written down and printed, often as <a href="/wiki/Broadside_ballad" title="Broadside ballad">broadsides</a> and as part of <a href="/wiki/Chapbooks" class="mw-redirect" title="Chapbooks">chapbooks</a>, later being recorded and noted in books by collectors including <a href="/wiki/Robert_Burns" title="Robert Burns">Robert Burns</a> and <a href="/wiki/Walter_Scott" title="Walter Scott">Walter Scott</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> From the seventeenth century they were used as a literary form by aristocratic authors including <a href="/wiki/Robert_Sempill_the_younger" title="Robert Sempill the younger">Robert Sempill</a> (c. 1595\xe2\x80\x93c. 1665), <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth,_Lady_Wardlaw" title="Elizabeth, Lady Wardlaw">Lady Elizabeth Wardlaw</a> (1627\xe2\x80\x931727) and <a href="/wiki/Grizel_Baillie" class="mw-redirect" title="Grizel Baillie">Lady Grizel Baillie</a> (1645\xe2\x80\x931746).<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The loss of a royal court also meant there was no force to counter the kirk\'s dislike of theatre, which struggled to survive in Scotland.<sup id="cite_ref-Brown2003pp253-4_42-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown2003pp253-4-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> However, it was not entirely extinguished. The kirk used theatre for its own purposes in schools and was slow to suppress popular <a href="/wiki/Folk_drama" class="mw-redirect" title="Folk drama">folk dramas</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Brown2011pp1-3_37-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown2011pp1-3-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> Surviving plays for the period include William Alexander\'s <i>Monarchicke Tragedies</i>, written just before his departure with the king for England in 1603. They were <a href="/wiki/Closet_drama" title="Closet drama">closet dramas</a>, designed to be read rather than performed, and already indicate Alexander\'s preference for southern English over the Scots language.<sup id="cite_ref-Carpenter2011p15_39-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carpenter2011p15-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> There were some attempts to revive Scottish drama. In 1663 Edinburgh lawyer William Clerke wrote <i>Marciano or the Discovery</i>, a play about the restoration of a legitimate dynasty in Florence after many years of civil war. It was performed at the Tennis-Court Theatre at <a href="/wiki/Holyrood_Palace" title="Holyrood Palace">Holyrood Palace</a> before the parliamentary high commissioner <a href="/wiki/John_Leslie,_1st_Duke_of_Rothes" title="John Leslie, 1st Duke of Rothes">John Leslie, Earl of Rothes</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> Thomas Sydsurf\'s <i>Tarugo\'s Wiles or the Coffee House</i>, was first performed in London in 1667 and then in Edinburgh the year after and drew on <a href="/wiki/Comedia_(Spanish_play)" title="Comedia (Spanish play)">Spanish comedy</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> A relative of Sydsurf, physician <a href="/wiki/Archibald_Pitcairne" title="Archibald Pitcairne">Archibald Pitcairne</a> (1652\xe2\x80\x931713) wrote <i>The Assembly or Scotch Reformation</i> (1692), a ribald satire on the morals of the Presbyterian kirk, circulating in manuscript, but not published until 1722, helping to secure the association between <a href="/wiki/Jacobitism" title="Jacobitism">Jacobitism</a> and professional drama that discouraged the creation of professional theatre.<sup id="cite_ref-Brown2011bpp28-9_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown2011bpp28-9-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Eighteenth_century">Eighteenth century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scottish_literature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Eighteenth century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Scottish_literature_in_the_eighteenth_century" title="Scottish literature in the eighteenth century">Scottish literature in the eighteenth century</a> and <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Enlightenment" title="Scottish Enlightenment">Scottish Enlightenment</a></div>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Scotland_in_the_modern_era" title="Scotland in the modern era">Scotland in the modern era</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Allan-Ramsay.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/Allan-Ramsay.jpg/170px-Allan-Ramsay.jpg" width="170" height="203" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/Allan-Ramsay.jpg/255px-Allan-Ramsay.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/Allan-Ramsay.jpg/340px-Allan-Ramsay.jpg 2x" data-file-width="355" data-file-height="424" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Allan-Ramsay.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Allan_Ramsay_(poet)" title="Allan Ramsay (poet)">Alan Ramsay</a>, the most influential literary figure in early eighteenth-century Scotland</div></div></div>\n<p>After the Union in 1707 and the shift of political power to England, the use of Scots was discouraged by many in authority and education.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> Nevertheless, Scots remained the vernacular of many rural communities and the growing number of urban working-class Scots.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> Literature developed a distinct national identity and began to enjoy an international reputation. <a href="/wiki/Allan_Ramsay_(poet)" title="Allan Ramsay (poet)">Allan Ramsay</a> (1686\xe2\x80\x931758) was the most important literary figure of the era, often described as leading a "vernacular revival". He laid the foundations of a reawakening of interest in older Scottish literature, publishing <i>The Ever Green</i> (1724), a collection that included many major poetic works of the Stewart period.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup> He led the trend for <a href="/wiki/Pastoral" title="Pastoral">pastoral</a> poetry, helping to develop the <a href="/wiki/Habbie_stanza" class="mw-redirect" title="Habbie stanza">Habbie stanza</a>, which would be later be used by Robert Burns as a <a href="/wiki/Poetic_form" class="mw-redirect" title="Poetic form">poetic form</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup> His <i>Tea-Table Miscellany</i> (1724\xe2\x80\x9337) contained poems old Scots folk material, his own poems in the folk style and "gentilizings" of Scots poems in the English neo-classical style.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup> His pastoral opera <i><a href="/wiki/The_Gentle_Shepherd" title="The Gentle Shepherd">The Gentle Shepherd</a></i> was one of the most influential works of the era.<sup id="cite_ref-Brown2011bpp28-9_54-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown2011bpp28-9-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> He would also play a leading role in supporting drama in Scotland and the attempt to found a permanent theatre in the capital.<sup id="cite_ref-Bell2007p288_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bell2007p288-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Ramsay was part of a community of poets working in Scots and English. These included <a href="/wiki/William_Hamilton_(comic_poet)" title="William Hamilton (comic poet)">William Hamilton of Gilbertfield</a> (c. 1665\xe2\x80\x931751), Robert Crawford (1695\xe2\x80\x931733), <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Ross_(poet)" title="Alexander Ross (poet)">Alexander Ross</a> (1699\xe2\x80\x931784), the Jacobite <a href="/wiki/William_Hamilton_(Jacobite_poet)" title="William Hamilton (Jacobite poet)">William Hamilton</a> of Bangour (1704\xe2\x80\x9354), socialite <a href="/wiki/Alison_Rutherford" class="mw-redirect" title="Alison Rutherford">Alison Rutherford Cockburn</a> (1712\xe2\x80\x9394), and poet and playwright <a href="/wiki/James_Thompson_(poet)" class="mw-redirect" title="James Thompson (poet)">James Thompson</a>\'s (1700\xe2\x80\x9348), most famous for the nature poetry of his <i>Seasons</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Tobias_Smollett" title="Tobias Smollett">Tobias Smollett</a> (1721\xe2\x80\x9371) was a poet, essayist, satirist and playwright, but is best known for his <a href="/wiki/Picaresque_novel" title="Picaresque novel">picaresque novels</a>, such as <i><a href="/wiki/The_Adventures_of_Roderick_Random" title="The Adventures of Roderick Random">The Adventures of Roderick Random</a></i> (1748) and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Adventures_of_Peregrine_Pickle" title="The Adventures of Peregrine Pickle">The Adventures of Peregrine Pickle</a></i> (1751) for which he is often seen as Scotland\'s first novelist.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> His work would be a major influence on later novelists such as <a href="/wiki/Thackeray" class="mw-redirect" title="Thackeray">Thackeray</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dickens" class="mw-redirect" title="Dickens">Dickens</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The early eighteenth century was also a period of innovation in Gaelic vernacular poetry. Major figures included <a href="/wiki/Rob_Donn" title="Rob Donn">Rob Donn Mackay</a> (1714\xe2\x80\x9378) and <a href="/wiki/Donnchadh_B%C3%A0n_Mac_an_t-Saoir" class="mw-redirect" title="Donnchadh B\xc3\xa0n Mac an t-Saoir">Donnchadh B\xc3\xa0n Mac an t-Saoir</a> (Duncan Ban MacIntyre) (1724\xe2\x80\x931812). The most significant figure in the tradition was <a href="/wiki/Alasdair_mac_Mhaighstir_Alasdair" title="Alasdair mac Mhaighstir Alasdair">Alasdair mac Mhaighstir Alasdair</a> (Alasdair MacDonald) (c. 1698\xe2\x80\x931770). His interest in traditional forms can be seen in his most significant poem <i>Clanranald\'s Gallery</i>. He also mixed these traditions with influences from the Lowlands, including Thompson\'s <i>Seasons</i>, which helped inspire a new form of nature poetry in Gaelic, which was not focused on their relations to human concerns.<sup id="cite_ref-MacDonald2001pp255-7_47-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MacDonald2001pp255-7-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Robert_burns.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Robert_burns.jpg/170px-Robert_burns.jpg" width="170" height="155" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Robert_burns.jpg/255px-Robert_burns.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Robert_burns.jpg/340px-Robert_burns.jpg 2x" data-file-width="700" data-file-height="639" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Robert_burns.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Robert_Burns" title="Robert Burns">Robert Burns</a> (1759\xe2\x80\x9396) considered by many to be the Scottish national poet</div></div></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/James_Macpherson" title="James Macpherson">James Macpherson</a> was the first Scottish poet to gain an international reputation, claiming to have found poetry written by <a href="/wiki/Ossian" title="Ossian">Ossian</a>, he published translations that acquired international popularity, being proclaimed as a Celtic equivalent of the <a href="/wiki/Classical_antiquity" title="Classical antiquity">Classical</a> <a href="/wiki/Epic_poetry" title="Epic poetry">epics</a>. <i>Fingal</i> written in 1762 was speedily translated into many European languages, and its deep appreciation of natural beauty and the melancholy tenderness of its treatment of the ancient legend did more than any single work to bring about the <a href="/wiki/Romanticism" title="Romanticism">Romantic movement</a> in European, and especially in <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>, literature, influencing <a href="/wiki/Johann_Gottfried_von_Herder" class="mw-redirect" title="Johann Gottfried von Herder">Herder</a> and <a href="/wiki/Johann_Wolfgang_von_Goethe" title="Johann Wolfgang von Goethe">Goethe</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup> Eventually it became clear that the poems were not direct translations from the Gaelic, but flowery adaptations made to suit the aesthetic expectations of his audience.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Robert Burns was highly influenced by the Ossian cycle. Burns, an Ayrshire poet and lyricist, is widely regarded as the <a href="/wiki/National_poet" title="National poet">national poet</a> of Scotland and a major figure in the Romantic movement. As well as making original compositions, Burns also collected <a href="/wiki/Folk_music" title="Folk music">folk songs</a> from across Scotland, often revising or <a href="/wiki/Literary_adaptation" title="Literary adaptation">adapting</a> them. His poem (and song) "<a href="/wiki/Auld_Lang_Syne" title="Auld Lang Syne">Auld Lang Syne</a>" is often sung at <a href="/wiki/Hogmanay" title="Hogmanay">Hogmanay</a> (the last day of the year), and "<a href="/wiki/Scots_Wha_Hae" title="Scots Wha Hae">Scots Wha Hae</a>" served for a long time as an unofficial <a href="/wiki/National_anthem" title="National anthem">national anthem</a> of the country.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup> Burns\'s poetry drew upon a substantial familiarity with and knowledge of <a href="/wiki/Classics" title="Classics">Classical</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">Biblical</a>, and <a href="/wiki/English_literature" title="English literature">English literature</a>, as well as the Scottish Makar tradition.<sup id="cite_ref-Literary-Style_67-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Literary-Style-67">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup> Burns was skilled in writing not only in the <a href="/wiki/Scots_language" title="Scots language">Scots language</a> but also in the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_English" title="Scottish English">Scottish English</a> <a href="/wiki/Dialect" title="Dialect">dialect</a> of the <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English language</a>. Some of his works, such as "Love and Liberty" (also known as "The Jolly Beggars"), are written in both Scots and English for various effects.<sup id="cite_ref-some-hae-meat_68-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-some-hae-meat-68">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup> His themes included <a href="/wiki/Republicanism" title="Republicanism">republicanism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Radicalism_(historical)" title="Radicalism (historical)">Radicalism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scottish_patriotism" class="mw-redirect" title="Scottish patriotism">Scottish patriotism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anti-clericalism" title="Anti-clericalism">anticlericalism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Social_class" title="Social class">class</a> inequalities, <a href="/wiki/Gender_roles" class="mw-redirect" title="Gender roles">gender roles</a>, commentary on the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Kirk" class="mw-redirect" title="Scottish Kirk">Scottish Kirk</a> of his time, <a href="/wiki/Scottish_cultural_identity" class="mw-redirect" title="Scottish cultural identity">Scottish cultural identity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Poverty" title="Poverty">poverty</a>, <a href="/wiki/Human_sexuality" title="Human sexuality">sexuality</a>, and the beneficial aspects of popular socialising.<sup id="cite_ref-Address-to-the-Kibble_69-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Address-to-the-Kibble-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup> Other major literary figures connected with Romanticism include the poets and novelists <a href="/wiki/James_Hogg" title="James Hogg">James Hogg</a> (1770\xe2\x80\x931835), <a href="/wiki/Allan_Cunningham_(author)" title="Allan Cunningham (author)">Allan Cunningham</a> (1784\xe2\x80\x931842) and <a href="/wiki/John_Galt_(novelist)" title="John Galt (novelist)">John Galt</a> (1779\xe2\x80\x931839),<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup> as well as claims for the Scottish origins of one of the most significant figures of the Romantic movement in <a href="/wiki/Lord_Byron" title="Lord Byron">Lord Byron</a>, who was brought up in Scotland until he acquired his English title.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:BailliePortrait.jpeg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/BailliePortrait.jpeg/170px-BailliePortrait.jpeg" width="170" height="215" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/bc/BailliePortrait.jpeg 1.5x" data-file-width="237" data-file-height="300" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:BailliePortrait.jpeg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Engraving of playwright <a href="/wiki/Joanna_Baillie" title="Joanna Baillie">Joanna Baillie</a></div></div></div>\n<p>Drama was pursued by Scottish playwrights in London such as <a href="/wiki/Catherine_Trotter" class="mw-redirect" title="Catherine Trotter">Catherine Trotter</a> (1679\xe2\x80\x931749), born in London to Scottish parents and later moving to Aberdeen. Her plays and included the verse-tragedy <i>Fatal Friendship</i> (1698), the comedy <i>Love at a Loss</i> (1700) and the history <i>The Revolution in Sweden</i> (1706). David Crawford\'s (1665\xe2\x80\x931726) plays included the <a href="/wiki/Restoration_comedy" title="Restoration comedy">Restoration comedies</a> <i>Courtship A-la-Mode</i> (1700) and <i>Love at First Sight</i> (1704). These developed the character of the stage Scot, often a clown, but cunning and loyal. <a href="/wiki/Newburgh_Hamilton" title="Newburgh Hamilton">Newburgh Hamilton</a> (1691\xe2\x80\x931761), born in Ireland of Scottish descent, produced the comedies <i>The Petticoat-Ploter</i> (1712) and <i>The Doating Lovers</i> or <i>The Libertine</i> (1715). He later wrote the libretto for Handel\xe2\x80\x99s <i><a href="/wiki/Samson_(Handel)" title="Samson (Handel)">Samson</a></i> (1743), closely based on <a href="/wiki/John_Milton" title="John Milton">John Milton</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Samson_Agonistes" title="Samson Agonistes">Samson Agonistes</a></i>. James Thompson\'s plays often dealt with the contest between public duty and private feelings, included <i>Sophonisba</i> (1730), <i>Agamemnon</i> (1738) and <i>Tancrid and Sigismuda</i> (1745), the last of which was an international success. <a href="/wiki/David_Mallet_(writer)" title="David Mallet (writer)">David Mallet</a>\'s (c. 1705\xe2\x80\x9365) <i><a href="/wiki/Eurydice" title="Eurydice">Eurydice</a></i> (1731) was accused of being a coded Jacobite play and his later work indicates opposition to the <a href="/wiki/Robert_Walpole" title="Robert Walpole">Walpole</a> administration. The opera <i><a href="/wiki/Masque_of_Alfred" class="mw-redirect" title="Masque of Alfred">Masque of Alfred</a></i> (1740) was a collaboration between Thompson, Mallet and composer <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Arne" title="Thomas Arne">Thomas Arne</a>, with Thompson supplying the lyrics for the his most famous work, the patriotic song "<a href="/wiki/Rule,_Britannia!" title="Rule, Britannia!">Rule, Britannia!</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-Brown2011bpp30-31_72-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown2011bpp30-31-72">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In Scotland performances were largely limited to performances by visiting actors, who faced hostility from the Kirk.<sup id="cite_ref-Brown2011bpp28-9_54-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown2011bpp28-9-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> The Edinburgh Company of Players were able to perform in Dundee, Montrose, Aberdeen and regular performances at the Taylor\'s Hall in Edinburgh under the protection of a Royal Patent.<sup id="cite_ref-Brown2011bpp28-9_54-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown2011bpp28-9-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> Ramsay was instrumental in establishing them in a small theatre in Carruber\'s Close in Edinburgh,<sup id="cite_ref-Garlick2004pp170-1_73-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Garlick2004pp170-1-73">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup> but the passing of the <a href="/wiki/Licensing_Act_1737" title="Licensing Act 1737">1737 Licensing Act</a> made their activities illegal and the theatre soon closed.<sup id="cite_ref-Bell2007p288_60-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bell2007p288-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup> A new theatre was opened at Cannongate in 1747 and operated without a licence into the 1760s.<sup id="cite_ref-Garlick2004pp170-1_73-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Garlick2004pp170-1-73">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup> In the later eighteenth century, many plays were written for and performed by small amateur companies and were not published and so most have been lost. Towards the end of the century there were "<a href="/wiki/Closet_dramas" class="mw-redirect" title="Closet dramas">closet dramas</a>", primarily designed to be read, rather than performed, including work by Hogg, Galt and <a href="/wiki/Joanna_Baillie" title="Joanna Baillie">Joanna Baillie</a> (1762\xe2\x80\x931851), often influenced by the ballad tradition and <a href="/wiki/Gothic_literature" class="mw-redirect" title="Gothic literature">Gothic</a> Romanticism.<sup id="cite_ref-Brown2007pp229-30_74-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown2007pp229-30-74">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nineteenth_century">Nineteenth century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scottish_literature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Nineteenth century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Scottish_literature_in_the_nineteenth_century" title="Scottish literature in the nineteenth century">Scottish literature in the nineteenth century</a> and <a href="/wiki/Romanticism_in_Scotland" title="Romanticism in Scotland">Romanticism in Scotland</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sir_Henry_Raeburn_-_Portrait_of_Sir_Walter_Scott.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Sir_Henry_Raeburn_-_Portrait_of_Sir_Walter_Scott.jpg/170px-Sir_Henry_Raeburn_-_Portrait_of_Sir_Walter_Scott.jpg" width="170" height="206" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Sir_Henry_Raeburn_-_Portrait_of_Sir_Walter_Scott.jpg/255px-Sir_Henry_Raeburn_-_Portrait_of_Sir_Walter_Scott.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Sir_Henry_Raeburn_-_Portrait_of_Sir_Walter_Scott.jpg/340px-Sir_Henry_Raeburn_-_Portrait_of_Sir_Walter_Scott.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1649" data-file-height="2000" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Sir_Henry_Raeburn_-_Portrait_of_Sir_Walter_Scott.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Walter_Scott" title="Walter Scott">Walter Scott</a> whose <a href="/wiki/Waverley_Novels" title="Waverley Novels">Waverley Novels</a> helped define Scottish identity in the 19th century.</div></div></div>\n<p>Scottish poetry is often seen as entering a period of decline in the nineteenth century,<sup id="cite_ref-Mandell2007pp301-07_75-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mandell2007pp301-07-75">&#91;75&#93;</a></sup> with a descent of into infantalism as exemplified by the highly popular <a href="/wiki/Whistle_Binkie" title="Whistle Binkie">Whistle Binkie</a> anthologies (1830\xe2\x80\x9390).<sup id="cite_ref-Carruthers2009pp58-9_76-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carruthers2009pp58-9-76">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup> However, Scotland continued to produce talented and successful poets, including weaver-poet <a href="/wiki/William_Thom_(poet)" title="William Thom (poet)">William Thom</a> (1799\xe2\x80\x931848), Lady Margaret Maclean Clephane Compton Northampton (d. 1830), <a href="/wiki/William_Edmondstoune_Aytoun" title="William Edmondstoune Aytoun">William Edmondstoune Aytoun</a> (1813\xe2\x80\x9365) and <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Campbell_(poet)" title="Thomas Campbell (poet)">Thomas Campbell</a> (1777\xe2\x80\x931844), whose works were extensively reprinted in the period 1800\xe2\x80\x9360.<sup id="cite_ref-Mandell2007pp301-07_75-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mandell2007pp301-07-75">&#91;75&#93;</a></sup> Among the most influential poets of the later nineteenth century were <a href="/wiki/James_Thomson_(B.V.)" class="mw-redirect" title="James Thomson (B.V.)">James Thomson</a> (1834\xe2\x80\x9382) and <a href="/wiki/John_Davidson_(poet)" title="John Davidson (poet)">John Davidson</a> (1857\xe2\x80\x931909), whose work would have a major impact on modernist poets including Hugh MacDiarmid, <a href="/wiki/Wallace_Stevens" title="Wallace Stevens">Wallace Stevens</a> and <a href="/wiki/T._S._Eliot" title="T. S. Eliot">T. S. Eliot</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-LindsayandDuncan2005ppxxxiv-xxxv_77-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LindsayandDuncan2005ppxxxiv-xxxv-77">&#91;77&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Highland_Clearances" title="Highland Clearances">Highland Clearances</a> and widespread emigration significantly weakened Gaelic language and culture and had a profound impact on the nature of Gaelic poetry. The best poetry in this vein contained a strong element of protest, including Uilleam Mac Dhun L\xc3\xa8ibhe\'s (William Livingstone, 1808\xe2\x80\x9370) protest against the <a href="/wiki/Islay" title="Islay">Islay</a> and Seonaidh Ph\xc3\xa0draig Iarsiadair\'s (John Smith, 1848\xe2\x80\x9381)  condemnation of those responsible for the clearances. The best known Gaelic poet of the era was M\xc3\xa0iri Mh\xc3\xb2r nan \xc3\x93ran (Mary MacPherson, 1821\xe2\x80\x9398), whose evocation of place and mood has made her among the most enduring Gaelic poets.<sup id="cite_ref-MacDonald2001pp255-7_47-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MacDonald2001pp255-7-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Walter Scott began as a poet and also collected and published Scottish ballads. His first prose work, <i><a href="/wiki/Waverley_(novel)" title="Waverley (novel)">Waverley</a></i> in 1814, is often called the first <a href="/wiki/Historical_novel" class="mw-redirect" title="Historical novel">historical novel</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">&#91;78&#93;</a></sup> It launched a highly successful career, with other historical novels such as <i><a href="/wiki/Rob_Roy_(novel)" title="Rob Roy (novel)">Rob Roy</a></i> (1817), <i><a href="/wiki/The_Heart_of_Midlothian" title="The Heart of Midlothian">The Heart of Midlothian</a></i> (1818) and <i><a href="/wiki/Ivanhoe" title="Ivanhoe">Ivanhoe</a></i> (1820). Scott probably did more than any other figure to define and popularise Scottish cultural identity in the nineteenth century.<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">&#91;79&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Scottish "national drama" emerged in the early 1800s, as plays with specifically Scottish themes began to dominate the Scottish stage.\nThe existing repertoire of Scottish-themed plays included <a href="/wiki/John_Home" title="John Home">John Home</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Douglas_(play)" title="Douglas (play)">Douglas</a></i> (1756) and Ramsay\'s <i>The Gentle Shepherd</i> (1725), with the last two being the most popular plays among amateur groups.<sup id="cite_ref-Brown2007p231_80-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown2007p231-80">&#91;80&#93;</a></sup> Scott was keenly interested in drama, becoming a shareholder in the <a href="/wiki/Theatre_Royal,_Edinburgh" title="Theatre Royal, Edinburgh">Theatre Royal, Edinburgh</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Brown2007pp185-6_81-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown2007pp185-6-81">&#91;81&#93;</a></sup> Baillie\'s Highland themed <i>Family Legend</i> was first produced in Edinburgh in 1810 with the help of Scott, as part of a deliberate attempt to stimulate a national Scottish drama.<sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82">&#91;82&#93;</a></sup> Scott also wrote five plays, of which <i>Hallidon Hill</i> (1822) and <i>MacDuff\'s Cross</i> (1822), were patriotic Scottish histories.<sup id="cite_ref-Brown2007pp185-6_81-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown2007pp185-6-81">&#91;81&#93;</a></sup> Adaptations of the Waverley novels, largely first performed in minor theatres, rather than the larger <a href="/wiki/Patent_theatre" title="Patent theatre">Patent theatres</a>, included <i>The Lady in the Lake</i> (1817), <i>The Heart of Midlothian</i> (1819), and <i>Rob Roy</i>, which underwent over 1,000 performances in Scotland in this period. Also adapted for the stage were <i><a href="/wiki/Guy_Mannering" title="Guy Mannering">Guy Mannering</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Bride_of_Lammermoor" title="The Bride of Lammermoor">The Bride of Lammermoor</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Abbot" title="The Abbot">The Abbot</a></i>. These highly popular plays saw the social range and size of the audience for theatre expand and helped shape theatre-going practices in Scotland for the rest of the century.<sup id="cite_ref-Brown2007p231_80-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown2007p231-80">&#91;80&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Scotland was also the location of two of the most important literary magazines of the era, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Edinburgh_Review" class="mw-redirect" title="The Edinburgh Review">The Edinburgh Review</a></i>, founded in 1802 and <i><a href="/wiki/Blackwood%27s_Magazine" title="Blackwood&#39;s Magazine">Blackwood\'s Magazine</a></i>, founded in 1817. Together they had a major impact on the development of British literature and drama in the era of Romanticism.<sup id="cite_ref-83" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83">&#91;83&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-84" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-84">&#91;84&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>\nIn the late 19th century, a number of Scottish-born authors achieved international reputations. <a href="/wiki/Robert_Louis_Stevenson" title="Robert Louis Stevenson">Robert Louis Stevenson</a>\'s work included the <a href="/wiki/Urban_Gothic" title="Urban Gothic">urban Gothic</a> novella <i><a href="/wiki/Strange_Case_of_Dr_Jekyll_and_Mr_Hyde" title="Strange Case of Dr Jekyll and Mr Hyde">Strange Case of Dr Jekyll and Mr Hyde</a></i> (1886), and played a major part in developing the historical adventure in books like <i><a href="/wiki/Kidnapped_(novel)" title="Kidnapped (novel)">Kidnapped</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Treasure_Island" title="Treasure Island">Treasure Island</a></i>. <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Conan_Doyle" title="Arthur Conan Doyle">Arthur Conan Doyle</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Sherlock_Holmes" title="Sherlock Holmes">Sherlock Holmes</a></i> stories helped found the tradition of detective fiction. The "<a href="/wiki/Kailyard_school" title="Kailyard school">kailyard tradition</a>" at the end of the century, brought elements of <a href="/wiki/Fantasy" title="Fantasy">fantasy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Folklore" title="Folklore">folklore</a> back into fashion as can be seen in the work of figures like <a href="/wiki/J._M._Barrie" title="J. M. Barrie">J. M. Barrie</a>, most famous for his creation of <a href="/wiki/Peter_Pan" title="Peter Pan">Peter Pan</a> and <a href="/wiki/George_MacDonald" title="George MacDonald">George MacDonald</a> whose works including <i><a href="/wiki/Phantastes" title="Phantastes">Phantastes</a></i> played a major part in the creation of the fantasy genre.<sup id="cite_ref-85" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-85">&#91;85&#93;</a></sup></p><div style="clear:both;"></div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="20th_century_to_the_present">20th century to the present</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scottish_literature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: 20th century to the present">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Literature_in_modern_Scotland" title="Literature in modern Scotland">Literature in modern Scotland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Renaissance" title="Scottish Renaissance">Scottish Renaissance</a></div>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Scotland_in_the_modern_era" title="Scotland in the modern era">Scotland in the modern era</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Lamb-Hugh_MacDiarmid.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Lamb-Hugh_MacDiarmid.jpg/170px-Lamb-Hugh_MacDiarmid.jpg" width="170" height="223" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Lamb-Hugh_MacDiarmid.jpg/255px-Lamb-Hugh_MacDiarmid.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Lamb-Hugh_MacDiarmid.jpg/340px-Lamb-Hugh_MacDiarmid.jpg 2x" data-file-width="516" data-file-height="676" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Lamb-Hugh_MacDiarmid.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A bust of <a href="/wiki/Hugh_MacDiarmid" title="Hugh MacDiarmid">Hugh MacDiarmid</a> sculpted in 1927 by <a href="/wiki/William_Lamb_(sculptor)" title="William Lamb (sculptor)">William Lamb</a></div></div></div>\n<p>In the early 20th century there was a new surge of activity in Scottish literature, influenced by <a href="/wiki/Modernism" title="Modernism">modernism</a> and resurgent nationalism, known as the Scottish Renaissance.<sup id="cite_ref-VisitingArtsScotland_86-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VisitingArtsScotland-86">&#91;86&#93;</a></sup> The leading figure in the movement was <a href="/wiki/Hugh_MacDiarmid" title="Hugh MacDiarmid">Hugh MacDiarmid</a> (the pseudonym of Christopher Murray Grieve). MacDiarmid attempted to revive the Scots language as a medium for serious literature in poetic works including "<a href="/wiki/A_Drunk_Man_Looks_at_the_Thistle" title="A Drunk Man Looks at the Thistle">A Drunk Man Looks at the Thistle</a>" (1936), developing a form of <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_Scots" class="mw-redirect" title="Synthetic Scots">Synthetic Scots</a> that combined different regional dialects and archaic terms.<sup id="cite_ref-VisitingArtsScotland_86-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VisitingArtsScotland-86">&#91;86&#93;</a></sup> Other writers that emerged in this period, and are often treated as part of the movement, include the poets <a href="/wiki/Edwin_Muir" title="Edwin Muir">Edwin Muir</a> and <a href="/wiki/William_Soutar" title="William Soutar">William Soutar</a>, the novelists <a href="/wiki/Neil_Gunn" class="mw-redirect" title="Neil Gunn">Neil Gunn</a>, <a href="/wiki/George_Blake" title="George Blake">George Blake</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nan_Shepherd" title="Nan Shepherd">Nan Shepherd</a>, <a href="/wiki/A_J_Cronin" class="mw-redirect" title="A J Cronin">A J Cronin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Naomi_Mitchison" title="Naomi Mitchison">Naomi Mitchison</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eric_Linklater" title="Eric Linklater">Eric Linklater</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lewis_Grassic_Gibbon" title="Lewis Grassic Gibbon">Lewis Grassic Gibbon</a>, and the playwright <a href="/wiki/James_Bridie" title="James Bridie">James Bridie</a>. All were born within a fifteen-year period (1887\xe2\x80\x931901) and, although they cannot be described as members of a single school they all pursued an exploration of identity, rejecting nostalgia and parochialism and engaging with social and political issues.<sup id="cite_ref-VisitingArtsScotland_86-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VisitingArtsScotland-86">&#91;86&#93;</a></sup> This period saw the emergence of a tradition of popular or working class theatre. Hundreds of amateur groups were established, particularly in the growing urban centres of the Lowlands. Amateur companies encouraged native playwrights, including <a href="/wiki/Robert_McLellan" title="Robert McLellan">Robert McLellan</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-87" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-87">&#91;87&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Some writers that emerged after the Second World War followed MacDiarmid by writing in Scots, including <a href="/wiki/Robert_Garioch" title="Robert Garioch">Robert Garioch</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sydney_Goodsir_Smith" title="Sydney Goodsir Smith">Sydney Goodsir Smith</a>. Others demonstrated a greater interest in English language poetry, among them <a href="/wiki/Norman_MacCaig" title="Norman MacCaig">Norman MacCaig</a>, George Bruce and <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Lindsay" title="Maurice Lindsay">Maurice Lindsay</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-VisitingArtsScotland_86-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VisitingArtsScotland-86">&#91;86&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/George_Mackay_Brown" title="George Mackay Brown">George Mackay Brown</a> from Orkney, and <a href="/wiki/Iain_Crichton_Smith" title="Iain Crichton Smith">Iain Crichton Smith</a> from Lewis, wrote both poetry and prose fiction shaped by their distinctive island backgrounds.<sup id="cite_ref-VisitingArtsScotland_86-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VisitingArtsScotland-86">&#91;86&#93;</a></sup> The Glaswegian poet <a href="/wiki/Edwin_Morgan_(poet)" title="Edwin Morgan (poet)">Edwin Morgan</a> became known for translations of works from a wide range of European languages. He was also the first <a href="/wiki/Scots_Makar" class="mw-redirect" title="Scots Makar">Scots Makar</a> (the official national poet), appointed by the inaugural Scottish government in 2004.<sup id="cite_ref-88" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-88">&#91;88&#93;</a></sup> The shift to drama that focused on working class life in the post-war period gained momentum with Robert McLeish\'s <i>The Gorbals Story</i><sup id="cite_ref-Kershaw&amp;Thompson2004p208_89-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kershaw&amp;Thompson2004p208-89">&#91;89&#93;</a></sup> and the work of <a href="/wiki/Ena_Lamont_Stewart" title="Ena Lamont Stewart">Ena Lamont Stewart</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-90" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-90">&#91;90&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Robert_Kemp_(playwright)" title="Robert Kemp (playwright)">Robert Kemp</a> and George Munro.<sup id="cite_ref-Kershaw&amp;Thompson2004p208_89-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kershaw&amp;Thompson2004p208-89">&#91;89&#93;</a></sup> Many major Scottish post-war novelists, such as <a href="/wiki/Muriel_Spark" title="Muriel Spark">Muriel Spark</a>, <a href="/wiki/James_Kennaway" title="James Kennaway">James Kennaway</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Trocchi" title="Alexander Trocchi">Alexander Trocchi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jessie_Kesson" title="Jessie Kesson">Jessie Kesson</a> and <a href="/wiki/Robin_Jenkins" title="Robin Jenkins">Robin Jenkins</a> spent much or most of their lives outside Scotland, but often dealt with Scottish themes, as in Spark\'s Edinburgh-set <i><a href="/wiki/The_Prime_of_Miss_Jean_Brodie_(novel)" title="The Prime of Miss Jean Brodie (novel)">The Prime of Miss Jean Brodie</a></i> (1961)<sup id="cite_ref-VisitingArtsScotland_86-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VisitingArtsScotland-86">&#91;86&#93;</a></sup> and Kennaway\'s script for the film <i><a href="/wiki/Tunes_of_Glory" title="Tunes of Glory">Tunes of Glory</a></i> (1956).<sup id="cite_ref-91" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-91">&#91;91&#93;</a></sup>  Successful mass-market works included the action novels of <a href="/wiki/Alistair_MacLean" title="Alistair MacLean">Alistair MacLean</a>, and the historical fiction of <a href="/wiki/Dorothy_Dunnett" title="Dorothy Dunnett">Dorothy Dunnett</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-VisitingArtsScotland_86-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VisitingArtsScotland-86">&#91;86&#93;</a></sup> A younger generation of novelists that emerged in the 1960s and 1970s included <a href="/wiki/Shena_Mackay" title="Shena Mackay">Shena Mackay</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alan_Spence" title="Alan Spence">Alan Spence</a>, <a href="/wiki/Allan_Massie" title="Allan Massie">Allan Massie</a> and the work of <a href="/wiki/William_McIlvanney" title="William McIlvanney">William McIlvanney</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-VisitingArtsScotland_86-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VisitingArtsScotland-86">&#91;86&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Carol_Ann_Duffy_(cropped).jpg" Ann Duffy</a> the first Scottish <a href="/wiki/Poet_Laureate" class="mw-redirect" title="Poet Laureate">Poet Laureate</a>.</div></div></div>\n<p>From the 1980s Scottish literature enjoyed another major revival, particularly associated with a group of Glasgow writers focused around meetings in the house of critic, poet and teacher <a href="/wiki/Philip_Hobsbaum" title="Philip Hobsbaum">Philip Hobsbaum</a>. Also important in the movement was <a href="/wiki/Peter_Kravitz" title="Peter Kravitz">Peter Kravitz</a>, editor of <a href="/wiki/Birlinn_(publisher)" title="Birlinn (publisher)">Polygon Books</a>. Members of the group that would come to prominence as writers included <a href="/wiki/James_Kelman" title="James Kelman">James Kelman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alasdair_Gray" title="Alasdair Gray">Alasdair Gray</a>, <a href="/wiki/Liz_Lochhead" title="Liz Lochhead">Liz Lochhead</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tom_Leonard_(poet)" title="Tom Leonard (poet)">Tom Leonard</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aonghas_MacNeacail" title="Aonghas MacNeacail">Aonghas MacNeacail</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-VisitingArtsScotland_86-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VisitingArtsScotland-86">&#91;86&#93;</a></sup> In the 1990s major, prize winning, Scottish novels that emerged from this movement included <a href="/wiki/Irvine_Welsh" title="Irvine Welsh">Irvine Welsh</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Trainspotting_(novel)" title="Trainspotting (novel)">Trainspotting</a></i> (1993), Warner\xe2\x80\x99s <i><a href="/wiki/Morvern_Callar" title="Morvern Callar">Morvern Callar</a></i> (1995), Gray\xe2\x80\x99s <i><a href="/wiki/Poor_Things" title="Poor Things">Poor Things</a></i> (1992) and Kelman\xe2\x80\x99s <i><a href="/wiki/How_Late_It_Was,_How_Late" title="How Late It Was, How Late">How Late It Was, How Late</a></i> (1994).<sup id="cite_ref-VisitingArtsScotland_86-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VisitingArtsScotland-86">&#91;86&#93;</a></sup> These works were linked by a sometimes overtly political reaction to <a href="/wiki/Thatcherism" title="Thatcherism">Thatcherism</a> that explored marginal areas of experience and used vivid vernacular language (including expletives and Scots). Scottish crime fiction has been a major area of growth with the success of novelists including <a href="/wiki/Val_McDermid" title="Val McDermid">Val McDermid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Frederic_Lindsay" title="Frederic Lindsay">Frederic Lindsay</a>, <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Brookmyre" title="Christopher Brookmyre">Christopher Brookmyre</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quintin_Jardine" title="Quintin Jardine">Quintin Jardine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Denise_Mina" title="Denise Mina">Denise Mina</a> and particularly the success of Edinburgh\'s <a href="/wiki/Ian_Rankin" title="Ian Rankin">Ian Rankin</a> and his <a href="/wiki/Inspector_Rebus" title="Inspector Rebus">Inspector Rebus</a> novels.<sup id="cite_ref-VisitingArtsScotland_86-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VisitingArtsScotland-86">&#91;86&#93;</a></sup> Scottish play writing became increasingly internationalised, with Scottish writers such as Liz Lochhead and Edwin Morgan adapting classic texts, while Jo Clifford and <a href="/wiki/David_Greig_(dramatist)" title="David Greig (dramatist)">David Greig</a> investigated European themes.<sup id="cite_ref-92" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-92">&#91;92&#93;</a></sup> This period also saw the emergence of a new generation of Scottish poets that became leading figures on the UK stage, including <a href="/wiki/Don_Paterson" title="Don Paterson">Don Paterson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Crawford_(Scottish_poet)" title="Robert Crawford (Scottish poet)">Robert Crawford</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kathleen_Jamie" title="Kathleen Jamie">Kathleen Jamie</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carol_Ann_Duffy" title="Carol Ann Duffy">Carol Ann Duffy</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-VisitingArtsScotland_86-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VisitingArtsScotland-86">&#91;86&#93;</a></sup> Glasgow-born Duffy was named as <a href="/wiki/Poet_Laureate" class="mw-redirect" title="Poet Laureate">Poet Laureate</a> in May 2009, the first woman, the first Scot and the first openly gay poet to take the post.<sup id="cite_ref-93" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-93">&#91;93&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span
