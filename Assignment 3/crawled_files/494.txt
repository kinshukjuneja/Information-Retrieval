Sports governing bodies come in various forms, and have a variety of regulatory functions. Examples of this can include disciplinary action for rule infractions and deciding on rule changes in the <a href="/wiki/Sport" title="Sport">sport</a> that they govern. Governing bodies have different scopes. They may cover a range of sport at an International level, such as the <a href="/wiki/International_Olympic_Committee" title="International Olympic Committee">International Olympic Committee</a> and the <a href="/wiki/International_Paralympic_Committee" title="International Paralympic Committee">International Paralympic Committee</a>, or only a single sport at a national level, such as the <a href="/wiki/Rugby_Football_League" title="Rugby Football League">Rugby Football League</a>. National bodies may or may not be affiliated to international bodies for the same sport. The first international federations were formed at the end of the 19th century.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_sports_governing_bodies">Types of sports governing bodies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sports_governing_body&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Types of sports governing bodies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Every sport has a different governing body that can define the way that the sport operates through its affiliated clubs and societies. This is because sports have different levels of difficulty and skill, so they can try to organize the people playing their sport by ability and by age. The different types of sport governing bodies are all shown below:\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/List_of_international_sports_federations" title="List of international sports federations">International sports federations</a> are responsible for one sport (or a group of similar sport disciplines, such as <a href="/wiki/Aquatic_sports" class="mw-redirect" title="Aquatic sports">aquatics</a> or <a href="/wiki/Skiing" title="Skiing">skiing</a>). They create a common set of rules and organize international competitions. The promotion of the sport are also a task of an international federation.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Trust_(property)" class="mw-redirect" title="Trust (property)">Trusts</a> are organizations or groups that have control over money that will be used to help someone else, such as the <a href="/wiki/Youth_Sport_Trust" title="Youth Sport Trust">Youth Sport Trust</a>.\n</p><p>National federations have the same objectives as an international federation, but within the scope of one country, or even part of a country, as the name implies. They support local clubs and are often responsible for national teams. <a href="/wiki/National_Olympic_Committee" title="National Olympic Committee">National Olympic Committees</a> and <a href="/wiki/National_Paralympic_Committee" title="National Paralympic Committee">National Paralympic Committees</a> are both a type of National Federation, as they are responsible for a country\'s participation in the <a href="/wiki/Olympic_Games" title="Olympic Games">Olympic Games</a> and in the <a href="/wiki/Paralympic_Games" title="Paralympic Games">Paralympic Games</a> respectively. However, a national governing body (NGB) can be different from a national federation due to government recognition requirements.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Also, NGBs can be a <a href="/wiki/Supraorganization" title="Supraorganization">supraorganization</a> representing a range of unrelated organizations operating in a particular sport as evident in the example of the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Federation_of_Sub-Aqua_Clubs" title="Northern Ireland Federation of Sub-Aqua Clubs">Northern Ireland Federation of Sub-Aqua Clubs</a>.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Multi-sport_event" title="Multi-sport event">Multi-sport event</a> organizers are responsible for the organization of an event that includes more than one sport. The best-known example is the <a href="/wiki/International_Olympic_Committee" title="International Olympic Committee">International Olympic Committee</a> (IOC), the organizer of the modern <a href="/wiki/Olympic_Games" title="Olympic Games">Olympic Games</a>. General sports organizations are responsible for sports related topics, usually for a certain group, such as the <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Catholic</a> or <a href="/wiki/Jewish" class="mw-redirect" title="Jewish">Jewish</a> sports groups. General sports organizations can also exist for the army<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> and other groups, but they usually are medium-sized, as they do not have that much of a budget to work with.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/List_of_professional_sports_leagues" title="List of professional sports leagues">Professional sports leagues</a> are usually the highest level of play in sport, specifically if they consist of the best players around the world in a certain sport. Because of this, they usually work with national or international federations, but there is usually a separation between the different federations. Most North American professional leagues usually do not have amateur divisions, as the <a href="/wiki/Amateur" title="Amateur">amateur</a> divisions are mostly run in separate leagues. In addition, most professional leagues are related to other leagues, as players usually attempt to play in the league with the highest level of play. Because of this, <a href="/wiki/Promotion_and_relegation" title="Promotion and relegation">promotion and relegation</a> can occur; or, in league systems without promotion and relegation, clubs in professional leagues can have a team in the <a href="/wiki/Minor_league" title="Minor league">minor leagues</a>. This enables them to shuffle players who are not doing well to the minor leagues, which will inspire them to contribute more to the team by playing better.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline"
