&#39;nigh&#39;">n</span><span title="/\xc9\x99r/: &#39;er&#39; in &#39;letter&#39;">\xc9\x99r</span><span title="&#39;d&#39; in &#39;dye&#39;">d</span></span>/</a></span></span> <a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key"><i title="English pronunciation respelling"><span style="font-size:90%">SLEEV</span> <span style="font-size:90%">DON</span>-\xc9\x99rd</i></a>; from <a href="/wiki/Irish_language" title="Irish language">Irish</a>: <i lang="ga">Sliabh D\xc3\xb3nairt</i>, meaning "D\xc3\xb3nairt\'s height of 850 metres (2,790&#160;ft).<sup id="cite_ref-mountainviews_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mountainviews-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-OSI_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OSI-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> One of the <a href="/wiki/Mourne_Mountains" title="Mourne Mountains">Mourne Mountains</a>, it is near the town of <a href="/wiki/Newcastle,_County_Down" title="Newcastle, County Down">Newcastle</a> on the eastern coast of <a href="/wiki/County_Down" title="County Down">County Down</a>, overlooking the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Sea" title="Irish Sea">Irish Sea</a>. It is also the 19th highest peak on the island of Ireland.<sup id="cite_ref-mountainviews_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mountainviews-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Mourne_Wall" title="Mourne Wall">Mourne Wall</a> \xe2\x80\x93 built in the early 20th century \xe2\x80\x93 runs up the western and southern slopes of the mountain, joining a small stone tower at the summit. Also on the summit are the remains of two ancient burial <a href="/wiki/Cairn" title="Cairn">cairns</a>, one of which is the remains of the highest known <a href="/wiki/Passage_grave" title="Passage grave">passage tomb</a> in Ireland. In <a href="/wiki/Irish_mythology" title="Irish mythology">Irish mythology</a> the mountain was associated with the mythical figures Boirche and Sl\xc3\xa1ngha. It was later associated with, and named after, Saint Donard, who was said to have made the summit his <a href="/wiki/Hermitage_(religious_retreat)" title="Hermitage (religious retreat)">hermitage</a>. Up until the 1830s, people would climb the mountain as part of a yearly pilgrimage, which may have originally been a <a href="/wiki/Lughnasadh" title="Lughnasadh">Lughnasadh</a> ritual. <a href="/wiki/Royal_Engineers" title="Royal Engineers">Royal Engineers</a> camped on the summit for four months in 1826 as part of the <a href="/wiki/Ordnance_Survey" title="Ordnance Survey">Ordnance Survey</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Principal_Triangulation_of_Great_Britain" title="Principal Triangulation of Great Britain">Principal Triangulation</a>.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input href="/w/index.php?title=Slieve_Donard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Slieve Donard sits at the northeastern edge of the Mournes, overlooking Newcastle and Dundrum Bay. It has two lesser summits on the seaward side \xe2\x80\x93 Millstone Mountain (460 m),<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> and Crossone (540 m). Two <a href="/wiki/Glen" title="Glen">glens</a> separate Slieve Donard from the neighbouring mountains of <a href="/wiki/Slieve_Commedagh" title="Slieve Commedagh">Slieve Commedagh</a> (to the northwest) and Chimney Rock Mountain or Slieve Neir (to the south).<sup id="cite_ref-OSI_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OSI-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Slieve Commedagh, at 767&#160;m (2,516&#160;ft), is the second-highest of the Mourne Mountains.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The mountain is an easy climb although the path is very eroded at places. The most popular route begins at Slieve Donard Forest on the north side of the mountain and follows the Glen River and then the Mourne Wall to the summit. In recent years a stone path has been made on the steepest parts of the mountain. The summit has views of the coast, and as far afield as <a href="/wiki/Belfast_Lough" title="Belfast Lough">Belfast Lough</a>, 30&#160;miles north, <a href="/wiki/Dublin_Bay" title="Dublin Bay">Dublin Bay</a>, 55 miles south, and the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man" title="Isle of Man">Isle of Man</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Burial_cairns">Burial cairns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slieve_Donard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Burial cairns">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>On the summit are the remains of two prehistoric burial <a href="/wiki/Cairn" title="Cairn">cairns</a>. The Great Cairn is at the highest point and measures about 1m high, 36.5 metres from north to south and 43m from east to west. It appears to have been a <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">Neolithic</a> <a href="/wiki/Passage_grave" title="Passage grave">passage tomb</a>, dating to 3300\xe2\x80\x933000 BCE. It is the highest known passage tomb in Britain and Ireland.<sup id="cite_ref-moore3_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-moore3-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The Lesser Cairn lies 210 metres to the northeast, overlooking Newcastle. It measures about 4.5m high, 18m from north to south, and 16m from east to west. It appears to have been an Early <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a> multiple-<a href="/wiki/Cist" title="Cist">cist</a> cairn, dating to 2300\xe2\x80\x931950 BCE.<sup id="cite_ref-moore3_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-moore3-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> In the past, the cairns had a much more well-defined shape and it is thought the Great Cairn had an east-facing entrance leading to an inner chamber.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> They appear to parallel the two cairns on <a href="/wiki/Slieve_Gullion" title="Slieve Gullion">Slieve Gullion</a>, which can be seen from Slieve Donard.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> \n</p><p>Both cairns have been badly damaged and altered over time, and modern hillwalkers often add stones on top of them.<sup id="cite_ref-moore3_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-moore3-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Irish folklore holds that it is bad luck to damage or disrespect such tombs and that deliberately doing so could bring a <a href="/wiki/Curse" title="Curse">curse</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_and_folklore">History and folklore</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slieve_Donard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History and folklore">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a data-file-width="640" data-file-height="480" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Slieve_Donard_Summit_Cairn_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1521811.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>One of the summit cairns in 2009</div></div></div>\n<p>It is likely that Slieve Donard was seen as a sacred mountain in the far past.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Irish_mythology" title="Irish mythology">Irish mythology</a> identifies the Great Cairn as being the tomb or abode of mythical figures, and an entrance to <a href="/wiki/Celtic_Otherworld" title="Celtic Otherworld">the Otherworld</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-moore17_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-moore17-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> In <i><a href="/wiki/Cath_Maige_Tuired" title="Cath Maige Tuired">Cath Maige Tuired</a></i> ("The Battle of Moytura"), Slieve Donard is called one of the "twelve chief mountains" of Ireland,<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> while in the <a href="/wiki/Triads_of_Ireland" title="Triads of Ireland">Triads of Ireland</a> it is called one of the "three great heights" of Ireland, along with <a href="/wiki/Croagh_Patrick" title="Croagh Patrick">Croagh Patrick</a> and <a href="/wiki/Great_Sugar_Loaf" title="Great Sugar Loaf">Great Sugar Loaf</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-moore17_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-moore17-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The earliest sources give two names for the mountain: <i>Benn mBoirchi</i> (modern spelling: <i>Beann Boirche</i>) and <i>Sliab Sl\xc3\xa1nga</i> (modern spelling: <i>Sliabh Sl\xc3\xa1ngha</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-moore17_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-moore17-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> <i>Beann Boirche</i> means "Boirche\'s peak", referring to a mythical <a href="/wiki/Herder" title="Herder">cowherd</a> and king who had seemingly supernatural powers. The plural name <i>Beanna Boirche</i> ("Boirche\'s peaks") was given to the Mourne Mountains. The name <i>Sliabh Sl\xc3\xa1ngha</i> means "Sl\xc3\xa1ngha\'s mountain". This refers to the mythical Sl\xc3\xa1ngha, son of <a href="/wiki/Parthol%C3%B3n" title="Parthol\xc3\xb3n">Parthol\xc3\xb3n</a>, who was said to be the first physician in Ireland. According to the <i><a href="/wiki/Annals_of_the_Four_Masters" title="Annals of the Four Masters">Annals of the Four Masters</a></i>, he died in <a href="/wiki/Anno_Mundi" title="Anno Mundi">Anno Mundi</a> 2533 (2533 years after "the creation of the world") and was buried in the passage tomb.<sup id="cite_ref-moore17_13-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-moore17-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Ireland was Christianized from the 5th to 8th centuries. A local Christian missionary, Saint Donard (known in Irish as <i>Domhanghart</i>),<sup id="cite_ref-O&#39;Mulconry_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-O&#39;Mulconry-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Joyce_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Joyce-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> became associated with the mountain. According to tradition, he was a follower of <a href="/wiki/Saint_Patrick" title="Saint Patrick">Saint Patrick</a> and founded a monastery at Maghera, a few miles north of the mountain. Donard is said to have "appropriated the mountain and the monument for Christianity",<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> allegedly by making the Great Cairn into a <a href="/wiki/Hermit" title="Hermit">hermit</a>\'s cell and using the Lesser Cairn as an <a href="/wiki/Oratory_(worship)" title="Oratory (worship)">oratory</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> According to the <i>Life of Saint Patrick</i> and the <i>Tripartite Life of Saint Patrick</i>, Patrick blessed Donard in the womb, declaring that Donard would not die but <a href="/wiki/King_in_the_mountain" title="King in the mountain">abide inside the mountain as a perpetual guardian</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> According to folklore, a cave runs from the seashore to the cairn on the summit, and it is here that Donard (or Boirche) lives.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> The writings of <a href="/wiki/Gerald_of_Wales" title="Gerald of Wales">Gerald de Barri</a> indicate that in the late 12th century the name <i>Sliabh Sl\xc3\xa1ngha</i> was going out of use and being replaced by <i>Sliabh Domhanghairt</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> <i>Sliabh D\xc3\xb3nairt</i> is the modernised spelling.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a data-file-width="640" data-file-height="480" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:The_summit_of_Slieve_Donard_-_geograph.org.uk_-_103268.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Mourne Wall and the stone tower at the summit</div></div></div>\n<p>Up until the 1830s,<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> people made a pilgrimage to the mountaintop in late July each year. It is likely that this was originally a <a href="/wiki/Lughnasadh" title="Lughnasadh">Lughnasadh</a> ritual that became Christianized.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> The church at Maghera and St Mary\'s Church at Ballaghanery Upper may have been starting points for the pilgrimage.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1826, as part of the <a href="/wiki/Principal_Triangulation_of_Great_Britain" title="Principal Triangulation of Great Britain">Principal Triangulation</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Engineers" title="Royal Engineers">Royal Engineers</a> used Slieve Donard as a base <a href="/wiki/Cartography" title="Cartography">to map</a> Ireland. They camped on the mountaintop from late July until late November that year and used the two cairns to make <a href="/wiki/Triangulation_station" title="Triangulation station">triangulation points</a>, badly damaging the cairns.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> During the Survey, two men died in a snowstorm on their way down the mountain and others were hurt during storms on the summit.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Mourne_Wall" title="Mourne Wall">Mourne Wall</a> was built in the early 20th century and passes over fifteen mountains, including Slieve Donard. The wall goes up the western slope to a stone tower and then down the southern slope. It took 18 years to build, from 1904 to 1922, with stonemasons working in the mountains from March to mid-October each year. Some stones from the cairns were used to build it.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In April 2006, a man was killed on Slieve Donard after being struck by lightning. A brass plaque on the summit commemorates him.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slieve_Donard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: See also">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/List_of_mountains_in_Ireland" title="List of mountains in Ireland">List of mountains in Ireland</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/List_of_Irish_counties_by_highest_point" title="List of Irish counties by highest point">List of Irish counties by highest point</a></li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slieve_Donard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist columns references-column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 21em;
