<b>Republic of Ireland\xe2\x80\x93United Kingdom border</b>, also known as the <b>Irish border</b>, runs for 499&#160;km (310&#160;mi)<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> from <a href="/wiki/Lough_Foyle" title="Lough Foyle">Lough Foyle</a> in the north of <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a> to <a href="/wiki/Carlingford_Lough" title="Carlingford Lough">Carlingford Lough</a> in the northeast, separating the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Republic of Ireland</a> from <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>. \n</p><p>Border markings are comparatively inconspicuous, in common with many inter-state borders in the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>. As both states share a <a href="/wiki/Common_Travel_Area" title="Common Travel Area">Common Travel Area</a> and are part of the <a href="/wiki/European_Single_Market" title="European Single Market">European Single Market</a>, the border is essentially <a href="/wiki/Open_border" title="Open border">open</a>, allowing free passage of people since 1923 and of goods since 1993. There are more than 200 public roads that cross the border.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Following the <a href="/wiki/Brexit" title="Brexit">Brexit</a> vote, the <a href="/wiki/Irish_border_question" title="Irish border question">future of the border</a> is uncertain and its status is one of the key points in the <a href="/wiki/Brexit_negotiations" title="Brexit negotiations">UK withdrawal negotiations</a>.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" speed limit sign</div></div></div>\n<p>Originally intended as an internal boundary within the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_of_Great_Britain_and_Ireland" title="United Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland">United Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland</a>, the border was created in 1921 under the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_Parliament" class="mw-redirect" title="United Kingdom Parliament">United Kingdom Parliament</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Ireland_Act_1920" title="Government of Ireland Act 1920">Government of Ireland Act 1920</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Prior to this, a <a href="/wiki/First_D%C3%A1il" title="First D\xc3\xa1il">separatist Irish parliament had been established</a> in Dublin, which did not recognise the Government of Ireland Act, and was actively engaged in the <a href="/wiki/Irish_War_of_Independence" title="Irish War of Independence">Irish War of Independence</a>. The Act was intended to deliver <a href="/wiki/Irish_Home_Rule" class="mw-redirect" title="Irish Home Rule">Home Rule</a> in Ireland, with separate parliaments for <a href="/wiki/Southern_Ireland_(1921%E2%80%9322)" title="Southern Ireland (1921\xe2\x80\x9322)">Southern Ireland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>. Six of the thirty-two <a href="/wiki/Counties_of_Ireland" title="Counties of Ireland">counties of Ireland</a> were assigned to Northern Ireland, and the rest of Ireland comprising 26 counties to Southern Ireland.\n</p><p>The conclusion of the <a href="/wiki/Irish_War_of_Independence" title="Irish War of Independence">Irish War of Independence</a>, and the subsequent signing of the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Irish_Treaty" title="Anglo-Irish Treaty">Anglo-Irish Treaty</a>, led to the creation of the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Free_State" title="Irish Free State">Irish Free State</a> \xe2\x80\x93 a <a href="/wiki/Dominion" title="Dominion">dominion</a> established for the whole island of Ireland on 6 December 1922. The border became an international frontier after the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Northern_Ireland" title="Parliament of Northern Ireland">Parliament of Northern Ireland</a> exercised <a href="/wiki/Irish_Free_State#Northern_Ireland_opts_out" title="Irish Free State">its right to opt out of the Free State</a> on 7 December 1922. The partition of 1921 created only a provisional boundary; <a href="/wiki/Boundary_Commission_(Ireland)" class="mw-redirect" title="Boundary Commission (Ireland)">a Boundary Commission</a> met in 1924\xe2\x80\x9325 to fix a permanent border between the two jurisdictions "in accordance with the wishes of the inhabitants, so far as may be compatible with economic and geographic conditions".<sup id="cite_ref-rptbc_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rptbc-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The manner in which the Boundary Commission clause was drafted in the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Irish_Treaty" title="Anglo-Irish Treaty">Anglo-Irish Treaty</a> was only explicit in its ambiguity.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceC_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceC-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Amongst politicians in Southern Ireland, there was remarkably little attention paid to the clause during the debates on the Treaty. The Republican activist <a href="/wiki/Sean_MacEntee" class="mw-redirect" title="Sean MacEntee">Sean MacEntee</a> was a "lone voice" in warning that the commission would involve an exercise "in transferring from the jurisdiction of the Government of Northern Ireland certain people and certain districts which that Government cannot govern; and by giving instead to Northern Ireland, certain other districts\xe2\x80\x94unionist districts of <a href="/wiki/County_Monaghan" title="County Monaghan">Monaghan</a>, <a href="/wiki/County_Cavan" title="County Cavan">Cavan</a> and <a href="/wiki/County_Donegal" title="County Donegal">Donegal</a>, so that not only under this Treaty are we going to partition Ireland, not only are we going to partition Ulster, but we are going to partition even the counties of Ulster."<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The interim boundary was formalised in December 1925 by an inter-government agreement that was ratified by the three parliaments in London, Dublin and Belfast, without changes from the 1920 demarcation lines.<sup id="cite_ref-rptbc_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rptbc-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The border agreement was then lodged with the <a href="/wiki/League_of_Nations" title="League of Nations">League of Nations</a> on 8 February 1926, making it a matter of <a href="/wiki/International_law" title="International law">international law</a>. The Boundary Commission\'s report was superseded by events and so it was not published until 1969.<sup id="cite_ref-rptbc_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rptbc-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Irish Free State <a href="/wiki/Names_of_the_Irish_state" title="Names of the Irish state">was renamed <i>Ireland</i></a> (<a href="/wiki/Irish_language" title="Irish language">Irish</a>: <i lang="ga">\xc3\x89ire</i>) by the <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_Ireland" title="Constitution of Ireland">1937 constitution</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland_Act_1948" title="Republic of Ireland Act 1948">Republic of Ireland Act 1948</a> formally declared that the state was a republic with the official description <i>Republic of Ireland</i> while not changing its name, which remains <i>Ireland</i>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Customs_and_identity_checks">Customs and identity checks</span><span The Republic of Ireland begins just in front of the speed signs and lies to the north of Northern Ireland at this location.</div></div></div>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Common_Travel_Area" title="Common Travel Area">Common Travel Area</a></div>\n<p>Customs controls were introduced on the frontier on 1 April 1923, shortly after the establishment of the Irish Free State.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> These controls were maintained, with varying degrees of severity, until 1 January 1993, when systematic customs checks were abolished between European Community member states as part of the <a href="/wiki/Single_European_Act" title="Single European Act">single market</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> There are no longer any operational customs posts along either side of the border. Except during a brief period during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, it has never been necessary for Irish or British citizens to produce a passport to cross the border. However during the 1970s <a href="/wiki/The_Troubles" title="The Troubles">troubles</a>, security forces regularly asked travellers for identification.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Military_checkpoints">Military checkpoints</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Republic_of_Ireland%E2%80%93United_Kingdom_border&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Military checkpoints">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>During <a href="/wiki/The_Troubles" title="The Troubles">the Troubles</a> in Northern Ireland, there were British military checkpoints on main border crossings and UK security forces made the remaining crossings impassable. By about 2005, in phase with <a href="/wiki/Good_Friday_Agreement#History_of_implementation" title="Good Friday Agreement">implementation</a> of the 1998 <a href="/wiki/Good_Friday_Agreement" title="Good Friday Agreement">Good Friday Agreement</a>, remaining controls were definitively removed.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dropped_proposals_to_reinstate_border_controls">Dropped proposals to reinstate border controls</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Republic_of_Ireland%E2%80%93United_Kingdom_border&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Dropped proposals to reinstate border controls">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In October 2007, details began to emerge of a <a href="/wiki/British_Government" class="mw-redirect" title="British Government">British Government</a> plan that might end the Common Travel Area encompassing the United Kingdom and Ireland (and also the Isle of Man and Channel Islands) in 2009, possibly creating an anomalous position for Northern Ireland in the process.<sup id="cite_ref-Sharrock_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sharrock-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> In a statement to <a href="/wiki/D%C3%A1il_%C3%89ireann" title="D\xc3\xa1il \xc3\x89ireann">D\xc3\xa1il \xc3\x89ireann</a>, the Irish <a href="/wiki/Taoiseach" title="Taoiseach">Taoiseach</a> <a href="/wiki/Bertie_Ahern" title="Bertie Ahern">Bertie Ahern</a> assured the House that "British authorities have no plans whatsoever to introduce any controls on the land border between North and South. I want to make that clear. All they are looking at is increased cross-border cooperation, targeting illegal immigrants."<sup id="cite_ref-Sharrock_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sharrock-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> This immediately raised concerns north of the border. <a href="/wiki/Jim_Allister" title="Jim Allister">Jim Allister</a>, a former member of the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Unionist_Party" title="Democratic Unionist Party">Democratic Unionist Party</a> and then a <a href="/wiki/Member_of_the_European_Parliament" title="Member of the European Parliament">Member of the European Parliament</a>, told <i><a href="/wiki/The_Times" title="The Times">The Times</a></i> that it would be "intolerable and preposterous if citizens of the UK had to present a passport to enter another part of the UK".<sup id="cite_ref-Sharrock_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sharrock-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In July 2008, the UK and Irish governments announced their intent to resume controls over their common border and the Common Travel Area in general. Each proposed to introduce detailed passport control over travellers from the other state, where travel is by air or sea.<sup id="cite_ref-times-25July2008_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-times-25July2008-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> However, the land border will be \'lightly controlled\'.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> In a joint statement, <a href="/wiki/Jacqui_Smith" title="Jacqui Smith">Jacqui Smith</a>, the British Home Secretary, and <a href="/wiki/Dermot_Ahern" title="Dermot Ahern">Dermot Ahern</a>, the Irish Justice Minister, said:\n</p>\n<blockquote><p> It is crucial that our two countries work closely together to ensure our borders are stronger than ever. Both governments fully recognise the particular circumstances of Northern Ireland. Both governments reaffirm that they have no plans to introduce fixed controls on either side of the Irish land border.<sup id="cite_ref-times-25July2008_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-times-25July2008-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p></blockquote>\n<p><i>The Times</i> reported that another consultation paper was to be published in the autumn of 2008 on whether people travelling between Northern Ireland and the rest of the United Kingdom should be subject to further checks.\n</p>\n<blockquote><p>One proposal is expected to suggest extending the electronic borders scheme, requiring travellers from Northern Ireland to provide their personal details in advance. This would mean residents of one part of the UK being treated differently from others when travelling within the country, something to which Unionists would object.<sup id="cite_ref-times-25July2008_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-times-25July2008-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p></blockquote>\n<p>However, in 2011, the governments renewed the \'de facto\' agreement.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_2011_inter-government_agreement">The 2011 inter-government agreement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Republic_of_Ireland%E2%80%93United_Kingdom_border&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: The 2011 inter-government agreement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>2011 marked the first public agreement between the UK  and Irish governments concerning the maintenance of the Common Travel Area. Officially entitled the "Joint Statement Regarding Co-Operation on Measures to Secure the External Common Travel Area Border", it was signed in Dublin on 20 December 2011 by the UK\'s immigration minister, <a href="/wiki/Damian_Green" title="Damian Green">Damian Green</a> and Ireland\'s Minister for Justice, <a href="/wiki/Alan_Shatter" title="Alan Shatter">Alan Shatter</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Traffic">Traffic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Republic_of_Ireland%E2%80%93United_Kingdom_border&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Traffic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The border is linked with 200 border crossing points (to 300).<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Every month around 177,000 lorries, 208,000 vans, and 1,850,000 cars cross the border.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Around 30,000 people cross the border daily to travel to work.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Troubles">The Troubles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Republic_of_Ireland%E2%80%93United_Kingdom_border&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: The Troubles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/The_Troubles" title="The Troubles">The Troubles</a></div>\n<p>The Troubles in Northern Ireland required that attempts were made from the early 1970s until the late 1990s to enforce border controls. Many smaller cross-border roads were cratered or blockaded with the intention of making them impassable to regular traffic. Bridges were also destroyed to prevent access at unauthorised border crossings (known officially as "unapproved roads"). In particular, the border area in south <a href="/wiki/County_Armagh" title="County Armagh">County Armagh</a> was dominated by <a href="/wiki/British_Army" title="British Army">British Army</a> surveillance posts. <a href="/wiki/Derry" title="Derry">Derry</a>, the second-largest city in Northern Ireland, is close to the border with <a href="/wiki/County_Donegal" title="County Donegal">County Donegal</a>. This meant that there was a heavy security presence around the city, often impeding traffic and general movement between Derry City and County Donegal. Despite these measures, the border was simply too long and had too many minor access roads to enable control of all cross-border movements. In any case, authorised crossing-points on the border remained open to civilian traffic in both directions at all times although vehicles and their occupants were subject to detailed searches while some crossing points were closed to vehicle traffic at night when customs posts were unstaffed.\n</p><p>Difficulty in patrolling parts of the border and large taxation/currency differences (particularly during the 1980s) led to widespread smuggling. However, greater European integration has led to roughly similar tax rates on most items and easing of restrictions on cross-border trade. Smuggling nowadays is mostly limited to fuel, livestock and a seasonal trade in illegal fireworks, which are strictly regulated in Ireland \xe2\x80\x93 in both countries there are restrictions on the types which can be used and a licence is required to possess or use fireworks, but in Ireland such licences are seldom issued to private individuals.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>While it still exists <i><a href="/wiki/De_jure" title="De jure">de jure</a></i>, the border presents <a href="#Identification">no impediments</a> to traffic in either direction. This has been mainly due to the <a href="/wiki/Common_Travel_Area" title="Common Travel Area">Common Travel Area</a> between Ireland and the United Kingdom, as well as a sharp reduction in cross-border activity by <a href="/wiki/Paramilitary" title="Paramilitary">paramilitaries</a>. EU integration has also played a part. Following the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_peace_process" title="Northern Ireland peace process">Northern Ireland peace process</a>, military surveillance has been replaced by routine <a href="/wiki/Police_Service_of_Northern_Ireland" title="Police Service of Northern Ireland">PSNI</a> patrols.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Status_post-Brexit">Status post-Brexit</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Republic_of_Ireland%E2%80%93United_Kingdom_border&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Status post-Brexit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Irish_border_question" title="Irish border question">Irish border question</a></div>\n<p><span id="brexit"></span>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:262px;"><a href="/wiki/File:No_Border_No_Barrier.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/No_Border_No_Barrier.jpg/260px-No_Border_No_Barrier.jpg" width="260" height="173" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/No_Border_No_Barrier.jpg/390px-No_Border_No_Barrier.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/No_Border_No_Barrier.jpg/520px-No_Border_No_Barrier.jpg 2x" data-file-width="5184" data-file-height="3456" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:No_Border_No_Barrier.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/Sinn_F%C3%A9in" title="Sinn F\xc3\xa9in">Sinn F\xc3\xa9in</a> protest against a hard border. Post-Brexit border controls are a controversial issue.</div></div></div>\n<p>The UK voted to leave the European Union in a <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_European_Union_membership_referendum,_2016" title="United Kingdom European Union membership referendum, 2016">referendum on 23 June 2016</a>. Their <a href="/wiki/Brexit" title="Brexit">withdrawal</a> would effectively make the Republic of Ireland-Northern Ireland border an <a href="/wiki/External_border_of_the_European_Union" title="External border of the European Union">external EU border</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Smith_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Smith-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> The Irish government, the UK government and EU representatives, have all stated that they do not wish for a hard border in Ireland due to the sensitive nature of the border.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In order to forestall this the European Union proposed a "backstop agreement" within the Withdrawal Agreement that would put Northern Ireland under a range of EU rules in order to forestall the need for border checks.  This has been opposed by the British government as producing an effective border between Great Britain and Northern Ireland.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> \n</p><p>As well as immigration and trade, a range of other issues of cross border cooperation, such as on health,<sup id="cite_ref-nibma_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nibma-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> have been raised.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distinctive_physical_characteristics">Distinctive physical characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Republic_of_Ireland%E2%80%93United_Kingdom_border&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Distinctive physical characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Northern_Ireland.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Northern_Ireland.png/300px-Northern_Ireland.png" width="300" height="225" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Northern_Ireland.png/450px-Northern_Ireland.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Northern_Ireland.png/600px-Northern_Ireland.png 2x" data-file-width="1613" data-file-height="1210" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Northern_Ireland.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Map of Northern Ireland and the border</div></div></div>\n<p>The border is extremely irregular. Its irregularities were inherited from well-established county boundaries.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceC_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceC-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\nThe 499&#160;km (310&#160;mi)<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> border is not explicitly described in statute but only implicitly inferred from the territorial definition of Northern\nIreland as contained in the 1920 Government of Ireland Act. It states that "Northern Ireland shall consist of the parliamentary counties of Antrim, Armagh, Down, Fermanagh, Londonderry and Tyrone, and the parliamentary boroughs of Belfast and Londonderry, and Southern Ireland shall consist of\nso much of Ireland as is not\ncomprised within the said parliamentary counties and boroughs."<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> The border has some distinctive features. Firstly, how irregular it is: the border is about four  times the  straight-line distance  between Lough Foyle and Carlingford Lough.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceC_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceC-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>  Second, County Donegal is only connected to the rest of the Republic of Ireland by a territorial  <a href="/wiki/Isthmus" title="Isthmus">isthmus</a>  9&#160;km (6&#160;mi)  wide, making it more convenient for some journeys from Donegal to other parts of the Republic of Ireland to pass through Northern Ireland. Third, for only two relatively short sections of the border does it align with the provincial boundary of nine county <a href="/wiki/Ulster" title="Ulster">Ulster</a> between Fermanagh-Leitrim and Armagh-Louth. And lastly, as the border skirts Tyrone to the west and again to the south east, it encompasses Co. Fermanagh on three sides.\n</p><p>As a remnant of 17th century county limits, the border\'s physical profile indicates that it follows many water courses but only in the highlands of the Cavan-Fermanagh section could the border be said to accord with any significant physical impediments to movement.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceC_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceC-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> There are around 200 road border crossings. Some roads cross the border twice or three times. The road <a href="/wiki/N54_road_(Ireland)" title="N54 road (Ireland)">N54</a>/<a href="/wiki/A3_road_(Northern_Ireland)" title="A3 road (Northern Ireland)">A3</a> crosses the border four times within 10&#160;km/6 miles. Before its closure in 1957, a section of one railway line between <a href="/wiki/Clones,_County_Monaghan" title="Clones, County Monaghan">Clones, County Monaghan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cavan" title="Cavan">Cavan</a> crossed the border six times in eight miles, initially with customs checks at each crossing.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Maritime_border">Maritime border</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Republic_of_Ireland%E2%80%93United_Kingdom_border&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Maritime border">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>A 1988 treaty demarcates the boundary of the <a href="/wiki/Exclusive_economic_zone" title="Exclusive economic zone">exclusive economic zones</a> across the <a href="/wiki/Continental_shelf" title="Continental shelf">continental shelf</a> from the south Irish Sea southwest to the <a href="/wiki/Celtic_Sea" title="Celtic Sea">Celtic Sea</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Waters_around_Northern_Ireland">Waters around Northern Ireland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Republic_of_Ireland%E2%80%93United_Kingdom_border&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Waters around Northern Ireland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The exact division of territorial waters between Northern Ireland and <a href="/wiki/Southern_Ireland_(1921%E2%80%9322)" title="Southern Ireland (1921\xe2\x80\x9322)">Southern Ireland</a> (\'Southern Ireland\' being coterminous with the territory of the modern-day Irish state) was a matter of some controversy from the outset. Section 1(2) of the Government of Ireland Act 1920 defined the respective territories of Northern Ireland and the then Southern Ireland as follows:<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>...Northern Ireland shall consist of the parliamentary counties of Antrim, Armagh, Down, Fermanagh, Londonderry and Tyrone, and the parliamentary boroughs of Belfast and Londonderry, and Southern Ireland shall consist of so much of Ireland as is not comprised within the said parliamentary counties and boroughs.\n</p></blockquote>\n<p>At the time of that Act, both Northern Ireland and Southern Ireland were to remain parts of the United Kingdom. Perhaps because of this, the Act did not explicitly address the position of territorial waters although Section 11(4) provided that neither Southern Ireland nor Northern Ireland would have any competence to make laws in respect of "Lighthouses, buoys, or beacons (except so far as they can consistently with any general Act of the Parliament of the United Kingdom be constructed or maintained by a local harbour authority)".\n</p><p>When the territory that initially was Southern Ireland ultimately became a separate self-governing dominion outside the United Kingdom known as the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Free_State" title="Irish Free State">Irish Free State</a>, the status of the territorial waters naturally took on a significance it had not had before. Northern Ireland\'s Unionists were conscious of this matter from an early stage. They were keen to put it beyond doubt that the territorial waters around Northern Ireland would not belong to the Irish Free State. In this regard, <a href="/wiki/James_Craig,_1st_Viscount_Craigavon" title="James Craig, 1st Viscount Craigavon">James Craig</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Northern_Ireland" title="Prime Minister of Northern Ireland">Prime Minister of Northern Ireland</a> put the following question in the British House of Commons on 27 November 1922 (the month before the establishment of the Irish Free State):<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Another important matter on which I should like a statement of the Government\'s intentions, is with regard to the territorial waters surrounding Ulster. Under the Act of 1920, the areas handed over to the Governments of Northern Ireland and Southern Ireland respectively, were defined as the six Parliamentary counties of Northern Ireland and the twenty-six Parliamentary counties of Southern Ireland. I understand there is considerable doubt in the minds of lawyers and others as to whether these Parliamentary counties carry with them the ordinary territorial waters, extending three miles out from the shore. It has been asserted in some quarters that the Parliamentary counties only extend to low water mark. That has been exercising the minds of a good many people in Ulster, and I shall be glad if the Government in due course will inform the House what is their opinion on the subject and what steps they are taking to make it clear..... Am I to understand that the Law Officers have actually considered this question, and that they have given a decision in favour of the theory that the territorial waters go with the counties that were included in the six counties of Northern Ireland?\n</p></blockquote>\n<p>In response the Attorney General, Sir <a href="/wiki/Douglas_Hogg,_1st_Viscount_Hailsham" title="Douglas Hogg, 1st Viscount Hailsham">Douglas Hogg</a>, said that "I have considered the question, and I have given an opinion that that is so [i.e. the territorial waters do go with the counties]".\n</p><p>However, this interpretation that the territorial waters went with the counties was later disputed by Irish governments. A good summary of the Irish position was given by the then Taoiseach, <a href="/wiki/Jack_Lynch" title="Jack Lynch">Jack Lynch</a>, during a <a href="/wiki/D%C3%A1il" class="mw-redirect" title="D\xc3\xa1il">D\xc3\xa1il</a> debate on 29 February 1972:\n</p>\n<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>...[W]e claim that the territorial waters around the whole island of Ireland are ours and our claim to the territorial waters around Northern Ireland is based on the Government of Ireland Act of 1920. This Act is so referred to in the 1921 Treaty that the Northern Ireland which withdrew from the Irish Free State is identical with the Northern Ireland defined in the Government of Ireland Act, 1920, and defined as consisting of named counties and boroughs. It is, I think, common case between us that in English law the counties do not include adjacent territorial waters and, therefore, according to our claim these territorial waters were retained by the Irish Free State.\n</p></blockquote>\n<p>A particular dispute arose between the Government of the Irish Free State of the one part and the Northern Ireland and UK governments of the other part over territorial waters in <a href="/wiki/Lough_Foyle" title="Lough Foyle">Lough Foyle</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-books.google.ie_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-books.google.ie-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> Lough Foyle lies between <a href="/wiki/County_Londonderry" title="County Londonderry">County Londonderry</a> in Northern Ireland and <a href="/wiki/County_Donegal" title="County Donegal">County Donegal</a> in the then Irish Free State. A court case in the Free State in 1923 relating to fishing rights in Lough Foyle held that the Free State\'s territorial waters ran right up to the shore of County Londonderry.<sup id="cite_ref-books.google.ie_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-books.google.ie-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> In 1927, illegal fishing on Lough Foyle had become so grave that Northern Ireland Prime Minister James Craig entered into correspondence with his Free State counterpart, <a href="/wiki/W._T._Cosgrave" title="W. T. Cosgrave">W. T. Cosgrave</a>. Craig indicated to Cosgrave that he proposed to introduce a Bill giving the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Ulster_Constabulary" title="Royal Ulster Constabulary">Royal Ulster Constabulary</a> powers to stop and search vessels on Lough Foyle. Cosgrave asserted all of Lough Foyle was Free State territory and that as such a Bill of that nature would be rejected by the Free State and its introduction would create "a very serious situation".<sup id="cite_ref-books.google.ie_30-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-books.google.ie-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> Cosgrave then raised the matter with the British government. Claims by <a href="/wiki/The_Honourable_The_Irish_Society" title="The Honourable The Irish Society">The Honourable The Irish Society</a> that its fishing rights in the Foyle were being violated by poachers from the Donegal side led to the 1952 creation of the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Foyle_Fisheries_Commission&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Foyle Fisheries Commission (page does not exist)">Foyle Fisheries Commission</a> under the joint authority of the Dublin and Belfast governments, which solved problems of jurisdiction without addressing the sovereignty question. The Commission was superseded by the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Foyle,_Carlingford_and_Irish_Lights_Commission&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Foyle, Carlingford and Irish Lights Commission (page does not exist)">Foyle, Carlingford and Irish Lights Commission</a> established after the <a href="/wiki/Good_Friday_Agreement" title="Good Friday Agreement">Good Friday Agreement</a> under the remit of the <a href="/wiki/North/South_Ministerial_Council" title="North/South Ministerial Council">North/South Ministerial Council</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Symmons2009_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Symmons2009-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>It is not evident that the territorial waters generally remain disputed in practice between the two states<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;a&#93;</a></sup> but the precise  <a href="/wiki/Maritime_boundary" title="Maritime boundary">maritime boundary</a> between Ireland and the United Kingdom concerning Lough Foyle (and similarly <a href="/wiki/Carlingford_Lough" title="Carlingford Lough">Carlingford Lough</a>) remains disputed in principle. As recently as 2005, when asked to list those areas of EU member states where border definition is in dispute, a British Government minister responding for the <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_Foreign_and_Commonwealth_Affairs" title="Secretary of State for Foreign and Commonwealth Affairs">Secretary of State for Foreign and Commonwealth Affairs</a> stated:\n</p>\n<dl><dd>Border definition (ie the demarcation of borders between two internationally recognised sovereign states with an adjoining territorial or <a href="/wiki/Maritime_border" class="mw-redirect" title="Maritime border">maritime border</a>) is politically disputed [between] Ireland [and the] UK (Lough Foyle, Carlingford Lough\xe2\x80\x94quiescent)<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup></dd></dl>\n<p>During D\xc3\xa1il debates on the Carlingford Fisheries Bill, a contributor to the debate stated that he welcomed "the Bill\'s aim of defining the area of jurisdiction over the Foyle."<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> However, the Irish "Foyle and Carlingford Fisheries Act 2007" does not mention this issue;<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> neither does the virtually identical British "Foyle and Carlingford Fisheries (Northern Ireland) Order 2007":<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>  each merely refers to "the Commission\'s waters".\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Identification">Identification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Republic_of_Ireland%E2%80%93United_Kingdom_border&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Identification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Kmh_warning_sign.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Kmh_warning_sign.jpg/220px-Kmh_warning_sign.jpg" width="220" height="157" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Kmh_warning_sign.jpg/330px-Kmh_warning_sign.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Kmh_warning_sign.jpg/440px-Kmh_warning_sign.jpg 2x" data-file-width="624" data-file-height="445" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Kmh_warning_sign.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A bilingual traffic sign in <a href="/wiki/County_Louth" title="County Louth">County Louth</a>, in the Republic of Ireland, warning drivers travelling south across the border that metric speed limits are used in ROI, whereas the UK uses imperial units in driving.</div></div></div>\n<p>It is estimated that there are 300 major and minor crossings along the 310-mile (499&#160;km) border.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> The border is marked only by a small number of "Welcome To Northern Ireland" road signs on the UK side, placed there by the Northern Ireland Roads Service.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> This can make identifying the border difficult for those unfamiliar with landmarks known to locals as the crossing point. At some crossings, there are signs welcoming visitors to the relevant local government authority district or, occasionally, reminding motorists of the need to ensure that their insurance is valid in the relevant jurisdiction. \n</p><p>Generally, signposts in Ireland which indicate distances to destinations are bilingual (in Irish and English) and give distances in kilometres while such signposts in Northern Ireland are only in English and give distances in miles. On larger roads, the approximate location of the border can be determined by signs reminding the driver of the change in units. In Northern Ireland, place-name and street/road-name signs are usually (but not always) English-only and street/road-name signs are more standardised and extensively used.\n</p><p>There are other immediate indicators when crossing the border: differences in the design of road signage and a change in road markings. The hard shoulder on non-motorway roads in Ireland is marked with a yellow, usually broken, line. The same marking in Northern Ireland is white and usually continuous. In Northern Ireland, roads use A (major) and B (minor) route prefixes, whereas Ireland\'s route prefixes are N (major, standing for <i>national</i>) and R (minor, standing for <i>regional</i>). <a href="/wiki/Road_signs_in_Ireland" title="Road signs in Ireland">Road signs in Ireland</a> are mostly black/yellow and diamond shaped (similar to those in North America and <a href="/wiki/Australasia" title="Australasia">Australasia</a>) whereas <a href="/wiki/Road_signs_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Road signs in the United Kingdom">those in the UK</a> are mainly black/white/red triangles or circles (the same as the rest of the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>). Signs also have subtle differences in colouring and fonts.\n</p><p>Since the adoption by Ireland of <a href="/wiki/Metric_system" title="Metric system">metric</a> speed limits, warning signs have been placed on either side of the border to alert motorists to the change to or from <a href="/wiki/Miles_per_hour" title="Miles per hour">miles</a> or <a href="/wiki/Kilometres_per_hour" title="Kilometres per hour">kilometres per hour</a>. As the United Kingdom does not use the <a href="/wiki/Euro" title="Euro">euro</a>, advertised prices for service stations and shops will change currency on crossing, although many places along the border will accept cross-border currency informally (albeit usually at a rate favourable to the trader). Other typical signs of crossing a European border are also noticeable. These include subtle differences in the paving materials of road surfaces and pavements, sometimes with visible differences in colour between materials across the border, changes to street lighting when crossing the border, (though these also vary across intra-county borders), and changes in the colour of postboxes (green in Ireland, red in Northern Ireland).\n</p><p>By rail, there is no immediate sign of crossing the border, but the trackside mileage markers change from Irish-style<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (December 2011)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> markers at the 59\xc2\xbe-mile post (from <a href="/wiki/Dublin_Connolly_railway_station" title="Dublin Connolly railway station">Dublin Connolly railway station</a>) to black-on-yellow markers, common to the United Kingdom, at the 60-mile (97&#160;km) post, between <a href="/wiki/Dundalk_railway_station" title="Dundalk railway station">Dundalk</a> and <a href="/wiki/Newry_railway_station" title="Newry railway station">Newry</a> stations.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mobile_phone_roaming_charges">Mobile phone roaming charges</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Republic_of_Ireland%E2%80%93United_Kingdom_border&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Mobile phone roaming charges">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>As in most places, radio signals from the cellular networks on both sides of the border often travel several kilometres (a few miles) across it. This is a source of annoyance to those resident in border areas as <a href="/wiki/Roaming" title="Roaming">roaming</a> charges are incurred with most service providers if the phone connects to the "wrong" network when making or even receiving a call.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Mobile phone roaming charges <a href="/wiki/European_Union_roaming_regulations" title="European Union roaming regulations">were abolished in the European Union</a> from 15 June 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> Whether this arrangement will continue after <a href="/wiki/Brexit" title="Brexit">Brexit</a> is currently unclear.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_references">Cultural references</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Republic_of_Ireland%E2%80%93United_Kingdom_border&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Cultural references">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><i><a href="/wiki/Puckoon" title="Puckoon">Puckoon</a></i> is a <a href="/wiki/Comic_novel" title="Comic novel">comic novel</a> by <a href="/wiki/Spike_Milligan" title="Spike Milligan">Spike Milligan</a>, first published in 1963.
