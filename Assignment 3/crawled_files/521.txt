Wales</b> forms a distinctive <a href="/wiki/Polity" title="Polity">polity</a> in the wider <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Politics of the United Kingdom">politics of the United Kingdom</a>, with <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a> as one of the four constituent <a href="/wiki/Countries_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Countries of the United Kingdom">countries of the United Kingdom</a> (UK).\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/UK_constitution" class="mw-redirect" title="UK constitution">Constitutionally</a>, the United Kingdom is <i><a href="/wiki/De_jure" title="De jure">de jure</a></i> a <a href="/wiki/Unitary_state" title="Unitary state">unitary state</a> with one <a href="/wiki/Sovereignty" title="Sovereignty">sovereign</a> parliament and government. However, under a system of <a href="/wiki/Devolution" title="Devolution">devolution</a> (or home rule) adopted in the late 1990s three of the four constituent parts of the United Kingdom, Wales, <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>, voted for limited <a href="/wiki/Self-government" class="mw-redirect" title="Self-government">self-government</a>, subject to the ability of the <a href="/wiki/UK_Parliament" class="mw-redirect" title="UK Parliament">UK Parliament</a> in <a href="/wiki/Palace_of_Westminster" title="Palace of Westminster">Westminster</a>, nominally at will, to amend, change, broaden or abolish the national governmental systems. As such the <a href="/wiki/National_Assembly_for_Wales" title="National Assembly for Wales">National Assembly for Wales</a> (<i>Cynulliad Cenedlaethol Cymru</i>) is not <i>de jure</i> <a href="/wiki/Sovereignty" title="Sovereignty">sovereign</a>.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Executive_power" class="mw-redirect" title="Executive power">Executive power</a> in the United Kingdom is vested in the <a href="/wiki/Queen-in-Council" class="mw-redirect" title="Queen-in-Council">Queen-in-Council</a>, while <a href="/wiki/Legislative_power" class="mw-redirect" title="Legislative power">legislative power</a> is vested in the <a href="/wiki/Queen-in-Parliament" title="Queen-in-Parliament">Queen-in-Parliament</a> (the Crown and the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Parliament of the United Kingdom">Parliament of the United Kingdom</a> at <a href="/wiki/Palace_of_Westminster" title="Palace of Westminster">Westminster</a> in <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>). The <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Wales_Act_1998" title="Government of Wales Act 1998">Government of Wales Act 1998</a> established devolution in Wales, and certain executive and legislative powers have been constitutionally delegated to the National Assembly for Wales. The scope of these powers was further widened by the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Wales_Act_2006" title="Government of Wales Act 2006">Government of Wales Act 2006</a>.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" href="/w/index.php?title=Politics_of_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: BONN 17">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Northern_Ireland" title="Government of Northern Ireland">Government of Northern Ireland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Scotland" class="mw-redirect" title="Government of Scotland">Government of Scotland</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Governance_of_England" title="Governance of England">Governance of England</a></div>\n<p>The government of Wales, since 1998, composed of the <a href="/wiki/Welsh_National_Assembly" class="mw-redirect" title="Welsh National Assembly">Welsh National Assembly</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Welsh_Assembly_Government" class="mw-redirect" title="Welsh Assembly Government">Welsh Assembly Government</a> (styled Welsh Government).\n</p><p>But judicially it remains within part of the jurisdiction of <a href="/wiki/England_and_Wales" title="England and Wales">England and Wales</a>, although the Welsh National  Assembly was given the rights to enact Wales-specific <a href="/wiki/Measure_of_the_National_Assembly_for_Wales" title="Measure of the National Assembly for Wales">Measures</a>, and, with the <a href="/wiki/Welsh_devolution_referendum,_2011" title="Welsh devolution referendum, 2011">Welsh devolution referendum, 2011</a>, the Welsh National Assembly will be given the power to enact <a href="/wiki/Act_of_the_National_Assembly_for_Wales" title="Act of the National Assembly for Wales">Acts</a>.\n</p><p>Wales, together with Cheshire, used to have <a href="/wiki/Assizes" title="Assizes">Court of Grand Session</a>, and therefore not within the English circuit court system. Yet it has not been its own jurisdiction since King Edward I of England conquered the country, at which point Welsh Law was replaced by English Law in the north and Marcher Law in the south.\n</p><p>Before 1998, there was no separate government in Wales. Executive authority rested in the hands of the <a href="/wiki/HM_Government" class="mw-redirect" title="HM Government">HM Government</a>, with substantial authority within the <a href="/wiki/Welsh_Office" title="Welsh Office">Welsh Office</a> since 1965. Legislative power rested within the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Parliament of the United Kingdom">Parliament of the United Kingdom</a>. Judicial power has always been with the  <a href="/wiki/Courts_of_England_and_Wales" title="Courts of England and Wales">Courts of England and Wales</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Supreme Court of the United Kingdom">Supreme Court of the United Kingdom</a> (or its predecessor the <a href="/wiki/Law_Lords" class="mw-redirect" title="Law Lords">Law Lords</a>).\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_emergence_of_a_Welsh_politics">The emergence of a Welsh politics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Politics_of_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The emergence of a Welsh politics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>After the <a href="/wiki/Laws_in_Wales_Acts_1535%E2%80%931542" class="mw-redirect" title="Laws in Wales Acts 1535\xe2\x80\x931542">Laws in Wales Acts 1535\xe2\x80\x931542</a>, Wales was treated in legal terms as part of <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>.  The <a href="/wiki/Wales_and_Berwick_Act_1746" title="Wales and Berwick Act 1746">Wales and Berwick Act 1746</a> stated that all laws applying to England would also be applicable to Wales, unless the body of the law explicitly stated otherwise.  However, during the latter part of the 19th century and early part of the 20th century the notion of a distinctive Welsh polity gained credence.  In 1881 the <a href="/wiki/Sunday_Closing_(Wales)_Act_1881" title="Sunday Closing (Wales) Act 1881">Welsh Sunday Closing Act</a> was passed, the first such legislation exclusively concerned with Wales.  The Central Welsh Board was established in 1896 to inspect the grammar schools set up under the Welsh Intermediate Education Act 1889, and a separate Welsh Department of the Board of Education was formed in 1907.  The Agricultural Council for Wales was set up in 1912, and the <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Agriculture,_Fisheries_and_Food_(United_Kingdom)" title="Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food (United Kingdom)">Ministry of Agriculture and Fisheries</a> had its own Welsh Office from 1919.<sup id="cite_ref-archives_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-archives-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Despite the failure of popular political movements such as <a href="/wiki/Cymru_Fydd" title="Cymru Fydd">Cymru Fydd</a>, a number of institutions, such as the <a href="/wiki/National_Eisteddfod" class="mw-redirect" title="National Eisteddfod">National Eisteddfod</a> (1861), the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Wales" title="University of Wales">University of Wales</a> (<i>Prifysgol Cymru</i>) (1893), the <a href="/wiki/National_Library_of_Wales" title="National Library of Wales">National Library of Wales</a> (<i>Llyfrgell Genedlaethol Cymru</i>) (1911) and the <a href="/wiki/Welsh_Guards" title="Welsh Guards">Welsh Guards</a> (<i>Gwarchodlu Cymreig</i>) (1915) were created. The campaign for <a href="/wiki/Church_in_Wales#Disestablishment" title="Church in Wales">disestablishment of the Anglican Church</a> in Wales, achieved by the passage of the <a href="/wiki/Welsh_Church_Act_1914" title="Welsh Church Act 1914">Welsh Church Act 1914</a> (effective from 1920), was also significant in the development of Welsh political consciousness.  Without a popular base, the issue of <a href="/wiki/Home_rule" title="Home rule">home rule</a> did not feature as an issue in subsequent General Elections and was quickly eclipsed by the depression. By August 1925 unemployment in Wales rose to 28.5%, in contrast to the economic boom in the early 1920s, rendering constitutional debate an exotic subject.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> In the same year <a href="/wiki/Plaid_Cymru" title="Plaid Cymru">Plaid Cymru</a> was formed with the goal of securing a Welsh-speaking Wales.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Following the <a href="/wiki/Second_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Second World War">Second World War</a> the <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">Labour</a> Government of <a href="/wiki/Clement_Attlee" title="Clement Attlee">Clement Attlee</a> established the <a href="/wiki/Council_for_Wales_and_Monmouthshire" title="Council for Wales and Monmouthshire">Council for Wales and Monmouthshire</a>, an unelected assembly of 27 with the brief of advising the UK government on matters of Welsh interest.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>  By that time, most UK government departments had set up their own offices in Wales.<sup id="cite_ref-archives_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-archives-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>  The <a href="/wiki/Wales_Labour_Party" class="mw-redirect" title="Wales Labour Party">Labour Party</a> had also partly reappraised its view to devolution, establishing in 1947 the Welsh Regional Council of Labour from the constituent parts of the party in Wales and as part of a move to plan the economy on an all-Wales basis. However, resistance from other elements of the party meant that the machinery of government was not similarly reformed until much later.\n</p><p>The post of <a href="/wiki/Minister_of_Welsh_Affairs" class="mw-redirect" title="Minister of Welsh Affairs">Minister of Welsh Affairs</a> was first established in 1951, but was at first held by the UK <a href="/wiki/Home_Secretary" title="Home Secretary">Home Secretary</a>.  Further incremental changes also took place, including the establishment of a <a href="/w/index.php?title=Digest_of_Welsh_Statistics&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Digest of Welsh Statistics (page does not exist)">Digest of Welsh Statistics</a> in 1954, and the designation of  <a href="/wiki/Cardiff" title="Cardiff">Cardiff</a> (<i>Caerdydd</i>) as Wales\xe2\x80\x99s capital city in 1955. However, further reforms were catalysed partly as a result of the controversy surrounding the flooding of <a href="/wiki/Capel_Celyn" title="Capel Celyn">Capel Celyn</a> in 1956. Despite almost unanimous Welsh political opposition the scheme had been approved, a fact that seemed to underline Plaid Cymru\'s argument that the Welsh national community was powerless.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>  Welsh nationalism experienced a modest increase in support, with Plaid Cymru\xe2\x80\x99s share of the vote increasing from 0.3% in 1951 to 5.2% by 1959 throughout Wales.\n</p><p>In 1964 the incoming Labour Government of <a href="/wiki/Harold_Wilson" title="Harold Wilson">Harold Wilson</a> created the <a href="/wiki/Welsh_Office" title="Welsh Office">Welsh Office</a> under a <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_Wales" title="Secretary of State for Wales">Secretary of State for Wales</a>, with its powers augmented to include health, agriculture and education in 1968, 1969 and 1970 respectively. The creation of administration devolution effectively defined the territorial governance of modern Wales.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Labour\'s incremental embrace of a distinctive Welsh polity was arguably catalysed in 1966 when Plaid Cymru president <a href="/wiki/Gwynfor_Evans" title="Gwynfor Evans">Gwynfor Evans</a> won the <a href="/wiki/Carmarthen_by-election,_1966" title="Carmarthen by-election, 1966">Carmarthen by-election</a> (although in fact Labour had endorsed plans for an elected council for Wales weeks before the by-election). However, by 1967 Labour retreated from endorsing home rule mainly because of the open hostility expressed by other Welsh Labour MPs to anything "which could be interpreted as a concession to nationalism" and because of opposition by the <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_Scotland" title="Secretary of State for Scotland">Secretary of State for Scotland</a>, who was responding to a growth of <a href="/wiki/Scottish_independence" title="Scottish independence">Scottish nationalism</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In response to the emergence of Plaid Cymru and the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_National_Party" title="Scottish National Party">Scottish National Party</a> (SNP) Harold Wilson\'s Labour Government set up the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Commission_on_the_Constitution_(United_Kingdom)" title="Royal Commission on the Constitution (United Kingdom)">Royal Commission on the Constitution</a> (the Kilbrandon Commission) to investigate the UK\xe2\x80\x99s constitutional arrangements in 1969.<sup id="cite_ref-devolution_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-devolution-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Its eventual recommendations formed the basis of the 1974 <a href="/wiki/White_Paper" class="mw-redirect" title="White Paper">White Paper</a> <i>Democracy and Devolution: proposals for Scotland and Wales</i>.,<sup id="cite_ref-devolution_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-devolution-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> which proposed the creation of a Welsh Assembly. However, voters rejected the proposals by a majority of four to one in a <a href="/wiki/Wales_referendum,_1979" class="mw-redirect" title="Wales referendum, 1979">referendum held in 1979</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-devolution_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-devolution-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The election of a Labour Government in 1997 brought devolution back to the political agenda. In July 1997, the government published a White Paper, <i>A Voice for Wales</i>, which outlined its proposals for devolution, and in September 1997 an elected Assembly with competence over the Welsh Office\xe2\x80\x99s powers was narrowly approved in a <a href="/wiki/Welsh_devolution_referendum,_1997" title="Welsh devolution referendum, 1997">referendum</a>. The <a href="/wiki/National_Assembly_for_Wales" title="National Assembly for Wales">National Assembly for Wales</a> (<i>Cynulliad Cenedlaethol Cymru</i>) was created in 1999, with further authority devolved in 2007, with the creation of a Welsh legal system to adjudicate on specific cases of Welsh law.  Following devolution, the role of the Secretary of State for Wales greatly reduced.  Most of the powers of the Welsh Office were handed over to the National Assembly; the <a href="/wiki/Wales_Office" class="mw-redirect" title="Wales Office">Wales Office</a> was established in 1999 to supersede the Welsh Office and support the Secretary of State.<sup id="cite_ref-archives_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-archives-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Contemporary_Welsh_politics">Contemporary Welsh politics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Politics_of_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Contemporary Welsh politics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_National_Assembly_for_Wales">The National Assembly for Wales</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Politics_of_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The National Assembly for Wales">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Royal_Badge_of_Wales_(2008).svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Royal_Badge_of_Wales_%282008%29.svg/180px-Royal_Badge_of_Wales_%282008%29.svg.png" width="180" height="221" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Royal_Badge_of_Wales_%282008%29.svg/270px-Royal_Badge_of_Wales_%282008%29.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Royal_Badge_of_Wales_%282008%29.svg/360px-Royal_Badge_of_Wales_%282008%29.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="1009" data-file-height="1239" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Royal_Badge_of_Wales_(2008).svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Royal_Badge_of_Wales" title="Royal Badge of Wales">Royal Badge of Wales</a> appears on <a href="/wiki/Measure_of_the_National_Assembly_for_Wales" title="Measure of the National Assembly for Wales">Assembly Measures</a><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>\n<p>The National Assembly for Wales (NAW or NAfW) (<a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">Welsh</a>: <i lang="cy">Cynulliad Cenedlaethol Cymru [CCC]</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Devolution" title="Devolution">devolved</a> parliament  with power to make legislation in Wales. The assembly building is known as the <a href="/wiki/Senedd" title="Senedd">Senedd</a>. Both <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> and <a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">Welsh</a> languages are treated on a basis of equality in the conduct of business in the Assembly.\n</p><p>The Assembly was formed under the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Wales_Act_1998" title="Government of Wales Act 1998">Government of Wales Act 1998</a>, by the Labour government, following a <a href="/wiki/Wales_referendum_1997" class="mw-redirect" title="Wales referendum 1997">referendum</a> in 1997. The campaign for a \'yes\' vote in the referendum was supported by <a href="/wiki/Wales_Labour_Party" class="mw-redirect" title="Wales Labour Party">Welsh Labour</a>, <a href="/wiki/Plaid_Cymru" title="Plaid Cymru">Plaid Cymru</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Democrats_(UK)" title="Liberal Democrats (UK)">Liberal Democrats</a> and much of Welsh civic society, such as church groups and the trade union movement.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Conservative Party (UK)">Conservative Party</a> was the only major political party in Wales to oppose devolution.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The election in 2003 produced an assembly in which half of the assembly seats were held by women. This is thought to be the first time elections to a legislature have produced equal representation for women.<sup id="cite_ref-half_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-half-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The National Assembly consists of 60 elected members. They use the title Assembly Member (AM) or <i>Aelod y Cynulliad</i> (AC).<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The Assembly\'s <a href="/wiki/Presiding_Officer_of_the_National_Assembly_for_Wales" title="Presiding Officer of the National Assembly for Wales">presiding officer</a> is Welsh Labour member <a href="/wiki/Rosemary_Butler_(politician)" title="Rosemary Butler (politician)">Rosemary Butler</a>.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Welsh_Government" title="Welsh Government">Welsh Government</a> is led by First Minister <a href="/wiki/Carwyn_Jones" title="Carwyn Jones">Carwyn Jones</a> of <a href="/wiki/Wales_Labour_Party" class="mw-redirect" title="Wales Labour Party">Welsh Labour</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-cabinet_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cabinet-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The executive and civil servants are based in Cardiff\'s <a href="/wiki/Cathays_Park" title="Cathays Park">Cathays Park</a> while the Assembly Members, the Assembly Parliamentary Service and Ministerial support staff are based in <a href="/wiki/Cardiff_Bay" title="Cardiff Bay">Cardiff Bay</a> where a new \xc2\xa367 million Assembly Building, known as the Senedd, has recently been built.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Until May 2007 one important feature of the Assembly was that there was no legal or constitutional separation of the legislative and executive functions, since it was a single corporate entity. Even compared with other <a href="/wiki/Parliamentary_systems" class="mw-redirect" title="Parliamentary systems">parliamentary systems</a>, and other UK devolved countries, this was highly unusual. In reality however there was day to day separation, and the terms "Assembly Government" and "Assembly Parliamentary Service" were used to distinguish between the two arms. The <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Wales_Act_2006" title="Government of Wales Act 2006">Government of Wales Act 2006</a> regularised the separation once it comes into effect following the 2007 Assembly Election.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Senedd.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/Senedd.JPG/250px-Senedd.JPG" width="250" height="178" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/Senedd.JPG/375px-Senedd.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/Senedd.JPG/500px-Senedd.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2132" data-file-height="1514" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Senedd.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Senedd, home to the <a href="/wiki/National_Assembly_for_Wales" title="National Assembly for Wales">National Assembly for Wales</a>.</div></div></div>\n<p>The Assembly also has limited tax varying and borrowing powers.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> These include powers over Business Rates, Stamp Duty (now Land Transaction Tax), Landfills Tax (now Landfills Disposal Tax) and from 2019 a portion of Income Tax.\n</p><p>In terms of charges for government services it also has some discretion. Notable examples where this discretion has been used and varies significantly to other areas in the UK include:-\n</p>\n<ol><li><i>Charges for NHS prescriptions in Wales</i> - these have been abolished, while patients are still charged in England. Northern Ireland abolished charges in 2010, with Scotland following suit in 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li><i>Charges for University Tuition</i> - are different for Welsh resident students studying at Welsh Universities, compared with students from or studying elsewhere in the UK.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li><i>Charging for Residential Care</i> - In Wales there is a flat rate of contribution towards the cost of nursing care, (roughly comparable to the highest level of English Contribution) for those who require residential care.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup></li></ol>\n<p>This means in reality there is a wider definition of "nursing care" than in England and therefore less dependence on means testing in Wales than in England, meaning that more people are entitled to higher levels of <a href="/wiki/Welfare_state" title="Welfare state">state assistance</a>. These variations in the levels of charges, may be viewed as <i><a href="/wiki/De_facto" title="De facto">de facto</a></i> tax varying powers.\n</p><p>This model of more limited legislative powers is partly because Wales had a more similar legal system to England from 1536, when it was <a href="/wiki/Laws_in_Wales_Acts_1535-1542" class="mw-redirect" title="Laws in Wales Acts 1535-1542">annexed</a> and legally became an integral part of the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_England" title="Kingdom of England">Kingdom of England</a> (although Wales retained its own judicial system, the Great Sessions until 1830 <sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>). <a href="/wiki/Ireland#Union_with_Great_Britain" title="Ireland">Ireland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Act_of_Union_1707" class="mw-redirect" title="Act of Union 1707">Scotland</a> were incorporated into the United Kingdom through negotiations between the respective Kingdoms\' Parliaments, and so retained some more differences in their legal systems. The <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Parliament" title="Scottish Parliament">Scottish Parliament</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Assembly" title="Northern Ireland Assembly">Northern Ireland Assembly</a> have wider powers.\n</p><p>The Assembly inherited the powers and budget of the <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_Wales" title="Secretary of State for Wales">Secretary of State for Wales</a> and most of the functions of the <a href="/wiki/Welsh_Office" title="Welsh Office">Welsh Office</a>. Since May 2007 the National Assembly for Wales has had more extensive powers to legislate, in addition to the function of varying laws passed by Westminster using secondary legislation conferred under the original <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Wales_Act_1998" title="Government of Wales Act 1998">Government of Wales Act 1998</a>. The post of <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_Wales" title="Secretary of State for Wales">Secretary of State for Wales</a>, currently <a href="/wiki/Alun_Cairns" title="Alun Cairns">Alun Cairns</a>, retains a residual role.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Welsh_Government">Welsh Government</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Politics_of_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Welsh Government">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The Welsh Government (<a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">Welsh</a>: <i lang="cy">Llywodraeth Cymru</i>, is the <a href="/wiki/Executive_(government)" title="Executive (government)">executive</a> body of the National Assembly for Wales, consisting of the <a href="/wiki/First_Minister_of_Wales" title="First Minister of Wales">First Minister</a> and his <a href="/wiki/Cabinet_(government)" title="Cabinet (government)">Cabinet</a>.\n</p><p>Following the <a href="/wiki/National_Assembly_for_Wales_election,_2016" title="National Assembly for Wales election, 2016">National Assembly for Wales election, 2016</a>, a minority Welsh Labour-Welsh Liberal Democrats coalition Government was formed in May 2016. The current cabinet members of the 5th Welsh Government are:\n</p><p><b>First Minister</b>\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Rt_Hon" class="mw-redirect" title="Rt Hon">Rt Hon</a> <a href="/wiki/Carwyn_Jones" title="Carwyn Jones">Carwyn Jones</a> <a href="/wiki/Member_of_the_National_Assembly_for_Wales" title="Member of the National Assembly for Wales">AM</a> (Labour)</li></ul>\n<p><b>Welsh Cabinet Secretaries</b><sup id="cite_ref-cabinet_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cabinet-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Ken_Skates" title="Ken Skates">Ken Skates</a> <a href="/wiki/Member_of_the_National_Assembly_for_Wales" title="Member of the National Assembly for Wales">AM</a>, Cabinet Secretary for Economy and Transport (Labour)</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Vaughan_Gething" title="Vaughan Gething">Vaughan Gething</a> <a href="/wiki/Member_of_the_National_Assembly_for_Wales" title="Member of the National Assembly for Wales">AM</a>, Cabinet Secretary for Health &amp; The Assembly was established as a "body corporate" by the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Wales_Act_1998" title="Government of Wales Act 1998">Government of Wales Act 1998</a> and the executive, as a committee of the Assembly, only had those powers that the Assembly as a whole votes to vest in ministers. The <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Wales_Act_2006" title="Government of Wales Act 2006">Government of Wales Act 2006</a> has now formally separated the Assembly and the Welsh Government giving Welsh Ministers independent executive authority.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_parties">Political parties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Politics_of_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Political parties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_political_parties_in_Wales" title="List of political parties in Wales">List of political parties in Wales</a></div>\n<p>Throughout much of the 19th century, Wales was a bastion of the <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Party_(UK)" title="Liberal Party (UK)">Liberal Party</a>. From the early 20th century, the <a href="/wiki/Wales_Labour_Party" class="mw-redirect" title="Wales Labour Party">Labour Party</a> has emerged as the most popular political party in Wales, before 2009 having won the largest share of the vote at every UK <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_elections" class="mw-redirect" title="United Kingdom general elections">General Election</a>, National Assembly for Wales election and <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament" title="European Parliament">European Parliament</a> election since 1922.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> The Wales Labour Party has traditionally been most successful in the industrial <a href="/wiki/South_Wales_valleys" class="mw-redirect" title="South Wales valleys">south Wales valleys</a>, north east Wales and urban coastal areas, such as <a href="/wiki/Cardiff" title="Cardiff">Cardiff</a>, <a href="/wiki/Newport,_Wales" title="Newport, Wales">Newport</a> and <a href="/wiki/Swansea" title="Swansea">Swansea</a>.\n</p><p>The Welsh <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Conservative Party (UK)">Conservative Party</a> has historically been the second political party of Wales, having obtained the second largest share of the vote in Wales in a majority of UK general elections since 1885.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>  In three General Elections (<a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1906" title="United Kingdom general election, 1906">1906</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1997" title="United Kingdom general election, 1997">1997</a> and <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_2001" title="United Kingdom general election, 2001">2001</a>) no Conservative MPs were returned to Westminster, while on only two occasions in the 20th century (<a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1979" title="United Kingdom general election, 1979">1979</a> and <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1983" title="United Kingdom general election, 1983">1983</a>) have more than a quarter of Welsh constituencies been represented by Conservatives. However, in the <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament_election,_2009_(United_Kingdom)" title="European Parliament election, 2009 (United Kingdom)">2009 European Parliament elections</a> the Conservatives polled higher than the Labour party in Wales.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Plaid_Cymru" title="Plaid Cymru">Plaid Cymru</a> is the principal <a href="/wiki/Welsh_nationalism" title="Welsh nationalism">Welsh nationalist</a> political party in Wales. The Party was formed in 1925, but did not contest a majority of Welsh seats in any UK general election until <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1959" title="United Kingdom general election, 1959">1959</a>. In <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1966" title="United Kingdom general election, 1966">1966</a> the first Plaid Cymru MP was returned to Parliament. Plaid Cymru\'s share of the vote since has averaged 10%, with the highest share ever - 14.3% - gained in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_2001" title="United Kingdom general election, 2001">2001 general election</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> Plaid Cymru is strongest in rural Welsh-speaking areas of <a href="/wiki/North_Wales" title="North Wales">north</a> and <a href="/wiki/West_Wales" title="West Wales">west Wales</a>.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Welsh_Liberal_Democrats" title="Welsh Liberal Democrats">Welsh Liberal Democrats</a> are part of the UK  <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Democrats_(UK)" title="Liberal Democrats (UK)">Liberal Democrats</a>, and were formed by the merger of the <a href="/wiki/Social_Democratic_Party_(UK)" title="Social Democratic Party (UK)">Social Democratic Party</a> (the SDP) and the <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Party_(UK)" title="Liberal Party (UK)">Liberal Party</a> in 1988. Since then they have gained an average vote share of 14% with the highest share - 18% - gained at the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_2005" title="United Kingdom general election, 2005">2005 general election</a>. The Welsh Liberal Democrats have the strongest support in rural mid and west Wales. The party performs relatively strongly in local government elections.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_party_representation">Current party representation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Politics_of_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Current party representation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Following the 2017 elections:<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<table class="wikitable">\n\n<tbody><tr>\n<th colspan="2">Party\n</th>\n<th>MEPs\n</th>\n<th>MPs\n</th>\n<th>AMs\n</th>\n<th>Local Authority overall control\n</th></tr>\n<tr>\n<td style="width: 2px; Labour won the largest share of votes and seats in each election and has always been in government in Wales, either as a <a href="/wiki/Minority_government" title="Minority government">minority administration</a> or in coalition, first with the Liberal Democrats and more recently with Plaid Cymru.<sup id="cite_ref-McLean_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McLean-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> The predominance of coalitions is a result of the <a href="/wiki/Additional_Member_System" title="Additional Member System">Additional Member System</a> used for Assembly elections, which has worked to the benefit of Labour (it won a higher share of seats than votes in the 1999, 2003 and 2007 elections) but not given it the same advantage the party has enjoyed in <a href="/wiki/Plurality_voting_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Plurality voting system">first-past-the-post</a> elections to Welsh seats in the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Commons_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="House of Commons of the United Kingdom">House of Commons</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-McLean_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McLean-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Policy divergence between Wales and England has arisen largely because Welsh governments have not followed the <a href="/wiki/Free_market" title="Free market">market</a>-based English public service reforms introduced during the <a href="/wiki/Premiership_of_Tony_Blair" title="Premiership of Tony Blair">premiership of Tony Blair</a>.  In 2002, First Minister <a href="/wiki/Rhodri_Morgan" title="Rhodri Morgan">Rhodri Morgan</a> said that a key theme of the first four years of the Assembly was the creation of a new set of citizenship rights that are free at the point of use, universal and unconditional.  He accepted the <a href="/wiki/Tony_Blair" title="Tony Blair">Blairite</a> mantra of equality of opportunity and equality of access, but emphasised what he called "the fundamentally socialist aim of equality of outcome" - in stark contrast to the approach of Blair, who said that the true meaning of equality is specifically "not equality of outcome".<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Marking ten years of devolution in a 2009 speech, Morgan highlighted free prescriptions, primary school breakfasts and free swimming as \xe2\x80\x98Made in Wales\xe2\x80\x99 initiatives that had made "a real difference to people\xe2\x80\x99s everyday lives" since the National Assembly came into being.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> However, some authors have argued that the approach to public services in England has been more effective than that in Wales, with health and education "cost(ing) less and delivering more".<sup id="cite_ref-McLean_31-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McLean-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> Unfavourable comparisons between <a href="/wiki/National_Health_Service" title="National Health Service">National Health Service</a> waiting lists in England and Wales were a contentious issue in the first and second Assemblies.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Nevertheless, a \'progressive consensus\' based on faith in the power of government, universal rather than means-tested services, co-operation rather than <a href="/wiki/Quasi-market" title="Quasi-market">competition in public services</a>, a rejection of individual choice as a guide to policy and a focus on equality of outcome continued to underpin the <a href="/wiki/One_Wales" title="One Wales">One Wales</a> coalition government in the Third Assembly.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> The commitment to universalism may be tested by increasing budgetary constraints; in April 2009 a senior Plaid Cymru adviser warned of impending health and education cuts.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Local_politics">Local politics</span><span This is because the unit is the constitutional successor to the former <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_England" title="Kingdom of England">Kingdom of England</a>. If considered as a subdivision of the United Kingdom, England &amp; Wales would have a population of 53,390,300 and an area of 151,174&#160;km\xc2\xb2.\n</p><p>Scotland, Northern Ireland, and dependencies such as the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man" title="Isle of Man">Isle of Man</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Bailiwick" title="Bailiwick">Bailiwicks</a> of <a href="/wiki/Jersey" title="Jersey">Jersey</a> and <a href="/wiki/Guernsey" title="Guernsey">Guernsey</a>, are also separate units for this purpose (although they are not separate states under <a href="/wiki/Public_international_law" class="mw-redirect" title="Public international law">public international law</a>), each with their own legal system (see the more complete explanation in <a href="/wiki/English_law" title="English law">English law</a>).\n</p><p>Wales was brought under a common monarch with England through conquest with the <a href="/wiki/Statute_of_Rhuddlan" title="Statute of Rhuddlan">Statute of Rhuddlan</a> in 1284 and annexed to England for legal purposes by the <a href="/wiki/Laws_in_Wales_Acts_1535-1542" class="mw-redirect" title="Laws in Wales Acts 1535-1542">Laws in Wales Acts 1535-1542</a>. However, references in legislation for \'England\' were still taken as excluding Wales. The <a href="/wiki/Wales_and_Berwick_Act_1746" title="Wales and Berwick Act 1746">Wales and Berwick Act 1746</a> meant that in all future laws, \'England\' would by default include Wales (and <a href="/wiki/Berwick-upon-Tweed" title="Berwick-upon-Tweed">Berwick-upon-Tweed</a>). This was later repealed in 1967 and current laws use "England and Wales" as a single entity.\n<a href="/wiki/Cardiff" title="Cardiff">Cardiff</a> was proclaimed as the Welsh capital in In the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_2010" title="United Kingdom general election, 2010">2010 General Election</a>, the Labour Party lost approximately 12% of the vote across Wales, with losses varying by region. However, Labour managed to mitigate their losses by winning 26 seats. The Conservatives returned the three MP\'s elected in the 2005 general election as well as adding 5 more. The Liberal Democrats have 3 seats. Plaid Cymru, the Welsh nationalist party, also have 3 seats after gaining a seat from Labour.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_the_UK_Government">In the UK Government</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Politics_of_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: In the UK Government">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Wales_Office" class="mw-redirect" title="Wales Office">Wales Office</a> (<i>Swyddfa Cymru</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_government_department" class="mw-redirect" title="United Kingdom government department">United Kingdom government department</a>. It is a replacement for the old <a href="/wiki/Welsh_Office" title="Welsh Office">Welsh Office</a> (<i>Swyddfa Gymreig</i>), which had extensive responsibility for governing Wales prior to Welsh devolution in 1999. Its current incarnation is significantly less powerful: it is primarily responsible for carrying out the few functions remaining to the <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_Wales" title="Secretary of State for Wales">Secretary of State for Wales</a> that have not been transferred already to National Assembly for Wales and securing funds for Wales as part of the annual budget settlement.\n</p><p>The Secretary of State for Wales has overall responsibility for the office but it is located administratively within the <a href="/wiki/Department_for_Constitutional_Affairs" title="Department for Constitutional Affairs">Department for Constitutional Affairs</a>. This was carried out as part of the changes announced on 12 June 2003 that were part of a package intended toward replacing the <a href="/wiki/Lord_Chancellor%27s_Department" title="Lord Chancellor&#39;s Department">Lord Chancellor\'s Department</a>.\n</p><p>Ministers of the <a href="/wiki/Wales_Office" class="mw-redirect" title="Wales Office">Wales Office</a> as of 10 October
