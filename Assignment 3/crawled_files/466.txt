title="Yorkshire pudding">Yorkshire pudding</a></div></div></div>\n<p><b>British cuisine</b> is the set of cooking traditions and practices associated with the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>.  However, British cuisine has absorbed the cultural influence of those who have <a href="/wiki/Immigration_to_the_United_Kingdom_since_1922" class="mw-redirect" title="Immigration to the United Kingdom since 1922">settled in Britain</a>, producing many hybrid dishes, such as <a href="/wiki/Chicken_tikka_masala" title="Chicken tikka masala">chicken tikka masala</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Guardian_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-bbcenc_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbcenc-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Fish_and_chips.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Fish_and_chips" title="Fish and chips">Fish and chips</a>, a popular <a href="/wiki/Take-away_food" class="mw-redirect" title="Take-away food">take-away food</a> of the United Kingdom</div></div></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Celtic_fields" class="mw-redirect" title="Celtic fields">Celtic agriculture</a> and animal breeding produced a wide variety of foodstuffs for indigenous <a href="/wiki/Celts" title="Celts">Celts</a> and <a href="/wiki/Britons_(historic)" class="mw-redirect" title="Britons (historic)">Britons</a>. <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Anglo-Saxon England">Anglo-Saxon England</a> developed meat and savoury herb stewing techniques before the practice became common in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Norman_conquest" class="mw-redirect" title="Norman conquest">Norman conquest</a> introduced exotic spices into <a href="/wiki/England_in_the_Middle_Ages" title="England in the Middle Ages">England in the Middle Ages</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Spencer_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Spencer-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British Empire</a> facilitated a knowledge of <a href="/wiki/Indian_cuisine" title="Indian cuisine">Indian cuisine</a> with its "strong, penetrating spices and herbs".<sup id="cite_ref-Spencer_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Spencer-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Rationing_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Rationing in the United Kingdom">Food rationing</a> policies, put in place by the British government during wartime periods of the 20th century,<sup id="cite_ref-Ina_Zweiniger-Bargielowska_2002_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ina_Zweiniger-Bargielowska_2002-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> are said to have been the stimulus for British cuisine\'s poor international reputation.<sup id="cite_ref-Spencer_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Spencer-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Well-known traditional British dishes include <a href="/wiki/Full_breakfast" title="Full breakfast">full breakfast</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fish_and_chips" title="Fish and chips">fish and chips</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Christmas_dinner" title="Christmas dinner">Christmas dinner</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Spencer_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Spencer-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Sunday_roast" title="Sunday roast">Sunday roast</a>, <a href="/wiki/Steak_and_kidney_pie" title="Steak and kidney pie">steak and kidney pie</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shepherd%27s_pie" title="Shepherd&#39;s pie">shepherd\'s pie</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bangers_and_mash" title="Bangers and mash">bangers and mash</a>. People in Britain however eat a wide variety of foods based on the cuisines of Europe, India, and other parts of the world. British cuisine has many regional varieties within the broader categories of <a href="/wiki/English_cuisine" title="English cuisine">English</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scottish_cuisine" title="Scottish cuisine">Scottish</a> and <a href="/wiki/Welsh_cuisine" title="Welsh cuisine">Welsh cuisine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Northern_Irish_cuisine" title="Northern Irish cuisine">Northern Irish cuisine</a>. Each has developed its own regional or local dishes, many of which are <a href="/wiki/Geographically_Indicated_Food" class="mw-redirect" title="Geographically Indicated Food">geographically indicated foods</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Cornish_pasties" class="mw-redirect" title="Cornish pasties">Cornish pasties</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire_pudding" title="Yorkshire pudding">Yorkshire pudding</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cumberland_Sausage" class="mw-redirect" title="Cumberland Sausage">Cumberland Sausage</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arbroath_Smokie" class="mw-redirect" title="Arbroath Smokie">Arbroath Smokie</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Welsh_cake" title="Welsh cake">Welsh cakes</a>.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" fertile soils and advanced animal breeding produced a wide variety of very high quality foods for indigenous <a href="/wiki/Britons_(historic)" class="mw-redirect" title="Britons (historic)">Romano-British</a> people. <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Anglo-Saxon England">Anglo-Saxon England</a> developed meat and savoury herb stewing techniques and the <a href="/wiki/Norman_conquest" class="mw-redirect" title="Norman conquest">Norman conquest</a> reintroduced exotic spices and continental influences back into <a after. Following the <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Reformation" class="mw-redirect" title="Protestant Reformation">Protestant Reformation</a> in the 16th and 17th centuries "plain and robust" food remained the mainstay of the British diet, reflecting tastes which are still shared with neighbouring north European countries and <a href="/wiki/Cuisine_of_the_United_States#Colonial_period" class="mw-redirect" title="Cuisine of the United States">traditional North American Cuisine</a>. In the 18th and 19th centuries, as the Colonial <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British Empire</a> began to be influenced by <a href="/wiki/Indian_cuisine" title="Indian cuisine">India\'s elaborate food tradition</a> of "strong, penetrating spices and herbs", the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> developed a worldwide reputation<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> for the quality of British beef and <a href="/wiki/Hereford_(cattle)" class="mw-redirect" title="Hereford (cattle)">pedigree bulls</a> were exported to form the bloodline of major modern beef herds in the <a href="/wiki/New_World" title="New World">New World</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Spencer_4-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Spencer-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Developments in plant breeding produced a multiplicity of fruit and vegetable varieties, with British disease-resistant <a href="/wiki/Rootstock" title="Rootstock">rootstocks</a> still used globally for fruits such as apples.\n</p><p>During the World Wars of the 20th century difficulties of food supply were countered by official measures, which included rationing. The problem was worse in WWII, and the Ministry of Food was established to address the problems (see <a href="/wiki/Rationing_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Rationing in the United Kingdom">Rationing in the United Kingdom</a>). Due to the economic problems following the war, rationing continued for some years, and in some aspects was more strict than during wartime. Rationing was not fully lifted until almost a decade after war ended in Europe, so that a whole generation was raised without access to many previously common ingredients. These policies, put in place by the British government during wartime periods of the 20th century,<sup id="cite_ref-Ina_Zweiniger-Bargielowska_2002_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ina_Zweiniger-Bargielowska_2002-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> are often blamed for the decline of British cuisine in the 20th century.\n</p><p>The last half of the 20th century saw an increase in the availability of a greater range of good quality fresh products and greater willingness by many sections of the British population to vary their diets and select dishes from other cultures such as those of Italy and India.\n</p><p>Efforts have been made to re-introduce pre-20th-century recipes. Ingredients not native to the islands, particularly <a href="/wiki/Herb" title="Herb">herbs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spice" title="Spice">spices</a>, are frequently added to traditional dishes (echoing the highly spiced nature of much British food in the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">medieval era</a>).\n</p><p>Much of Modern British cooking also draws heavily on influences from <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_cuisine" title="Mediterranean cuisine">Mediterranean</a>, and more recently, <a href="/wiki/Middle_Eastern_cuisine" title="Middle Eastern cuisine">Middle Eastern</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Asian_cuisine" class="mw-redirect" title="South Asian cuisine">South Asian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Asian_cuisine#East_Asia" title="Asian cuisine">East Asian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Asian_cuisine#Southeast_Asia" title="Asian cuisine">Southeast Asian</a> cuisines. A hunger for exotic cooking was satisfied by writers such as <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_David" title="Elizabeth David">Elizabeth David</a>, who from 1950 produced evocative books, starting with <i><a href="/wiki/A_Book_of_Mediterranean_Food" title="A Book of Mediterranean Food">A Book of Mediterranean Food</a></i>, whose ingredients were then often impossible to find in Britain.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> By the 1960s foreign holidays, and foreign-style restaurants in Britain, further widened the popularity of foreign cuisine. It shares much with English cuisine, but has distinctive attributes and recipes of its own. Traditional Scottish dishes such as <a href="/wiki/Haggis" title="Haggis">haggis</a> and shortbread exist alongside international foodstuffs brought about by migration. Scotland is known for the high quality of its beef, lamb, potatoes, oats, and sea foods.
