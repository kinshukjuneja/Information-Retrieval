Ireland</a></li></ul>\n</div></td></tr></tbody></table></div></div></div>\n<p>The <b>Wars of the Three Kingdoms</b>,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;b&#93;</a></sup> sometimes known as the <b>British Civil Wars</b>,<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;c&#93;</a></sup> <sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;d&#93;</a></sup> formed an intertwined series of conflicts that took place in the kingdoms of <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_England" title="Kingdom of England">England</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Ireland" title="Kingdom of Ireland">Ireland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Scotland" title="Kingdom of Scotland">Scotland</a> between 1639 and 1651. The <a href="/wiki/English_Civil_War" title="English Civil War">English Civil War</a> proper has become the best-known of these conflicts; it included the abolition of the monarchy and the execution of the kingdom\'s monarch, <a href="/wiki/Charles_I_of_England" title="Charles I of England">Charles I</a>, by the English parliament in 1649.\n</p><p>The history of these wars is often extended to include the uprisings and conflicts that continued through the 1650s until the <a href="/wiki/Restoration_(England)" title="Restoration (England)">English Restoration</a> of the monarchy in 1660, and sometimes to include <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Venner" title="Thomas Venner">Venner\'s</a> uprising the following year. The wars were the outcome of broadly set tensions over religious and civil issues. Religious disputes centred on whether religion was to be dictated by the monarch or by choice, the conscience of the individual, with many people feeling that they ought to have freedom of religion (freedom of conscience). The related civil question was settling the extent to which the king\'s rule was to be constrained by parliament\xe2\x80\x94in particular the right to raise taxes and armed forces without consent of the parliament.\n</p><p>The wars also had elements of national conflict, as Ireland and Scotland rebelled against England\'s primacy within the Three Kingdoms. The broad and durable victory of the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_England" title="Parliament of England">English Parliament</a>\xe2\x80\x94ultimately (under <a href="/wiki/Oliver_Cromwell" title="Oliver Cromwell">Oliver Cromwell</a> and the Army) overcoming the king, the Irish and the Scots, and then outlasting the Cromwellian Protectorate itself\xe2\x80\x94helped establish the future of Great Britain and Ireland as a constitutional monarchy with political power centred on the Parliament in London.\n</p><p>These  wars included the <a href="/wiki/Bishops%27_Wars" title="Bishops&#39; Wars">Bishops\' Wars</a> of 1639 and 1640; the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Rebellion_of_1641" title="Irish Rebellion of 1641">Irish Rebellion of 1641</a>; <a href="/wiki/Confederate_Ireland" title="Confederate Ireland">Confederate Ireland</a>, 1642\xe2\x80\x931649; the <a href="/wiki/Scotland_in_the_Wars_of_the_Three_Kingdoms" title="Scotland in the Wars of the Three Kingdoms">Scottish Civil War</a> of 1644\xe2\x80\x931645; and the <a href="/wiki/Cromwellian_conquest_of_Ireland" title="Cromwellian conquest of Ireland">Cromwellian conquest of Ireland</a> in 1649 (collectively the Eleven Years War or <a href="/wiki/Irish_Confederate_Wars" title="Irish Confederate Wars">Irish Confederate Wars</a>); and the <a href="/wiki/First_English_Civil_War" title="First English Civil War">First</a>, <a href="/wiki/Second_English_Civil_War" title="Second English Civil War">Second</a> and <a href="/wiki/Third_English_Civil_War" title="Third English Civil War">Third</a> <a href="/wiki/English_Civil_War" title="English Civil War">English Civil Wars</a> of 1642\xe2\x80\x931646, 1648\xe2\x80\x931649 and 1650\xe2\x80\x931651.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" style="display:none" /><div class="toctitle" lang="en" dir="ltr"><h2>Contents</h2><span class="toctogglespan"><label class="toctogglelabel" for="toctogglecheckbox"></label></span></div>\n<ul>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-1"><a href="#Background"><span class="tocnumber">1</span> <span href="#External_links"><span class="tocnumber">8</span> <span class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wars_of_the_Three_Kingdoms&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="General">General</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wars_of_the_Three_Kingdoms&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: General">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>After 1541, monarchs of England styled their Irish territory as also a <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Ireland" title="Kingdom of Ireland">Kingdom</a>\xe2\x80\x94replacing the <a href="/wiki/Monarchy_of_Ireland#Lordship_of_Ireland:_1198\xe2\x80\x931542" title="Monarchy of Ireland">Lordship of Ireland</a>\xe2\x80\x94and ruled there with the assistance of a separate <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Ireland" title="Parliament of Ireland">Irish Parliament</a>, while <a href="/wiki/Henry_VIII_of_England" title="Henry VIII of England">Henry VIII</a> integrated <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a> more closely into the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_England" title="Kingdom of England">Kingdom of England</a>. Scotland, the third separate kingdom, was governed by the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Stuart" title="House of Stuart">House of Stuart</a>.\n</p><p>Via the <a href="/wiki/English_Reformation" title="English Reformation">English Reformation</a>, King Henry VIII made himself head of the Protestant <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">Church of England</a> and outlawed <a href="/wiki/Catholicism" class="mw-redirect" title="Catholicism">Catholicism</a> in <a href="/wiki/England_and_Wales" title="England and Wales">England and Wales</a>. In the course of the 16th century <a href="/wiki/Protestantism" title="Protestantism">Protestantism</a> became intimately associated with <a href="/wiki/National_identity" title="National identity">national identity</a> in England: English folk in general saw Catholicism as the national enemy, especially as it was embodied in the rivals <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>. But Catholicism remained the religion of most people in Ireland; for many Irish it was a symbol of native resistance to the <a href="/wiki/Tudor_conquest_of_Ireland" title="Tudor conquest of Ireland">Tudor conquest/reconquest of Ireland</a> in 1541.\n</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Scotland" title="Kingdom of Scotland">Kingdom of Scotland</a> the <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Reformation" class="mw-redirect" title="Protestant Reformation">Protestant Reformation</a> was a popular movement led by <a href="/wiki/John_Knox" title="John Knox">John Knox</a>. The Scottish Parliament legislated for a national <a href="/wiki/Presbyterian" class="mw-redirect" title="Presbyterian">Presbyterian</a> church\xe2\x80\x94namely the  <a href="/wiki/Church_of_Scotland" title="Church of Scotland">Church of Scotland</a> or the "<a href="/wiki/Kirk" title="Kirk">Kirk</a>"\xe2\x80\x94and the Catholic <a href="/wiki/Mary,_Queen_of_Scots" title="Mary, Queen of Scots">Mary, Queen of Scots</a> was forced to abdicate in favour of her son <a href="/wiki/James_VI_of_Scotland" class="mw-redirect" title="James VI of Scotland">James VI of Scotland</a>. James grew up under a regency disputed between Catholic and Protestant factions; when he took power he aspired to be a "universal King" favouring the English <a href="/wiki/Episcopal_polity" title="Episcopal polity">Episcopalian</a> system of bishops appointed by the king. In 1584, he introduced bishops into the Church of Scotland, but met with vigorous opposition, and he had to concede that the <a href="/wiki/General_Assembly_of_the_Church_of_Scotland" title="General Assembly of the Church of Scotland">General Assembly</a> would continue running the church without his say.\n</p><p>The personal union of the three kingdoms under one monarch came about when King James VI of Scotland succeeded Elizabeth I to the English throne in 1603, when he also became King James I of England and of Ireland. In 1625, <a href="/wiki/Charles_I_of_England" title="Charles I of England">Charles I</a> succeeded his father, and marked three main concerns regarding England and Wales: how to fund his government, how to reform the church, and how to limit (the English) Parliament\'s interference in his rule. At that time he showed little interest in his other two kingdoms, Scotland and Ireland.<sup id="cite_ref-OpenU_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OpenU-10">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Scotland">Scotland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wars_of_the_Three_Kingdoms&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Scotland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Bishops%27_Wars" title="Bishops&#39; Wars">Bishops\' Wars</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:332px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Riot_against_Anglican_prayer_book_1637.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Riot_against_Anglican_prayer_book_1637.jpg/330px-Riot_against_Anglican_prayer_book_1637.jpg" width="330" height="215" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Riot_against_Anglican_prayer_book_1637.jpg/495px-Riot_against_Anglican_prayer_book_1637.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Riot_against_Anglican_prayer_book_1637.jpg/660px-Riot_against_Anglican_prayer_book_1637.jpg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="520" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Riot_against_Anglican_prayer_book_1637.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The spark\xe2\x80\x94riot in <a href="/wiki/St_Giles%27_Cathedral" title="St Giles&#39; Cathedral">St Giles\' Cathedral</a>, Edinburgh, reputedly started by <a href="/wiki/Jenny_Geddes" title="Jenny Geddes">Jenny Geddes</a></div></div></div>\n<p>James VI remained Protestant, taking care to maintain his hopes of succession to the English throne. He duly became <a href="/wiki/James_VI_and_I#Accession_in_England" title="James VI and I">James I of England</a> in 1603 and moved to London. His diplomatic and political skills now concentrated fully in dealing with the English Court and <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_England" title="Parliament of England">Parliament</a>\xe2\x80\x94at the same time running Scotland by writing instructions to the <a href="/wiki/Privy_Council_of_Scotland#History" title="Privy Council of Scotland">Privy Council of Scotland</a> and controlling the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Scotland" title="Parliament of Scotland">Parliament of Scotland</a> through the <a href="/wiki/Lords_of_the_Articles" class="mw-redirect" title="Lords of the Articles">Lords of the Articles</a>. He contravened the sovereign authority of the Scottish General Assembly and stopped it from meeting, then increased the number of bishops in the Church of Scotland. In 1618 he held a General Assembly and pushed through <i>Five Articles</i> of Episcopalian practices, which were widely boycotted. \n</p><p>After his death in 1625, James was succeeded by his son Charles I, who was crowned in <a href="/wiki/St_Giles%27_Cathedral" title="St Giles&#39; Cathedral">St Giles\' Cathedral</a>, <a href="/wiki/Edinburgh" title="Edinburgh">Edinburgh</a>\xe2\x80\x94the Scottish coronation\xe2\x80\x94in 1633, with full <a href="/wiki/Anglican" class="mw-redirect" title="Anglican">Anglican</a> rites. Charles was less skillful or restrained than his father; his attempts to enforce Anglican practices in the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_Scotland" title="Church of Scotland">Church of Scotland</a> created opposition that reached a flashpoint when he introduced the <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Common_Prayer" title="Book of Common Prayer">Book of Common Prayer</a>. His confrontation with the Scots came to a head in 1639, when he tried and failed to coerce Scotland by military means, the <a href="/wiki/Bishops%27_wars" class="mw-redirect" title="Bishops&#39; wars">Bishops\' wars</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="England">England</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wars_of_the_Three_Kingdoms&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: England">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<dl><dd><i>See also the <a href="/wiki/English_Civil_War" title="English Civil War">English Civil War</a> (<a href="/wiki/English_Civil_War#Background" title="English Civil War">Background</a>).</i></dd></dl>\n<p>Charles shared his father\'s belief in the <a href="/wiki/Divine_Right_of_Kings" class="mw-redirect" title="Divine Right of Kings">Divine Right of Kings</a>, and his persistent assertion of this standard seriously disrupted relations between the Crown and the English Parliament. The Church of England remained dominant, but a powerful <a href="/wiki/Puritan" class="mw-redirect" title="Puritan">Puritan</a> minority, represented by about one third of Parliament, began to assert themselves; their religious precepts had much in common with the Presbyterian Scots.\n</p><p>The English Parliament and the king had repeated disputes over taxation, military expenditures and the role of parliament in government. While James I had held much the same opinions as his son regarding <a href="/wiki/Royal_Prerogative" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal Prerogative">royal prerogatives</a>, he had discretion and charisma enough to often persuade Parliamentarians to his thinking. Charles had no such skill in political or human management; faced with multiple crises during 1639\xe2\x80\x931642, he failed to prevent his kingdoms from sliding into civil war. When Charles approached the Parliament to pay for a campaign against the Scots, they refused; they then declared themselves to be permanently in session\xe2\x80\x94<a href="/wiki/The_Long_Parliament" class="mw-redirect" title="The Long Parliament">the Long Parliament</a>\xe2\x80\x94and soon presented Charles a long list of civil and religious grievances requiring his remedy before they approved any new legislation.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ireland">Ireland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wars_of_the_Three_Kingdoms&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Ireland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Meanwhile, in the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Ireland" title="Kingdom of Ireland">Kingdom of Ireland</a> (proclaimed such in 1541 but only fully conquered for the Crown in 1603), tensions had also begun to mount. <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Wentworth,_1st_Earl_of_Strafford" title="Thomas Wentworth, 1st Earl of Strafford">Thomas Wentworth</a>,  Charles I\'s <a href="/wiki/Lord_Deputy_of_Ireland" title="Lord Deputy of Ireland">Lord Deputy of Ireland</a>, angered Roman Catholics by enforcing new taxes while denying them full rights as subjects; he further antagonised the native Irish Catholics by repeated initiatives to confiscate and transfer their lands to English colonists. Conditions became explosive in 1639 when Wentworth offered Irish Catholics some reforms in return for them raising and funding an Irish army (led by Protestant officers) to put down the Scottish rebellion. The idea of an Irish Catholic army enforcing what many saw as already tyrannical government horrified both the Scottish and the English Parliaments, who in response threatened to invade Ireland.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Wars">Wars</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wars_of_the_Three_Kingdoms&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Wars">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Bishops%27_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Bishops&#39; War">Bishops\' War</a>, <a href="/wiki/Irish_Rebellion_of_1641" title="Irish Rebellion of 1641">Irish Rebellion of 1641</a>, <a href="/wiki/English_Civil_War" title="English Civil War">English Civil War</a>, <a href="/wiki/Irish_Confederate_Wars" title="Irish Confederate Wars">Irish Confederate Wars</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Scotland_in_the_Wars_of_the_Three_Kingdoms" title="Scotland in the Wars of the Three Kingdoms">Scotland in the Wars of the Three Kingdoms</a></div>\n<p>Modern historians have emphasised the lack of inevitability of the civil wars, noting that the sides resorted to \'violence first\' in situations marked by mutual distrust and paranoia. Charles\' initial failure to quickly end the <a href="/wiki/Bishops%27_Wars" title="Bishops&#39; Wars">Bishops\' Wars</a> informed the antagonists that force could serve them better than negotiation.\n</p><p>In Ireland, alienated by English Protestant domination and frightened by the rhetoric of the English and Scottish Parliaments, a small group of Irish conspirators launched the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Rebellion_of_1641" title="Irish Rebellion of 1641">Irish Rebellion of 1641</a>, ostensibly in support of the "King\'s Rights". The rising featured widespread assaults on Protestant communities in Ireland, which were propogated as massacres of the defenseless. In England and Scotland, rumours spread that the killings had the king\'s sanction, which, for many, foreshadowed their own fate if the king\'s Irish troops landed in Britain. Thus the English Parliament refused to pay for a royal army to put down the rebellion in Ireland; instead Parliament decided to raise its own armed forces. The king did likewise, rallying those <a href="/wiki/Cavalier" title="Cavalier">Royalists</a> (some of them members of Parliament) who believed their fortunes were best served by loyalty to the king.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:The_armies_embrace.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/The_armies_embrace.jpg/220px-The_armies_embrace.jpg" width="220" height="145" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/The_armies_embrace.jpg/330px-The_armies_embrace.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/The_armies_embrace.jpg/440px-The_armies_embrace.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1569" data-file-height="1033" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:The_armies_embrace.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The English and Scots armies lovingly embrace each other</div></div></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/English_Civil_War" title="English Civil War">English Civil War</a> ignited in 1642. Scottish <a href="/wiki/Covenanters" class="mw-redirect" title="Covenanters">Covenanters</a> (as Presbyterians there called themselves) joined forces with the English Parliament in late 1643 and played a major role in the Parliamentary victory. Over more than two years the king\'s forces were ground down by the efficiency of those of Parliament, including the <a href="/wiki/New_Model_Army" title="New Model Army">New Model Army</a>, backed as they were by the financial muscle of the <a href="/wiki/City_of_London" title="City of London">City of London</a>. On 5 May 1646 at Southwell, Charles I surrendered to the Scottish army besieging <a href="/wiki/Newark-on-Trent" title="Newark-on-Trent">Newark-on-Trent</a>. What remained of the English and Welsh Royalist armies and garrisons surrendered piecemeal over the next few months.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAtkinson1911403\xe2\x80\x93417_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAtkinson1911403\xe2\x80\x93417-11">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Meanwhile, the rebellious Irish Catholics formed their own government\xe2\x80\x94<a href="/wiki/Confederate_Ireland" title="Confederate Ireland">Confederate Ireland</a>\xe2\x80\x94intending to help the Royalists in return for religious toleration and political autonomy. Troops from England and Scotland fought in Ireland, and Irish Confederate troops mounted an expedition to Scotland in 1644, sparking the <a href="/wiki/Scotland_in_the_Wars_of_the_Three_Kingdoms" title="Scotland in the Wars of the Three Kingdoms">Scottish Civil War</a>. There, the Royalists gained a series of victories in 1644\xe2\x80\x931645, but were crushed after the main Covenanter armies returned to Scotland upon the end of  the first English Civil War.\n</p><p>The Scots handed Charles over to the English and returned to Scotland, the English Parliament having paid them a large sum for their expenses in the English campaign. After his surrender, Charles was approached by the Scots, the Presbyterians in the English Parliament, and the <i><a href="/wiki/Grandee_(New_Model_Army)" class="mw-redirect" title="Grandee (New Model Army)">Grandees</a></i> of the New Model Army, all attempting to reach an accommodation with him and among themselves that would gain the peace while preserving the crown. But now, a breach between the New Model Army and Parliament widened day by day, until the Presbyterians in Parliament, with allies among the Scots and the remaining Royalists, saw themselves strong enough to challenge the Army, which began the <a href="/wiki/Second_English_Civil_War" title="Second English Civil War">Second English Civil War</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAtkinson1911417_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAtkinson1911417-12">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The New Model Army vanquished the English Royalist and Parliamentarians, and their Scottish <a href="/wiki/Engager" class="mw-redirect" title="Engager">Engager</a> allies. On account of his secret machinations with the Scottish Engagers, Charles was charged with treason against England.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGardiner1906371_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGardiner1906371-13">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Subsequently, the Grandees and their civilian supporters would not reconcile with the king or the Presbyterian majority in Parliament. The Grandees acted; soldiers were used to <a href="/wiki/Pride%27s_purge" class="mw-redirect" title="Pride&#39;s purge">purge</a> the English Parliament of those who opposed the Army. The resultant <a href="/wiki/Rump_Parliament" title="Rump Parliament">Rump Parliament</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Long_Parliament" title="Long Parliament">Long Parliament</a> then passed enabling legislation for <a href="/wiki/Trial_of_Charles_I" class="mw-redirect" title="Trial of Charles I">putting Charles I on trial for treason</a>. He was found guilty of treason against the English commons and was executed on 30 January 1649.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAtkinson1911417\xe2\x80\x93418_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAtkinson1911417\xe2\x80\x93418-14">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>After the execution of King Charles I the Rump Parliament passed a series of acts declaring England a republic and that the House of Commons\xe2\x80\x94without the House of Lords\xe2\x80\x94would sit as the legislature and a <a href="/wiki/Council_of_State_(England)" class="mw-redirect" title="Council of State (England)">Council of State</a> would act as the executive power. In the other two kingdoms the execution of Charles caused the warring parties to unite, and they recognised <a href="/wiki/Charles_II_of_England" title="Charles II of England">Charles II</a> as king of Great Britain, France and Ireland, which would lead to a <a href="/wiki/Third_English_Civil_War" title="Third English Civil War">Third English Civil War</a>.\n</p><p>To deal with the threat to the English Commonwealth posed by the two kingdoms (Ireland and Scotland), the Rump Parliament first charged  Cromwell to invade and subdue Ireland. In August 1649, he landed an English army at <a href="/wiki/Rathmines" title="Rathmines">Rathmines</a>  shortly after the <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Dublin_(1649)" title="Siege of Dublin (1649)">Siege of Dublin</a> was abandoned by the Royalists following the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Rathmines" title="Battle of Rathmines">Battle of Rathmines</a>. Then, in late May 1650, Cromwell left one army to continue <a href="/wiki/Cromwellian_conquest_of_Ireland" title="Cromwellian conquest of Ireland">the Irish conquest</a> and returned to England and to take command of a second English army preparing to invade Scotland. On 3 September 1650, he defeated the Scottish Covenanters at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Dunbar_(1650)" title="Battle of Dunbar (1650)">Battle of Dunbar</a>; his forces then occupied Edinburgh and Scotland south of the <a href="/wiki/River_Forth" title="River Forth">River Forth</a>. Cromwell was advancing the bulk of his army over the Forth towards <a href="/wiki/Stirling" title="Stirling">Stirling</a>, when <a href="/wiki/Charles_II_of_England" title="Charles II of England">Charles II</a>, commanding a Scottish Royalist army, stole the march on the English commander and invaded England from his base in Scotland. Cromwell divided his forces, leaving part in Scotland to complete the conquest there, then led the rest south in pursuit of Charles.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAtkinson1911418\xe2\x80\x93420_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAtkinson1911418\xe2\x80\x93420-15">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Royalist army failed to gather much support from English Royalists as it moved south into England; so, instead of heading directly towards London and certain defeat, Charles aimed for <a href="/wiki/Worcester" title="Worcester">Worcester</a> in hopes that Wales and the West and Midlands of England would rise against the Commonwealth. This didn\'t happen, and one year to the day after the Battle of Dunbar the New Model Army and English militia regiments vanquished the last Royalist army of the English Civil War at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Worcester" title="Battle of Worcester">Battle of Worcester</a>, on 3 September 1651. It was the last and most decisive battle in the Wars of the Three Kingdoms.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAtkinson1911420\xe2\x80\x93421_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAtkinson1911420\xe2\x80\x93421-16">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wars_of_the_Three_Kingdoms&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Having defeated all organized opposition, the Grandees of the Parliamentary New Model Army and their civilian supporters dominated the politics of all three nations for the next nine years (see <a href="/wiki/Interregnum_(1649%E2%80%931660)" title="Interregnum (1649\xe2\x80\x931660)">Interregnum (1649\xe2\x80\x931660)</a>). As for England, the Rump Parliament had already decreed it was a republic and a <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_England" title="Commonwealth of England">Commonwealth</a>; but Ireland and Scotland were now ruled by military governors, even as constituent representatives from both nations were seated in the Rump Parliament of <a href="/wiki/The_Protectorate" title="The Protectorate">the Protectorate</a>\xe2\x80\x94all which were dominated by <a href="/wiki/Oliver_Cromwell" title="Oliver Cromwell">Oliver Cromwell</a>, the Lord Protector.  When Cromwell died in 1658, control of the Commonwealth became unstable, until. in early 1660,  General <a href="/wiki/George_Monck" class="mw-redirect" title="George Monck">George Monck</a>, commanding English occupation forces in Scotland, ordered his troops from the <a href="/wiki/Coldstream" title="Coldstream">Coldstream</a> barracks,   marched them south into England, and seized control of London by February 1660.<sup id="cite_ref-EB1911_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-17">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> There he accumulated allies and  agreements among the English and London establishments including the newly constituted <a href="/wiki/Convention_Parliament_(1660)" title="Convention Parliament (1660)">Convention Parliament</a>, to which Monck was elected a member.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHenning1983_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHenning1983-18">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> First a <a href="/wiki/Royalist" title="Royalist">Royalist</a> campaigner, then a Parliamentary soldier, he now contrived for the <a href="/wiki/Restoration_(1660)" title="Restoration (1660)">Restoration</a> of the monarchy; <a href="/wiki/George_Monck,_1st_Duke_of_Albemarle#Restoration_of_the_monarchy" title="George Monck, 1st Duke of Albemarle">Monck arranged</a> that the Convention Parliament would invite Charles II to return as king of the three realms\xe2\x80\x94which was done by act of Parliament on 1 May 1660.\n</p><p>The Wars of the Three Kingdoms pre-figured many of the changes that ultimately would shape modern Britain; but <i>in the short term</i>, the conflicts actually resolved little for the kingdoms and peoples of the times. The <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_England" title="Commonwealth of England">English Commonwealth</a> did achieve a notable compromise between monarchy and a republic, even one that survived destabilizing issues for nearly the  next two hundred years. In practice, Oliver Cromwell exercised political power through his control over Parliament\'s military forces, but his legal position\xe2\x80\x94and provisions for his succession\xe2\x80\x94remained unclear, even after he became <a href="/wiki/Lord_Protector" title="Lord Protector">Lord Protector</a>. None of the several constitutions proposed during this period were realized. Thus the Commonwealth and Protectorate of the Parliamentarians\xe2\x80\x94the wars\' victors\xe2\x80\x94left no significant new forms of government in place after their time.\n</p><p>Still, <i>in the long term</i>, two abiding legacies of British democracy were established during this period:\n</p>\n<ul><li>after the execution of King Charles I for <a href="/wiki/High_treason_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="High treason in the United Kingdom">high treason</a>, no future British monarch could expect their subjects would tolerate perceived <a href="/wiki/Despotism" title="Despotism">despotism</a>\xe2\x80\x94\'the divine rights of kings\' was no more;<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJane1905376\xe2\x80\x93377_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJane1905376\xe2\x80\x93377-19">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>the excesses of the New Model Army, particularly that of the <a href="/wiki/Rule_of_the_Major-Generals" title="Rule of the Major-Generals">Rule of the Major-Generals</a>, left an abiding mistrust of military dictators and military rule that persists until today among peoples of British descent or national association.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;e&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>\n<p>English Protestants experienced religious freedom during the <a href="/wiki/English_Interregnum" class="mw-redirect" title="English Interregnum">Interregnum</a>, but there was none for English Roman Catholics. During the term of their control, the Presbyterian partisans abolished the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">Church of England</a> and the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Lords" title="House of Lords">House of Lords</a>. Cromwell <a href="/wiki/Oliver_Cromwell#Return_to_England_and_dissolution_of_the_Rump_Parliament:_1651\xe2\x80\x9353" title="Oliver Cromwell">denounced the Rump Parliament</a> and dissolved it by force,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECromwell1939501_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECromwell1939501-21">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> but he failed to establish an acceptable alternative. Nor did he and his supporters move in the direction of popular democracy, as the more radical Parliamentarians (the <a href="/wiki/Levellers" title="Levellers">Levellers</a>) wanted.\n</p><p>During the Interregnum, the New Model Army occupied Ireland and Scotland. In Ireland, the new government confiscated almost all lands belonging to Irish Catholics as punishment for the rebellion of 1641; harsh <a href="/wiki/Penal_Laws_(Ireland)" title="Penal Laws (Ireland)">Penal Laws</a> also restricted this community. Thousands of Parliamentarian soldiers settled in Ireland on confiscated lands. The Commonwealth abolished the Parliaments of Ireland and Scotland. In theory, these countries had representation in the English Parliament, but as this body never held real powers, representation was ineffective. When Cromwell died in 1658 the Commonwealth fell apart\xe2\x80\x94but without major violence. Historians record that adroit politicians of the time,  <a href="/wiki/George_Monck,_1st_Duke_of_Albemarle#Critiques_of_his_character" title="George Monck, 1st Duke of Albemarle">especially George Monck</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBurnet1753_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurnet1753-22">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> prevailed over the looming crisis; Monck in particular was deemed the <a href="/wiki/George_Monck#Restoration_of_the_monarchy" class="mw-redirect" title="George Monck">victor <i>sine sanguine,</i> i.e., "without blood"</a>, of the Restoration crisis.<sup id="cite_ref-EB1911_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-17">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPepys1660&#91;httpsenwikisourceorgwikiDiary_of_Samuel_Pepys1660March16th_Entry_for_16_March_1660&#93;_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPepys1660[httpsenwikisourceorgwikiDiary_of_Samuel_Pepys1660March16th_Entry_for_16_March_1660]-23">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> And in 1660, Charles II was restored as king of England, Scotland, and Ireland.\n</p><p>Under the <a href="/wiki/Restoration_(England)" title="Restoration (England)">English Restoration</a> the political system returned to the constitutional position of before the wars. Although Charles II\'s <a href="/wiki/Declaration_of_Breda" title="Declaration of Breda">Declaration of Breda, April 1660,</a> offering reconciliation and forgiveness, had promised a general pardon for crimes committed during the English Civil War,    the new r\xc3\xa9gime executed or imprisoned for life those directly involved in the <a href="/wiki/Regicide" title="Regicide">regicide</a> of Charles I. Royalists dug up Cromwell\'s corpse and performed a <a href="/wiki/Posthumous_execution" title="Posthumous execution">posthumous execution</a>. Those religious and political radicals held responsible for the wars suffered harsh repression. Scotland and Ireland regained their Parliaments and some Irish retrieved confiscated lands; the New Model Army was disbanded. However, the issues that had caused the wars\xe2\x80\x94religion, the powers of Parliament vis-\xc3\xa1-vis the king, and the relationships between the three kingdoms\xe2\x80\x94remained unresolved, postponed actually, to re-emerge as matters fought over again leading to the <a href="/wiki/Glorious_Revolution" title="Glorious Revolution">Glorious Revolution</a> of 1688. Only after this later time did the larger features of modern Britain foreshadowed in the civil wars emerge permanently, namely: a Protestant <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_monarchy" title="Constitutional monarchy">constitutional monarchy</a> with England dominant, and with a strong <a href="/wiki/Standing_army" title="Standing army">standing army</a> under civilian control.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wars_of_the_Three_Kingdoms&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: See also">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Catholicism_and_the_wars_of_religion" class="mw-redirect" title="Catholicism and the wars of religion">Catholicism and the wars of religion</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Thirty_Years%27_War" title="Thirty Years&#39; War">Thirty Years\' War</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Timeline_of_the_Wars_of_the_Three_Kingdoms" title="Timeline of the Wars of the Three Kingdoms">Timeline of the Wars of the Three Kingdoms</a></li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notes">Notes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wars_of_the_Three_Kingdoms&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Notes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist" style="list-style-type: lower-alpha;">\n<div class="mw-references-wrap"><ol class="references">\n<li id="cite_note-3"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-3">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">"While it is notoriously difficult to determine the number of casualties in any war, it has been estimated that the conflict in England and Wales claimed about 85,000 lives in combat, with a further 127,000 noncombat deaths (including some 40,000 civilians)."<sup id="cite_ref-EB2018_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EB2018-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> </span>\n</li>\n<li id="cite_note-4"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-4">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text"><a href="#CITEREFGentles2007">Gentles 2007</a>, p.&#160;3, citing John Morrill, states, "there is no stable, agreed title for the events....
