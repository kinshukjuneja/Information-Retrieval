href="#cite_note-Ref_-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Nationalization usually refers to private assets or assets owned by lower levels of government, such as <a href="/wiki/Municipality" title="Municipality">municipalities</a>, being transferred to the state. The opposites of nationalization are <a href="/wiki/Privatization" title="Privatization">privatization</a> and <a href="/wiki/Demutualization" title="Demutualization">demutualization</a>. When previously nationalized assets are privatized and subsequently returned to public ownership by a later government, they are said to have undergone <b>renationalization</b>. Industries that are usually subject to nationalization include <a href="/wiki/Transport" title="Transport">transport</a>, <a href="/wiki/Communications" class="mw-redirect" title="Communications">communications</a>, <a href="/wiki/Energy_industry" title="Energy industry">energy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Banking" class="mw-redirect" title="Banking">banking</a> and <a href="/wiki/Natural_resources" class="mw-redirect" title="Natural resources">natural resources</a>.\n</p><p>Nationalization may occur with or without compensation to the former owners. Nationalization is distinguished from <a href="/wiki/Property_redistribution" class="mw-redirect" title="Property redistribution">property redistribution</a> in that the government retains control of nationalized <a href="/wiki/Property" title="Property">property</a>. Some nationalizations take place when a government seizes property acquired illegally. For example, in 1945 the French government seized the car-makers <a href="/wiki/Renault" title="Renault">Renault</a> because its owners had collaborated with the <a href="/wiki/Nazi" class="mw-redirect" title="Nazi">Nazi</a> occupiers of France.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Nationalization is to be distinguished from "<a href="/wiki/Socialization_(economics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Socialization (economics)">socialization</a>", which refers to the process of restructuring the economic framework, organizational structure, and institutions of an economy on a <a href="/wiki/Socialist" class="mw-redirect" title="Socialist">socialist</a> basis. By contrast, nationalization does not necessarily imply social ownership and the restructuring of the <a href="/wiki/Economic_system" title="Economic system">economic system</a>. By itself, nationalization has nothing to do with <a href="/wiki/Socialism" title="Socialism">socialism</a>, having been historically carried out for various different purposes under a wide variety of different <a href="/wiki/Political_system" title="Political system">political systems</a> and <a href="/wiki/Economic_system" title="Economic system">economic systems</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> However, nationalization is, in most cases, opposed by <a href="/wiki/Laissez_faire_capitalism" class="mw-redirect" title="Laissez faire capitalism">laissez faire capitalists</a> as it is perceived as excessive government interference in, and control of, economic affairs of individual citizens.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input They may be obliged to operate loss-making activities where it is judged that social benefits are greater than social costs \xe2\x80\x94 for example, rural postal and transport services. The government has recognized these social obligations and, in some cases, provides subsidies for such non-commercial operations.\n</p><p>Since nationalized industries are state owned, the <a href="/wiki/Government" title="Government">government</a> is responsible for meeting any <a href="/wiki/Debt" title="Debt">debts</a>. The nationalized industries do not normally borrow from the domestic market other than for short-term borrowing. If they are profitable, the profit is often used to finance other state services, such as social programs and government research, which can help lower the tax burden.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Compensation">Compensation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nationalization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Compensation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The traditional Western stance on compensation was expressed by <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secretary_of_State" title="United States Secretary of State">United States Secretary of State</a> <a href="/wiki/Cordell_Hull" title="Cordell Hull">Cordell Hull</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Expropiaci%C3%B3n_petrolera" class="mw-redirect" title="Expropiaci\xc3\xb3n petrolera">Mexican nationalization of the petroleum industry</a> in 1938, saying that compensation should be "prompt, effective and adequate". According to this view, the nationalizing state is obligated under <a href="/wiki/International_law" title="International law">international law</a> to pay the deprived party the full value of the property taken.\n</p><p>The opposing position has been taken mainly by <a href="/wiki/Developing_countries" class="mw-redirect" title="Developing countries">developing countries</a>, claiming that the question of compensation should be left entirely up to the <a href="/wiki/Sovereign_state" title="Sovereign state">sovereign state</a>, in line with the <a href="/wiki/Calvo_Doctrine" title="Calvo Doctrine">Calvo Doctrine</a>.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Socialist_state" title="Socialist state">Socialist states</a> have held that no compensation is due, based on the view that <a href="/wiki/Private_property" title="Private property">private ownership</a> over <a href="/wiki/Socialization_(Marxism)" title="Socialization (Marxism)">socialized assets</a> is illegitimate, exploitative, or a hindrance to further economic development.\n</p><p>In 1962, the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_General_Assembly" title="United Nations General Assembly">United Nations General Assembly</a> adopted Resolution 1803, "Permanent Sovereignty over National Resources", which states that in the event of nationalization, the owner "shall be paid appropriate compensation in accordance with international law". In doing so, the UN rejected the traditional Calvo-doctrinal view and the Communist view. The term "appropriate compensation" represents a compromise between the traditional views, taking into account the need of <a href="/wiki/Developing_countries" class="mw-redirect" title="Developing countries">developing countries</a> to pursue reform, even without the ability to pay full compensation, and the Western concern for the protection of private property.\n</p><p>In the United States, the <a href="/wiki/Fifth_Amendment_to_the_United_States_Constitution" title="Fifth Amendment to the United States Constitution">Fifth Amendment</a> requires just compensation if private property is taken for public use.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_support">Political support</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a In this context, the goals of nationalization were to dispossess large capitalists, redirect the profits of industry to the public purse and establish some form of <a href="/wiki/Workers%27_self-management" title="Workers&#39; self-management">workers\' self-management</a> as a precursor to the establishment of a socialist economic system.<sup id="cite_ref-The_Economics_of_Feasible_Socialism_Revisted,_1991._P.176_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Economics_of_Feasible_Socialism_Revisted,_1991._P.176-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> after the <a href="/wiki/Second_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Second World War">Second World War</a>, nationalization gained support by the <a href="/wiki/Labour_party_(UK)" class="mw-redirect" title="Labour party (UK)">Labour party</a> and some social democratic parties throughout Europe. Although sometimes undertaken as part of a strategy to build socialism, more commonly nationalization was also undertaken and used to protect and develop industries perceived as being vital to the nation\'s competitiveness (such as aerospace and shipbuilding), or to protect jobs in certain industries.\n</p><p>A re-nationalization occurs when state-owned assets are privatized and later nationalized again, often when a different <a href="/wiki/Political_party" title="Political party">political party</a> or <a href="/wiki/Political_factions" class="mw-redirect" title="Political factions">faction</a> is in power. A re-nationalization process may also be called "reverse privatization". Nationalization has been used to refer to either direct state-ownership and management of an enterprise or to a government acquiring a large controlling share of a <a href="/wiki/Public_company" title="Public company">publicly listed corporation</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources.
