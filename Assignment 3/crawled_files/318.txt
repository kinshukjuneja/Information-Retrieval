style="text-align:right;">Nuttall\n</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><b>Skiddaw</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Mountain" title="Mountain">mountain</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Lake_District_National_Park" class="mw-redirect" title="Lake District National Park">Lake District National Park</a> in <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>. Its 931-metre (3,054&#160;ft) summit is <a href="/wiki/List_of_Furths#England" title="List of Furths">the sixth-highest in England</a>. It lies just north of the town of <a href="/wiki/Keswick,_Cumbria" title="Keswick, Cumbria">Keswick</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cumbria" title="Cumbria">Cumbria</a>, and dominates the skyline in this part of the northern lakes. It is the simplest of the Lake District mountains of this height to ascend (as there is a well-trodden tourist track from a car park to the north-east of Keswick, near the summit of <a href="/wiki/Latrigg" title="Latrigg">Latrigg</a>) and, as such, many walking guides recommend it to the occasional walker wishing to climb a mountain. This is the first summit of the <a href="/wiki/Fell_running" title="Fell running">fell running</a> challenge known as the <a href="/wiki/Bob_Graham_Round" title="Bob Graham Round">Bob Graham Round</a> when undertaken in a clockwise direction.\n</p><p>The mountain lends its name to the surrounding areas of \xe2\x80\x98Skiddaw Forest\xe2\x80\x99, and \xe2\x80\x98Back o\' Skidda\' \xe2\x80\x99 and to the isolated \xe2\x80\x98<a href="/w/index.php?title=Skiddaw_house&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Skiddaw house (page does not exist)">Skiddaw House</a>\xe2\x80\x99, situated to the east, formerly a shooting lodge and subsequently a <a href="/wiki/Youth_hostel" class="mw-redirect" title="Youth hostel">youth hostel</a>. It also provides the name for the slate derived from that region: <a href="/wiki/Skiddaw_slate" class="mw-redirect" title="Skiddaw slate">Skiddaw slate</a>. Tuned percussion musical instruments or <a href="/wiki/Lithophone" title="Lithophone">lithophones</a> exist which are made from the slate, such as the <a href="/wiki/Musical_Stones_of_Skiddaw" title="Musical Stones of Skiddaw">Musical Stones of Skiddaw</a> held at the <a href="/wiki/Keswick_Museum_and_Art_Gallery" title="Keswick Museum and Art Gallery">Keswick Museum and Art Gallery</a>.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" style="display:none" /><div class="toctitle" lang="en" the Skiddaw locality</div></div></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Northern_Fells" title="Northern Fells">Northern Fells</a> make up a roughly circular upland area approaching 10 miles (16&#160;km) in width. At the centre is the marshy depression of Skiddaw Forest, a treeless plateau, or valley, at an altitude of about 400 metres (1,300&#160;ft); flowing outwards from here are the rivers that divide the area into three sectors. The south-western sector, between the <a href="/wiki/Glenderaterra_Beck" title="Glenderaterra Beck">Glenderaterra Beck</a> and Dash Beck, contains Skiddaw and its satellites.<sup id="cite_ref-wainwright_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wainwright-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Skiddaw itself takes the form of a north\xe2\x80\x93south ridge about half a mile long, with steep slopes to east and west. The ridge continues northwards over Broad End to <a href="/wiki/Bakestall" title="Bakestall">Bakestall</a>, a fell overlooking the Whitewater Dash waterfall. Further ridges fan out east and west from the southern end of Skiddaw. To the south-east are <a href="/wiki/Skiddaw_Little_Man" title="Skiddaw Little Man">Skiddaw Little Man</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lonscale_Fell" title="Lonscale Fell">Lonscale Fell</a> and <a href="/wiki/Latrigg" title="Latrigg">Latrigg</a>, an easily accessible viewpoint for Keswick and <a href="/wiki/Derwentwater" title="Derwentwater">Derwentwater</a>. Beyond these fells are the <a href="/wiki/Glenderaterra_Beck" title="Glenderaterra Beck">Glenderaterra Beck</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Blencathra" title="Blencathra">Blencathra</a> group. The south-western ridge curves round through 180 degrees to run north above the shore of <a href="/wiki/Bassenthwaite_Lake" title="Bassenthwaite Lake">Bassenthwaite Lake</a>. This gives Skiddaw an \'outer wall\', comprising <a href="/wiki/Carl_Side" title="Carl Side">Carl Side</a>, <a href="/wiki/Long_Side" title="Long Side">Long Side</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ullock_Pike" title="Ullock Pike">Ullock Pike</a>, collectively referred to as Longside Edge. The final member of the Skiddaw Group is <a href="/wiki/Dodd_(Lake_District)" title="Dodd (Lake District)">Dodd</a>, a satellite of Carl Side.\n</p><p>Between Skiddaw and Longside Edge are the quiet valleys of Southerndale and Barkbethdale, separated by the spur of Buzzard Knott. These drain the western flanks of the fell to Bassenthwaite Lake. The eastern side of Skiddaw drains into Skiddaw Forest, much of the water reaching Candleseaves Bog. This marsh is the source of both the Dash Beck flowing north west to Bassenthwaite and the <a href="/wiki/River_Caldew" title="River Caldew">River Caldew</a>, beginning its long journey north-eastward to the <a href="/wiki/Solway_Firth" title="Solway Firth">Solway Firth</a> via <a href="/wiki/Carlisle,_Cumbria" class="mw-redirect" title="Carlisle, Cumbria">Carlisle</a>. Two smooth spurs on this eastern flank of Skiddaw, Sale How and Hare Crag, are listed in separate tops in some guidebooks.<sup id="cite_ref-birkett_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-birkett-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Sale How is also a <a href="/wiki/Nuttall_(hill)" class="mw-redirect" title="Nuttall (hill)">Nuttall</a>.\n</p><p>Skiddaw\'s slopes are generally rounded and convex, looking from a distance as though a thick velvet blanket has been draped over a supporting frame. On the ridges the general terrain is of loose stones, but elsewhere all is grass and heather. <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Wainwright" title="Alfred Wainwright">Wainwright</a> noted that "Its lines are smooth, its curves graceful; but because the slopes are steep everywhere, the quick build-up of the \xe2\x80\x9cmassif\xe2\x80\x9d from valley levels to central summit is appreciated at a glance \xe2\x80\x94 and it should be an appreciative glance, for such massive strength and such beauty of outline rarely go together."<sup id="cite_ref-wainwright_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wainwright-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geology">Geology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Skiddaw&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The bedrock of Skiddaw, commonly known as <a href="/wiki/Skiddaw_Slate" title="Skiddaw Slate">Skiddaw Slate</a>, is the Kirkstile Formation. This <a href="/wiki/Ordovician" title="Ordovician">Ordovician</a> rock is composed of laminated <a href="/wiki/Mudstone" title="Mudstone">mudstone</a> and <a href="/wiki/Siltstone" title="Siltstone">siltstone</a> with <a href="/wiki/Greywacke" title="Greywacke">greywacke</a> sandstone. At the summit this is overlain by <a href="/wiki/Scree" title="Scree">scree</a> and to the south are areas where the underlying Loweswater Formation surfaces.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" All now bear <a href="/wiki/Cairn" title="Cairn">cairns</a> and a number of stone windshelters have been erected.  Skiddaw has a subsidiary summit, <a href="/wiki/Skiddaw_Little_Man" title="Skiddaw Little Man">Little Man</a>, which lies about 1.5&#160;km south-south-east of the main peak. Despite its limited independence, Wainwright listed it as a separate fell in his influential <i><a href="/wiki/Pictorial_Guide_to_the_Lakeland_Fells" title="Pictorial Guide to the Lakeland Fells">Pictorial Guide to the Lakeland Fells</a></i>, a convention which is often followed. Skiddaw Little Man has its own subsidiary summit, known as Lesser Man.\n</p><p>The view is as panoramic as might be expected, given Skiddaw\'s <a href="/wiki/Topographic_prominence" title="Topographic prominence">topographic prominence</a>. From High Man the north east quadrant is filled by the quiet fells of Back o\'Skiddaw, with the Border hills, the <a href="/wiki/The_Cheviot" title="The Cheviot">Cheviots</a> and the North <a href="/wiki/Pennines" title="Pennines">Pennines</a> behind them. To the south east are Blencathra, the <a href="/wiki/Far_Eastern_Fells" title="Far Eastern Fells">Far Eastern Fells</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Helvellyn_range" title="Helvellyn range">Helvellyn range</a>; behind these are vistas of the <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire_Dales" title="Yorkshire Dales">Yorkshire Dales</a> and <a href="/wiki/Forest_of_Bowland" title="Forest of Bowland">Forest of Bowland</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Coniston_Fells" class="mw-redirect" title="Coniston Fells">Coniston Fells</a> are visible directly to the south. On the other side of South Top is a fine view of the <a href="/wiki/Scafells" title="Scafells">Scafells</a>, <a href="/wiki/Western_Fells" title="Western Fells">Western</a> and <a href="/wiki/North_Western_Fells" title="North Western Fells">North Western Fells</a>, with a portion of <a href="/wiki/Snowdonia" title="Snowdonia">Snowdonia</a> visible between <a href="/wiki/Kirk_Fell" title="Kirk Fell">Kirk Fell</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pillar_(Lake_District)" title="Pillar (Lake District)">Pillar</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man" title="Isle of Man">Isle of Man</a> is visible 60 miles (97&#160;km) away, as are the <a href="/wiki/Mourne_Mountains" title="Mourne Mountains">Mourne Mountains</a> 120 miles (190&#160;km) away (on exceptionally clear days). The final quarter is taken up by the coastal plain and the distant Solway Firth, backed by the hills of <a href="/wiki/Galloway" title="Galloway">Galloway</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Merrick,_Galloway" class="mw-redirect" title="Merrick, Galloway">Merrick</a>, <a href="/wiki/Criffel" title="Criffel">Criffel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Broad_Law" title="Broad Law">Broad Law</a>. <a href="/wiki/Goat_Fell" title="Goat Fell">Goat Fell</a> on <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Arran" title="Isle of Arran">Arran</a> can be seen at an angle of 313 degrees, 105 miles (169&#160;km) away.\n</p><p>Most distant view; Slieve Meelmore in <a href="/wiki/Mourne_(Northern_Ireland_Parliament_constituency)" title="Mourne (Northern Ireland Parliament constituency)">Mourne</a>, 120 miles (190&#160;km) distant.\n</p><p>By moving to South Top a superb view of <a href="/wiki/Borrowdale" title="Borrowdale">Borrowdale</a> can be brought into sight.<sup id="cite_ref-wainwright_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wainwright-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ascents">Ascents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Skiddaw&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Ascents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Summit_ridge_of_Skiddaw_(geograph_2707510).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Summit_ridge_of_Skiddaw_%28geograph_2707510%29.jpg/220px-Summit_ridge_of_Skiddaw_%28geograph_2707510%29.jpg" width="220" height="132" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Summit_ridge_of_Skiddaw_%28geograph_2707510%29.jpg/330px-Summit_ridge_of_Skiddaw_%28geograph_2707510%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Summit_ridge_of_Skiddaw_%28geograph_2707510%29.jpg/440px-Summit_ridge_of_Skiddaw_%28geograph_2707510%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1500" data-file-height="902" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Summit_ridge_of_Skiddaw_(geograph_2707510).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Summit ridge</div></div></div>\n<p>Many routes of ascent have been devised for Skiddaw; indeed, it is hard to devise a challenging approach in good conditions. The most popular tourist route starts from Keswick and first ascends behind Latrigg, before the climb continues over the slopes of Little Man to the summit. About 200m of ascent can be saved by driving to the top of Gale Road and beginning from the public carpark just behind the summit of Latrigg.\n</p><p>Another popular route (and the one recommended by Wainwright<sup id="cite_ref-wainwright_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wainwright-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>) is to follow Longside Edge, first ascending Ullock Pike, Longside and Carl Side before making the steep climb up from Carlside Col. Also from the north, a somewhat tougher alternative is to walk up Buzzard Knott between Southerndale and Barkbethdale: after crossing to the southern edge of the shoulder above Randel Crag ascend due east to the summit. Rather easier than either of these is the compass-walk due south from Cock Up (505m); reversing this route provides a safe descent, especially in bad weather. Scramblers may prefer simply to walk up Southerndale and climb Longside via a distinctive crevice seen easily from Skiddaw summit.\n</p><p>From the south-west at Millbeck, Carlside Col can be reached directly. A start from nearby Applethwaite can also be used to provide a variation to the tourist route. From the north-west a tough but picturesque ascent can be made to the northern end of Longside Edge before following the ridge route to the summit. Finally, ascents from due east are possible for the walker who first makes for Skiddaw House, a good distance from either Keswick, <a href="/wiki/Threlkeld" title="Threlkeld">Threlkeld</a> or Peter House. Once Skiddaw House is reached a fairly direct line is possible, climbing over either Sale How or Hare Crag.<sup id="cite_ref-wainwright_1-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wainwright-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Keswick_and_Skiddaw.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/Keswick_and_Skiddaw.jpg/220px-Keswick_and_Skiddaw.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/Keswick_and_Skiddaw.jpg/330px-Keswick_and_Skiddaw.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/Keswick_and_Skiddaw.jpg/440px-Keswick_and_Skiddaw.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4608" data-file-height="3456" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Keswick_and_Skiddaw.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Skiddaw with <a href="/wiki/Keswick,_Cumbria" title="Keswick, Cumbria">Keswick</a> at its foot</div></div></div>\n<p>From the north-east an unmarked but quite easy and fairly well-worn path starts at Whitewater Dash waterfall (on the <a href="/wiki/Cumbria_Way" title="Cumbria Way">Cumbrian Way</a>) where the walker can follow the fence (along Birkett Edge just south of Dead Crags) past the Bakestall outcrop, and follow the fence until just before the unnamed top at 831m. From the 831m top, a path leads directly to Skiddaw Man.\n</p><p>The first ascent of Skiddaw probably pre-dates recorded human history, however the first ascent of the mountain to be captured on film or video was made in 1995 by local Cumbrian photographer and film-maker Michael Lakey.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Skiddaw&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>According to <a href="/wiki/Eilert_Ekwall" title="Eilert Ekwall">Eilert Ekwall</a>, Skiddaw\'s name is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Old_Norse" title="Old Norse">Old Norse</a> elements <i>skyti</i> or <i>skut</i> + <i>haugr</i> meaning either "archer\'s hill" or "jutting crag hill".<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Diana Whaley likewise interprets it as "the mountain with the jutting crag", but also offers the alternative that the first element may be a personal name or Old Norse <i>sk\xc3\xadtr</i> \'dung, filth, shit\'.<sup id="cite_ref-Whaley_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Whaley-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Richard_Coates" title="Richard Coates">Richard Coates</a> suggests that "it is possible that a <a href="/wiki/Cumbric" title="Cumbric">Cumbric</a> solution is to be sought."<sup id="cite_ref-Whaley_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Whaley-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Skiddaw_House">Skiddaw House</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Skiddaw&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Skiddaw House">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Skiddaw_House_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1519820.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Skiddaw_House_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1519820.jpg/220px-Skiddaw_House_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1519820.jpg" width="220" height="120" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Skiddaw_House_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1519820.jpg/330px-Skiddaw_House_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1519820.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Skiddaw_House_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1519820.jpg/440px-Skiddaw_House_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1519820.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="348" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Skiddaw_House_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1519820.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Skiddaw House</div></div></div>\n<p>Below Sale How is Skiddaw House, a stone building which has variously served as a shooting lodge, shepherd\'s bothy and <a href="/wiki/Youth_Hostels_Association_(England_%26_Wales)" title="Youth Hostels Association (England &amp; Wales)">Youth Hostel</a>. Its windbreak comprises the only trees in Skiddaw Forest, and it is reached via a long access track up the <a href="/wiki/Dash_Valley" title="Dash Valley">Dash Valley</a>.\n</p><p>Built around 1829 by the <a href="/wiki/George_O%27Brien_Wyndham,_3rd_Earl_of_Egremont" class="mw-redirect" title="George O&#39;Brien Wyndham, 3rd Earl of Egremont">Earl of Egremont</a> it was originally a keeper\'s lodge; a base for grouse shooting and for the gamekeepers who managed the extensive land owned by Egremont in Skiddaw Forest.\nLittle is known of the house in the nineteenth century but it was used by both gamekeepers and shepherds beyond 1860 and there were rooms for Egremont and the shooting parties.<sup id="cite_ref-shf_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-shf-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Hardwicke_Rawnsley" title="Hardwicke Rawnsley">Canon Rawnsley</a>, a founder of the <a href="/wiki/National_Trust_for_Places_of_Historic_Interest_or_Natural_Beauty" title="National Trust for Places of Historic Interest or Natural Beauty">National Trust</a>, visited in about 1900 and mentions in one of his books the hospitality of the shepherd\'s family at that time.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>  <a href="/wiki/Hugh_Walpole" title="Hugh Walpole">Sir Hugh Walpole</a>, author of the Lake District novel <i>Rogue Herries</i>, was a visitor in the 1920s and 30s and used the house as the scene, set in 1854, of the murder by Uhland.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>These arrangements for accommodation continued into the 20th century up until the early 1950s. Several families brought up their children there until they were able to go to school, but the longest tenure was possibly from Pearson Dalton, a shepherd and bachelor from the <a href="/wiki/Caldbeck" title="Caldbeck">Caldbeck</a> area, who came to stay for a month in 1952 and left in 1969 aged 75. He lived there alone for five days a week, only going home for long weekends with his sister in Caldbeck, then returning on the Monday to resume his duties. By this time farming practices had changed and the house was no longer needed and declined although there was intermittent use by various schools and outdoor groups.<sup id="cite_ref-shf_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-shf-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Kitchen_Range,_Skiddaw_House_(geograph_4392542).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/The_Kitchen_Range%2C_Skiddaw_House_%28geograph_4392542%29.jpg/220px-The_Kitchen_Range%2C_Skiddaw_House_%28geograph_4392542%29.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/The_Kitchen_Range%2C_Skiddaw_House_%28geograph_4392542%29.jpg/330px-The_Kitchen_Range%2C_Skiddaw_House_%28geograph_4392542%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/The_Kitchen_Range%2C_Skiddaw_House_%28geograph_4392542%29.jpg/440px-The_Kitchen_Range%2C_Skiddaw_House_%28geograph_4392542%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="480" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Kitchen_Range,_Skiddaw_House_(geograph_4392542).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Kitchen range</div></div></div>\n<p>In 1986 the house was leased by John Bothamley who had created the <a href="/wiki/Youth_Hostels_Association_(England_%26_Wales)" title="Youth Hostels Association (England &amp; Wales)">YHA</a> Carrock Fell Hostel a few miles away and eventually the building was handed over to the YHA. It closed in 2002 following a disagreement over the lease. In 2007 it re-opened as an independent hostel run by the Skiddaw House Foundation, under the YHA\'s Enterprise franchising model.<sup id="cite_ref-shf_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-shf-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_literature">In literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Skiddaw&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: In literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Skiddaw is mentioned in the fourth book of <a href="/wiki/John_Keats" title="John Keats">John Keats</a>\'s poem <i><a href="/wiki/Endymion_(poem)" title="Endymion (poem)">Endymion</a></i>:\n"\xe2\x80\xa6with all the stress / Of
