title="Portugal">Portugal</a></th>\n</tr><tr style="display:none"><th colspan="2">\n</th></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>The <b>Anglo-Portuguese Alliance </b>(or <i><b>Alian\xc3\xa7a Luso-Brit\xc3\xa2nica</b></i>, "Luso-British Alliance", also known in Portugal as <i><b>Alian\xc3\xa7a Inglesa</b></i>, "English Alliance"), ratified at the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Windsor_(1386)" title="Treaty of Windsor (1386)">Treaty of Windsor</a> in 1386, between <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_England" title="Kingdom of England">England</a> (<a href="/wiki/Succession_of_states" title="Succession of states">succeeded</a> by the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a>, may be the oldest <a href="/wiki/Military_alliance" title="Military alliance">alliance</a> in the world that is still in force \xe2\x80\x93 with the earliest treaty dating back to the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Portuguese_Treaty_of_1373" title="Anglo-Portuguese Treaty of 1373">Anglo-Portuguese Treaty of 1373</a> - although this claim is disputed by some historians who believe the <a href="/wiki/Auld_Alliance" title="Auld Alliance">Auld Alliance</a> between Scotland and France, first signed in 1295, may still be in effect.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Historically, the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Portugal" title="Kingdom of Portugal">Kingdom of Portugal</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_England" title="Kingdom of England">Kingdom of England</a>, and later the modern <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a> and <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, have never waged war against each other nor have they participated in wars on opposite sides as independent states since the signing of the Treaty of Windsor. While Portugal was subsumed under the <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Union" title="Iberian Union">Iberian Union</a>, rebellious Portuguese factions and <a href="/wiki/Government_in_exile" title="Government in exile">government in exile</a> sought refuge and help in England. England spearheaded the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Spanish_War_(1585%E2%80%931604)" title="Anglo-Spanish War (1585\xe2\x80\x931604)">Anglo-Spanish War (1585\xe2\x80\x931604)</a> on the side of the <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_succession_crisis_of_1580" title="Portuguese succession crisis of 1580">deposed Portuguese royal house</a>.\n</p><p>The alliance has served both countries throughout their respective military histories, influencing the participation of the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_of_Great_Britain_and_Ireland" title="United Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland">United Kingdom</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">Iberian</a> <a href="/wiki/Peninsular_War" title="Peninsular War">Peninsular War</a>, the UK\'s major land contribution to the <a href="/wiki/Napoleonic_Wars" title="Napoleonic Wars">Napoleonic Wars</a> and the establishment of an Anglo-American base in Portugal. Portugal aided England (and later the UK) in times of need, for example, in the <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">First World War</a>. Today, Portugal and the United Kingdom are both part of <a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">NATO</a>, a larger intergovernmental military alliance between several North American and European states that accounts for over 70% of total global military spending.\n</p>\n<table class="vertical-navbox nowraplinks vcard plainlist" style="float:right;clear:right;width:22.0em;margin:0 0 1.0em Portugal</a>.</div></div></div>\n<p>English aid to the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Aviz" title="House of Aviz">House of Aviz</a> (which ruled Portugal from 1385 to 1580) set the stage for Portuguese cooperation with England that would become a cornerstone of Portugal\'s foreign policy for more than five hundred years.  However, English aid to Portugal went back much further to the 1147 <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Lisbon" title="Siege of Lisbon">Siege of Lisbon</a>, when English and other northern European crusaders \xe2\x80\x93 en route to the Holy Land to participate in the <a href="/wiki/Second_Crusade" title="Second Crusade">Second Crusade</a> \xe2\x80\x93 stopped and helped Portuguese King <a href="/wiki/Afonso_Henriques" class="mw-redirect" title="Afonso Henriques">Afonso Henriques</a> to conquer the city from the <a href="/wiki/Moors" title="Moors">Moors</a>. In May 1386, the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Windsor_1386" class="mw-redirect" title="Treaty of Windsor 1386">Treaty of Windsor</a> sealed the alliance - first started in 1294, renewed in the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Portuguese_Treaty_of_1373" title="Anglo-Portuguese Treaty of 1373">Anglo-Portuguese Treaty of 1373</a> and confirmed at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Aljubarrota" title="Battle of Aljubarrota">Battle of Aljubarrota</a> (1385) - with a pact of perpetual friendship between the two countries. The most important part of the treaty stated that:\n</p>\n<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote">\n<p>It is cordially agreed that if, in time to come, one of the kings or his heir shall need the support of the other, or his help, and in order to get such assistance applies to his ally in lawful manner, the ally shall be bound to give aid and succour to the other, so far as he is able (without any deceit, fraud, or pretence) to the extent required by the danger to his ally\xe2\x80\x99s realms, lands, domains, and subjects; and he shall be firmly bound by these present alliances to do this.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n</blockquote>\n<p>In July 1386, <a href="/wiki/John_of_Gaunt" title="John of Gaunt">John of Gaunt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Lancaster" title="Duke of Lancaster">Duke of Lancaster</a>, son of the late king <a href="/wiki/Edward_III_of_England" title="Edward III of England">Edward III of England</a> and father of the future King <a href="/wiki/Henry_IV_of_England" title="Henry IV of England">Henry IV of England</a>, landed in <a href="/wiki/Galicia_(Spain)" title="Galicia (Spain)">Galicia</a> with an expeditionary force to press his claim to the <a href="/wiki/Crown_of_Castile" title="Crown of Castile">Crown of Castile</a> with Portuguese aid. He failed to win the support of the Castilian nobility and returned to England with a cash compensation from the rival claimant.\n</p><p>John of Gaunt left behind his daughter, <a href="/wiki/Philippa_of_Lancaster" title="Philippa of Lancaster">Philippa of Lancaster</a>, to marry King <a href="/wiki/John_I_of_Portugal" title="John I of Portugal">John I of Portugal</a> (February 1387) in order to seal the Anglo-Portuguese alliance. By this marriage, John I became the father of a generation of princes called by the poet <a href="/wiki/Lu%C3%ADs_de_Cam%C3%B5es" title="Lu\xc3\xads de Cam\xc3\xb5es">Lu\xc3\xads de Cam\xc3\xb5es</a> the "<a href="/wiki/Illustrious_Generation" title="Illustrious Generation">Illustrious Generation</a>", which led Portugal into its <a href="/wiki/Golden_age" class="mw-redirect" title="Golden age">golden age</a>, during the period of the <a href="/wiki/Portugal_in_the_Age_of_Discovery" class="mw-redirect" title="Portugal in the Age of Discovery">Discoveries</a>.\n</p><p>Philippa brought to the <a href="/wiki/Court_(royal)" class="mw-redirect" title="Court (royal)">court</a> the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Normans" title="Anglo-Normans">Anglo-Norman</a> tradition of an aristocratic upbringing and gave her children good educations. Her personal qualities were of the highest standard, and she reformed the court and imposed rigid standards of moral behaviour. On the other hand, the more tolerant Portuguese aristocracy saw her methods as too traditional or outdated.\n</p><p>Philippa provided <a href="/wiki/Royal_family" title="Royal family">royal</a> patronage for English commercial interests that sought to meet the Portuguese desire for <a href="/wiki/Cod" title="Cod">cod</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cloth" class="mw-redirect" title="Cloth">cloth</a> in return for <a href="/wiki/Wine" title="Wine">wine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cork_(material)" title="Cork (material)">cork</a>, <a href="/wiki/Salt" title="Salt">salt</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Oil" title="Oil">oil</a> shipped through the English warehouses at <a href="/wiki/Porto" title="Porto">Porto</a>. Her eldest son, <a href="/wiki/Edward_of_Portugal" class="mw-redirect" title="Edward of Portugal">Duarte</a>, authored moral works and became <a href="/wiki/Monarch" title="Monarch">king</a> in 1433; <a href="/wiki/Infante_Peter,_Duke_of_Coimbra" class="mw-redirect" title="Infante Peter, Duke of Coimbra">Pedro</a>, who travelled widely and had an interest in history, became <a href="/wiki/Regent" title="Regent">regent</a> (1439-1448) after Duarte died of the <a href="/wiki/Bubonic_plague" title="Bubonic plague">plague</a> in 1438; <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_the_Saint_Prince" class="mw-redirect" title="Ferdinand the Saint Prince">Ferdinand the Saint Prince</a> (1402-1443), who became a <a href="/wiki/Crusade" class="mw-redirect" title="Crusade">crusader</a>, participated in the attack on <a href="/wiki/Tangiers" class="mw-redirect" title="Tangiers">Tangiers</a> in 1437; and Henrique \xe2\x80\x93 also known as <a href="/wiki/Prince_Henry_the_Navigator" title="Prince Henry the Navigator">Prince Henry the Navigator</a> (1394-1460) \xe2\x80\x93 became the master of the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Christ_(Portugal)" title="Order of Christ (Portugal)">Order of Christ</a> and the instigator and organiser of Portugal\'s early voyages of discovery.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Disruption">Disruption</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anglo-Portuguese_Alliance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Disruption">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" width="50" height="39" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="399" /></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>does not <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">cite</a> any <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">sources</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Anglo-Portuguese_Alliance&amp;action=edit">improve this section</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. Portuguese foreign policy became tied to Spanish hostility to England. As a result, Portugal and England were on opposite sides of the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Spanish_War_(1585%E2%80%931604)" title="Anglo-Spanish War (1585\xe2\x80\x931604)">Anglo-Spanish War (1585\xe2\x80\x931604)</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Dutch%E2%80%93Portuguese_War" title="Dutch\xe2\x80\x93Portuguese War">Dutch\xe2\x80\x93Portuguese War</a>. The alliance was reconfirmed after the <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Restoration_War" title="Portuguese Restoration War">Portuguese Restoration War</a> and the <a href="/wiki/English_Restoration" class="mw-redirect" title="English Restoration">English Restoration</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="17th_to_19th_centuries">17th to 19th centuries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anglo-Portuguese_Alliance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 17th to 19th During this time, important episodes in the alliance were:\n</p><ul><li>The <a href="/wiki/War_of_Spanish_Succession" class="mw-redirect" title="War of Spanish Succession">War of Spanish Succession</a>, when Portugal together with the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Savoy" title="Duchy of Savoy">Duchy of Savoy</a> initially sided with <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, but after the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Blenheim" title="Battle of Blenheim">Battle of Blenheim</a> reunited with its ally.</li>\n<li>The <a href="/wiki/Seven_Years%27_War" title="Seven Years&#39; War">Seven Years\' War</a>, when <a href="/wiki/Spanish_invasion_of_Portugal_(1762)" title="Spanish invasion of Portugal (1762)">Spain invaded Portugal in 1762</a>, Britain intervened as Portugal\'s ally. Although faced with vastly superior numbers, the Portuguese and British forces together with Portuguese guerrillas <a href="/wiki/Spanish_invasion_of_Portugal_(1762)" title="Spanish invasion of Portugal (1762)"> defeated</a> the Spanish and French forces, which suffered huge losses.</li>\n<li>The <a href="/wiki/Napoleonic_Wars" title="Napoleonic Wars">Napoleonic Wars</a>, when Portugal, isolated in a <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> wholly dominated by <a href="/wiki/Napoleon_I_of_France" class="mw-redirect" title="Napoleon I of France">Napoleon</a>, continued to trade with the United Kingdom despite <a href="/wiki/Continental_System" title="Continental System">French restrictions</a> and was finally <a href="/wiki/Invasion_of_Portugal_(1807)" title="Invasion of Portugal (1807)">invaded</a>, but with <a href="/wiki/Peninsular_War" title="Peninsular War">British help</a> finally regained total <a href="/wiki/Sovereignty" title="Sovereignty">sovereignty</a> and independence. Brazil">took refuge</a> in its then <a href="/wiki/Vice-royalty_of_Brazil" class="mw-redirect" title="Vice-royalty of Brazil">vice-royalty of Brazil</a>, under escort by the British fleet.</li>\n<li>The <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Wars" title="Liberal Wars">Portuguese civil war</a>, when the United Kingdom gave important support to the Liberal faction.</li>\n<li>The <a href="/wiki/1890_British_Ultimatum" title="1890 British Ultimatum">1890 British Ultimatum</a> was considered by Portuguese historians and politicians at that time to be the most outrageous and infamous action of the British against their oldest ally.<sup id="cite_ref-Ferreira_Duarte2000_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ferreira_Duarte2000-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The 1890 ultimatum was said to be one of the main causes for the <a href="/wiki/5_October_1910_revolution" title="5 October 1910 revolution">Republican Revolution</a>, which ended the monarchy in Portugal 20 years later.</li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="20th_century">20th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anglo-Portuguese_Alliance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 20th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>During the 20th century, the treaty was invoked several times:\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="First_World_War">First World War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anglo-Portuguese_Alliance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: First World War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<ul><li>After <a href="/wiki/German_Empire" title="German Empire">German</a> incursions in <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_East_Africa" class="mw-redirect" title="Portuguese East Africa">Portuguese East Africa</a> (today <a href="/wiki/Mozambique" title="Mozambique">Mozambique</a>), <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Expeditionary_Corps" title="Portuguese Expeditionary Corps">Portuguese troops</a> fought on the <a href="/wiki/Western_Front_(World_War_I)" title="Western Front (World War I)">Western Front</a> alongside <a href="/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_I" title="Allies of World War I">Allied</a> soldiers during the <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">First World War</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Second_World_War">Second World War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anglo-Portuguese_Alliance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Second World War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<ul><li>Upon the declaration of war in September 1939, the Portuguese Government announced on 1 September that the Anglo-Portuguese Alliance remained intact, but since the British did not seek Portuguese assistance, Portugal would remain neutral. In an <a href="/wiki/Aide-m%C3%A9moire" title="Aide-m\xc3\xa9moire">aide-m\xc3\xa9moire</a> of 5 September 1939, the British Government confirmed the understanding. British strategists regarded Portuguese non-belligerency as "essential to keep <a href="/wiki/Francoist_Spain" title="Francoist Spain">Spain</a> from entering the war on the side of the Axis."<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELeite1998185-199_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELeite1998185-199-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>Britain recognized the important role of the anti-democratic and authoritarian prime minister <a href="/wiki/Ant%C3%B3nio_de_Oliveira_Salazar" title="Ant\xc3\xb3nio de Oliveira Salazar">Ant\xc3\xb3nio de Oliveira Salazar</a> on May 15, 1940, when <a href="/wiki/Douglas_Veale" title="Douglas Veale">Douglas Veale</a>, <a href="/wiki/Registrar_of_the_University_of_Oxford" title="Registrar of the University of Oxford">Registrar of the University of Oxford</a>, informed Salazar that the University\'s <a href="/wiki/Hebdomadal_Council" title="Hebdomadal Council">Hebdomadal Council</a> had "unanimously decided at its meeting last Monday, to invite you [Salazar] to accept the Honorary Degree of Doctor of Civil Law".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMeneses2009240_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMeneses2009240-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>July 1940: Salazar\'s decision to stick with the Anglo-Portuguese Alliance allowed the Portuguese island of <a href="/wiki/Madeira" title="Madeira">Madeira</a> to help the Allies: that month, around 2,500 Gibraltar evacuees were shipped to Madeira.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>September 1940: Winston Churchill wrote to Salazar, congratulating him on his ability to keep Portugal out of the war, asserting that "as so often before during the many centuries of the Anglo-Portuguese alliance, British and Portuguese interests are identical on this vital question".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMeneses2009240_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMeneses2009240-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Samuel_Hoare,_1st_Viscount_Templewood" title="Samuel Hoare, 1st Viscount Templewood">Samuel Hoare, 1st Viscount Templewood</a>, the British Ambassador in Madrid from 1940 to 1944, recognised Salazar\'s crucial role in keeping Iberia neutral during the war. Lord Templewood asserted that in his thirty years of political life he had met most of the leading statesmen of Europe and that he placed Salazar very high on the list of those who impressed him. He stated that Salazar "being a man of one idea \xe2\x80\x93 the good of his country \xe2\x80\x93 he was convinced that the slightest step from the narrow path of neutrality would endanger the work of national regeneration to which he had devoted the whole of his public life". He also affirmed that "Salazar detested Hitler", that the Portuguese r\xc3\xa9gime differed fundamentally from Nazism and Fascism, and that Salazar never left a doubt in his mind that he desired a Nazi defeat.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHoare1946124-125_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHoare1946124-125-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>During the Second World War, Salazar steered Portugal down a middle path, but nevertheless provided aid to the Allies. The British Ambassador in Lisbon, <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Hugh_Campbell" title="Ronald Hugh Campbell">Ronald Campbell</a>, saw Salazar as fundamentally loyal to the Alliance and stated that "he [Salazar] would answer the call if it were made on grounds of dire necessity". When, in August 1943, the British requested base facilities in the <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">Azores</a> and invoked the alliance that had existed for over 600 years between Portugal and Great Britain,<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Salazar responded favorably and virtually at once:<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKay1970123_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKay1970123-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Portugal granted naval bases on Portuguese territory to Britain, in keeping with the traditional Anglo-Portuguese Alliance, letting them use the Azorean ports of <a href="/wiki/Horta,_Azores" title="Horta, Azores">Horta</a> (on the island of <a href="/wiki/Faial_Island" title="Faial Island">Faial</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Ponta_Delgada" title="Ponta Delgada">Ponta Delgada</a> (on the island of <a href="/wiki/S%C3%A3o_Miguel_Island" title="S\xc3\xa3o Miguel Island">S\xc3\xa3o Miguel</a>), and the airfields of <a href="/wiki/Lajes_Field" title="Lajes Field">Lajes Field</a> (on <a href="/wiki/Terceira_Island" title="Terceira Island">Terceira Island</a>) and Santana Field (on <a href="/wiki/S%C3%A3o_Miguel_Island" title="S\xc3\xa3o Miguel Island">S\xc3\xa3o Miguel Island</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>In November 1943, the British Ambassador in Lisbon, Sir Ronald Campbell, wrote (paraphrasing Salazar) that "strict neutrality was the price the allies paid for strategic benefits accruing from Portugal\'s neutrality and that if her neutrality instead of being strict had been more benevolent in our favour Spain would inevitably have thrown herself body and soul into the arms of Germany. If this had happened the Peninsula would have been occupied and then North Africa, with the result that the whole course of the war would have been altered to the advantage of the Axis."<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>From November 1943, when the British gained the use of the Azores, to June 1945, 8,689 U.S. aircraft departed from Lajes base in the Azores, including 1,200 <a href="/wiki/Boeing_B-17_Flying_Fortress" title="Boeing B-17 Flying Fortress">B-17</a> and <a href="/wiki/Consolidated_B-24_Liberator" title="Consolidated B-24 Liberator">B-24</a> bomber aircraft which were ferried across the Atlantic. Cargo aircraft carried vital personnel and equipment to North Africa, to the United Kingdom and \xe2\x80\x93 after the Allies gained a <a href="/wiki/Invasion_of_Normandy" title="Invasion of Normandy">foothold</a> in Western Europe \xe2\x80\x93 to <a href="/wiki/Orly_Field" class="mw-redirect" title="Orly Field">Orly Field</a> near Paris. Flights returning from Europe carried wounded servicemen. Medical personnel at Lajes, Azores, handled approximately 30,000 air evacuations <i>en route</i> to the United States for medical care and rehabilitation. By using Lajes Field, it was possible to reduce flying time between the United States and North Africa from 70 hours to 40. This considerable reduction in flying hours enabled aircraft to make almost twice as many crossings per month between the United States and North Africa and clearly demonstrated the geographic value of the Azores during the war.</li></ul>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Postwar">Postwar</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anglo-Portuguese_Alliance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Postwar">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<ul><li>The establishment of the <a href="/wiki/European_Free_Trade_Association" title="European Free Trade Association">European Free Trade Association</a> (EFTA) took place in 1960 following the establishment of the <a href="/wiki/European_Economic_Community" title="European Economic Community">European Economic Community</a> (EEC) in 1957. Portugal and the UK were two of the seven founding members of EFTA. In 1973 the UK left EFTA to join the EEC. Portugal did the same in 1986.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="so what? (August 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup></li>\n<li>In 1961, during the <a href="/wiki/Operation_Vijay_(1961)" class="mw-redirect" title="Operation Vijay (1961)">Indian invasion</a> of <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_India" title="Portuguese India">Portuguese India</a>, Portugal sought the help of Britain.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup></li>\n<li>During the 1982 <a href="/wiki/Falklands_War" title="Falklands War">Falklands War</a>, the facilities of the Azores were again offered to the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup></li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_times">Modern times</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span
