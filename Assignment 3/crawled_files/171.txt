srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/25/Heraldic_Visitation.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="293" data-file-height="400" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Heraldic_Visitation.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Frontispiece of the record of the visitation of <a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Dublin</a>, undertaken by <a href="/wiki/Ulster_King_of_Arms" class="mw-redirect" title="Ulster King of Arms">Ulster King of Arms</a> Daniel Molyneux in February 1607</div></div></div>\n<p><b>Heraldic visitations</b> were tours of inspection undertaken by <a href="/wiki/King_of_Arms" title="King of Arms">Kings of Arms</a> (and more often by junior <a href="/wiki/Officer_of_arms" title="Officer of arms">officers of arms (or Heralds)</a> as deputies) throughout <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>. Their purpose was to regulate and register the <a href="/wiki/Coat_of_arms" title="Coat of arms">coats of arms</a> of <a href="/wiki/Nobility" title="Nobility">nobility</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gentry" title="Gentry">gentry</a> and <a href="/wiki/Borough" title="Borough">boroughs</a>, and to record <a href="/wiki/Pedigree_chart" title="Pedigree chart">pedigrees</a>. They took place from 1530 to 1688, and their records (akin to an <a href="/wiki/Upper_class" title="Upper class">upper class</a> <a href="/wiki/Census" title="Census">census</a>) provide important source material for <a href="/wiki/Historian" title="Historian">historians</a> and <a href="/wiki/Genealogy" title="Genealogy">genealogists</a>.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" id="Visitations_in_England">Visitations in England</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heraldic_visitation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Visitations in England">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Process_of_visitations">Process of visitations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heraldic_visitation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Process of visitations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:242px;"><a href="/wiki/File:English_heraldic_visitations_map.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/English_heraldic_visitations_map.png/240px-English_heraldic_visitations_map.png" width="240" height="300" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/English_heraldic_visitations_map.png/360px-English_heraldic_visitations_map.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/English_heraldic_visitations_map.png/480px-English_heraldic_visitations_map.png 2x" data-file-width="642" data-file-height="802" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:English_heraldic_visitations_map.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Map showing the number of visitations by the <a href="/wiki/King_of_Arms" title="King of Arms">King of Arms</a> to <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>\'s counties, taken from <i>Burke\'s Landed Gentry</i>, 1937 edition</div></div></div>\n<p>By the fifteenth century, the use and abuse of <a href="/wiki/Coat_of_arms" title="Coat of arms">coats of arms</a> was becoming widespread in <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>. One of the duties conferred on <a href="/wiki/William_Bruges" title="William Bruges">William Bruges</a> (or <a href="/wiki/Brydges" title="Brydges">Brydges</a>), the first <a href="/wiki/Garter_Principal_King_of_Arms" title="Garter Principal King of Arms">Garter Principal King of Arms</a> was to survey and record the <a href="/wiki/Coat_of_arms" title="Coat of arms">armorial bearings</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pedigree_chart" title="Pedigree chart">pedigrees</a> of those using coats of arms and correct irregularities. <a href="/wiki/Officer_of_arms" title="Officer of arms">Officers of arms</a> had made occasional tours of various parts of the kingdom to enquire about armorial matters during the fifteenth century,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> however, it was not until the sixteenth century that the process began in earnest.\n</p><p>The first provincial visitations were carried out under <a href="/wiki/Warrant_(law)" title="Warrant (law)">warrant</a> granted by <a href="/wiki/Henry_VIII_of_England" title="Henry VIII of England">Henry VIII</a> to <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Benolt" title="Thomas Benolt">Thomas Benolt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Clarenceux_King_of_Arms" title="Clarenceux King of Arms">Clarenceux King of Arms</a><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> dated 6 April 1530.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> He was commissioned to travel throughout his province (i.e. south of the <a href="/wiki/River_Trent" title="River Trent">Trent</a>) with authority to enter all homes and churches. Upon entering these premises, he was authorized to "put down or otherwise deface at his discretion... those <a href="/wiki/Coat_of_arms" title="Coat of arms">arms</a> unlawfully used".<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> He was also required to enquire into all those using the titles of <a href="/wiki/Knight" title="Knight">knight</a>, <a href="/wiki/Esquire" title="Esquire">esquire</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Gentleman" title="Gentleman">gentleman</a> and decided if they were being lawfully used.\n</p><p>By this <a href="/wiki/Writ" title="Writ">writ</a>, Henry VIII also compelled the <a href="/wiki/Sheriff" title="Sheriff">sheriffs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mayor" title="Mayor">mayors</a> of each <a href="/wiki/County" title="County">county</a> or <a href="/wiki/City" title="City">city</a> visited by the <a href="/wiki/Officer_of_arms" title="Officer of arms">officers of arms</a> to give aid and assistance in gathering the needed information. When a <a href="/wiki/King_of_Arms" title="King of Arms">King of Arms</a>, or Herald, visited a county, his presence was proclaimed by presenting the King\'s royal commission to the local gentry and nobility, which required them to provide evidence of their right to use a <a href="/wiki/Coat_of_arms" title="Coat of arms">coat of arms</a>. The <a href="/wiki/High_Sheriff" class="mw-redirect" title="High Sheriff">Sheriff</a> would collect from the <a href="/wiki/Bailiff" title="Bailiff">bailiff</a> of each <a href="/wiki/Hundred_(county_division)" title="Hundred (county division)">hundred</a> within his county a list of all people using <a href="/wiki/Title" title="Title">titles</a> or arms. \n</p><p>In the early days, the visiting herald would tour the homes of the gentry and nobility, but from the late 1560s these persons were summoned to attend a central "place of sitting" \xe2\x80\x93 usually an inn \xe2\x80\x93 at a particular time.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> They were to bring their arms, and proof of their right to use them, most often by way of detailing their ancestral right to them, which would also be recorded. Where an official <a href="/wiki/Grant_of_arms" title="Grant of arms">grant of arms</a> had been made, this was also recorded. Other ancient arms, many of which predated the establishment of the <a href="/wiki/College_of_Arms" title="College of Arms">College of Arms</a>, were confirmed. The <a href="/wiki/Officer_of_arms" title="Officer of arms">officer</a> would record the information clearly and make detailed notes that could be entered into the records of the College of Arms when the party returned to <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>. \n</p><p>These volumes now make up the collection of Visitation Books at the College, which contain a wealth of information about all armigerous people from the period.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> If the officers of arms were not presented with sufficient proof of the right to use a coat of arms, they were also empowered to deface monuments which bore these arms and to force persons bearing such arms to sign a disclaimer that they would cease using them. The visitations were not always popular with members of the landed gentry, who were required to present proof of their gentility.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>Following the accession of <a href="/wiki/William_III_of_England" title="William III of England">William III</a> in 1689, no further commissions to carry out visitations were commanded. The reasons behind this cessation of the programme have been a matter of debate among historians. Philip Styles, for example, related it to a declining willingness of members of the gentry to attend visitations, which he traced to a growing proportion of "<a href="/wiki/Parvenu" title="Parvenu">newly risen</a>" families, who lacked long pedigrees and were therefore apathetic about registering them.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> However, Janet Verasano has challenged this interpretation, finding that (in Staffordshire, at least) gentry enthusiasm for coats of arms as an enhancement to social standing persisted to the end of the 17th century.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The end of the visitations did not have much effect on those counties far removed from London, some of which had only been rarely visited over the entire period of the visitations.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>There was never a systematic visitation of Wales. There were four visitations in the principality, and on 9 June 1551, Fulk ap Hywel, <a href="/wiki/Lancaster_Herald" title="Lancaster Herald">Lancaster Herald of Arms in Ordinary</a> was given a commission to visit all of Wales. This was not carried out, however, as he was degraded and executed for counterfeiting the seal of <a href="/wiki/Clarenceux_King_of_Arms" title="Clarenceux King of Arms">Clarenceux King of Arms</a>. This is regrettable, since no visitation of all Wales was ever made by the officers of arms.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Records">Records</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heraldic_visitation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Records">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Thomas_Hawley_Clarenceux_King_of_Arms.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Thomas_Hawley_Clarenceux_King_of_Arms.jpg/220px-Thomas_Hawley_Clarenceux_King_of_Arms.jpg" width="220" height="254" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Thomas_Hawley_Clarenceux_King_of_Arms.jpg/330px-Thomas_Hawley_Clarenceux_King_of_Arms.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Thomas_Hawley_Clarenceux_King_of_Arms.jpg/440px-Thomas_Hawley_Clarenceux_King_of_Arms.jpg 2x" data-file-width="878" data-file-height="1012" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Thomas_Hawley_Clarenceux_King_of_Arms.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Thomas_Hawley" title="Thomas Hawley">Thomas Hawley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Clarenceux_King_of_Arms" title="Clarenceux King of Arms">Clarenceux King of Arms</a>, wearing a <a href="/wiki/Tabard" title="Tabard">tabard</a> displaying the <a href="/wiki/Royal_arms_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal arms of England">Royal arms of England</a>; the manuscripts from his first tour of London are the earliest existing records of an English visitation.</div></div></div>\n<p>The principal records to emerge from the visitations were pedigrees, initially recorded on loose sheets of paper, and afterwards bound together as notebooks. In some cases, the sheets would include blank shields which had been drawn in advance (or at a later date printed), to simplify the process of recording coats of arms.<sup id="cite_ref-Wag_p167_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wag_p167-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The persons whose pedigrees were recorded were required (from about 1570 onwards) to certify them by signature, and where these original draft pedigrees have survived they are known as "originals with signatures".<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The signed copies were taken back to the College of Arms, where fair copies were made to a higher standard and preserved as the "office copies".<sup id="cite_ref-Wag_p167_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wag_p167-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Sometimes the signed copies were also retained at the College, but in other cases, no longer considered of official interest, they might pass into private hands: once in general circulation, further copies were often made, which might in turn be revised or augmented. As a result, a number of variant manuscript copies of any one visitation record may now survive, possessing varying degrees of accuracy and authority.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Harleian_Collection" class="mw-redirect" title="Harleian Collection">Harleian Collection</a> of the <a href="/wiki/British_Library" title="British Library">British Library</a> is particularly rich in such records. Many visitation records have been published over the years, by the <a href="/wiki/Harleian_Society" title="Harleian Society">Harleian Society</a>, by county <a href="/wiki/Text_publication_society" title="Text publication society">record societies</a>, and a few privately (see <a href="/wiki/Heraldic_visitation#Published_editions" title="Heraldic visitation">listing below</a>). However, because until relatively recently the College of Arms restricted access to its records, many of the older published editions were necessarily based on the unofficial second- or third-generation copies in other collections, and may therefore not always be reliable.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>From as early as the 1530s, officers of arms on visitation frequently also compiled what were known as "church notes". These were <a href="/wiki/Fieldnotes" title="Fieldnotes">fieldnotes</a> (usually in the form of sketches) of coats of arms observed on <a href="/wiki/English_church_monuments" title="English church monuments">church monuments</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Stained_glass" title="Stained glass">stained glass</a> windows, or on display in private houses.<sup id="cite_ref-Wag52_pp61-2_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wag52_pp61-2-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Sometimes, drawings were also made of non-heraldic antiquities, such as medieval architectural features, views of towns, Roman inscriptions and even <a href="/wiki/Stonehenge" title="Stonehenge">Stonehenge</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Wag52_pp61-2_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wag52_pp61-2-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The 17th-century visitations generated a growing number of supplementary papers, including warrants, lists of persons who disclaimed any pretence to arms, lists of persons summoned to appear before the heralds (including those who had not appeared), records of fees paid, and miscellaneous correspondence.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lists_of_visitations">Lists of visitations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heraldic_visitation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Lists of visitations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Visitations were conducted by or in the name of the two provincial <a href="/wiki/King_of_Arms" title="King of Arms">Kings of Arms</a>, <a href="/wiki/Clarenceux_King_of_Arms" title="Clarenceux King of Unsourced material may be challenged and removed.</span>  <small><i>(May 2016)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>Since the practices of <a href="/wiki/Ulster_King_of_Arms" class="mw-redirect" title="Ulster King of Arms">Ulster King of Arms</a> so closely followed those of the English College of Arms, it is hardly surprising that the Irish officers of arms undertook heraldic visitations in their province. The purpose behind these visitations was twofold: to prevent the assumption of arms by unqualified people, and to record the arms of the gentry that were unknown to Ulster office. The first visitation was held by <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nicholas_Narbon&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Nicholas Narbon (page does not exist)">Nicholas Narbon</a>, the second <a href="/wiki/Ulster_King_of_Arms" class="mw-redirect" title="Ulster King of Arms">Ulster King of Arms</a>, in 1569. He was authorized to reform practices which were contrary to good armorial practice. He conducted six visitations (<a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Dublin</a> in 1568&#8211;1573, <a href="/wiki/Drogheda" title="Drogheda">Drogheda</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ardee" title="Ardee">Ardee</a> in 1570, Dublin in 1572, <a href="/wiki/Swords,_Dublin" title="Swords, Dublin">Swords</a> in 1572, <a href="/wiki/Cork_(city)" title="Cork (city)">Cork</a> in 1574, and <a href="/wiki/Limerick" title="Limerick">Limerick</a> in 1574). One of his successors, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Daniel_Molyneux&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Daniel Molyneux (page does not exist)">Daniel Molyneux</a> had the commission renewed, and mounted several visitations. Although Molyneux\'s last visitation \xe2\x80\x93 of <a href="/wiki/Wexford" title="Wexford">Wexford</a> \xe2\x80\x93 was the last proper visitation, two other expeditions occurred after 1618 by subsequent Ulster Kings of Arms. The visitations were not very extensive. The officers would not often be found in the disturbed countryside. Thus the visitations are confined to areas under firm control of the <a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Dublin</a> administration.\n</p><p>Today, the original visitation and related manuscripts are in the custody of the <a href="/wiki/Chief_Herald_of_Ireland" class="mw-redirect" title="Chief Herald of Ireland">Chief Herald of Ireland</a>.
