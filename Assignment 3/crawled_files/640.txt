Broadly defined, it was a way of structuring society around relationships derived from the holding of land in exchange for service or labour.\nAlthough  derived from the Latin word <i>feodum</i> or <i>feudum</i> (fief),<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> then in use, the term <i>feudalism</i> and the system it describes were not conceived of as a formal <a href="/wiki/Political_system" title="Political system">political system</a> by the people living in the Middle Ages.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> In its classic definition, by <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois-Louis_Ganshof" title="Fran\xc3\xa7ois-Louis Ganshof">Fran\xc3\xa7ois-Louis Ganshof</a> (1944),<sup id="cite_ref-ganshof_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ganshof-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> <i>feudalism</i> describes a set of reciprocal legal and military obligations among the warrior nobility revolving around the three key concepts of <a href="/wiki/Lord" title="Lord">lords</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vassal" title="Vassal">vassals</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fief" title="Fief">fiefs</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ganshof_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ganshof-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>A broader definition of feudalism, as described by <a href="/wiki/Marc_Bloch" title="Marc Bloch">Marc Bloch</a> (1939), includes not only the obligations of the warrior nobility but also those of all three <a href="/wiki/Estates_of_the_realm" title="Estates of the realm">estates of the realm</a>: the nobility, the <a href="/wiki/Clergy" title="Clergy">clergy</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Peasant" title="Peasant">peasantry</a> bound by <a href="/wiki/Manorialism" title="Manorialism">manorialism</a>; this is sometimes referred to as a "feudal society". Since the publication of <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_A._R._Brown" title="Elizabeth A. R. Brown">Elizabeth A. R. Brown</a>\'s "The Tyranny of a Construct" (1974) and <a href="/wiki/Susan_Reynolds" title="Susan Reynolds">Susan Reynolds</a>\'s <i>Fiefs and Vassals</i> (1994), there has been ongoing inconclusive discussion among medieval historians as to whether feudalism is a useful construct for understanding medieval society.<sup id="cite_ref-ebo_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ebo-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ebrown_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ebrown-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-reynolds_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-reynolds-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-halsall_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-halsall-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-auto_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">Definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Feudalism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>There is no commonly accepted modern definition of feudalism, at least among scholars.<sup id="cite_ref-ebo_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ebo-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-halsall_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-halsall-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>  The adjective <i>feudal</i> was coined in the 17th century, and the noun <i>feudalism</i>, often used in a political and propaganda context, was not coined until the 19th century,<sup id="cite_ref-ebo_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ebo-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> from the French <i>f\xc3\xa9odalit\xc3\xa9</i> (<i>feudality</i>), itself an 18th-century creation.\n</p><p>In a classic definition by <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois-Louis_Ganshof" title="Fran\xc3\xa7ois-Louis Ganshof">Fran\xc3\xa7ois-Louis Ganshof</a> (1944),<sup id="cite_ref-ganshof_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ganshof-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> <i>feudalism</i> describes a set of reciprocal legal and military obligations among the warrior nobility, revolving around the three key concepts of <a href="/wiki/Lord" title="Lord">lords</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vassal" title="Vassal">vassals</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fief" title="Fief">fiefs</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-ganshof_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ganshof-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> though Ganshof himself noted that his treatment related only to the "narrow, technical, legal sense of the word".\n</p><p>A broader definition, as described in <a href="/wiki/Marc_Bloch" title="Marc Bloch">Marc Bloch</a>\'s <i>Feudal Society</i> (1939),<sup id="cite_ref-bloch_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bloch-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> includes not only the obligations of the warrior nobility but those of all three <a href="/wiki/Estates_of_the_realm" title="Estates of the realm">estates of the realm</a>: the nobility, the <a href="/wiki/Clergy" title="Clergy">clergy</a>, and those living by their labour, most directly the <a href="/wiki/Peasant" title="Peasant">peasantry</a> bound by <a href="/wiki/Manorialism" title="Manorialism">manorialism</a>; this order is often referred to as "feudal society", echoing Bloch\'s usage.\n</p><p>Since the publication of <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_A._R._Brown" title="Elizabeth A. R. Brown">Elizabeth A. R. Brown</a>\'s "The Tyranny of a Construct" (1974)<sup id="cite_ref-ebrown_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ebrown-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Susan_Reynolds" title="Susan Reynolds">Susan Reynolds</a>\'s <i>Fiefs and Vassals</i> (1994),<sup id="cite_ref-reynolds_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-reynolds-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> there has been ongoing inconclusive discussion among medieval historians as to whether feudalism is a useful construct for understanding medieval society.<sup id="cite_ref-ebo_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ebo-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-halsall_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-halsall-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-auto_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Outside a European context, the concept of feudalism is often used only by <a href="/wiki/Analogy" title="Analogy">analogy</a> (called <i>semi-feudal</i>), most often in discussions of <a href="/wiki/Feudal_Japan" class="mw-redirect" title="Feudal Japan">feudal Japan</a> under the <i><a href="/wiki/Sh%C5%8Dgun" title="Sh\xc5\x8dgun">sh\xc5\x8dguns</a></i>, and sometimes medieval and <a href="/wiki/Gondar" title="Gondar">Gondarine</a> <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-hmco_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hmco-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>  However, some have taken the feudalism analogy further, seeing feudalism (or traces of it) in places as diverse as <a href="/wiki/Spring_and_Autumn_period" title="Spring and Autumn period">Spring and Autumn period</a> in <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">ancient Egypt</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Parthia" title="Parthia">Parthian empire</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Indian_feudalism" title="Indian feudalism">Indian subcontinent</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Antebellum_era" class="mw-redirect" title="Antebellum era">Antebellum</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jim_Crow" class="mw-redirect" title="Jim Crow">Jim Crow American South</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-hmco_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hmco-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The term <i>feudalism</i> has also been applied\xe2\x80\x94often inappropriately or pejoratively\xe2\x80\x94to non-Western societies where institutions and attitudes similar to those of <a href="/wiki/Medieval" class="mw-redirect" title="Medieval">medieval</a> Europe are perceived to prevail.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Some historians and political theorists believe that the term <i>feudalism</i> has been deprived of specific meaning by the many ways it has been used, leading them to reject it as a useful concept for understanding society.<sup id="cite_ref-ebo_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ebo-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ebrown_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ebrown-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Feudalism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Codex_Manesse_Reinmar_von_Zweter.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Codex_Manesse_Reinmar_von_Zweter.jpg/220px-Codex_Manesse_Reinmar_von_Zweter.jpg" width="220" height="330" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Codex_Manesse_Reinmar_von_Zweter.jpg/330px-Codex_Manesse_Reinmar_von_Zweter.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Codex_Manesse_Reinmar_von_Zweter.jpg/440px-Codex_Manesse_Reinmar_von_Zweter.jpg 2x" data-file-width="984" data-file-height="1474" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Codex_Manesse_Reinmar_von_Zweter.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Herr <a href="/wiki/Reinmar_von_Zweter" title="Reinmar von Zweter">Reinmar von Zweter</a>, a 13th-century <a href="/wiki/Minnesang" title="Minnesang">Minnesinger</a>, was depicted with his noble arms in <a href="/wiki/Codex_Manesse" title="Codex Manesse">Codex Manesse</a>.</div></div></div>\n<p>The term "f\xc3\xa9odal" was used in 17th-century French legal treatises (1614)<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> and translated into English legal treatises as an adjective, such as "feodal government".\n</p><p>In the 18th century, <a href="/wiki/Adam_Smith" title="Adam Smith">Adam Smith</a>, seeking to describe economic systems, effectively coined the forms "feudal government" and "feudal system" in his book <i><a href="/wiki/Wealth_of_Nations" class="mw-redirect" title="Wealth of Nations">Wealth of Nations</a></i> (1776).<sup id="cite_ref-cheyette_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cheyette-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> In the 19th century the adjective "feudal" evolved into a noun: "feudalism".<sup id="cite_ref-cheyette_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cheyette-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The term <i>feudalism</i> is recent, first appearing in French in 1823, Italian in 1827, English in 1839, and in German in the second half of the 19th century.<sup id="cite_ref-cheyette_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cheyette-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The term "feudal" or "feodal" is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Medieval_Latin" title="Medieval Latin">medieval Latin</a> word <i>feodum</i>. The etymology of <i>feodum</i> is complex with multiple theories, some suggesting a Germanic origin (the most widely held view) and others suggesting an <a href="/wiki/Arabic" title="Arabic">Arabic</a> origin. Initially in medieval Latin European documents, a land grant in exchange for service was called a <i>beneficium</i> (Latin).<sup id="cite_ref-lubetski_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lubetski-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Later, the term <i>feudum</i>, or <i>feodum</i>, began to replace <i>beneficium</i> in the documents.<sup id="cite_ref-lubetski_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lubetski-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> The first attested instance of this is from 984, although more primitive forms were seen up to one-hundred years earlier.<sup id="cite_ref-lubetski_16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lubetski-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> The origin of the <i>feudum</i> and why it replaced <i>beneficium</i> has not been well established, but there are multiple theories, described below.<sup id="cite_ref-lubetski_16-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lubetski-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The most widely held theory was proposed by <a href="/wiki/Johan_Hendrik_Caspar_Kern" title="Johan Hendrik Caspar Kern">Johan Hendrik Caspar Kern</a> in 1870,<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> being supported by, amongst others, <a href="/wiki/William_Stubbs" title="William Stubbs">William Stubbs</a><sup id="cite_ref-lubetski_16-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lubetski-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Marc_Bloch" title="Marc Bloch">Marc Bloch</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-lubetski_16-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lubetski-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-bloch-ety1_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bloch-ety1-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-bloch-ety2_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bloch-ety2-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Kern derived the word from a putative Frankish term <i>*fehu-\xc3\xb4d</i>, in which <i>*fehu</i> means "cattle" and <i>-\xc3\xb4d</i> means "goods", implying "a moveable object of value."<sup id="cite_ref-bloch-ety1_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bloch-ety1-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-bloch-ety2_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bloch-ety2-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Bloch explains that by the beginning of the 10th century it was common to value land in monetary terms but to pay for it with moveable objects of equivalent value, such as arms, clothing, horses or food. This was known as <i>feos</i>, a term that took on the general meaning of paying for something in lieu of money. This meaning was then applied to land itself, in which land was used to pay for fealty, such as to a vassal. Thus the old word <i>feos</i> meaning movable property changed little by little to <i>feus</i> meaning the exact opposite: landed property.<sup id="cite_ref-bloch-ety1_20-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bloch-ety1-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-bloch-ety2_21-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bloch-ety2-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Another theory was put forward by <a href="/wiki/Archibald_R._Lewis" class="mw-redirect" title="Archibald R. Lewis">Archibald R. Lewis</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-lubetski_16-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lubetski-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Lewis said the origin of \'fief\' is not <i>feudum</i> (or <i>feodum</i>), but rather <i>foderum</i>, the earliest attested use being in <a href="/wiki/Astronomus" class="mw-redirect" title="Astronomus">Astronomus</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Vita_Hludovici" title="Vita Hludovici">Vita Hludovici</a></i> (840).<sup id="cite_ref-arlewis_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-arlewis-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> In that text is a passage about <a href="/wiki/Louis_the_Pious" title="Louis the Pious">Louis the Pious</a> that says <i>annona militaris quas vulgo foderum vocant</i>, which can be translated as "Louis forbade that military <a href="/wiki/Provender" title="Provender">provender</a> (which they popularly call "fodder") be furnished.."<sup id="cite_ref-lubetski_16-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lubetski-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Another theory by <a href="/w/index.php?title=Alauddin_Samarrai&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Alauddin Samarrai (page does not exist)">Alauddin Samarrai</a> suggests an Arabic origin, from <i>fuy\xc5\xab</i> (the plural of <i>fay</i>, which literally means "the returned", and was used especially for \'land that has been conquered from enemies that did not fight\').<sup id="cite_ref-lubetski_16-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lubetski-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-samarrai_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-samarrai-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Samarrai\'s theory is that early forms of \'fief\' include <i>feo</i>, <i>feu</i>, <i>feuz</i>, <i>feuum</i> and others, the plurality of forms strongly suggesting origins from a <a href="/wiki/Loanword" title="Loanword">loanword</a>. The first use of these terms is in <a href="/wiki/Languedoc" title="Languedoc">Languedoc</a>, one of the least Germanic areas of Europe and bordering Muslim Spain. Further, the earliest use of <i>feuum</i> (as a replacement for <i>beneficium</i>) can be dated to 899, the same year a Muslim base at <a href="/wiki/Fraxinetum" class="mw-redirect" title="Fraxinetum">Fraxinetum</a> (<a href="/wiki/La_Garde-Freinet" title="La Garde-Freinet">La Garde-Freinet</a>) in <a href="/wiki/Provence" title="Provence">Provence</a> was established. It is possible, Samarrai says, that French scribes, writing in Latin, attempted to <a href="/wiki/Transliteration" title="Transliteration">transliterate</a> the Arabic word <i>fuy\xc5\xab</i> (the plural of <i>fay</i>), which was being used by the Muslim invaders and occupiers at the time, resulting in a plurality of forms \xe2\x80\x93 <i>feo, feu, feuz, feuum</i> and others \xe2\x80\x93 from which eventually <i>feudum</i> derived. Samarrai, however, also advises to handle this theory with care, as Medieval and Early Modern Muslim scribes often used etymologically "fanciful roots" in order to claim the most outlandish things to be of Arabian or Muslim origin.<sup id="cite_ref-samarrai_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-samarrai-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Feudalism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<table class="vertical-navbox nowraplinks" style="float:right;clear:right;width:22.0em;margin:0 0 1.0em #aaa;padding-top:0.15em;border-bottom:1px solid #aaa;">\n<a class="mw-selflink selflink">Feudalism</a></td></tr><tr><td style="text-align:right;font-size:115%"><div class="plainlinks hlist navbar various forms, usually emerged as a result of the <a href="/wiki/Decentralization" title="Decentralization">decentralization</a> of an empire: especially in the <a href="/wiki/Carolingian" class="mw-redirect" title="Carolingian">Carolingian</a> empires, which lacked the bureaucratic infrastructure<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (September 2012)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> necessary to support cavalry without the ability to allocate land to these mounted troops. Mounted soldiers began to secure a system of hereditary rule over their allocated land and their power over the territory came to encompass the social, political, judicial, and economic spheres.<sup id="cite_ref-Gat,_Azar_2006._pp._332_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gat,_Azar_2006._pp._332-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>These acquired powers significantly <a href="/wiki/Federation" title="Federation">diminished unitary power</a> in these empires. Only when the infrastructure existed to maintain unitary power\xe2\x80\x94as with the European monarchies\xe2\x80\x94did feudalism begin to yield to this new power structure and eventually disappear.<sup id="cite_ref-Gat,_Azar_2006._pp._332_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gat,_Azar_2006._pp._332-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Classic_feudalism">Classic feudalism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Feudalism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Classic feudalism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<dl><dd><i>See also <a href="/wiki/Feudalism_in_England" title="Feudalism in England">Feudalism in England</a>, <a href="/wiki/Feudalism_in_the_Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Feudalism in the Holy Roman Empire">Feudalism in the Holy Roman Empire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Examples_of_feudalism" title="Examples of feudalism">Examples of feudalism</a></i></dd></dl>\n<p>The classic <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois-Louis_Ganshof" title="Fran\xc3\xa7ois-Louis Ganshof">Fran\xc3\xa7ois-Louis Ganshof</a> version of feudalism<sup id="cite_ref-ebo_4-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ebo-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ganshof_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ganshof-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> describes a set of reciprocal legal and military obligations among the warrior nobility, revolving around the three key concepts of <a href="/wiki/Lord" title="Lord">lords</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vassal" title="Vassal">vassals</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fief" title="Fief">fiefs</a>. A lord was in broad terms a noble who held land, a vassal was a person who was granted possession of the land by the lord, and the land was known as a fief. In exchange for the use of the fief and the protection of the lord, the vassal would provide some sort of  service to the lord. There were many varieties of <a href="/wiki/Feudal_land_tenure" class="mw-redirect" title="Feudal land tenure">feudal land tenure</a>, consisting of  military and non-military service. The obligations and corresponding rights between lord and vassal concerning the fief form the basis of the feudal relationship.<sup id="cite_ref-ganshof_3-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ganshof-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vassalage">Vassalage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Feudalism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Vassalage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Hommage_du_comt%C3%A9_de_Clermont-en-Beauvaisis.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Hommage_du_comt%C3%A9_de_Clermont-en-Beauvaisis.png/220px-Hommage_du_comt%C3%A9_de_Clermont-en-Beauvaisis.png" width="220" height="256" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Hommage_du_comt%C3%A9_de_Clermont-en-Beauvaisis.png/330px-Hommage_du_comt%C3%A9_de_Clermont-en-Beauvaisis.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Hommage_du_comt%C3%A9_de_Clermont-en-Beauvaisis.png/440px-Hommage_du_comt%C3%A9_de_Clermont-en-Beauvaisis.png 2x" data-file-width="514" data-file-height="598" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Hommage_du_comt%C3%A9_de_Clermont-en-Beauvaisis.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Homage_(feudal)" title="Homage (feudal)">Homage</a> of <a href="/wiki/Clermont-en-Beauvaisis" class="mw-redirect" title="Clermont-en-Beauvaisis">Clermont-en-Beauvaisis</a></div></div></div>\n<p>Before a lord could grant land (a fief) to someone, he had to make that person a vassal. This was done at a formal and symbolic ceremony called a <a href="/wiki/Commendation_ceremony" title="Commendation ceremony">commendation ceremony</a>,  which was composed of the two-part act of <a href="/wiki/Homage_(medieval)" class="mw-redirect" title="Homage (medieval)">homage</a> and oath of <a href="/wiki/Fealty" title="Fealty">fealty</a>. During homage, the lord and vassal entered into a contract in which the vassal promised to fight for the lord at his command, whilst the lord agreed to protect the vassal from external forces. <i>Fealty</i> comes from the Latin <i>fidelitas</i> and denotes the <a href="/wiki/Fidelity" title="Fidelity">fidelity</a> owed by a vassal to his feudal lord. "Fealty" also refers to an oath that more explicitly reinforces the commitments of the vassal made during homage. Such an oath follows homage.<sup id="cite_ref-stephenson_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stephenson-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Once the commendation ceremony was complete, the lord and vassal were in a feudal relationship with agreed obligations to one another. The vassal\'s principal obligation to the lord was to "aid", or military service. Using whatever equipment the vassal could obtain by virtue of the revenues from the fief, the vassal was responsible to answer calls to military service on behalf of the lord. This security of military help was the primary reason the lord entered into the feudal relationship. In addition, the vassal could have other obligations to his lord, such as attendance at his court, whether manorial, baronial, both termed <a href="/wiki/Court_baron" class="mw-redirect" title="Court baron">court baron</a>, or at the king\'s court.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Map_France_1477-en.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Map_France_1477-en.svg/220px-Map_France_1477-en.svg.png" width="220" height="237" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Map_France_1477-en.svg/330px-Map_France_1477-en.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Map_France_1477-en.svg/440px-Map_France_1477-en.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="900" data-file-height="970" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Map_France_1477-en.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>France in the late 15th century: a mosaic of feudal territories</div></div></div>\n<p>It could also involve the vassal providing "counsel", so that if the lord faced a major decision he would summon all his vassals and hold a council. At the level of the <a href="/wiki/Manor" title="Manor">manor</a> this might be a fairly mundane matter of agricultural policy, but also included sentencing by the lord for criminal offences, including capital punishment in some cases. Concerning the king\'s feudal court, such deliberation could include the question of declaring war. These are examples; depending on the period of time and location in Europe, feudal customs and practices varied; see <a href="/wiki/Examples_of_feudalism" title="Examples of feudalism">examples of feudalism</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="The_.22Feudal_Revolution.22_in_France"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="The_&quot;Feudal_Revolution&quot;_in_France">The "Feudal Revolution" in France</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Feudalism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: The &quot;Feudal Revolution&quot; in France">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In its origin, the feudal grant of land had been seen in terms of a personal bond between lord and vassal, but with time and the transformation of fiefs into hereditary holdings, the nature of the system came to be seen as a form of "politics of land" (an expression used by the historian <a href="/wiki/Marc_Bloch" title="Marc Bloch">Marc Bloch</a>).  The 11th century in France saw what has been called by historians a "feudal revolution" or "mutation" and a "fragmentation of powers" (Bloch) that was unlike the development of feudalism in England or Italy or Germany in the same period or later:<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> Counties and duchies began to break down into smaller holdings as <a href="/wiki/Castellan" title="Castellan">castellans</a> and lesser seigneurs took control of local lands, and (as comital families had done before them) lesser lords usurped/privatized a wide range of prerogatives and rights of the state, most importantly the highly profitable rights of justice, but also travel dues, market dues, fees for using woodlands, obligations to use the lord\'s mill, etc.<sup id="cite_ref-auto1_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto1-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> (what <a href="/wiki/Georges_Duby" title="Georges Duby">Georges Duby</a> called collectively the "<i>seigneurie banale</i>"<sup id="cite_ref-auto1_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto1-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>).  Power in this period became more personal.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>This "fragmentation of powers" was not, however, systematic throughout France, and in certain counties (such as Flanders, Normandy, Anjou, Toulouse), counts were able to maintain control of their lands into the 12th century or later.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>  Thus, in some regions (like Normandy and Flanders), the vassal/feudal system was an effective tool for ducal and comital control, linking vassals to their lords; but in other regions, the system led to significant confusion, all the more so as vassals could and frequently did pledge themselves to two or more lords.  In response to this, the idea of a "<a href="/wiki/Liege_lord" class="mw-redirect" title="Liege lord">liege lord</a>" was developed (where the obligations to one lord are regarded as superior) in the 12th century.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span id="End_of_European_feudalism_.281500.E2.80.931850s.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="End_of_European_feudalism_(1500\xe2\x80\x931850s)">End of European feudalism (1500\xe2\x80\x931850s)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Feudalism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: End of European feudalism (1500\xe2\x80\x931850s)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Abolition_of_feudalism_in_France" title="Abolition of feudalism in France">Abolition of feudalism in France</a></div>\n<p>Feudalism effectively ended by about 1500.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> This was partly since the military shifted from armies consisting of the nobility to professional fighters thus reducing the nobility\'s claim on power, but also because the <a href="/wiki/Black_Death" title="Black Death">Black Death</a> reduced the nobility\'s hold over the lower classes. Vestiges of the Feudal system hung on in France until the French Revolution, and the system lingered on in parts of Central and Eastern Europe as late as the 1850s. Russia finally abolished <a href="/wiki/Serfdom" title="Serfdom">serfdom</a> in 1861.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Even when the original feudal relationships had disappeared, there were many institutional remnants of feudalism left in place. Historian <a href="/wiki/Georges_Lefebvre" title="Georges Lefebvre">Georges Lefebvre</a> explains how at an early stage of the <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">French Revolution</a>, on just one night of August 4, 1789, France abolished the long-lasting remnants of the feudal order. It announced, "The National Assembly abolishes the feudal system entirely." Lefebvre explains:\n</p>\n<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Without debate the Assembly enthusiastically adopted equality of taxation and redemption of all manorial rights except for those involving personal servitude \xe2\x80\x94 which were to be abolished without indemnification. Other proposals followed with the same success: the equality of legal punishment, admission of all to public office, abolition of venality in office, conversion of the tithe into payments subject to redemption, freedom of worship, prohibition of plural holding of benefices.... Privileges of provinces and towns were offered as a last sacrifice.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>\n</p></blockquote>\n<p>Originally the peasants were supposed to pay for the release of seigneurial dues; these dues affected more than a fourth of the farmland in France and provided most of the income of the large landowners.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> The majority refused to pay and in 1793 the obligation was cancelled. Thus the peasants got their land free, and also no longer paid the tithe to the church.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Feudal_society">Feudal society</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Feudalism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Feudal society">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Manorialism" title="Manorialism">Manorialism</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Reeve_and_Serfs.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Reeve_and_Serfs.jpg/220px-Reeve_and_Serfs.jpg" width="220" height="127" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Reeve_and_Serfs.jpg/330px-Reeve_and_Serfs.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Reeve_and_Serfs.jpg/440px-Reeve_and_Serfs.jpg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="461" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Reeve_and_Serfs.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Depiction of <a href="/wiki/Socage" title="Socage">socage</a> on the royal <a href="/wiki/Demesne" title="Demesne">demesne</a> in  feudal England, c. 1310</div></div></div>\n<p>The phrase "feudal society" as defined by <a href="/wiki/Marc_Bloch" title="Marc Bloch">Marc Bloch</a><sup id="cite_ref-bloch_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bloch-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> offers a wider definition than Ganshof\'s and includes within the feudal structure not only the warrior aristocracy bound by vassalage, but also the <a href="/wiki/Peasant" title="Peasant">peasantry</a> bound by manorialism, and the estates of the Church. Thus the feudal order embraces society from top to bottom, though the "powerful and well-differentiated social group of the urban classes" came to occupy a distinct position to some extent outside the classical feudal hierarchy.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historiography">Historiography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Feudalism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Historiography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The idea of <i>feudalism</i> was unknown and the system it describes was not conceived of as a formal political system by the people living in the Medieval Period. This section describes the history of the idea of feudalism, how the concept originated among scholars and thinkers, how it changed over time, and modern debates about its use.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Evolution_of_the_concept">Evolution of the concept</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Feudalism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Evolution of the concept">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The concept of a feudal state or period, in the sense of either a regime or a period dominated by lords who possess financial or social power and prestige, became widely held in the middle of the 18th century, as a result of works such as <a href="/wiki/Charles_de_Secondat,_Baron_de_Montesquieu" class="mw-redirect" title="Charles de Secondat, Baron de Montesquieu">Montesquieu\'s</a> <i>De L\'Esprit des Lois</i> (1748; published in English as <i><a href="/wiki/The_Spirit_of_the_Laws" title="The Spirit of the Laws">The Spirit of the Laws</a></i>), and <a href="/wiki/Henri_de_Boulainvilliers" title="Henri de Boulainvilliers">Henri de Boulainvilliers</a>\xe2\x80\x99s <i>Histoire des anciens Parlements de France</i> (1737; published in English as <i>An Historical Account of the Ancient Parliaments of France or States-General of the Kingdom</i>, 1739).<sup id="cite_ref-cheyette_15-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cheyette-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> In the 18th century, writers of the Enlightenment wrote about feudalism to denigrate the antiquated system of the <i><a href="/wiki/Ancien_R%C3%A9gime" title="Ancien R\xc3\xa9gime">Ancien R\xc3\xa9gime</a></i>, or French monarchy. This was <a href="/wiki/The_Age_of_Enlightenment" class="mw-redirect" title="The Age of Enlightenment">the Age of Enlightenment</a> when writers valued reason and the Middle Ages were viewed as the "<a href="/wiki/Dark_Ages_(historiography)" title="Dark Ages (historiography)">Dark Ages</a>". Enlightenment authors generally mocked and ridiculed anything from the "Dark Ages" including feudalism, projecting its negative characteristics on the current French monarchy as a means of political gain.<sup id="cite_ref-bartlett_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bartlett-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> For them "feudalism" meant <a href="/wiki/Fiefdom" class="mw-redirect" title="Fiefdom">seigneurial</a> privileges and prerogatives. When the <a href="/wiki/National_Constituent_Assembly_(France)" title="National Constituent Assembly (France)">French Constituent Assembly</a> abolished the "feudal regime" in August 1789 this is what was meant.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Adam_Smith" title="Adam Smith">Adam Smith</a> used the term "feudal system" to describe a social and economic system defined by inherited social ranks, each of which possessed inherent social and economic privileges and obligations. In such a system wealth derived from agriculture, which was arranged not according to market forces but on the basis of customary labour services owed by <a href="/wiki/Serf" class="mw-redirect" title="Serf">serfs</a> to landowning nobles.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Karl_Marx">Karl Marx</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Feudalism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Karl Marx">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Karl_Marx" title="Karl Marx">Karl Marx</a> also used the term in the 19th century in his analysis of society\'s economic and political development, describing feudalism (or more usually feudal society or the feudal <a href="/wiki/Mode_of_production" title="Mode of production">mode of production</a>) as the order coming before <a href="/wiki/Capitalism" title="Capitalism">capitalism</a>. For Marx, what defined feudalism was the power of the ruling class (the <a href="/wiki/Aristocracy" title="Aristocracy">aristocracy</a>) in their control of arable land, leading to a <a href="/wiki/Class_society" class="mw-redirect" title="Class society">class society</a> based upon the exploitation of the peasants who farm these lands, typically under <a href="/wiki/Serfdom" title="Serfdom">serfdom</a> and principally by means of labour, produce and money rents.<sup id="cite_ref-daileader_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-daileader-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> Marx thus defined feudalism primarily by its economic characteristics.\n</p><p>He also took it as a paradigm for understanding the power-relationships between capitalists and wage-labourers in his own time: \xe2\x80\x98in pre-capitalist systems it was obvious that most people did not control their own destiny \xe2\x80\x94 under feudalism, for instance, serfs had to work for their lords. Capitalism seems different because people are in theory free to work for themselves or for others as they choose. Yet most workers have as little control over their lives as feudal serfs\xe2\x80\x99.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> Some later Marxist theorists (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Eric_Wolf" title="Eric Wolf">Eric Wolf</a>) have applied this label to include non-European societies, grouping feudalism together with Imperial Chinese and pre-Columbian Incan societies as \'tributary\'.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_studies">Later studies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Feudalism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Later studies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In the late 19th and early 20th centuries, <a href="/wiki/John_Horace_Round" class="mw-redirect" title="John Horace Round">John Horace Round</a> and <a href="/wiki/Frederic_William_Maitland" title="Frederic William Maitland">Frederic William Maitland</a>, both historians of medieval Britain, arrived at different conclusions as to the character of English society before the <a href="/wiki/Norman_Conquest" class="mw-redirect" title="Norman Conquest">Norman Conquest</a> in 1066. Round argued that the Normans had brought feudalism with them to England, while Maitland contended that its fundamentals were already in place in Britain before 1066. The debate continues today, but a consensus viewpoint is that England before the Conquest had commendation (which embodied some of the personal elements in feudalism) while <a href="/wiki/William_I_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="William I of England">William the Conqueror</a> introduced a modified and stricter northern French feudalism to England incorporating (1086) oaths of loyalty to the king by all who held by feudal tenure, even the vassals of his principal vassals (Holding by feudal tenure meant that vassals must provide the quota of knights required by the king or a money payment in substitution).\n</p><p>In the 20th century, two outstanding historians offered still more widely differing perspectives. The French historian <a href="/wiki/Marc_Bloch" title="Marc Bloch">Marc Bloch</a>, arguably the most influential 20th-century medieval historian.,<sup id="cite_ref-daileader_40-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-daileader-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> approached feudalism not so much from a legal and military point of view but from a sociological one, presenting in <i>Feudal Society</i> (1939; English 1961) a feudal order not limited solely to the nobility. It is his radical notion that peasants were part of the feudal relationship that sets Bloch apart from his peers: while the vassal performed military service in exchange for the fief, the peasant performed physical labour in return for protection \xe2\x80\x93 both are a form of feudal relationship. According to Bloch, other elements of society can be seen in feudal terms; all the aspects of life were centered on "lordship", and so we can speak usefully of a feudal church structure, a feudal courtly (and anti-courtly) literature, and a feudal economy.<sup id="cite_ref-daileader_40-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-daileader-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In contradistinction to Bloch, the Belgian historian <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois-Louis_Ganshof" title="Fran\xc3\xa7ois-Louis Ganshof">Fran\xc3\xa7ois-Louis Ganshof</a> defined feudalism from a narrow legal and military perspective, arguing that feudal relationships existed only within the medieval nobility itself. Ganshof articulated this concept in <i>Qu\'est-ce que la f\xc3\xa9odalit\xc3\xa9?</i> ("What is feudalism?", 1944; translated in English as <i>Feudalism</i>). His classic definition of feudalism is widely accepted today among medieval scholars,<sup id="cite_ref-daileader_40-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-daileader-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> though questioned both by those who view the concept in wider terms and by those who find insufficient uniformity in noble exchanges to support such a model.\n</p><p>Although he was never formally a student in the circle of scholars around Marc Bloch and <a href="/wiki/Lucien_Febvre" title="Lucien Febvre">Lucien Febvre</a> that came to be known as the <a href="/wiki/Annales_School" class="mw-redirect" title="Annales School">Annales School</a>, <a href="/wiki/Georges_Duby" title="Georges Duby">Georges Duby</a> was an exponent of the <i>Annaliste</i> tradition. In  a published version of his 1952 doctoral thesis entitled <i>La soci\xc3\xa9t\xc3\xa9 aux XIe et XIIe si\xc3\xa8cles dans la r\xc3\xa9gion m\xc3\xa2connaise</i> (<i>Society in the 11th and 12th centuries in the <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A2connais" title="M\xc3\xa2connais">M\xc3\xa2connais</a> region</i>), and working from the extensive documentary sources surviving from the Burgundian <a href="/wiki/Cluny_Abbey" title="Cluny Abbey">monastery of Cluny</a>, as well as the dioceses of <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Diocese_of_M%C3%A2con" title="Ancient Diocese of M\xc3\xa2con">M\xc3\xa2con</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dijon" title="Dijon">Dijon</a>, Duby excavated the complex social and economic relationships among the individuals and institutions of the <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A2connais" title="M\xc3\xa2connais">M\xc3\xa2connais</a> region and charted a profound shift in the social structures of medieval society around the year 1000. He argued that in early 11th century, governing institutions\xe2\x80\x94particularly comital courts established under the <a href="/wiki/Carolingian" class="mw-redirect" title="Carolingian">Carolingian</a> monarchy\xe2\x80\x94that had represented public justice and order in Burgundy during the 9th and 10th centuries receded and gave way to a new feudal order wherein independent aristocratic knights wielded power over peasant communities through strong-arm tactics and threats of violence.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Challenges_to_the_feudal_model">Challenges to the feudal model</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Feudalism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Challenges to the feudal model">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In 1974, U.S. historian <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_A._R._Brown" title="Elizabeth A. R. Brown">Elizabeth A. R. Brown</a><sup id="cite_ref-ebrown_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ebrown-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> rejected the label <i>feudalism</i> as an anachronism that imparts a false sense of uniformity to the concept. Having noted the current use of many, often contradictory, definitions of <i>feudalism</i>, she argued that the word is only a construct with no basis in medieval reality, an invention of modern historians read back "tyrannically" into the historical record. Supporters of Brown have suggested that the term should be expunged from history textbooks and lectures on medieval history entirely.<sup id="cite_ref-daileader_40-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-daileader-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> In <i>Fiefs and Vassals: The Medieval Evidence Reinterpreted</i> (1994),<sup id="cite_ref-reynolds_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-reynolds-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Susan_Reynolds" title="Susan Reynolds">Susan Reynolds</a> expanded upon Brown\'s original thesis. Although some contemporaries questioned Reynolds\'s methodology, other historians have supported it and her argument.<sup id="cite_ref-daileader_40-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-daileader-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> Reynolds argues:\n</p>\n<dl><dd>Too many models of feudalism used for comparisons, even by Marxists, are still either constructed on the 16th-century basis or incorporate what, in a Marxist view, must surely be superficial or irrelevant features from it.  Even when one restricts oneself to Europe and to feudalism in its narrow sense it is extremely doubtful whether feudo-vassalic institutions formed a coherent bundle of institutions or concepts that were structurally separate from other  institutions  and concepts of the time.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup></dd></dl>\n<p>The term <i>feudal</i> has also been applied to non-Western societies in which institutions and attitudes similar to those of medieval Europe are perceived to have prevailed (See <a href="/wiki/Examples_of_feudalism" title="Examples of feudalism">Examples of feudalism</a>). Japan has been extensively studied in this regard.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>  Friday notes that in the 21st century historians of Japan rarely invoke feudalism; instead of looking at similarities, specialists attempting comparative analysis concentrate on fundamental differences.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> Ultimately, critics say, the many ways the term <i>feudalism</i> has been used have deprived it of specific meaning, leading some historians and <a href="/wiki/Political_theorist" class="mw-redirect" title="Political theorist">political theorists</a> to reject it as a useful concept for understanding society.<sup id="cite_ref-daileader_40-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-daileader-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Richard Abels notes that "Western Civilization and World Civilization textbooks now shy away from the term \'feudalism\'."<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Feudalism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: See also">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="div-col columns column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 22em;
