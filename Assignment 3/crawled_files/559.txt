Ives</a></li></ul>\n</div></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><b>Tate Britain</b> (known from 1897 to 1932 as the <b>National Gallery of British Art</b> and from 1932 to 2000 as the <b>Tate Gallery</b>) is an art museum on <a href="/wiki/Millbank" title="Millbank">Millbank</a> in the <a href="/wiki/City_of_Westminster" title="City of Westminster">City of Westminster</a> in London. It is part of the <a href="/wiki/Tate_Gallery" class="mw-redirect" title="Tate Gallery">Tate</a> network of galleries in England, with <a href="/wiki/Tate_Modern" title="Tate Modern">Tate Modern</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tate_Liverpool" title="Tate Liverpool">Tate Liverpool</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tate_St_Ives" title="Tate St Ives">Tate St Ives</a>. It is the oldest gallery in the network, having opened in 1897. It houses a substantial collection of the <a href="/wiki/Art_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Art of the United Kingdom">art of the United Kingdom</a> since Tudor times, and in particular has large holdings of the works of <a href="/wiki/J._M._W._Turner" title="J. M. W. Turner">J. M. W. Turner</a>, who bequeathed all his own collection to the nation. It is one of the <a href="/wiki/List_of_largest_art_museums_in_the_world" class="mw-redirect" title="List of largest art museums in the world">largest</a> museums in the country.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tate_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The gallery is situated on <a href="/wiki/Millbank" title="Millbank">Millbank</a>, on the site of the former <a href="/wiki/Millbank_Prison" title="Millbank Prison">Millbank Prison</a>. Construction, undertaken by <a href="/wiki/Higgs_and_Hill" title="Higgs and Hill">Higgs and Hill</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> commenced in 1893, and the gallery opened on 21 July 1897 as the National Gallery of British Art. However, from the start it was commonly known as the Tate Gallery, after its founder <a href="/wiki/Sir_Henry_Tate" class="mw-redirect" title="Sir Henry Tate">Sir Henry Tate</a>, and in 1932 it officially adopted that name.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Before 2000, the gallery housed and displayed both British and modern collections, but the launch of <a href="/wiki/Tate_Modern" title="Tate Modern">Tate Modern</a> saw Tate\'s modern collections move there, while the old Millbank gallery became dedicated to the display of historical and contemporary <a href="/wiki/British_art" class="mw-redirect" title="British art">British art</a>. As a consequence, it was renamed Tate Britain in March 2000.\n</p><p>The front part of the building was designed by <a href="/wiki/Sidney_R._J._Smith" title="Sidney R. J. Smith">Sidney R. J. Smith</a> with a classical <a href="/wiki/Portico" title="Portico">portico</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dome" title="Dome">dome</a> behind, and the central sculpture gallery was designed by <a href="/wiki/John_Russell_Pope" title="John Russell Pope">John Russell Pope</a>. Tate Britain includes the Clore Gallery of 1987, designed by <a href="/wiki/James_Stirling_(architect)" title="James Stirling (architect)">James Stirling</a>, which houses work by <a href="/wiki/J._M._W._Turner" title="J. M. W. Turner">J. M. W. Turner</a>. The Clore Gallery has been regarded as an important example of <a href="/wiki/Postmodern_architecture" title="Postmodern architecture">Postmodern architecture</a>, especially in the use of contextual irony: each section of the external facade quotes liberally from the building next to it in regard to materials and detailing.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Crises during its existence include flood damage to work from the <a href="/wiki/River_Thames" title="River Thames">River Thames</a>, and bomb damage during World War II. However, most of the collection was in safe storage elsewhere during the war, and a large <a href="/wiki/Stanley_Spencer" title="Stanley Spencer">Stanley Spencer</a> painting, deemed too big to move, had a protective brick wall built in front of it.\n</p><p>In 1970, the building was given <a href="/wiki/Listed_building" title="Listed building">Grade II*</a> listed status.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 2012, Tate Britain announced that it had raised the \xc2\xa345&#160;million<sup id="cite_ref-Sulcas_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sulcas-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> required to complete a major renovation, largely thanks to a \xc2\xa34.9&#160;million grant from the <a href="/wiki/Heritage_Lottery_Fund" title="Heritage Lottery Fund">Heritage Lottery Fund</a> and \xc2\xa31&#160;million given by Tate Members.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The museum stayed open throughout the three phases<sup id="cite_ref-Merrick_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Merrick-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> of renovation.<sup id="cite_ref-Sulcas_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sulcas-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Completed in 2013, the newly designed sections were conceived by the architects <a href="/wiki/Caruso_St_John" title="Caruso St John">Caruso St John</a> and included a total of nine new galleries, with reinforced flooring to accommodate heavy sculptures. A second part was unveiled later that year, the centrepiece being the reopening of the building\'s Thames-facing entrance as well as a new spiral staircase beneath its rotunda.<sup id="cite_ref-Merrick_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Merrick-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The circular balcony of the rotunda\'s domed atrium, closed to visitors since the 1920s, was reopened. The gallery also now has a dedicated schools\' entrance and reception beneath its entrance steps on Millbank and a new archive gallery for the presentation of temporary displays.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Facilities">Facilities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tate_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Facilities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Millbank_Pier,_London.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/Millbank_Pier%2C_London.JPG/220px-Millbank_Pier%2C_London.JPG" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/Millbank_Pier%2C_London.JPG/330px-Millbank_Pier%2C_London.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/Millbank_Pier%2C_London.JPG/440px-Millbank_Pier%2C_London.JPG 2x" data-file-width="3264" data-file-height="2448" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Millbank_Pier,_London.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Millbank_Millennium_Pier" title="Millbank Millennium Pier">Millbank Millennium Pier</a> outside Tate Britain, which is linked by a high-speed boat to <a href="/wiki/Tate_Modern" title="Tate Modern">Tate Modern</a></div></div></div>\n<p>The front entrance is accessible by steps. A side entrance at a lower level has a ramp for wheelchair access. The gallery provides a restaurant and a caf\xc3\xa9, as well as a Friends room, open only to members of the Tate. This membership is open to the public on payment of an annual subscription. As well as administration offices the building complex houses the Prints and Drawings Rooms (in the Clore galleries),<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> as well as the Library<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> and Archive<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> in the Hyman Kreitman Reading Rooms.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The restaurant features a mural by <a href="/wiki/Rex_Whistler" title="Rex Whistler">Rex Whistler</a>.\n</p><p>Tate Britain and Tate Modern are now connected by a high speed boat along the <a href="/wiki/River_Thames" title="River Thames">River Thames</a>, which runs from <a href="/wiki/Millbank_Millennium_Pier" title="Millbank Millennium Pier">Millbank Millennium Pier</a> immediately outside Tate Britain. The boat is decorated with spots, based on paintings of similar appearance by <a href="/wiki/Damien_Hirst" title="Damien Hirst">Damien Hirst</a>. The lighting artwork incorporated in the pier\'s structure is by <a href="/wiki/Angela_Bulloch" title="Angela Bulloch">Angela Bulloch</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Displays">Displays</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tate_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Displays">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The main display spaces show the permanent collection of historic British art, as well as contemporary work. It has rooms dedicated to works by one artist, such as: <a href="/wiki/Tracey_Emin" title="Tracey Emin">Tracey Emin</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Latham_(artist)" title="John Latham (artist)">John Latham</a>, <a href="/wiki/Douglas_Gordon" title="Douglas Gordon">Douglas Gordon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sam_Taylor-Wood" class="mw-redirect" title="Sam Taylor-Wood">Sam Taylor-Wood</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tacita_Dean" title="Tacita Dean">Tacita Dean</a>, Marcus Gheeraerts II, though these, like the rest of the collection, are subject to rotation.\n</p><p>The gallery also organises career retrospectives of British artists and temporary major exhibitions of British Art. Every three years the gallery stages a Triennial exhibition in which a guest curator provides an overview of contemporary British Art. The 2003 Tate Triennial was called <i>Days Like These</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> <i>Art Now</i> is a small changing show of a contemporary artist\'s work in a dedicated room.\n</p><p>Tate Britain is the home of the annual and usually controversial <a href="/wiki/Turner_Prize" title="Turner Prize">Turner Prize</a> exhibition, featuring four artists selected by a jury chaired by the director of Tate Britain. This is spread out over the year with the four nominees announced in May, the show of their work opened in October and the prize itself given in December. Each stage of the prize generates media coverage, and there have also been a number of demonstrations against the prize, notably since 2000 an <a href="/wiki/Stuckist_demonstrations" title="Stuckist demonstrations">annual picket</a> by <a href="/wiki/Stuckism" title="Stuckism">Stuckist</a> artists. In recent years the exhibition and award ceremony have taken place at locations other than in Tate Britain: for example in Liverpool (2007), Derry-Londonderry (2013), Glasgow (2015) and Hull (2017).\n</p><p>Tate Britain has attempted to reach out to a different and younger audience with <i>Late at Tate Britain</i> on the first Friday of every month, with half-price admission to exhibitions, live music and performance art.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Other public involvement has included the display of visitors\', as opposed to curators\', interpretation of certain artworks.\n</p><p>Regular free tours operate on the hour, and at 1.15&#160;pm on Tuesday, Wednesday and Thursday short 15-minute talks are given on paintings, artists and artistic styles.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Permanent_collection">Permanent collection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tate_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Permanent collection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Tate Britain is the national gallery of British art from 1500 to the present day. As such, it is the most comprehensive collection of its kind in the world (only the <a href="/wiki/Yale_Center_for_British_Art" title="Yale Center for British Art">Yale Center for British Art</a> can claim similar expansiveness, but with less depth).  More recent artists include <a href="/wiki/David_Hockney" title="David Hockney">David Hockney</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peter_Blake_(artist)" title="Peter Blake (artist)">Peter Blake</a> and <a href="/wiki/Francis_Bacon_(painter)" class="mw-redirect" title="Francis Bacon (painter)">Francis Bacon</a>. class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tate_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Statue of Millais">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Millais_statue_1.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Millais_statue_1.jpg/170px-Millais_statue_1.jpg" width="170" height="227" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Millais_statue_1.jpg/255px-Millais_statue_1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Millais_statue_1.jpg/340px-Millais_statue_1.jpg 2x" data-file-width="450" data-file-height="600" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Millais_statue_1.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Statue of <a href="/wiki/John_Everett_Millais" title="John Everett Millais">John Everett Millais</a> by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Brock" title="Thomas Brock">Thomas Brock</a> at Tate Britain</div></div></div>\n<p>When the <a href="/wiki/Pre-Raphaelite_Brotherhood" title="Pre-Raphaelite Brotherhood">Pre-Raphaelite</a> painter and President of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Academy" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal Academy">Royal Academy</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Everett_Millais" title="John Everett Millais">John Everett Millais</a>, died in 1896, the Prince of Wales (later to become <a href="/wiki/King_Edward_VII" class="mw-redirect" title="King Edward VII">King Edward VII</a>) chaired a memorial committee, which commissioned a statue of the artist.<sup id="cite_ref-birchall_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-birchall-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> This was installed at the front of the gallery in the garden on the east side in 1905. On 23 November that year, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Pall_Mall_Gazette" title="The Pall Mall Gazette">The Pall Mall Gazette</a></i> called it "a breezy statue, representing the man in the characteristic attitude in which we all knew him".<sup id="cite_ref-birchall_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-birchall-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1953, Tate Director, <a href="/wiki/Norman_Reid_(museum_director)" title="Norman Reid (museum director)">Sir Norman Reid</a>, attempted to have it replaced by <a href="/wiki/Auguste_Rodin" title="Auguste Rodin">Rodin</a>\'s <i>John the Baptist</i>, and in 1962 again proposed its removal, calling its presence "positively harmful". His efforts were frustrated by the statue\'s owner, the <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Works_(United_Kingdom)" title="Ministry of Works (United Kingdom)">Ministry of Works</a>. Ownership was transferred from the Ministry to <a href="/wiki/English_Heritage" title="English Heritage">English Heritage</a> in 1996, and by them in turn to the Tate.<sup id="cite_ref-birchall_19-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-birchall-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> In 2000 the statue was removed to the rear of the building.<sup id="cite_ref-birchall_19-3" class="reference"><a
