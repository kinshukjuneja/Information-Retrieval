title="The Blue Boy">The Blue Boy</a></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><b>Thomas Gainsborough</b> <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society_of_Arts" title="Royal Society of Arts">FRSA</a> (14 May 1727 (baptised) \xe2\x80\x93 2 August 1788) was an English portrait and landscape painter, draughtsman, and printmaker. He surpassed his rival <a href="/wiki/Sir_Joshua_Reynolds" class="mw-redirect" title="Sir Joshua Reynolds">Sir Joshua Reynolds</a> to become the dominant British <a href="/wiki/Portrait" title="Portrait">portraitist</a> of the second half of the 18th century. He painted quickly, and the works of his maturity are characterised by a light palette and easy strokes. He preferred landscapes to portraits, and is credited (with <a href="/wiki/Richard_Wilson_(painter)" title="Richard Wilson (painter)">Richard Wilson</a>) as the originator of the 18th-century British landscape school. Gainsborough was a founding member of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Academy_of_Arts" title="Royal Academy of Arts">Royal Academy</a>.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" He later resided there, following the death of his father in 1748 and before his move to Ipswich.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The original building still survives and is now a dedicated House to his life and art.\n</p><p>When he was still a boy he impressed his father with his drawing and painting skills, and he almost certainly had painted heads and small landscapes by the time he was ten years old, including a miniature self-portrait.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Gainsborough was allowed to leave home in 1740 to study art in <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>, where he trained under engraver <a href="/wiki/Hubert-Fran%C3%A7ois_Gravelot" title="Hubert-Fran\xc3\xa7ois Gravelot">Hubert Gravelot</a><sup id="cite_ref-NGA_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NGA-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> but became associated with <a href="/wiki/William_Hogarth" title="William Hogarth">William Hogarth</a> and his school. He assisted <a href="/wiki/Francis_Hayman" title="Francis Hayman">Francis Hayman</a> in the decoration of the supper boxes at <a href="/wiki/Vauxhall_Gardens" title="Vauxhall Gardens">Vauxhall Gardens</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-NGA_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NGA-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> and contributed to the decoration of what is now the <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Coram_Foundation_for_Children" title="Thomas Coram Foundation for Children">Thomas Coram Foundation for Children</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thomas_Gainsborough&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Suffolk">Suffolk</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thomas_Gainsborough&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Suffolk">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In 1746, Gainsborough married Margaret Burr, an illegitimate daughter of the <a href="/wiki/Henry_Scudamore,_3rd_Duke_of_Beaufort" title="Henry Scudamore, 3rd Duke of Beaufort">Duke of Beaufort</a>, who settled a \xc2\xa3200 annuity on them. The artist\'s work, then mostly consisting of landscape paintings, was not selling well. He returned to Sudbury in 1748\xe2\x80\x931749 and concentrated on painting <a href="/wiki/Portrait" title="Portrait">portraits</a>.\n</p><p>In 1752, he and his family, now including two daughters, moved to <a href="/wiki/Ipswich" title="Ipswich">Ipswich</a>. Commissions for personal portraits increased, but his clientele included mainly local merchants and squires. The Huntington, California.</div></div></div>\n<p>In 1759, Gainsborough and his family moved to <a href="/wiki/Bath,_Somerset" title="Bath, Somerset">Bath</a>, living at number 17 <a href="/wiki/The_Circus,_Bath" class="mw-redirect" title="The Circus, Bath">The Circus</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> There, he studied portraits by <a href="/wiki/Anthony_van_Dyck" title="Anthony van Dyck">van Dyck</a> and was eventually able to attract a fashionable clientele. In 1761, he began to send work to the Society of Arts exhibition in London (now the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Academy_of_Arts" title="Royal Academy of Arts">Royal Society of Arts</a>, of which he was one of the earliest members); and from 1769 he submitted works to the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Academy_of_Arts" title="Royal Academy of Arts">Royal Academy</a>\'s annual exhibitions. He selected portraits of well-known or notorious clients in order to attract attention. The exhibitions helped him acquire a national reputation, and he was invited to become a founding member of the Royal Academy in 1769. His relationship with the academy was not an easy one and he stopped exhibiting his paintings in 1773.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="London">London</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thomas_Gainsborough&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: London">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Thomas_Gainsborough,_Frances_Browne,_Mrs_John_Douglas_(1746_-_1811),_1783-84_at_Waddesdon_Manor.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Thomas_Gainsborough%2C_Frances_Browne%2C_Mrs_John_Douglas_%281746_-_1811%29%2C_1783-84_at_Waddesdon_Manor.jpg/170px-Thomas_Gainsborough%2C_Frances_Browne%2C_Mrs_John_Douglas_%281746_-_1811%29%2C_1783-84_at_Waddesdon_Manor.jpg" width="170" height="272" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Thomas_Gainsborough%2C_Frances_Browne%2C_Mrs_John_Douglas_%281746_-_1811%29%2C_1783-84_at_Waddesdon_Manor.jpg/255px-Thomas_Gainsborough%2C_Frances_Browne%2C_Mrs_John_Douglas_%281746_-_1811%29%2C_1783-84_at_Waddesdon_Manor.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Thomas_Gainsborough%2C_Frances_Browne%2C_Mrs_John_Douglas_%281746_-_1811%29%2C_1783-84_at_Waddesdon_Manor.jpg/340px-Thomas_Gainsborough%2C_Frances_Browne%2C_Mrs_John_Douglas_%281746_-_1811%29%2C_1783-84_at_Waddesdon_Manor.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2684" data-file-height="4302" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Thomas_Gainsborough,_Frances_Browne,_Mrs_John_Douglas_(1746_-_1811),_1783-84_at_Waddesdon_Manor.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>Frances Browne, Mrs John Douglas (1746 - 1811)</i>, 1783-84 at <a href="/wiki/Waddesdon_Manor" title="Waddesdon Manor">Waddesdon Manor</a></div></div></div>\n<p>In 1774, Gainsborough and his family moved to London to live in <a href="/wiki/Schomberg_House" title="Schomberg House">Schomberg House</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pall_Mall,_London" title="Pall Mall, London">Pall Mall</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-NGA_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NGA-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> A commemorative <a href="/wiki/Blue_plaque" title="Blue plaque">blue plaque</a> was put on the house in 1951.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> In 1777, he again began to exhibit his paintings at the Royal Academy, including portraits of contemporary celebrities, such as the Duke and Duchess of Cumberland. Exhibitions of his work continued for the next six years. About this time, Gainsborough began experimenting with <a href="/wiki/Printmaking" title="Printmaking">printmaking</a> using the then-novel techniques of <a href="/wiki/Aquatint" title="Aquatint">aquatint</a> and <a href="/wiki/Soft-ground_etching" class="mw-redirect" title="Soft-ground etching">soft-ground etching</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Rosenthal_Oxford_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rosenthal_Oxford-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Thomas_Gainsborough_(English_-_Portrait_of_Anne,_Countess_of_Chesterfield_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Thomas_Gainsborough_%28English_-_Portrait_of_Anne%2C_Countess_of_Chesterfield_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg/170px-Thomas_Gainsborough_%28English_-_Portrait_of_Anne%2C_Countess_of_Chesterfield_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg" width="170" height="242" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Thomas_Gainsborough_%28English_-_Portrait_of_Anne%2C_Countess_of_Chesterfield_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg/255px-Thomas_Gainsborough_%28English_-_Portrait_of_Anne%2C_Countess_of_Chesterfield_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Thomas_Gainsborough_%28English_-_Portrait_of_Anne%2C_Countess_of_Chesterfield_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg/340px-Thomas_Gainsborough_%28English_-_Portrait_of_Anne%2C_Countess_of_Chesterfield_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg 2x" data-file-width="5751" data-file-height="8175" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Thomas_Gainsborough_(English_-_Portrait_of_Anne,_Countess_of_Chesterfield_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>His later pictures are characterized by a light palette and easy strokes. Portrait of Anne, Countess of Chesterfield, 1777\xe2\x80\x931778</div></div></div>\n<p>During the 1770s and 1780s Gainsborough developed a type of portrait in which he integrated the sitter into the landscape. A splendid example of this is his portrait of Frances Browne, Mrs John Douglas (1746-1811) which can be seen at <a href="/wiki/Waddesdon_Manor" title="Waddesdon Manor">Waddesdon Manor</a>. The sitter has withdrawn to a secluded and overgrown corner of a garden to read a letter, her pose recalling the traditional representation of Melancholy. Gainsborough emphasised the relationship between Mrs Douglas and her environment by painting the clouds behind her and the drapery billowing across her lap with similar silvery mauves and fluid brushstrokes. This portrait was included in his first private exhibition at Schomberg House in 1784.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1776, Gainsborough painted a portrait of <a href="/wiki/Johann_Christian_Bach" title="Johann Christian Bach">Johann Christian Bach</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-JCBach_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JCBach-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> the youngest son of <a href="/wiki/Johann_Sebastian_Bach" title="Johann Sebastian Bach">Johann Sebastian Bach</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Burnett_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Burnett-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Bach\'s former teacher <a href="/wiki/Padre_Martini" class="mw-redirect" title="Padre Martini">Padre Martini</a> of Bologna, Italy, was assembling a collection of portraits of musicians, and Bach asked Gainsborough to paint his portrait as part of this collection.<sup id="cite_ref-JCBach_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JCBach-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The portrait now hangs in the <a href="/wiki/National_Portrait_Gallery,_London" title="National Portrait Gallery, London">National Portrait Gallery</a> in London.<sup id="cite_ref-JCBach_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JCBach-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1780, he painted the portraits of King <a href="/wiki/George_III_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="George III of the United Kingdom">George III</a> and his queen and afterwards received many royal commissions. This gave him some influence with the Academy and allowed him to dictate the manner in which he wished his work to be exhibited. However, in 1783, he removed his paintings from the forthcoming exhibition and transferred them to Schomberg House.\n</p><p>In 1784, royal painter <a href="/wiki/Allan_Ramsay_(artist)" title="Allan Ramsay (artist)">Allan Ramsay</a> died and the King was obliged to give the job to Gainsborough\'s rival and Academy president, <a href="/wiki/Joshua_Reynolds" title="Joshua Reynolds">Joshua Reynolds</a>. Gainsborough remained the Royal Family\'s favorite painter, however.\n</p><p>In his later years, Gainsborough often painted relatively simple, ordinary landscapes. With <a href="/wiki/Richard_Wilson_(painter)" title="Richard Wilson (painter)">Richard Wilson</a>, he was one of the originators of the eighteenth-century British landscape school; though simultaneously, in conjunction with Sir Joshua Reynolds, he was the dominant British <a href="/wiki/Portrait" title="Portrait">portraitist</a> of the second half of the 18th century.\n</p><p>William Jackson in his contemporary essays said of him "to his intimate friends he was sincere and honest and that his heart was always alive to every feeling of honour and generosity".<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Gainsborough did not particularly enjoy reading but letters written to his friends were penned in such an exceptional conversational manner that the style could not be equalled.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> As a letter writer Henry Bate-Dudley said of him "a selection of his letters would offer the world as much originality and beauty as is ever traced in his paintings".<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the 1780s, Gainsborough used a device he called a "Showbox" to compose landscapes and display them backlit on glass. The original box is on display in the Victoria &amp; Albert Museum with a reproduction transparency.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>\nHe died of cancer on 2 August 1788 at the age of 61. According to his daughter Peggy, his last words were "<a href="/wiki/Van_Dyck" class="mw-redirect" title="Van Dyck">van Dyck</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> He is interred in the churchyard <a href="/wiki/St._Anne%27s_Church,_Kew" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Anne&#39;s Church, Kew">St. Anne\'s Church, Kew</a>, Surrey (located on Kew Green). It was his express wish to be buried near his friend <a href="/wiki/Joshua_Kirby" title="Joshua Kirby">Joshua Kirby</a>. Later his wife and nephew, <a href="/wiki/Gainsborough_Dupont" title="Gainsborough Dupont">Gainsborough Dupont</a>, were interred with him. Coincidentally <a href="/wiki/Johan_Zoffany" title="Johan Zoffany">Johan Zoffany</a> and <a href="/wiki/Franz_Bauer" title="Franz Bauer">Franz Bauer</a> are also buried in the graveyard. As of 2011, an appeal is underway to pay the costs of restoration of his tomb.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> A street in Kew, Gainsborough Road, class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thomas_Gainsborough&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Technique">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Girl_with_Pigs_by_Thomas_Gainsborough.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Girl_with_Pigs_by_Thomas_Gainsborough.jpg/170px-Girl_with_Pigs_by_Thomas_Gainsborough.jpg" width="170" height="143" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Girl_with_Pigs_by_Thomas_Gainsborough.jpg/255px-Girl_with_Pigs_by_Thomas_Gainsborough.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Girl_with_Pigs_by_Thomas_Gainsborough.jpg/340px-Girl_with_Pigs_by_Thomas_Gainsborough.jpg 2x" data-file-width="525" data-file-height="442" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Girl_with_Pigs_by_Thomas_Gainsborough.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>Girl with Pigs</i>, 1781\xe2\x80\x9382, said by Sir Joshua Reynolds to be "the best picture he ever painted".<sup id="cite_ref-Willes_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Willes-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>\n<p>The art historian <a href="/wiki/Michael_Rosenthal_(art_historian)" class="mw-redirect" title="Michael Rosenthal (art historian)">Michael Rosenthal</a> described Gainsborough as "one of the most technically proficient and, at the same time, most experimental artists of his time".<sup id="cite_ref-Rosenthal_Oxford_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rosenthal_Oxford-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> He was noted for the speed with which he applied paint, and he worked more from observations of nature (and of human nature) than from application of formal academic rules.<sup id="cite_ref-Rosenthal_Oxford_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rosenthal_Oxford-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The poetic sensibility of his paintings caused <a href="/wiki/John_Constable" title="John Constable">Constable</a> to say, "On looking at them, we find tears in our eyes and know not what brings them."\n</p><p>Gainsborough\'s enthusiasm for landscapes is shown in the way he merged figures of the portraits with the scenes behind them. He said, "I\'m sick of portraits, and wish very much to take my viol-da-gam and walk off to some sweet village, where I can paint landskips <i>(sic)</i> and enjoy the fag end of life in quietness and ease." His landscapes were often painted at night by candlelight, using a tabletop arrangement of stones, pieces of mirrors, broccoli, and the like as a model.<sup id="cite_ref-Rosenthal_Oxford_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rosenthal_Oxford-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> His later work was characterised by a light palette and easy, economical strokes.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Gainsborough\'s only known assistant was his nephew, <a href="/wiki/Gainsborough_Dupont" title="Gainsborough Dupont">Gainsborough Dupont</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-NGA_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NGA-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> In the last year of his life he collaborated with <a href="/wiki/John_Hoppner" title="John Hoppner">John Hoppner</a> in painting a full-length portrait of <a href="/wiki/John_Chetwynd-Talbot,_1st_Earl_Talbot" title="John Chetwynd-Talbot, 1st Earl Talbot">Lady Charlotte Talbot</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reputation">Reputation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thomas_Gainsborough&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Reputation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>His most famous works, <i>Portrait of Mrs. Graham</i>; <i>Mary and Margaret: The Painter\'s Daughters</i>; <i>William Hallett and His Wife Elizabeth, nee Stephen</i>, known as <i>The Morning Walk</i>; and <i>Cottage Girl with Dog and Pitcher</i>, display the unique individuality of his subjects. <a href="/wiki/Joshua_Reynolds" title="Joshua Reynolds">Joshua Reynolds</a> considered <i>Girl with Pigs</i> "the best picture he (Gainsborough) ever painted or perhaps ever will".<sup id="cite_ref-Willes_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Willes-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Gainsborough\'s works became popular with collectors from the 1850s on, after <a href="/wiki/Lionel_de_Rothschild" title="Lionel de Rothschild">Lionel de Rothschild</a> began buying his portraits. The rapid rise in the value of pictures by Gainsborough and also by Reynolds in the mid 19th century was partly because the Rothschild family, including <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_de_Rothschild" title="Ferdinand de Rothschild">Ferdinand de Rothschild</a> began collecting them.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 2011, Gainsborough\'s portrait of <i>Miss Read</i> (Mrs Frances Villebois) was sold by <a href="/wiki/Michael_Pearson,_4th_Viscount_Cowdray" title="Michael Pearson, 4th Viscount Cowdray">Michael Pearson, 4th Viscount Cowdray</a>, for a record price of \xc2\xa36.5M.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> She was a matrilineal descendant of <a href="/wiki/Cecily_Neville,_Duchess_of_York" title="Cecily Neville, Duchess of York">Cecily Neville, Duchess of York</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Mitochondrial_DNA" title="Mitochondrial DNA">mitochondrial DNA</a> descent through which the remains of <a href="/wiki/Richard_III_of_England" title="Richard III of England">Richard III of England</a> were <a
