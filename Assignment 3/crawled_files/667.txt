Cymru</i>) existed between 1216 and 1536, encompassing two-thirds of modern <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a> during its height between 1267 and 1277. For most of its history it was "annexed and united" to the English Crown except for its earliest few decades. However, for a few generations, specifically the period from its foundation in 1216 to <a href="/wiki/Edward_I_of_England" title="Edward I of England">Edward I</a>\'s completion of the <a href="/wiki/Conquest_of_Wales_by_Edward_I" class="mw-redirect" title="Conquest of Wales by Edward I">conquest of Wales</a> in 1284, it was <i>de facto</i> independent under a Welsh <a href="/wiki/Prince_of_Wales" title="Prince of Wales">Prince of Wales</a>, albeit one who swore <a href="/wiki/Fealty" title="Fealty">fealty</a> to the <a href="/wiki/List_of_English_monarchs" title="List of English monarchs">King of England</a>.\n</p><p>The Principality was formally founded in 1216 at the <a href="/wiki/History_of_Gwynedd_during_the_High_Middle_Ages#Aberdyfi,_Worcester,_and_Strata_Florida;_1216\xe2\x80\x931240" title="History of Gwynedd during the High Middle Ages">Council of Aberdyfi</a> and later recognised by the 1218 <a href="/wiki/Llywelyn_the_Great#Treaty_of_Worcester_and_border_campaigns_1218.E2.80.931229" title="Llywelyn the Great">Treaty of Worcester</a> between <a href="/wiki/Llywelyn_the_Great" title="Llywelyn the Great">Llywelyn the Great</a> of Wales and <a href="/wiki/Henry_III_of_England" title="Henry III of England">Henry III</a> of <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Treaty_of_Aberdyfi_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Treaty_of_Aberdyfi-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Treaty_of_Aberdovey_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Treaty_of_Aberdovey-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Emerging_Principality_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Emerging_Principality-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The treaty gave substance to the political reality of 13th-century Wales and England, and the relationship of the former with the <a href="/wiki/Angevin_Empire" title="Angevin Empire">Angevin Empire</a>. The principality retained a great degree of autonomy, characterized by a separate legal jurisprudence based on the well established laws of <i><a href="/wiki/Cyfraith_Hywel" title="Cyfraith Hywel">Cyfraith Hywel</a></i>, and by the increasingly sophisticated <a href="/wiki/Curia_regis" title="Curia regis">court</a> of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Aberffraw" title="House of Aberffraw">House of Aberffraw</a>. Although it owed <a href="/wiki/Fealty" title="Fealty">fealty</a> to the Angevin king of England, the principality was <i><a href="/wiki/De_facto" title="De facto">de facto</a></i> independent, with a similar status in the empire to the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Scotland" title="Kingdom of Scotland">Kingdom of Scotland</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-statehood_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-statehood-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>  Its existence has been seen as proof that all the elements necessary for the growth of Welsh <a href="/wiki/Sovereign_state" title="Sovereign state">statehood</a> were in place.<sup id="cite_ref-statehood_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-statehood-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The period of <i>de facto</i> independence ended with <a href="/wiki/Conquest_of_Wales_by_Edward_I" class="mw-redirect" title="Conquest of Wales by Edward I">Edward I\'s conquest of the Principality</a> between 1277 and 1283. Under the <a href="/wiki/Statute_of_Rhuddlan" title="Statute of Rhuddlan">Statute of Rhuddlan</a> the Principality lost its independence and became effectively an annexed territory of the English crown. From 1301, the crown\'s lands in north and west Wales formed part of the <a href="/wiki/Appanage" title="Appanage">appanage</a> of England\'s heir apparent, with the title "<a href="/wiki/Prince_of_Wales" title="Prince of Wales">Prince of Wales</a>". On accession of the Prince to the English throne, the lands and title became merged with the Crown again. On two occasions Welsh claimants to the title rose up in rebellion during this period, although neither ultimately succeeded.\n</p><p>Since the <a href="/wiki/Laws_in_Wales_Acts_1535%E2%80%931542" class="mw-redirect" title="Laws in Wales Acts 1535\xe2\x80\x931542">Laws in Wales Acts 1535\xe2\x80\x931542</a>, which formally incorporated all of Wales within the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_England" title="Kingdom of England">Kingdom of England</a>, there has been no geographical or constitutional basis for describing any of the territory of Wales as a principality, although the term has occasionally been used in an informal sense to describe the country, and in relation to the honorary title of Prince of Wales.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" class="toctext">References</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Foundations">Foundations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Principality_of_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Foundations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The 13th-century Principality of Wales was based on the historic lands ruled by the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Aberffraw" title="House of Aberffraw">Aberffraw</a> <a href="/wiki/Dynasty" title="Dynasty">family</a>, lands in <a href="/wiki/North_Wales" title="North Wales">north Wales</a> traditionally including <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Anglesey" class="mw-redirect" title="Isle of Anglesey">Ynys M\xc3\xb4n</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Gwynedd#Administration" title="Kingdom of Gwynedd">Gwynedd-Uwch-Conwy</a> (<i>Gwynedd above the Conwy</i>, or <i>Upper Gwynedd</i>), and the <a href="/wiki/Perfeddwlad" title="Perfeddwlad">Perfeddwlad</a> (<i>the Middle Country</i>) also known as Gwynedd-Is-Conwy (<i>Gwynedd below the Conwy</i>, or <i>Lower Gwynedd</i>). Additional lands were acquired through vassalage or conquest, and by regaining lands lost to <a href="/wiki/Welsh_Marches" title="Welsh Marches">Marcher lords</a>, particularly that of the Perfeddwlad, <a href="/wiki/Powys_Fadog" title="Powys Fadog">Powys Fadog</a>, <a href="/wiki/Powys_Wenwynwyn" title="Powys Wenwynwyn">Powys Wenwynwyn</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ceredigion" title="Ceredigion">Ceredigion</a>.\n</p><p>Previous Welsh rulers had styled themselves in a variety of ways, usually in relation to a certain patrimony like "Lord of Ceredigion" or "King of Builth". The most powerful were often referred to (by others at least) as "King of the Britons". As Wales was a defined geographical area with agreed borders, yet outside the bounds of England, anyone bestowed with the title Prince of <i>Wales</i> would have <a href="/wiki/Suzerainty" title="Suzerainty">suzerainty</a> over any local Welsh ruler but without the territorial ambitions on England of a <i>King of the Britons</i> \xe2\x80\x93 which implied "liberating" the <a href="/wiki/Romano-British_culture" title="Romano-British culture">Britons</a> who still resided in places long considered a part of England such as Devon, Cornwall, Cumberland and other places, albeit in fewer and fewer numbers.\n</p><p>The Aberffraw family had long claimed primacy over all other Welsh lords, including over those rulers of <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Powys" title="Kingdom of Powys">Powys</a> and of <a href="/wiki/Deheubarth" title="Deheubarth">Deheubarth</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-The_Aberffraw_dynasty_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Aberffraw_dynasty-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-The_Aberffraw_family_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Aberffraw_family-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> In <i>The History of Gruffydd ap Cynan</i>, written in the late 12th century, the family asserted its rights as the senior line of descendants from <a href="/wiki/Rhodri_the_Great" title="Rhodri the Great">Rhodri the Great</a>, who had ruled most of Wales between 820\xe2\x80\x93870, and whose sons came to rule in Gwynedd, Deheubarth, and Powys.<sup id="cite_ref-The_Aberffraw_dynasty_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Aberffraw_dynasty-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Gruffudd_ap_Cynan" title="Gruffudd ap Cynan">Gruffudd ap Cynan</a>\'s biography was first written in <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> and intended for a wider audience outside Wales.<sup id="cite_ref-The_Aberffraw_dynasty_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Aberffraw_dynasty-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>  The significance of this claim was that the Aberffraw family owed nothing to the English king for its position in Wales, and that they held authority in Wales "by absolute right through descent," wrote historian John Davies.<sup id="cite_ref-The_Aberffraw_dynasty_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Aberffraw_dynasty-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Prior_to_1284:_under_the_House_of_Aberffraw">Prior to 1284: under the House of Aberffraw</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Principality_of_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Prior to 1284: under the House of Aberffraw">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Gwynedd_in_the_High_Middle_Ages" class="mw-redirect" title="Gwynedd in the High Middle Ages">Gwynedd in the High Middle Ages</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Powys" title="Kingdom of Powys">Kingdom of Powys</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Deheubarth" class="mw-redirect" title="Kingdom of Deheubarth">Kingdom of Deheubarth</a></div>\n<p>The Principality of Wales was created in 1216 at the Council of Aberdyfi, when it was agreed between <a href="/wiki/Llywelyn_the_Great" title="Llywelyn the Great">Llywelyn the Great</a> and the other sovereign princes among the Welsh that he was the paramount ruler amongst them, and they would pay homage to him. Later he obtained recognition, at least in part, of this agreement from the King of England, who agreed that Llywelyn\'s heirs and successors would enjoy the title "Prince of Wales" but with certain limitations to his realm and other conditions, including homage to the King of England as vassal, and adherence to rules regarding a legitimate succession. Llywelyn had been at pains to ensure that his heirs and successors would follow the "approved" (by the Pope at least) system of inheritance which excluded illegitimate sons. In so doing he excluded his elder bastard son <a href="/wiki/Gruffydd_ap_Llywelyn" title="Gruffydd ap Llywelyn">Gruffydd ap Llywelyn</a> from the inheritance, a decision which would have later ramifications. In 1240 Llywelyn died and <a href="/wiki/Henry_III_of_England" title="Henry III of England">Henry III of England</a> (who succeeded John) promptly invaded large areas of his former realm, usurping them from him. However, the two sides came to peace and Henry honoured at least part of the agreement and bestowed upon <a href="/wiki/Dafydd_ap_Llywelyn" title="Dafydd ap Llywelyn">Dafydd ap Llywelyn</a> the title \'Prince of Wales\'. This title would be granted to his successor Llywelyn in 1267 (after a campaign by him to achieve it) and was later claimed by his brother Dafydd and other members of the princely <a href="/wiki/House_of_Aberffraw" title="House of Aberffraw">House of Aberffraw</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aberffraw_Princes">Aberffraw Princes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Principality_of_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Aberffraw Princes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The traditional numbering of the Princes of Wales (according to Welsh sources) begins with <a href="/wiki/Owain_Gwynedd" title="Owain Gwynedd">Owain Gwynedd</a> who ruled from 1137 until 1170. He was never acknowledged as Prince of Wales, and in fact never used that title; however he was considered by later chroniclers to have been the first Welsh prince to unite Wales. This was demonstrated when <a href="/wiki/Owain_Glynd%C5%B5r" title="Owain Glynd\xc5\xb5r">Owain Glynd\xc5\xb5r</a> was explicitly crowned as <b>Owain IV of Wales</b> in 1404.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The English viewed it very differently and considered the title to be bestowed by them and with their grace on only <a href="/wiki/Dafydd_ap_Llywelyn" title="Dafydd ap Llywelyn">Dafydd ap Llywelyn</a> in 1240 and <a href="/wiki/Llywelyn_ap_Gruffudd" title="Llywelyn ap Gruffudd">Llywelyn ap Gruffudd</a> in 1267. After 1301 the title was granted to the eldest son and heir of an English sovereign.\n</p>\n<h4><span id="Owain_Gwynedd_1137.E2.80.9370"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Owain_Gwynedd_1137\xe2\x80\x9370">Owain Gwynedd 1137\xe2\x80\x9370</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Principality_of_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Owain Gwynedd 1137\xe2\x80\x9370">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Owain_Gwynedd" title="Owain Gwynedd">Owain Gwynedd</a></div>\n<p>The prodigious<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (June 2013)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> <a href="/wiki/Owain_Gwynedd" title="Owain Gwynedd">Owain Gwynedd</a> succeeded in retaining for his family the primary position in Wales which his father had achieved. In 1154 he defeated an English and Powysian invasion, but was forced to give up some territory bordering the <a href="/wiki/River_Dee,_Wales" title="River Dee, Wales">River Dee</a>. In later years he recaptured these areas and achieved a dominant position for Gwynedd in Wales which had not been seen for centuries. During Owain\'s reign he changed his title from "King of Gwynedd" to "Prince of the Welsh" (J. B. Smith, <i>Owain Gwynedd</i>, 14\xe2\x80\x9316).\n</p>\n<h4><span id="Dafydd_ab_Owain_Gwynedd_c.1170.E2.80.9395"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Dafydd_ab_Owain_Gwynedd_c.1170\xe2\x80\x9395">Dafydd ab Owain Gwynedd c.1170\xe2\x80\x9395</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Principality_of_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Dafydd ab Owain Gwynedd c.1170\xe2\x80\x9395">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Dafydd_ab_Owain_Gwynedd" title="Dafydd ab Owain Gwynedd">Dafydd ab Owain Gwynedd</a></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Dafydd_ab_Owain_Gwynedd" title="Dafydd ab Owain Gwynedd">Dafydd ab Owain Gwynedd</a> had usurped the crown from his siblings in a debilitating civil war within Gwynedd. He married the half-sister of king <a href="/wiki/Henry_II_of_England" title="Henry II of England">Henry II of England</a> in 1174. He was eventually ousted in 1195 from his much reduced domain by his nephew Llywelyn.\n</p>\n<h4><span id="Llywelyn_ap_Iorwerth_1195.E2.80.931240"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Llywelyn_ap_Iorwerth_1195\xe2\x80\x931240">Llywelyn ap Iorwerth 1195\xe2\x80\x931240</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Principality_of_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Llywelyn ap Iorwerth 1195\xe2\x80\x931240">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:232px;"><a href="/wiki/File:LlywelynFawr.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/LlywelynFawr.jpg/230px-LlywelynFawr.jpg" width="230" height="406" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/LlywelynFawr.jpg/345px-LlywelynFawr.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/LlywelynFawr.jpg/460px-LlywelynFawr.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1322" data-file-height="2336" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:LlywelynFawr.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Llywelyn_ap_Iorwerth" class="mw-redirect" title="Llywelyn ap Iorwerth">Llywelyn ap Iorwerth</a> (Llywelyn Fawr) ruled <a href="/wiki/Principality_of_Gwynedd" class="mw-redirect" title="Principality of Gwynedd">Gwynedd</a> and most of Wales from 1195 to 1240</div></div></div>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Llywelyn_ap_Iorwerth" class="mw-redirect" title="Llywelyn ap Iorwerth">Llywelyn ap Iorwerth</a></div>\n<p>By 1200 Owain Gwynedd\'s grandson Llywelyn Fawr (the Great) ap Iorwerth ruled over all of Gwynedd, with England endorsing all of Llywelyn\'s holdings that year.<sup id="cite_ref-Llywelyn_&amp;_Crown_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Llywelyn_&amp;_Crown-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> England\'s endorsement was part of a larger strategy of reducing the influence of Powys Wenwynwyn, as King John had given William de Breos licence in 1200 to "seize as much as he could" from the native Welsh.<sup id="cite_ref-English_policy_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-English_policy-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> However, de Breos was in disgrace by 1208, and Llywelyn seized both Powys Wenwynwyn and northern Ceredigon.\n</p><p>In his expansion, the Prince was careful not to antagonise King John, his father-in-law.<sup id="cite_ref-Llywelyn_&amp;_Crown_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Llywelyn_&amp;_Crown-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Llywelyn had married <a href="/wiki/Joan,_Lady_of_Wales" title="Joan, Lady of Wales">Joan</a>, King John\'s illegitimate daughter, in 1204.<sup id="cite_ref-The_Aberffraw_dynasty_5-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Aberffraw_dynasty-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> In 1209 Prince Llywelyn joined King John on his campaign in Scotland. However, by 1211 King John recognised the growing influence of Prince Llywelyn as a threat to English authority in Wales.<sup id="cite_ref-English_policy_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-English_policy-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> King John invaded Gwynedd and reached the banks of the Menai, and Llywelyn was forced to cede the Perfeddwlad, and recognize John as his heir presumptive if Llywelyn\'s marriage to Joan did not produce any legitimate successors.<sup id="cite_ref-English_policy_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-English_policy-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Succession was a complicated matter given that Welsh law recognized children born out of wedlock as equal to those in born in wedlock and sometimes accepted claims through the female line.<sup id="cite_ref-Welsh_law_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Welsh_law-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> By then, Llywelyn had several illegitimate children. Many of Llywelyn\'s Welsh allies had abandoned him during England\'s invasion of Gwynedd, preferring an overlord far away rather than one nearby.<sup id="cite_ref-Welsh_lords_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Welsh_lords-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> These Welsh lords expected an unobtrusive English crown; but King John had a castle built at <a href="/wiki/Aberystwyth_Castle" title="Aberystwyth Castle">Aberystwyth</a>, and his direct interference in Powys and the Perfeddwlad caused many of these Welsh lords to rethink their position.<sup id="cite_ref-Welsh_lords_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Welsh_lords-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Llywelyn capitalised on Welsh resentment against King John, and led a church-sanctioned revolt against him.<sup id="cite_ref-Welsh_lords_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Welsh_lords-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> As King John was an <a href="/wiki/Excommunication" title="Excommunication">enemy of the church</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pope_Innocent_III" title="Pope Innocent III">Pope Innocent III</a> gave his blessing to Llywelyn\'s revolt. \n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:CymruLlywelyn.PNG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/CymruLlywelyn.PNG/250px-CymruLlywelyn.PNG" width="250" height="275" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/CymruLlywelyn.PNG/375px-CymruLlywelyn.PNG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/CymruLlywelyn.PNG/500px-CymruLlywelyn.PNG 2x" data-file-width="514" data-file-height="566" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:CymruLlywelyn.PNG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Wales c. 1217. Yellow: areas directly ruled by Llywelyn; Grey: areas ruled by Llywelyn\'s vassals; Green: Anglo-Norman <a href="/wiki/Marcher_lords" class="mw-redirect" title="Marcher lords">marcher lordships</a> in Wales.</div></div></div>\n<p>Early in 1212 Llywelyn had regained the Perfeddwlad and burned the castle at Aberystwyth. Llywelyn\'s revolt caused John to postpone his invasion of France, and <a href="/wiki/Philip_II_of_France" title="Philip II of France">Philip Augustus</a>, the <a href="/wiki/King_of_France" class="mw-redirect" title="King of France">King of France</a>, was so moved as to contact Llywelyn and propose that they ally against the English king<sup id="cite_ref-Foreign_policy_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Foreign_policy-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> King John ordered the execution by hanging of his Welsh hostages, the sons of many of Llywelyn\'s supporters<sup id="cite_ref-English_policy_10-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-English_policy-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Llywelyn I was the first prince to receive the fealty of other Welsh lords at the 1216 Council of Aberdyfi, thus becoming the <i>de facto</i> Prince of Wales and giving substance to the Aberffraw claims.\n</p>\n<h4><span id="Dafydd_ap_Llywelyn_1240.E2.80.9346"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Dafydd_ap_Llywelyn_1240\xe2\x80\x9346">Dafydd ap Llywelyn 1240\xe2\x80\x9346</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Principality_of_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Dafydd ap Llywelyn 1240\xe2\x80\x9346">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Dafydd_ap_Llywelyn" title="Dafydd ap Llywelyn">Dafydd ap Llywelyn</a></div>\n<p>On succeeding his father, Dafydd immediately had to contend with the claims of his half-brother, Gruffudd, to the throne. Having imprisoned Gruffudd, his ambitions were curbed by an invasion of Wales led by Henry III in league with a number of the captive Gruffudd\'s supporters. In August 1241, Dafydd capitulated and signed the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Gwerneigron" title="Treaty of Gwerneigron">Treaty of Gwerneigron</a>, further restricting his powers. By 1244, however, Gruffudd was dead, and Dafydd seems to have benefited from the backing of many of his brother\'s erstwhile supporters. He was acknowledged by the Pope as <a href="/wiki/Prince_of_Wales" title="Prince of Wales">Prince of Wales</a> for a time, and defeated Henry III in battle in 1245 during the English king\'s second invasion of Wales. A truce was agreed in the autumn, and Henry withdrew; but Dafydd died unexpectedly in 1246 without issue. His wife, <a href="/wiki/Isabella_de_Braose" title="Isabella de Braose">Isabella de Braose</a>, returned to England; she was dead by 1248.\n</p><p>Dafydd married Isabella de Braose in 1231. Their marriage produced no children, and there is no contemporary evidence that Dafydd sired any heirs. According to late genealogical sources collected by Bartrum (1973), Dafydd had two children by an unknown woman (or women), a daughter, Annes, and a son, Llywelyn ap Dafydd, who apparently later became Constable of Rhuddlan and was succeeded in that post by his son Cynwrig ap Llywelyn.\n</p>\n<h4><span id="Owain_Goch_ap_Gruffydd_1246.E2.80.9353_.28d._1282.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Owain_Goch_ap_Gruffydd_1246\xe2\x80\x9353_(d._1282)">Owain Goch ap Gruffydd 1246\xe2\x80\x9353 (d. 1282)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Principality_of_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Owain Goch ap Gruffydd 1246\xe2\x80\x9353 (d. 1282)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Owain_Goch_ap_Gruffydd" title="Owain Goch ap Gruffydd">Owain Goch ap Gruffydd</a></div>\n<p>Following Dafydd\'s death, Gwynedd was divided between Owain Goch and his younger brother Llywelyn. This situation lasted until 1252 when their younger brother <a href="/wiki/Dafydd_ap_Gruffudd" class="mw-redirect" title="Dafydd ap Gruffudd">Dafydd ap Gruffudd</a> reached his majority. Disagreement about how to further divide the realm led to conflict in 1253 in which Llywelyn was victorious. Owain spent the remainder of his days a prisoner of his brother.\n</p>\n<h4><span id="Llywelyn_ap_Gruffudd_1246.E2.80.9382"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Llywelyn_ap_Gruffudd_1246\xe2\x80\x9382">Llywelyn ap Gruffudd 1246\xe2\x80\x9382</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Principality_of_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Llywelyn ap Gruffudd 1246\xe2\x80\x9382">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Llywelyn_ap_Gruffudd" title="Llywelyn ap Gruffudd">Llywelyn ap Gruffudd</a></div>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Conquest_of_Wales_by_Edward_I" class="mw-redirect" title="Conquest of Wales by Edward I">Conquest of Wales by Edward I</a></div>\n<p>After achieving victory over his brothers, Llywelyn went on to reconquer the areas of Gwynedd occupied by England (the Perfeddwlad and others). His alliance with <a href="/wiki/Simon_de_Montfort,_6th_Earl_of_Leicester" title="Simon de Montfort, 6th Earl of Leicester">Simon de Montfort, 6th Earl of Leicester</a>, in 1265 against King <a href="/wiki/Henry_III_of_England" title="Henry III of England">Henry III of England</a> allowed him to reconquer large areas of mid Wales from the English <a href="/wiki/Marcher_Lord" title="Marcher Lord">Marcher Lords</a>. At the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Montgomery" title="Treaty of Montgomery">Treaty of Montgomery</a> between England and Wales in 1267 Llywelyn was granted the title "Prince of Wales" for his heirs and successors and allowed to keep the lands he had conquered as well as the homage of lesser Welsh princes in return for his own homage to the King of England and payment of a substantial fee. Disputes between him, his brother Dafydd and English lords bordering his own led to renewed conflict with England (now ruled by <a href="/wiki/Edward_I_of_England" title="Edward I of England">Edward I</a>) in 1277. Following the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Aberconwy" title="Treaty of Aberconwy">Treaty of Aberconwy</a> Llywelyn was confined to Gwynedd-uwch-Conwy. He joined a revolt instigated by his brother Dafydd in 1282 in which he died in battle.\n</p>\n<h4><span id="Dafydd_ap_Gruffudd_1282.E2.80.9383"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Dafydd_ap_Gruffudd_1282\xe2\x80\x9383">Dafydd ap Gruffudd 1282\xe2\x80\x9383</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Principality_of_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Dafydd ap Gruffudd 1282\xe2\x80\x9383">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Dafydd_ap_Gruffudd" class="mw-redirect" title="Dafydd ap Gruffudd">Dafydd ap Gruffudd</a></div>\n<p>Dafydd assumed his elder brother\'s title in 1282 and led a brief period of continued resistance against England. He was captured and executed in 1283.\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Government.2C_administration_and_law"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Government,_administration_and_law">Government, administration and law</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Principality_of_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Government, administration and law">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Laws_of_Hywel_Dda_(f.4.r)_Judge_cropped.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Laws_of_Hywel_Dda_%28f.4.r%29_Judge_cropped.jpg/200px-Laws_of_Hywel_Dda_%28f.4.r%29_Judge_cropped.jpg" width="200" height="198" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/cf/Laws_of_Hywel_Dda_%28f.4.r%29_Judge_cropped.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="250" data-file-height="248" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Laws_of_Hywel_Dda_(f.4.r)_Judge_cropped.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Drawing of a Welsh judge from the Peniarth 28 manuscript</div></div></div>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Welsh_law" title="Welsh law">Welsh law</a></div>\n<p>The political maturation of the principality\'s government fostered a more defined relationship between prince and the people. Emphasis was placed on the territorial integrity of the principality, with the prince as lord of all the land, and other Welsh lords swearing fealty to the prince directly, a distinction with which the Prince of Wales paid yearly tribute to the King of England.<sup id="cite_ref-Welsh_Treasury_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Welsh_Treasury-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>  By treaty the principality was obliged to pay the kingdom large annual sums.<sup id="cite_ref-Welsh_Treasury_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Welsh_Treasury-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Between 1267 and 1272 Wales made a total payment of \xc2\xa311,500, "proof of a growing money economy... and testimony of the effectiveness of the principality\'s financial administration," wrote historian Dr. John Davies.<sup id="cite_ref-Welsh_Treasury_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Welsh_Treasury-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>  Additionally, modifications and amendments to the Law Codes of Hywel Dda encouraged the declined of the galanas (blood-fine) and the use of the jury system.  The Aberffraw dynasty maintained vigorous <a href="/wiki/Diplomacy" title="Diplomacy">diplomatic</a> and domestic policies; and patronized the Church in Wales, particularly that of the <a href="/wiki/Cistercian_Order" class="mw-redirect" title="Cistercian Order">Cistercian Order</a>.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="The_princely_court">The princely court</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Principality_of_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: The princely court">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>At the end of the twelfth century, beginning of the thirteenth century, <a href="/wiki/Llywelyn_ab_Iorwerth" class="mw-redirect" title="Llywelyn ab Iorwerth">Llywelyn ab Iorwerth</a> (Llywelyn Fawr or Llywelyn the Great), built a royal home at <a href="/wiki/Abergwyngregyn" title="Abergwyngregyn">Abergwyngregyn</a>, known as <i>T\xc5\xb7 Hir</i>, the Long House, in later documents. To the east was the newly endowed Cistercian Monastery of <a href="/wiki/District_of_Aberconwy" title="District of Aberconwy">Aberconwy</a>; to the west the cathedral city of <a href="/wiki/Bangor,_Gwynedd" title="Bangor, Gwynedd">Bangor</a>. In 1211, King <a href="/wiki/John_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="John of England">John of England</a> brought an army across the river Conwy, and occupied the royal home for a brief period; his troops went on to burn Bangor. Llywelyn\'s wife, John\'s daughter <a href="/wiki/Joan,_Lady_of_Wales" title="Joan, Lady of Wales">Joan</a>, also known as Joanna, negotiated between the two men, and John withdrew. Joan died at Abergwyngregyn in 1237; Dafydd ap Llywelyn died there in 1246; Eleanor de Montfort, Lady of Wales, wife of <a href="/wiki/Llywelyn_ap_Gruffudd" title="Llywelyn ap Gruffudd">Llywelyn ap Gruffudd</a>, died there on 19 June 1282, giving birth to a baby, <a href="/wiki/Gwenllian_of_Wales" title="Gwenllian of Wales">Gwenllian of Wales</a>\n</p>\n<h3><span id="Population.2C_culture_and_society"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Population,_culture_and_society">Population, culture and society</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Principality_of_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Population, culture and society">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The 13th-century Principality of Wales encompassed three-quarters of the surface area of modern Wales; "from <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Anglesey" class="mw-redirect" title="Isle of Anglesey">Anglesey</a> to <a href="/wiki/Machen" title="Machen">Machen</a>, from the outskirts of <a href="/wiki/Chester" title="Chester">Chester</a> to the outskirts of <a href="/wiki/Cydweli" class="mw-redirect" title="Cydweli">Cydweli</a>," wrote Davies.<sup id="cite_ref-stability_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stability-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-stable_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stable-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> By 1271, Prince Llywelyn II could claim a growing population of about 200,000 people, or a little less than three-quarters of the total Welsh population.<sup id="cite_ref-Emerging_Principality_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Emerging_Principality-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-stability_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stability-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>  The population increase was common throughout Europe in the 13th century, but in Wales it was more pronounced.<sup id="cite_ref-stability_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stability-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> By Llywelyn II\'s reign as much as 10 percent of the population were town-dwellers.<sup id="cite_ref-stability_15-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stability-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Additionally, "unfree slaves... had long disappeared" from within the territory of the principality, wrote Davies.<sup id="cite_ref-stability_15-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stability-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The increase in men allowed the prince to call on and field a far more substantial army.<sup id="cite_ref-stability_15-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stability-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Laws_of_Hywel_Dda_(f.4.r)_Hawker_cropped.jpg" height="197" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/f2/Laws_of_Hywel_Dda_%28f.4.r%29_Hawker_cropped.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="237" data-file-height="233" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Laws_of_Hywel_Dda_(f.4.r)_Hawker_cropped.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Drawing of a <a href="/wiki/Falconry" title="Falconry">falconer</a> from Peniarth 28 manuscript. Wales exported hawks.</div></div></div>\n<p>A more stable social and political environment provided by the Aberffraw administration allowed for the natural development of Welsh culture, particularly in literature, law, and religion.<sup id="cite_ref-stable_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stable-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-GruffyddapCynan_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GruffyddapCynan-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Tradition originating from <i>The History of Gruffydd ap Cynan</i> attributes Gruffydd I as reforming the orders of <a href="/wiki/Bard" title="Bard">bards</a> and <a href="/wiki/Musician" title="Musician">musicians</a>;<sup id="cite_ref-The_Aberffraw_dynasty_5-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Aberffraw_dynasty-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Medieval_Welsh_literature" title="Medieval Welsh literature">Welsh literature</a> demonstrated "vigor and a sense of commitment" as new ideas reached Wales, even in "the wake of the invaders", according to historian John Davies.<sup id="cite_ref-The_Aberffraw_dynasty_5-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Aberffraw_dynasty-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>  Contacts with continental Europe "sharpened Welsh pride", wrote Davies in his <i>History of Wales</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-The_Aberffraw_dynasty_5-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Aberffraw_dynasty-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy_and_trade">Economy and trade</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Principality_of_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Economy and trade">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The increase in the Welsh population, especially in the lands of the principality, allowed for a greater diversification of the economy. The Meirionnydd tax rolls give evidence to the thirty-seven various professions present in Meirionnydd directly before the conquest.  Of these professions, there were eight <a href="/wiki/Goldsmith" title="Goldsmith">goldsmiths</a>, four bards (poets) by trade, 26 <a href="/wiki/Shoemaker" class="mw-redirect" title="Shoemaker">shoemakers</a>, a doctor in <a href="/wiki/Cynwyd,_Denbighshire" title="Cynwyd, Denbighshire">Cynwyd</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Hotel" title="Hotel">hotel</a> keeper in <a href="/wiki/Maentwrog" title="Maentwrog">Maentwrog</a>, and 28 priests; two of whom were university graduates. Also present were a significant number of <a href="/wiki/Fisherman" title="Fisherman">fishermen</a>, administrators, professional men and craftsmen.\n</p><p>With the average temperature of Wales a degree or two higher than it is today, more Welsh lands were <a href="/wiki/Arable_land" title="Arable land">arable</a> for agriculture, "a crucial bonus for a country like Wales," wrote the historian John Davies.<sup id="cite_ref-economy_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-economy-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>  Of significant importance for the principality included more developed trade routes, which allowed for the introduction of new energy sources such as the <a href="/wiki/Windmill" title="Windmill">windmill</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Fulling" title="Fulling">fulling mill</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Horse_collar" title="Horse collar">horse collar</a> (which doubled the efficiency of horse-power).\n</p><p>The principality traded <a href="/wiki/Cattle" title="Cattle">cattle</a>, skins, <a href="/wiki/Cheese" title="Cheese">cheese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lumber" title="Lumber">timber</a>, horses, <a href="/wiki/Wax" title="Wax">wax</a>, dogs, hawks, and fleeces, but also <a href="/wiki/Flannel" title="Flannel">flannel</a> (with the growth of fulling mills). Flannel was second only to cattle among the principality\'s exports. In exchange, the principality imported salt, wine, wheat, and other luxuries from <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a>. But most importantly for the defence of the principality, <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> and specialised <a href="/wiki/Weapon" title="Weapon">weaponry</a> were also imported.  Welsh dependence on foreign imports was a tool that England used to wear down the principality during times of conflict between the two countries.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="1284_to_1542:_annexed_to_the_English_crown">1284 to 1542: annexed to the English crown</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Principality_of_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: 1284 to 1542: annexed to the English crown">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Wales_in_the_Late_Middle_Ages" title="Wales in the Late Middle Ages">Wales in the Late Middle Ages</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:332px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Wales_14C_Map.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Wales_14C_Map.png/330px-Wales_14C_Map.png" width="330" height="350" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Wales_14C_Map.png/495px-Wales_14C_Map.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Wales_14C_Map.png/660px-Wales_14C_Map.png 2x" data-file-width="3399" data-file-height="3610" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Wales_14C_Map.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Wales in the 14th century showing the Principality</div></div></div>\n<p>The governance and constitutional position of the principality after its conquest was set out in the <a href="/wiki/Statute_of_Rhuddlan" title="Statute of Rhuddlan">Statute of Rhuddlan</a> of 1284. In the words of the Statute, the principality was "annexed and united" to the English crown,<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> It was the king\'s personal fief. In 1301, this modified principality was bestowed on the English monarch\'s heir apparent and thereafter became the territorial endowment of the heir to the throne.<sup id="cite_ref-Oxford_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Oxford-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> The rest of Wales continued to be constituted as the  "<a href="/wiki/Welsh_Marches" title="Welsh Marches">March of Wales</a>" which remained outside of the Principality under the rule of Anglo-Norman <a href="/wiki/Marcher_Lord" title="Marcher Lord">Marcher Lords</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Governance">Governance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Principality_of_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Governance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The Principality\'s administration was overseen by the Prince of Wales\'s Council comprising between 8 and 15 councillors sitting in London or, later, <a href="/wiki/Ludlow" title="Ludlow">Ludlow</a> in <a href="/wiki/Shropshire" title="Shropshire">Shropshire</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Jones_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jones-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> The Council acted as the Principality\'s final Court of Appeal.<sup id="cite_ref-Jones_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jones-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> By 1476, the Council, which became known as the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Wales_and_the_Marches" title="Council of Wales and the Marches">Council of Wales and the Marches</a>, began taking responsibility not only for the Principality itself but its authority was extended over the whole of Wales.<sup id="cite_ref-Oxford_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Oxford-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The territory of the principality fell into two distinct areas: the lands under direct royal control and lands which Edward I had distributed by feudal grants.<sup id="cite_ref-Prestwich_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Prestwich-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>For lands under royal control, the administration, under the Statute of Rhuddlan, was divided into the two territories: North Wales based at Caernarfon and West Wales based at Camarthen.<sup id="cite_ref-Oxford_20-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Oxford-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> The Statute organized the Principality into <a href="/wiki/Shire" title="Shire">shire counties</a>. <a href="/wiki/Carmarthenshire" title="Carmarthenshire">Carmarthenshire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cardiganshire" class="mw-redirect" title="Cardiganshire">Cardiganshire</a> were administered by the <a href="/wiki/Justiciar_of_South_Wales" title="Justiciar of South Wales">Justiciar of South Wales</a> (or "of West Wales") at Carmarthen. In the North, the counties of <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Anglesey" class="mw-redirect" title="Isle of Anglesey">Anglesey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Merionethshire" title="Merionethshire">Merionethshire</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Caernarfonshire" title="Caernarfonshire">Caernarfonshire</a> were created under the control of <a href="/wiki/Justiciar_of_North_Wales" title="Justiciar of North Wales">Justiciar of North Wales</a> and a provincial exchequer at Caernarfon, run by the <a href="/wiki/Chamberlain_of_North_Wales" title="Chamberlain of North Wales">Chamberlain of North Wales</a>, who accounted for the revenues he collected to the <a href="/wiki/Exchequer" title="Exchequer">Exchequer</a> at <a href="/wiki/Westminster" title="Westminster">Westminster</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Jones1990_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jones1990-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>  Under them were royal officials such as <a href="/wiki/Sheriff" title="Sheriff">sheriffs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coroner" title="Coroner">coroners</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bailiff" title="Bailiff">bailiffs</a> to collect taxes and administer justice.<sup id="cite_ref-BlakeleyCollins1993_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BlakeleyCollins1993-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Another county, <a href="/wiki/Flintshire_(historic)" title="Flintshire (historic)">Flintshire</a>, was created out of the lordships of <a href="/wiki/Tegeingl" title="Tegeingl">Tegeingl</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hope,_Flintshire" title="Hope, Flintshire">Hopedale</a> and <a href="/wiki/Maelor_Saesneg" class="mw-redirect" title="Maelor Saesneg">Maelor Saesneg</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Jones1990_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jones1990-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> and was administered with the <a href="/wiki/County_palatine" title="County palatine">Palatinate</a> of <a href="/wiki/Cheshire" title="Cheshire">Cheshire</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Justice_of_Chester" title="Justice of Chester">Justiciar of Chester</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Edward_I_%26_II_Prince_of_Wales_1301.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Edward_I_%26_II_Prince_of_Wales_1301.jpg/150px-Edward_I_%26_II_Prince_of_Wales_1301.jpg" width="150" height="273" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/ab/Edward_I_%26_II_Prince_of_Wales_1301.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="165" data-file-height="300" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Edward_I_%26_II_Prince_of_Wales_1301.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Edward_I_of_England" title="Edward I of England">Edward I</a> creating his son <a href="/wiki/Edward_of_Caernarfon" class="mw-redirect" title="Edward of Caernarfon">Edward of Caernarfon</a> Prince of Wales in 1301 (early 14th-century manuscript)</div></div></div>\n<p>The remainder of the principality comprised lands which Edward I had granted to supporters shortly after the completion of the conquest in 1284, and which, in practice, became <a href="/wiki/Marcher_lordship" class="mw-redirect" title="Marcher lordship">Marcher lordships</a>: for example, the <a href="/wiki/Lordship_of_Denbigh" title="Lordship of Denbigh">lordship of Denbigh</a> granted to the <a href="/wiki/Henry_de_Lacy,_3rd_Earl_of_Lincoln" title="Henry de Lacy, 3rd Earl of Lincoln">Earl of Lincoln</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Powys_Wenwynwyn#The_Lordship_of_Powys" title="Powys Wenwynwyn">lordship of Powys</a> granted to Owain ap Gruffydd ap Gwenwynwyn, who became <a href="/wiki/Owen_de_la_Pole" title="Owen de la Pole">Owen de la Pole</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Prestwich_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Prestwich-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> These lands after 1301 were held as <a href="/wiki/Tenants-in-chief" class="mw-redirect" title="Tenants-in-chief">tenants-in-chief</a> of the Principality of Wales, rather than from the Crown directly,<sup id="cite_ref-Prestwich_22-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Prestwich-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> but were, for all practical purposes, not part of the principality.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Law">Law</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Principality_of_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Law">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The Statute of Rhuddlan introduced <a href="/wiki/English_common_law" class="mw-redirect" title="English common law">English common law</a> to the principality, albeit with some local variation.<sup id="cite_ref-Barnett2004_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Barnett2004-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> Criminal law became entirely based on common law: the Statute stated that "in thefts, larcenies, burnings, murders, manslaughters and manifest and notorious robberies \xe2\x80\x94 we will that they shall use the laws of England".<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> However, <a href="/wiki/Welsh_law" title="Welsh law">Welsh law</a> continued to be used in civil cases such as land inheritance, contracts, sureties and similar matters, though with changes, for example illegitimate sons could no longer claim part of the inheritance, which Welsh law had allowed them to do.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Plantagenet_and_Tudor_Princes">Plantagenet and Tudor Princes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Principality_of_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Plantagenet and Tudor Princes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>From 1301, the <a href="/wiki/Plantagenet" class="mw-redirect" title="Plantagenet">Plantagenet</a> (and later, <a href="/wiki/House_of_Tudor" title="House of Tudor">Tudor</a>) English kings gave their heir apparent, if he was the king\'s son or grandson, the lands and title of "Prince of Wales". The one exception was Edward II\'s son, <a href="/wiki/Edward_III" class="mw-redirect" title="Edward III">Edward of Windsor</a>, who later became Edward III.<sup id="cite_ref-POW_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-POW-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> Upon the heir\'s accession to the throne, the lands and title merged in the Crown.\n</p><p>The first "English" Prince of Wales was Edward I\'s son, <a href="/wiki/Edward_of_Caernarfon" class="mw-redirect" title="Edward of Caernarfon">Edward of Caernarfon</a>. A late 16th-century story claimed that Edward I gave him the title following his declaration to the Welsh that there would be a Prince of Wales "that was borne in Wales and could speake never a word of English": Edward was born at <a href="/wiki/Caernarfon_Castle" title="Caernarfon Castle">Caernarfon Castle</a> and, in common with rest of the English ruling elite, spoke French.<sup id="cite_ref-SP_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SP-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> However, there seems to be no basis for the story.<sup id="cite_ref-SP_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SP-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> On 7 February 1301, the king granted to Edward all the lands under royal control in Wales, mainly the territory of the former Principality.<sup id="cite_ref-Phillips85_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Phillips85-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> Although the documents granting the land made no reference to the title "Prince of Wales", it seems likely that Edward was invested with it at the same time, since, within a month of the grant, he was referred to as the "Prince of Wales" in official documents.<sup id="cite_ref-Phillips85_32-1" href="/w/index.php?title=Principality_of_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Welsh claimants to the title">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Two rebellions occurred during the period in support of Welsh claimants to the title of Prince of Wales.\n</p>\n<h4><span id="Owain_Lawgoch_1372.E2.80.9378"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Owain_Lawgoch_1372\xe2\x80\x9378">Owain Lawgoch 1372\xe2\x80\x9378</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Principality_of_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Owain Lawgoch 1372\xe2\x80\x9378">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Owain_Lawgoch" title="Owain Lawgoch">Owain Lawgoch</a></div>\n<p>Owain was the great-nephew of Llywelyn ap Gruffudd and Dafydd ap Gruffudd. He claimed the title in exile in <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> and loyalists revolted in his name across Wales. He was assassinated before being able to return to Wales to lead them.\n</p>\n<h4><span id="Owain_Glynd.C5.B5r_1400.E2.80.93c._1415"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Owain_Glynd\xc5\xb5r_1400\xe2\x80\x93c._1415">Owain Glynd\xc5\xb5r 1400\xe2\x80\x93c. 1415</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Principality_of_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Owain Glynd\xc5\xb5r 1400\xe2\x80\x93c. 1415">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Owain_Glynd%C5%B5r" title="Owain Glynd\xc5\xb5r">Owain Glynd\xc5\xb5r</a></div>\n<p>Glynd\xc5\xb5r was crowned at <a href="/wiki/Machynlleth" title="Machynlleth">Machynlleth</a> in 1404 during a <a href="/wiki/Glynd%C5%B5r_Rising" title="Glynd\xc5\xb5r Rising">revolt</a> against <a href="/wiki/Henry_IV_of_England" title="Henry IV of England">Henry IV of England</a>. He claimed descent from <a href="/wiki/Rhodri_Mawr" class="mw-redirect" title="Rhodri Mawr">Rhodri Mawr</a> through the House of <a href="/wiki/Powys_Fadog" title="Powys Fadog">Powys Fadog</a>. He went on to establish diplomatic relations with foreign powers and liberated Wales from English rule. He was ultimately unsuccessful and was driven to the mountains where he led a <a href="/wiki/Guerrilla_war" class="mw-redirect" title="Guerrilla war">guerrilla war</a>. When and where he died is not known, but it is believed he died disguised as a <a href="/wiki/Friar" title="Friar">friar</a> in the company of his daughter, Alys, at Monnington Straddle in <a href="/wiki/Herefordshire" title="Herefordshire">Herefordshire</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="After_1542:_union_with_England">After 1542: union with England</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Principality_of_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: After 1542: union with England">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The Principality of Wales came to an end as a legally defined territory with the Laws in Wales Acts 1535\xe2\x80\x931542.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_administration">Later administration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Principality_of_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Later administration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/History_of_Wales" title="History of Wales">History of Wales</a></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Encyclopaedia_of_Wales" title="Encyclopaedia of Wales">Encyclopaedia of Wales</a> notes that the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Wales_and_the_Marches" title="Council of Wales and the Marches">Council of Wales and the Marches</a> was created by <a href="/wiki/Edward_IV" class="mw-redirect" title="Edward IV">Edward IV</a> in 1471 as a household institution to manage the Prince of Wales\'s lands and finances. In 1473 it was enlarged and given the additional duty of maintaining law and order in the Principality and the Marches of Wales. Its meetings appear to have been intermittent, but it was revived by <a href="/wiki/Henry_VII_of_England" title="Henry VII of England">Henry VII</a> for his heir, <a href="/wiki/Arthur,_Prince_of_Wales" title="Arthur, Prince of Wales">Prince Arthur</a>. The Council was placed on a statutory basis in 1543 and played a central role in co-ordinating law and administration. It declined in the early 17th century and was abolished by Parliament in 1641. It was revived at the <a href="/wiki/Restoration_(England)" title="Restoration (England)">Restoration</a> before being finally abolished in 1689.\n</p><p>From 1689 to 1948 there was no differentiation between the government of England and government in Wales. All laws relating to England included Wales and Wales was considered by the British Government as an indivisible part of England within the United Kingdom. The first piece of legislation to relate specifically to Wales was the <a href="/wiki/Sunday_Closing_(Wales)_Act_1881" title="Sunday Closing (Wales) Act 1881">Sunday Closing (Wales) Act 1881</a>.  A further exception was the <a href="/wiki/Welsh_Church_Act_1914" title="Welsh Church Act 1914">Welsh Church Act 1914</a>, which disestablished the <a href="/wiki/Church_in_Wales" title="Church in Wales">Church in Wales</a> (which had formerly been part of the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">Church of England</a>) in 1920.\n</p><p>In 1948 the practice was established that all laws passed in the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Parliament of the United Kingdom">Parliament of the United Kingdom</a> were designated as applicable to either "<a href="/wiki/England_and_Wales" title="England and Wales">England and Wales</a>" or "<a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>", thus returning a legal identity to Wales which had not existed for hundreds of years following the <a href="/wiki/Acts_of_Union_1707" title="Acts of Union 1707">Act of Union</a> with Scotland in 1707. Also in 1948 a new <a href="/wiki/Council_for_Wales_and_Monmouthshire" title="Council for Wales and Monmouthshire">Council for Wales</a> was established as a parliamentary committee. In 1964 the <a href="/wiki/Welsh_Office" title="Welsh Office">Welsh Office</a> was established, based in London, to oversee and recommend improvements to the application of laws in Wales. This situation would continue until the devolution of government in Wales and the establishment of the autonomous <a href="/wiki/National_Assembly_for_Wales" title="National Assembly for Wales">National Assembly for Wales</a> in 1998.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_uses_of_the_term">Other uses of the term</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Principality_of_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Other uses of the term">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Although no principality has ever been created that covers Wales as a whole, the term "Principality" has been occasionally used since the sixteenth century as a synonym for Wales. For instance, the first <a href="/wiki/Atlas" title="Atlas">atlas</a> of Wales, by Thomas Taylor in 1718, was titled <i>The Principality of Wales exactly described ...</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> and the term is still used by such publications as <i><a href="/wiki/Burke%27s_Landed_Gentry" title="Burke&#39;s Landed Gentry">Burke\'s Landed Gentry</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>  However, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i> style guide advises writers to "avoid the word \'principality<span style="padding-right:.2em;">\'</span>" in relation to Wales.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/International_Organisation_for_Standardisation" class="mw-redirect" title="International Organisation for Standardisation">International Organisation for Standardisation</a> (ISO) has defined Wales as a "country" rather than a "principality" since 2011, following a recommendation by the <a href="/wiki/British_Standards_Institute" class="mw-redirect" title="British Standards Institute">British Standards Institute</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Welsh_Government" title="Welsh Government">Welsh Government</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The use of the term to refer to the territory of Wales should be distinguished from its use to refer to the title of <a href="/wiki/Prince_of_Wales" title="Prince of Wales">Prince of Wales</a>, which has been traditionally granted (together with the title <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Cornwall" title="Duke of Cornwall">Duke of Cornwall</a> and various Scottish titles) to the <a href="/wiki/Heir_apparent" title="Heir apparent">heir apparent</a> of the reigning monarch. It confers no responsibility for government in Wales,<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> and has no constitutional meaning. <a href="/wiki/Plaid_Cymru" title="Plaid Cymru">Plaid Cymru</a> are in favour of scrapping the title altogether.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Honours_of_the_Principality_of_Wales" title="Honours of the Principality of Wales">Honours of the Principality of Wales</a> are the <a href="/wiki/Crown_Jewels" class="mw-redirect" title="Crown Jewels">Crown Jewels</a> used at the investiture of Princes of Wales.<sup id="cite_ref-Jewels_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jewels-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sources">Sources</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Principality_of_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Sources">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/BBC_Wales" class="mw-redirect" title="BBC Wales">BBC Wales</a>: History, <i><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.bbc.co.uk/wales/history/sites/nation/pages/principality02.shtml">The emergence of the principality of Wales</a></i>; extracted 26 March 2008</li>\n<li><cite class="citation book"><a href="/wiki/John_Davies_(historian)" title="John Davies (historian)">Davies, John</a> (1994). <i>A History of Wales</i>.
