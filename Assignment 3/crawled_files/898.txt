class="external text" href="https://www.census.gov/">www<wbr />.census<wbr />.gov</a></span></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>The <b>United States Census Bureau</b> (<b>USCB</b>; officially the <b>Bureau of the Census</b>, as defined in Title <span class="plainlinksneverexpand"><a href="/wiki/Title_13_of_the_United_States_Code" title="Title 13 of the United States Code">13 U.S.C.</a>&#160;<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/13/1.html">\xc2\xa7&#160;11</a></span>) is a principal agency of the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Statistical_System_of_the_United_States" title="Federal Statistical System of the United States">U.S. Federal Statistical System</a>, responsible for producing data about the <a href="/wiki/Americans" title="Americans">American</a> <a href="/wiki/Demographics" class="mw-redirect" title="Demographics">people</a> and <a href="/wiki/Economy" title="Economy">economy</a>. The Census Bureau is part of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Commerce" title="United States Department of Commerce">U.S. Department of Commerce</a> and its <a href="/wiki/Director_of_the_United_States_Census_Bureau" title="Director of the United States Census Bureau">director</a> is appointed by the <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">President of the United States</a>.\n</p><p>The Census Bureau\'s primary mission is conducting the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Census" title="United States Census">U.S. Census</a> every ten years, which allocates the seats of the <a href="/wiki/U.S._House_of_Representatives" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. House of Representatives">U.S. House of Representatives</a> to the <a href="/wiki/U.S._state" title="U.S. state">states</a> based on their population.<sup id="cite_ref-USCB_DOC-D1026_QVC_Manual_01/03/09_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-USCB_DOC-D1026_QVC_Manual_01/03/09-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The Bureau\'s various censuses and surveys help allocate over $400 billion in federal funds every year and it helps <a href="/wiki/U.S._state" title="U.S. state">states</a>, local communities, and businesses make informed decisions.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The information provided by the census informs decisions on where to build and maintain schools, hospitals, transportation infrastructure, and police and fire departments.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In addition to the decennial census, the Census Bureau continually conducts dozens of other <a href="/wiki/Census" title="Census">censuses</a> and <a href="/wiki/Survey_methodology" title="Survey methodology">surveys</a>, including the <a href="/wiki/American_Community_Survey" title="American Community Survey">American Community Survey</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Economic_Census" title="United States Economic Census">U.S. Economic Census</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Current_Population_Survey" title="Current Population Survey">Current Population Survey</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-USCB_DOC-D1026_QVC_Manual_01/03/09_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-USCB_DOC-D1026_QVC_Manual_01/03/09-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Furthermore, economic and foreign trade indicators released by the federal government typically contain data produced by the Census Bureau.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" id="Legal_mandate">Legal mandate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Census_Bureau&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Legal mandate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Census_Bureau_headquarters,_Suitland,_Maryland,_2007.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/Census_Bureau_headquarters%2C_Suitland%2C_Maryland%2C_2007.jpg/220px-Census_Bureau_headquarters%2C_Suitland%2C_Maryland%2C_2007.jpg" width="220" height="121" class="thumbimage" //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/Census_Bureau_headquarters%2C_Suitland%2C_Maryland%2C_2007.jpg/440px-Census_Bureau_headquarters%2C_Suitland%2C_Maryland%2C_2007.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3264" data-file-height="1800" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Census_Bureau_headquarters,_Suitland,_Maryland,_2007.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Census headquarters in <a href="/wiki/Suitland,_Maryland" title="Suitland, Maryland">Suitland, Maryland</a></div></div></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Article_One_of_the_United_States_Constitution" title="Article One of the United States Constitution">Article One of the United States Constitution</a> (section II) directs the <a href="/wiki/Population" title="Population">population</a> be enumerated at least once every ten years and the resulting counts used to <a href="/wiki/United_States_congressional_apportionment" title="United States congressional apportionment">set the number of members from each state</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_House_of_Representatives" title="United States House of Representatives">House of Representatives</a> and, by extension, in the <a href="/wiki/Electoral_College_(United_States)" class="mw-redirect" title="Electoral College (United States)">Electoral College</a>. The Census Bureau now conducts a full <a href="/wiki/Census" title="Census">population count</a> every 10 years in years ending with a zero and uses the term "<a href="/wiki/Decennial" class="mw-redirect" title="Decennial">decennial</a>" to describe the operation. Between censuses, the Census Bureau makes population estimates and projections.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In addition, Census data directly affects how more than $400 billion per year in federal and state funding is allocated to communities for neighborhood improvements, <a href="/wiki/Public_health" title="Public health">public health</a>, education, transportation and more.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The Census Bureau is mandated with fulfilling these obligations: the collecting of statistics about the nation, its people, and economy. The Census Bureau\'s <a href="/wiki/Rational-legal_authority" title="Rational-legal authority">legal authority</a> is codified in <a href="/wiki/Title_13_of_the_United_States_Code" title="Title 13 of the United States Code">Title 13 of the United States Code</a>.\n</p><p>The Census Bureau also conducts surveys on behalf of various <a href="/wiki/Federal_government_of_the_United_States" title="Federal government of the United States">federal government</a> and <a href="/wiki/Local_government" title="Local government">local government</a> agencies on topics such as employment, crime, health, <a href="/wiki/Consumer_expenditures" class="mw-redirect" title="Consumer expenditures">consumer expenditures</a>, and housing. Within the bureau, these are known as "demographic surveys" and are conducted perpetually between and during decennial (10-year) population counts. The Census Bureau also conducts economic surveys of manufacturing, retail, service, and other establishments and of domestic governments.\n</p><p>Between 1790 and 1840, the census was taken by <a href="/wiki/United_States_Marshals_Service" title="United States Marshals Service">marshals</a> of the judicial districts.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The Census Act of 1840 established a central office<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> which became known as the Census Office. Several acts followed that revised and authorized new censuses, typically at the 10-year intervals. In 1902, the temporary Census Office was moved under the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_the_Interior" title="United States Department of the Interior">Department of Interior</a>, and in 1903 it was renamed the Census Bureau under the new <a href="/wiki/Department_of_Commerce_and_Labor" class="mw-redirect" title="Department of Commerce and Labor">Department of Commerce and Labor</a>. The department was intended to consolidate overlapping statistical agencies, but Census Bureau officials were hindered by their subordinate role in the department.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>An act in 1920 changed the date and authorized manufacturing censuses every two years and agriculture censuses every 10 years.<sup id="cite_ref-History1920_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-History1920-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> In 1929, a bill was passed mandating the House of Representatives be reapportioned based on the results of the <a href="/wiki/1930_United_States_Census" title="1930 United States Census">1930 Census</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-History1920_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-History1920-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> In 1954, various acts were codified into Title 13 of the US Code.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>By law, the Census Bureau must count everyone and submit state population totals to the U.S. President by December 31 of any year ending in a zero. id="Data_stewardship">Data stewardship</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Census_Bureau&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Data stewardship">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The United States Census Bureau is committed to confidentiality, and guarantees non-disclosure of any addresses or personal information related to individuals or establishments. <a href="/wiki/Title_13_of_the_United_States_Code" title="Title 13 of the United States Code">Title 13</a> of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Code" title="United States Code">U.S. Code</a> establishes penalties for the disclosure of this information. All Census employees must sign an <a href="/wiki/Affidavit" title="Affidavit">affidavit</a> of non-disclosure prior to employment.\n</p><p>The Bureau cannot share responses, addresses or personal information with anyone including United States or foreign government and law enforcement agencies such as the <a href="/wiki/IRS" class="mw-redirect" title="IRS">IRS</a> or the <a href="/wiki/FBI" class="mw-redirect" title="FBI">FBI</a> or <a href="/wiki/Interpol" title="Interpol">Interpol</a>. "Providing quality data, for public good\xe2\x80\x94while respecting individual privacy and, at the same time, protecting confidentiality\xe2\x80\x94is the Census Bureau\'s core responsibility", "Keeping the public\'s trust is critical to the Census\'s ability to carry out the mission as the leading source of quality data about the Nation\'s people and economy."<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Only after <a href="/wiki/72-year_rule" class="mw-redirect" title="72-year rule">72 years</a> does the information collected become available to other agencies or the general public.<sup id="cite_ref-The_72-Year_Rule_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_72-Year_Rule-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Despite these guarantees of confidentiality, the Census Bureau has some history of disclosures to other government agencies. In 1918, the Census Bureau released individual information regarding several hundred young men to the Justice Department and <a href="/wiki/Selective_Service" class="mw-redirect" title="Selective Service">Selective Service</a> system for the purpose of prosecutions for draft evasion.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> During <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, the United States Census Bureau assisted the government\'s <a href="/wiki/Japanese_American_internment" class="mw-redirect" title="Japanese American internment">Japanese American internment</a> efforts by providing confidential neighborhood information on Japanese-Americans. The Bureau\'s role was denied for decades but was finally proven in 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>United States census data are valuable for the country\'s political parties; <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_(United_States)" title="Democratic Party (United States)">Democrats</a> and <a href="/wiki/Republican_Party_(United_States)" title="Republican Party (United States)">Republicans</a> are highly interested in knowing accurate number of persons in their respective districts.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> These insights are often linked to financial and economic strategies that are central to federal, state and city investments for locations of particular populations.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Such apportionments are designed to distribute political power across neutral spatial allocations; however, "because so much is at stake, the census also runs the risk of being politicized."<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Such political tensions highlight the complexity of <a href="/wiki/Identity_(social_science)" title="Identity (social science)">identity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Classification" title="Classification">classification</a>; some argue that unclear results from the population data "is due to distortions brought about by political pressures."<sup id="cite_ref-Petersen_1987_187\xe2\x80\x93234_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Petersen_1987_187\xe2\x80\x93234-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> One frequently used example includes ambiguous ethnic counts, which often involves underenumeration and/or undercounting of minority populations.<sup id="cite_ref-Petersen_1987_187\xe2\x80\x93234_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Petersen_1987_187\xe2\x80\x93234-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> Ideas about race, ethnicity and identity have also evolved in the United States, and such changes warrant examination of how these shifts have impacted the accuracy of census data over time.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The United States Census Bureau began pursuing technological innovations to improve the precision of its census data collection in the 1980s. Robert W. Marx, the Chief of the Geography Division of the USCB teamed up with the US Geological Survey and oversaw the creation of the <a href="/wiki/Topologically_Integrated_Geographic_Encoding_and_Referencing" title="Topologically Integrated Geographic Encoding and Referencing">Topologically Integrated Geographic Encoding and Referencing</a> (TIGER) database system.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> Census officials were able to evaluate more sophisticated and detailed results that the TIGER system produced; furthermore, TIGER data are also available to the public. And while the TIGER system does not directly amass demographic data, as a <a href="/wiki/Geographic_information_system" title="Geographic information system">geographic information system</a> (GIS), it can be used to merge <a href="/wiki/Demographics" class="mw-redirect" title="Demographics">demographics</a> to conduct more accurate geospatial and mapping analysis.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Census Bureau distributes data it collects via censuses and surveys on its American FactFinder website.<sup census-taking agency of the United States government has been the Bureau of the Census. The Census Bureau is headed by a Director, assisted by a Deputy Director and an Executive Staff composed of the associate directors.\n</p><p>The Census Bureau has had headquarters in <a href="/wiki/Suitland,_Maryland" title="Suitland, Maryland">Suitland, Maryland</a>, since 1942. A new headquarters complex was completed in 2007 and supports over 4,000 employees.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> The Bureau operates regional offices in 12 cities: <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>, <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia">Philadelphia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Detroit" title="Detroit">Detroit</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kansas_City,_Kansas" title="Kansas City, Kansas">Kansas City</a>, <a href="/wiki/Seattle" title="Seattle">Seattle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charlotte" class="mw-redirect" title="Charlotte">Charlotte</a>, <a href="/wiki/Atlanta" title="Atlanta">Atlanta</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dallas" title="Dallas">Dallas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Denver" title="Denver">Denver</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a>. The National Processing Center is in <a href="/wiki/Jeffersonville,_Indiana" title="Jeffersonville, Indiana">Jeffersonville, Indiana</a>. Additional temporary processing facilities facilitate the decennial census, which employs more than a million people. The cost of the 2000 Census was $4.5 billion. During the years just prior to the decennial census, parallel census offices, known as "Regional Census Centers" are opened in the field office cities. The decennial operations are carried out from these facilities. The Regional Census Centers oversee the openings and closings of smaller "Local Census Offices" within their collection jurisdictions. The estimated cost of the 2010 Census is $14.7 billion.\n</p><p>On January 1, 2013, the Census Bureau was to consolidate its 12 regional offices into 6. Increasing costs of data collection, changes in survey management tools such as laptops and the increasing use of multi-modal surveys (i.e. internet, telephone, and in-person) has led the Census Bureau to consolidate.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> The remaining regional offices will be in: <a href="/wiki/32_Old_Slip" title="32 Old Slip">New York City</a>, <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia">Philadelphia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a>, <a href="/wiki/Atlanta" title="Atlanta">Atlanta</a>, <a href="/wiki/Denver" title="Denver">Denver</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Census Bureau also runs the Census Information Center cooperative program that involves 58 "national, regional, and local non-profit organizations". class="mw-headline" id="Handheld_computers_(HHC)">Handheld computers (HHC)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Census_Bureau&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Handheld computers (HHC)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Historically, the census information was gathered by census takers going door-to-door collecting information in a ledger. Beginning in 1970 information was gathered via mailed forms. To reduce paper usage, reduce payroll expense and acquire the most comprehensive list of addresses ever compiled, 500,000 handheld computers (HHCs) (specifically designed, single purpose devices) were used for the first time in 2009 during the address canvassing portion of the 2010 Decennial Census Project. Projected savings were estimated to be over $1 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Security_precautions">Security precautions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Census_Bureau&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Security precautions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Device_fingerprint" title="Device fingerprint">Device fingerprint</a></div>\n<p>The HHC was manufactured by <a href="/wiki/Harris_Corporation" title="Harris Corporation">Harris Corporation</a>, an established <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Defense" title="United States Department of Defense">Department of Defense</a> contractor, via a controversial<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> contract with the <a href="/wiki/Department_of_Commerce" class="mw-redirect" title="Department of Commerce">Department of Commerce</a>. Secured access via a <a href="/wiki/Fingerprint#Livescan_devices" title="Fingerprint">fingerprint swipe</a> guaranteed only the verified user could access the unit. A <a href="/wiki/GPS" class="mw-redirect" title="GPS">GPS</a> capacity was integral to the daily address management and the transfer of gathered information. Of major importance was the security and integrity of the populace\'s private information.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Success_and_failure">Success and failure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Census_Bureau&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Success and failure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Enumerators (information gatherers) that had operational problems with the device understandably made negative reports. During the 2009 Senate confirmation hearings for <a href="/wiki/Robert_Groves" title="Robert Groves">Robert Groves</a>, President Obama\'s Census Director appointee, there was much mention of contracting problems but very little criticism of the units.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_54-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> In rural areas, the sparsity of cell phone towers caused problems with data transmission to and from the HHC. Since the units were updated nightly with important changes and reprogramming, operator implementation of proper procedure was imperative. Dramatic dysfunction and delays occurred if the units were not put into <a href="/wiki/Sleep_mode" title="Sleep mode">sleep mode</a> overnight.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_alumni">Notable alumni</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Census_Bureau&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Notable alumni">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="div-col columns column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 20em; -webkit-column-width: 20em; column-width: 20em;">\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/John_Shaw_Billings" title="John Shaw Billings">John Shaw Billings</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Rattan_Chand" title="Rattan Chand">Rattan Chand</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Jim_Corbett_(politician)" title="Jim Corbett (politician)">James Corbett</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/W._Edwards_Deming" title="W.
