class="tocnumber">8</span> <span class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Atlantic_slave_trade">Atlantic slave trade</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slave_ship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Atlantic slave trade">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Only a few decades after the arrival of <a href="/wiki/European_emigration" title="European emigration">Europeans</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">Americas</a>,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Vagueness" title="Wikipedia:Vagueness"><span title="This information is too vague. (February 2016)">vague</span></a></i>&#93;</sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> demand for unpaid labor to work plantations made slave-trading a profitable business. The peak time of <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_slave_trade" title="Atlantic slave trade">slave ships to the Atlantic passage</a> was between the 18th and early-19th centuries, when large plantations developed in the colonies of America.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>To ensure <a href="/wiki/Profit_(accounting)" title="Profit (accounting)">profitability</a>, the owners of the ships divided their <a href="/wiki/Hull_(watercraft)" title="Hull (watercraft)">hulls</a> into holds with little headroom, so they could transport as many slaves as possible. Unhygienic conditions, <a href="/wiki/Dehydration" title="Dehydration">dehydration</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dysentery" title="Dysentery">dysentery</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Scurvy" title="Scurvy">scurvy</a> led to a high <a href="/wiki/Mortality_rate" title="Mortality rate">mortality rate</a>, on average 15%<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> and up to a third of captives. Often the ships carried hundreds of slaves, who were chained tightly to plank beds. For example, the slave ship <i>Henrietta Marie</i> carried about 200 slaves on the long <a href="/wiki/Middle_Passage" title="Middle Passage">Middle Passage</a>. They were confined to cargo holds with each slave chained with little room to move.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The most significant routes of the slave ships led from the north-western and western coasts of Africa to <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a> and the south-east coast of what is today the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a>. As many as 20 million Africans were transported by ship.<sup id="cite_ref-Shillington_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shillington-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The transportation of slaves from Africa to America was known as the Middle Passage.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conditions_on_slave_ships">Conditions on slave ships</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slave_ship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Conditions on slave ships">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Enslaved_people">Enslaved people</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slave_ship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Enslaved people">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Navio_negreiro_-_Rugendas_1830.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Navio_negreiro_-_Rugendas_1830.jpg/220px-Navio_negreiro_-_Rugendas_1830.jpg" width="220" height="132" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Navio_negreiro_-_Rugendas_1830.jpg/330px-Navio_negreiro_-_Rugendas_1830.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Navio_negreiro_-_Rugendas_1830.jpg/440px-Navio_negreiro_-_Rugendas_1830.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3423" data-file-height="2052" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Navio_negreiro_-_Rugendas_1830.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A painting c.1830 by the German artist <a href="/wiki/Johann_Moritz_Rugendas" title="Johann Moritz Rugendas">Johann Moritz Rugendas</a> depicts a scene below deck of a slave ship headed to Brazil; Rugendas had been an eyewitness to the scene</div></div></div>\n<p>Owners of slave ships did their best to hold as many enslaved people as possible by cramming, chaining, and selectively grouping slaves to maximize space and make travel more profitable. Slaves onboard were underfed and treated brutally causing many to die before even arriving at their destination; dead or dying slaves were dumped overboard. These people also were not treated as human: living like animals throughout their long voyage to the New World. The enslaved were naked and shackled together with several different types of chains, stored on the floor beneath bunks with little to no room to move due to the crammed conditions. They spent a large portion of time pinned to floorboards which would wear skin on their elbows down to the bone. Firsthand accounts from former slaves, such as <a href="/wiki/Olaudah_Equiano" title="Olaudah Equiano">Olaudah Equiano</a>, describe the horrific conditions that enslaved people were forced to endure.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Slave_Trade_Act_1788" title="Slave Trade Act 1788">Slave Trade Act 1788</a> regulated conditions on board British slave ships for the first time. It was introduced to the United Kingdom parliament by <a href="/wiki/Sir_William_Dolben,_3rd_Baronet" title="Sir William Dolben, 3rd Baronet">Sir William Dolben</a>, an advocate for the abolition of slavery. For the first time, limits were placed on the number of enslaved people that could be carried. Under the terms of the act, ships could transport 1.67 slaves per ton up to a maximum of 207 tons burthen, after which only one slave per ton could be carried.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The well-known slave ship <i><a href="/wiki/Brookes_(ship)" title="Brookes (ship)">Brookes</a></i> was limited to carrying 454 people; it had previously transported as many as 609 enslaved.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Olaudah Equiano was among the supporters of the act but it was opposed by some abolitionists, such as <a href="/wiki/William_Wilberforce" title="William Wilberforce">William Wilberforce</a>, who feared it would establish the idea that the slave trade simply needed reform and regulation, rather than complete abolition.<sup id="cite_ref-Hochschild_140_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hochschild_140-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>This limited reduction in the overcrowding on slave ships may have reduced the on-board death rate, but this is disputed by some historians.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sailors_and_crew">Sailors and crew</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slave_ship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Sailors and crew">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In the eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries, the sailors on slave ships were often badly paid and subject to brutal discipline.<sup id="cite_ref-Hochschild114_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hochschild114-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Furthermore, a crew mortality rate of around 20% was expected during a voyage, with sailors dying as a result of disease, flogging or slave uprisings.<sup id="cite_ref-Edwards(Captain.)2007_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Edwards(Captain.)2007-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Hochschild94_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hochschild94-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> While conditions for the crew were vastly better than those of the enslaved people, they remained harsh and contributed to a high death rate. Sailors often had to live and sleep without shelter on the open deck for the entirety of the Atlantic voyage as the space below deck was occupied by slaves.<sup id="cite_ref-Hochschild114_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hochschild114-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Disease (malaria or yellow fever) was the most common cause of death among sailors. A high crew mortality rate on the return voyage was in the captain\'s interests as it reduced the number of sailors who had to be paid on reaching the home port.<sup id="cite_ref-Hochschild94_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hochschild94-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Crew members who survived were frequently cheated out of their wages on their return.<sup id="cite_ref-Hochschild114_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hochschild114-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> \n</p><p>These aspects of the slave trade were widely known; the notoriety of slave ships amongst sailors meant those joining slave ship crews did so through coercion or because they could find no other employment. This was often the case for sailors who had spent time in prison.<sup id="cite_ref-Rediker2007_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rediker2007-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Abolition_of_the_slave_trade">Abolition of the slave trade</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slave_ship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Abolition of the slave trade">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:HMS_Black_Joke_(1827).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/HMS_Black_Joke_%281827%29.jpg/220px-HMS_Black_Joke_%281827%29.jpg" width="220" height="167" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/HMS_Black_Joke_%281827%29.jpg/330px-HMS_Black_Joke_%281827%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/HMS_Black_Joke_%281827%29.jpg/440px-HMS_Black_Joke_%281827%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="600" data-file-height="456" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:HMS_Black_Joke_(1827).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The former slave ship HMS <i>Black Joke</i> (left) fires on the Spanish ship <i>El Almirante</i> before capturing her, January 1829 (painting by <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_Matthews_Condy" title="Nicholas Matthews Condy">Nicholas Matthews Condy</a>)</div></div></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/African_slave_trade" class="mw-redirect" title="African slave trade">African slave trade</a> was outlawed by the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> in 1807. The applicable UK act was the <a href="/wiki/Abolition_of_the_Slave_Trade_Act" class="mw-redirect" title="Abolition of the Slave Trade Act">Abolition of the Slave Trade Act</a> and outlawed the slave trade throughout the <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British Empire</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Act_Prohibiting_Importation_of_Slaves" title="Act Prohibiting Importation of Slaves">US law</a> took effect on 1 January 1808.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> After that date, all US and British slave ships leaving Africa were legally <a href="/wiki/Pirate" class="mw-redirect" title="Pirate">pirate</a> vessels subject to capture by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">United States Navy</a> or <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> In 1815,<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> at the <a href="/wiki/Congress_of_Vienna" title="Congress of Vienna">Council of Vienna</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a>, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> also agreed to abolish their slave trade.\n</p><p>After abolition, slave ships adopted quicker, more maneuverable forms to evade capture by naval warships, one favorite form being the <a href="/wiki/Baltimore_Clipper" title="Baltimore Clipper">Baltimore Clipper</a>. Some had hulls fitted with <a href="/wiki/Copper_sheathing" title="Copper sheathing">copper sheathing</a>. This was very expensive work that at this time was only commonly done to Royal Navy vessels; however, it increased speed by preventing the growth of marine weed on the hull, which would otherwise cause drag.<sup id="cite_ref-mccarthy108_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mccarthy108-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> The speed of slave ships made them attractive ships to repurpose for piracy,<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> and also made them attractive for naval use after capture; <a href="/wiki/USS_Nightingale_(1851)" title="USS Nightingale (1851)">USS&#160;<i>Nightingale</i></a> and <a href="/wiki/HMS_Black_Joke_(1827)" title="HMS Black Joke (1827)">HMS&#160;<i>Black Joke</i></a> were examples of such vessels. HMS <i>Black Joke</i> had a notable career in Royal Navy service and was responsible for capturing a number of slave ships and freeing many hundreds of slaves.\n</p><p>There have been attempts by descendants of African slaves to sue <a href="/wiki/Lloyd%27s_of_London" title="Lloyd&#39;s of London">Lloyd\'s of London</a> for playing a key role in <a href="/wiki/Underwriting#Insurance_underwriting" title="Underwriting">underwriting insurance policies</a> taken out on slave ships bringing slaves from Africa to the Americas.<sup id="cite_ref-20"
