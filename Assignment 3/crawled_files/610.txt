Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/French" title="Help:IPA/French">[\xc9\x91\xcc\x83t\xc9\x91\xcc\x83t k\xc9\x94\xca\x81djal]</a></span>) was a series of agreements signed on 8 April 1904 between the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_of_Great_Britain_and_Ireland" title="United Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland">United Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland</a> and the <a href="/wiki/French_Third_Republic" title="French Third Republic">French Republic</a> which saw a significant improvement in <a href="/wiki/France%E2%80%93United_Kingdom_relations" title="France\xe2\x80\x93United Kingdom relations">Anglo-French relations</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Beyond the immediate concerns of colonial expansion addressed by the agreement, the signing of the Entente Cordiale marked the end of almost a thousand years of intermittent conflict between the two states and their predecessors, and replaced the <i><a href="/wiki/Modus_vivendi" title="Modus vivendi">modus vivendi</a></i> that had existed since the end of the <a href="/wiki/Napoleonic_Wars" title="Napoleonic Wars">Napoleonic Wars</a> in 1815 with a more formal agreement.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The Entente Cordiale was the culmination of the policy of <a href="/wiki/Th%C3%A9ophile_Delcass%C3%A9" title="Th\xc3\xa9ophile Delcass\xc3\xa9">Th\xc3\xa9ophile Delcass\xc3\xa9</a>, France\'s foreign minister from 1898, who believed that a Franco-British understanding would give France some security against any German system of alliances in Western Europe. Credit for the success of the negotiation belongs chiefly to <a href="/wiki/Paul_Cambon" title="Paul Cambon">Paul Cambon</a>, France\'s ambassador in London, and to the British foreign secretary <a href="/wiki/Henry_Petty-Fitzmaurice,_5th_Marquess_of_Lansdowne" title="Henry Petty-Fitzmaurice, 5th Marquess of Lansdowne">Lord Lansdowne</a>.\n</p><p>The most important feature of the agreement was that it granted freedom of action to the <a href="/wiki/History_of_Egypt_under_the_British" title="History of Egypt under the British">UK in Egypt</a> and to <a href="/wiki/French_Morocco" class="mw-redirect" title="French Morocco">France in Morocco</a> (with the proviso that France\'s eventual dispositions for Morocco include reasonable allowance for Spain\'s interests there). At the same time, the UK ceded the <a href="/wiki/Los_Islands" class="mw-redirect" title="Los Islands">Los Islands</a> (off French Guinea) to France, defined the frontier of <a href="/wiki/Colonial_Nigeria" title="Colonial Nigeria">Nigeria</a> in France\'s favour, and agreed to French control of the upper <a href="/wiki/Gambia_Colony_and_Protectorate" title="Gambia Colony and Protectorate">Gambia</a> valley, while France renounced its exclusive right to certain fisheries off <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland" class="mw-redirect" title="Newfoundland">Newfoundland</a>. Furthermore, French and British zones of influence in the not to be colonialized <a href="/wiki/Siam" class="mw-redirect" title="Siam">Siam</a> (<a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a>) were outlined, with the eastern territories, adjacent to <a href="/wiki/French_Indochina" title="French Indochina">French Indochina</a>, becoming a French zone, and the western, adjacent to <a href="/wiki/Colonial_Burma" class="mw-redirect" title="Colonial Burma">Burmese</a> <a href="/wiki/Tenasserim_Division" class="mw-redirect" title="Tenasserim Division">Tenasserim</a>, a British zone.  Arrangements were also made to allay the rivalry between British and French colonists in the <a href="/wiki/New_Hebrides" title="New Hebrides">New Hebrides</a>.\n</p><p>By the Entente Cordiale both powers reduced the virtual isolation into which they had withdrawn\xe2\x80\x94France involuntarily, the UK complacently\xe2\x80\x94while they had eyed each other over African affairs. The UK had no ally <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Japanese_Alliance" title="Anglo-Japanese Alliance">but Japan (1902)</a>, of little use if war should break out in European waters; France had none but Russia, soon to be discredited in the <a href="/wiki/Russo-Japanese_War" title="Russo-Japanese War">Russo-Japanese War</a> of 1904\xe2\x80\x9305. The agreement was upsetting to Germany, whose policy had long been to rely on Franco-British antagonism. A German attempt to check the French in <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a> in 1905 (the Tangier Incident, or <a href="/wiki/First_Moroccan_Crisis" title="First Moroccan Crisis">First Moroccan Crisis</a>), and thus upset the Entente, served only to strengthen it. Military discussions between the French and the British <a href="/wiki/General_staff" class="mw-redirect" title="General staff">general staffs</a> were soon initiated. Franco-British solidarity was confirmed at the <a href="/wiki/Algeciras_Conference" title="Algeciras Conference">Algeciras Conference</a> (1906) and reconfirmed in the <a href="/wiki/Agadir_Crisis" title="Agadir Crisis">Second Moroccan Crisis</a> (1911).<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Entente_Cordiale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Germany_GB_France.gif" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Germany_GB_France.gif/220px-Germany_GB_France.gif" width="220" height="287" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Germany_GB_France.gif/330px-Germany_GB_France.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Germany_GB_France.gif/440px-Germany_GB_France.gif 2x" data-file-width="4353" data-file-height="5682" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Germany_GB_France.gif" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A cartoon on the Entente Cordiale from <a href="/wiki/Punch_(magazine)" title="Punch (magazine)">Punch</a>, with <a href="/wiki/John_Bull" title="John Bull">John Bull</a> stalking off with the harlot <a href="/wiki/Marianne" title="Marianne">Marianne</a> (in what is supposed to be a <a href="/wiki/Flag_of_France" title="Flag of France">Tricolour</a> dress) and turning his back on the Kaiser, who pretends not to care. The tip of the scabbard of a cavalry sabre protrudes from beneath the Kaiser\'s army overcoat, implying a potential resort to force.</div></div></div>\n<p>The French term <i>Entente Cordiale</i> (usually translated as "cordial agreement" or "cordial understanding") comes from a letter written in 1843 by the British Foreign Secretary <a href="/wiki/George_Hamilton-Gordon,_4th_Earl_of_Aberdeen" title="George Hamilton-Gordon, 4th Earl of Aberdeen">Lord Aberdeen</a> to his brother, in which he mentioned "a cordial, good understanding" between the two nations. This was translated into French as <i>Entente Cordiale</i> and used by <a href="/wiki/Louis_Philippe_I" title="Louis Philippe I">Louis Philippe I</a> in the French Chamber that year.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> When used today the term almost always denotes the <i>second</i> Entente Cordiale, that is to say, the written and partly secret agreement signed in London between the two powers on 8 April 1904.\n</p><p>The agreement was a change for both countries. France had been isolated from the other European powers, mostly as a result of the efforts of German Chancellor <a href="/wiki/Otto_von_Bismarck" title="Otto von Bismarck">Otto von Bismarck</a> to estrange France from potential allies, as it was thought that France might seek revenge for its defeat in the <a href="/wiki/Franco-Prussian_War" title="Franco-Prussian War">Franco-Prussian War</a> of 1870\xe2\x80\x9371. The United Kingdom had maintained a policy of "<a href="/wiki/Splendid_isolation" title="Splendid isolation">splendid isolation</a>" on the European continent for nearly a century, intervening in continental affairs only when it was considered necessary to protect British interests and to maintain the continental <a href="/wiki/Balance_of_power_in_international_relations" class="mw-redirect" title="Balance of power in international relations">balance of power</a>. The situation for both countries changed in the last decade of the 19th century.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The change had its roots in a British loss of confidence after the <a href="/wiki/Second_Boer_War" title="Second Boer War">Second Boer War</a>, and a growing fear that the country was isolated in the face of a potentially aggressive Germany. As early as March 1881, the French statesman <a href="/wiki/L%C3%A9on_Gambetta" title="L\xc3\xa9on Gambetta">L\xc3\xa9on Gambetta</a> and the then-<a href="/wiki/Prince_of_Wales" title="Prince of Wales">Prince of Wales</a>, <a href="/wiki/Edward_VII_of_the_United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="Edward VII of the United Kingdom">Albert Edward</a>, met at the <a href="/wiki/Ch%C3%A2teau_de_Breteuil" title="Ch\xc3\xa2teau de Breteuil">Ch\xc3\xa2teau de Breteuil</a> to discuss an alliance against Germany. The <a href="/wiki/Scramble_for_Africa" title="Scramble for Africa">Scramble for Africa</a> prevented the countries from coming to terms, however. On the initiative of Colonial Secretary <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Chamberlain" title="Joseph Chamberlain">Joseph Chamberlain</a>, there were three rounds of British-German talks between 1898 and 1901. The UK decided not to join the <a href="/wiki/Triple_Alliance_(1882)" title="Triple Alliance (1882)">Triple Alliance</a>, broke off the negotiations with Berlin, and revived the idea of a British-French alliance.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> \n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:British_and_French_empires_1920.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/British_and_French_empires_1920.png/250px-British_and_French_empires_1920.png" width="250" height="110" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/British_and_French_empires_1920.png/375px-British_and_French_empires_1920.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/British_and_French_empires_1920.png/500px-British_and_French_empires_1920.png 2x" data-file-width="1425" data-file-height="625" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:British_and_French_empires_1920.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The British and French colonial empires reached their peaks after World War I, a reflection of the power of this agreement.</div></div></div>\n<p>When the <a href="/wiki/Russo-Japanese_War" title="Russo-Japanese War">Russo-Japanese War</a> was about to erupt, France and the UK found themselves on the verge of being dragged into the conflict on the side of their respective allies. France was firmly allied with Russia, while the UK had recently signed the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Japanese_Alliance" title="Anglo-Japanese Alliance">Anglo-Japanese Alliance</a>. In order to avoid going to war, both powers "shucked off their ancient rivalry"<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> and resolved their differences in Africa, the Americas, Asia, and the Pacific. Toward this end, French foreign minister <a href="/wiki/Th%C3%A9ophile_Delcass%C3%A9" title="Th\xc3\xa9ophile Delcass\xc3\xa9">Th\xc3\xa9ophile Delcass\xc3\xa9</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Henry_Petty-Fitzmaurice,_5th_Marquess_of_Lansdowne" title="Henry Petty-Fitzmaurice, 5th Marquess of Lansdowne">Lord Lansdowne</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_Foreign_Affairs" class="mw-redirect" title="Secretary of State for Foreign Affairs">British Foreign Secretary</a>, negotiated an agreement on colonial matters, and Lord Lansdowne and <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Paul_Cambon" class="mw-redirect" title="Pierre Paul Cambon">Paul Cambon</a>, the French Ambassador to the UK, signed the resulting convention on 8 April 1904.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Documents_signed">Documents signed</span><span In return for the French promising not to "obstruct" British actions in Egypt, the British promised to allow the French to "preserve order \xe2\x80\xa6 and provide assistance" in Morocco. Free passage through the Suez Canal was guaranteed, finally putting the <a href="/wiki/Convention_of_Constantinople" title="Convention of Constantinople">Convention of Constantinople</a> into force, and the erection of fortifications on part of the Moroccan coast forbidden. The treaty contained a secret annex dealing with the possibility of "changed circumstances" in the administration of either of the two countries.</li>\n<li>The second document dealt with <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_(island)" title="Newfoundland (island)">Newfoundland</a> and portions of West and Central Africa. The French gave up their rights (stemming from the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Utrecht" class="mw-redirect" title="Treaty of Utrecht">Treaty of Utrecht</a>) over the western coast of Newfoundland, although they retained the right to fish the coast. In return, the British gave the French the town of <a href="/w/index.php?title=Yarbutenda&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Yarbutenda (page does not exist)">Yarbutenda</a> (near the modern border between <a href="/wiki/Senegal" title="Senegal">Senegal</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Gambia" title="The Gambia">The Gambia</a>) and the <a href="/wiki/Iles_de_Los" class="mw-redirect" title="Iles de Los">Iles de Los</a> (part of modern <a href="/wiki/Guinea" title="Guinea">Guinea</a>). An additional provision dealt with the border between French and British possessions east of the <a href="/wiki/River_Niger" class="mw-redirect" title="River Niger">River Niger</a> (present-day Niger and Nigeria).</li>\n<li>The final declaration concerned <a href="/wiki/Siam" class="mw-redirect" title="Siam">Siam</a> (Thailand), <a href="/wiki/Madagascar" title="Madagascar">Madagascar</a> and the <a href="/wiki/New_Hebrides" title="New Hebrides">New Hebrides</a> (Vanuatu). In Siam, the British recognised a French <a href="/wiki/Sphere_of_influence" title="Sphere of influence">sphere of influence</a> to the east of the River <a href="/wiki/Menam" class="mw-redirect" title="Menam">Menam</a>\'s basin; in turn, the French recognised British influence over the territory to the west of the Menam basin. Both parties disclaimed any idea of annexing Siamese territory. The British withdrew their objection to the French introducing a <a href="/wiki/Tariff" title="Tariff">tariff</a> in Madagascar. The parties came to an agreement which would "put an end to the difficulties arising from the lack of jurisdiction over the natives of the New Hebrides".</li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Entente_Cordiale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>It is unclear what exactly the <i>Entente</i> meant to the British <a href="/wiki/Foreign_Office" class="mw-redirect" title="Foreign Office">Foreign Office</a>. For example, in early 1911 following French press reports contrasting the virility of the <a href="/wiki/Triple_Alliance_(1882)" title="Triple Alliance (1882)">Triple Alliance</a> with the moribund state of the <i>Entente</i> <a href="/wiki/Eyre_Crowe" title="Eyre Crowe">Eyre Crowe</a> minuted: "The fundamental fact of course is that the <i>Entente</i> is not an alliance.  For purposes of ultimate emergencies it may be found to have no substance at all. For the <i>Entente</i> is nothing more than a frame of mind, a view of general policy which is shared by the governments of two countries, but which may be, or become, so vague as to lose all content."<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Triple Alliance collapsed when Italy remained neutral at the outbreak of <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>, while the Entente endured.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Commemoration">Commemoration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Entente_Cordiale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Commemoration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The hundredth anniversary of the Entente cordiale in 2004 was marked by a number of official and unofficial events, including a <a href="/wiki/State_visit" title="State visit">state visit</a> to France in April by Queen <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_II_of_the_United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="Elizabeth II of the United Kingdom">Elizabeth II</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Prince_Philip,_Duke_of_Edinburgh" title="Prince Philip, Duke of Edinburgh">Duke of Edinburgh</a>, and a return visit by President <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Chirac" title="Jacques Chirac">Chirac</a> in November. British troops (the band of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Marines" title="Royal Marines">Royal Marines</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Household_Cavalry" title="Household Cavalry">Household Cavalry Mounted Regiment</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grenadier_Guards" title="Grenadier Guards">Grenadier Guards</a> and <a href="/wiki/King%27s_Troop,_Royal_Horse_Artillery" title="King&#39;s Troop, Royal Horse Artillery">King\'s Troop, Royal Horse Artillery</a>) also led the <a href="/wiki/Bastille_Day" title="Bastille Day">Bastille Day</a> parade in Paris for the first time, with the <a href="/wiki/Red_Arrows" title="Red Arrows">Red Arrows</a> flying overhead.\n</p><p>At both London <a href="/wiki/Waterloo_International_railway_station" title="Waterloo International railway station">Waterloo International</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gare_du_Nord" title="Gare du Nord">Paris Gare du Nord</a>, the flags of the <a href="/wiki/Flag_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Flag of the United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> and of <a href="/wiki/Flag_of_France" title="Flag of France">France</a> were depicted connected with the words \'Entente cordiale\' superimposed on posters. Some French political leaders had complained<sup id="cite_ref-BBC_Waterloo_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC_Waterloo-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> about the name "Waterloo" for the destination of trains from Paris because the British terminus is named after <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Waterloo" title="Battle of Waterloo">the 1815 battle</a> where a British-led alliance defeated <a href="/wiki/Napoleon" title="Napoleon">Napoleon</a>\'s army, and in 1998 French politician <a href="/w/index.php?title=Florent_Longuep%C3%A9e&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Florent Longuep\xc3\xa9e (page does not exist)">Florent Longuep\xc3\xa9e</a> wrote to British Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Tony_Blair" title="Tony Blair">Tony Blair</a> demanding, without success, that the name be changed.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC_Waterloo_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC_Waterloo-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> In November 2007 <a href="/wiki/St_Pancras_International" class="mw-redirect" title="St Pancras International">St Pancras International</a> became the new London terminus for the Eurostar service.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Entente_Cordiale_Scholarships">Entente Cordiale Scholarships</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Entente_Cordiale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Entente Cordiale Scholarships">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The name "Entente Cordiale" is used for the <a href="/wiki/Entente_Cordiale_Scholarships" title="Entente Cordiale Scholarships">Entente Cordiale Scholarship</a> scheme, a selective Franco-British scholarship scheme which was announced on 30 October 1995 by British Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/John_Major" title="John Major">John Major</a> and French President <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Chirac" title="Jacques Chirac">Jacques Chirac</a> at an Anglo-French summit in London.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> \nIt provides funding for British and French students to study for one academic year on the other side of the Channel. The scheme is administered by the French embassy in London for British students,<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> and by the <a href="/wiki/British_Council" title="British Council">British Council</a> France and the UK embassy in Paris for French students.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Funding is provided by the private sector and foundations.\nThe scheme aims to foster mutual understanding and to promote exchanges between the British and French leaders of tomorrow.\nThe programme was initiated by Sir <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Mallaby" title="Christopher Mallaby">Christopher Mallaby</a>, British ambassador to France between 1993 and 1996.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a
