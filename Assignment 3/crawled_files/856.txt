class="mw-redirect" title="River Tyne, England">River Tyne</a> in <a href="/wiki/North_East_England" title="North East England">North East England</a> which includes <a href="/wiki/Newcastle_upon_Tyne" title="Newcastle upon Tyne">Newcastle upon Tyne</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gateshead" title="Gateshead">Gateshead</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tynemouth" The population at the 2011 census was 774,891.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Historic_counties_of_England" title="Historic counties of England">Historically</a> part of <a href="/wiki/County_Durham" title="County Durham">County Durham</a> and <a href="/wiki/Northumberland" title="Northumberland">Northumberland</a>, Tyneside spans four <a href="/wiki/Districts_of_England" title="Districts of England">local authority districts</a>, the City of Newcastle upon Tyne and the Metropolitan Boroughs of <a href="/wiki/Gateshead" title="Gateshead">Gateshead</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_Tyneside" title="North Tyneside">North Tyneside</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Tyneside" title="South Tyneside">South Tyneside</a>, with a combined estimated population in 2013 of 832,469.<sup id="cite_ref-Pop2012_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pop2012-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Tyneside is the <a href="/wiki/List_of_conurbations_in_the_United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="List of conurbations in the United Kingdom">7th largest conurbation in England</a>, and home to over 70 per cent of the population of <a href="/wiki/Tyne_and_Wear" title="Tyne and Wear">Tyne and Wear</a>; <a href="/wiki/Sunderland,_Tyne_and_Wear" class="mw-redirect" title="Sunderland, Tyne and Wear">Sunderland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Washington_(Tyne_and_Wear)" class="mw-redirect" title="Washington (Tyne and Wear)">Washington</a> form the separate <a href="/wiki/Wearside" title="Wearside">Wearside</a> conurbation, although the latter has a Newcastle Upon Tyne postcode. href="/w/index.php?title=Tyneside&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geordies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The people of the Tyneside area, called "<a href="/wiki/Geordie" title="Geordie">Geordies</a>", have a reputation for their distinctive dialect and accent. Tynesiders may have been given this name, a local diminutive of the name "George", because their <a href="/wiki/Miner" title="Miner">miners</a> used <a href="/wiki/George_Stephenson" title="George Stephenson">George Stephenson</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Geordie_lamp" title="Geordie lamp">safety lamp</a> (invented in 1815 and called a "Georgie lamp") to prevent <a href="/wiki/Firedamp" title="Firedamp">firedamp</a> explosions, rather than the <a href="/wiki/Davy_lamp" title="Davy lamp">Davy lamp</a> used elsewhere. An alternative explanation relates that during the <a href="/wiki/Jacobitism" title="Jacobitism">Jacobite</a> risings of 1715 and 1745 the Tynesiders declared their allegiance to the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Hanover" title="House of Hanover">Hanoverian</a> kings of Great Britain <a href="/wiki/George_I_of_Great_Britain" title="George I of Great Britain">George I</a> and <a href="/wiki/George_II_of_Great_Britain" title="George II of Great Britain">George II</a>; whereas the rest of the county of <a href="/wiki/Northumberland" title="Northumberland">Northumberland</a>, to the north, stood loyal to <a href="/wiki/James_Francis_Edward_Stuart" title="James Francis Edward Stuart">James Francis Edward Stuart</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Coal_production">Coal production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tyneside&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Coal production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>While Newcastle upon Tyne had been an important local centre since Roman times, and was a major local market town from the Middle Ages, the development of Newcastle and Tyneside is owed to <a href="/wiki/Coal_mining" title="Coal mining">coal mining</a>. Coal was first known to be dug in Tyneside from superficial seams in around 1200, but there is some evidence from <a href="/wiki/Bede" title="Bede">Bede</a>\'s writings that it may have been dug as early as 800 AD. Coal was dug from local drift mines and bell pits, and although initially only used locally, it was exported from the port of Newcastle from the mid 14th century onwards. Tyneside had a strategic advantage as far as the coal trade was concerned, because <a href="/wiki/Collier_brig" class="mw-redirect" title="Collier brig">collier brigs</a> could be loaded with coal on the Tyne and could sail down the east coast to London. In fact, the burgesses of Newcastle formed a cartel, and were known as the <a href="/wiki/The_Hostmen_of_Newcastle-upon-Tyne" class="mw-redirect" title="The Hostmen of Newcastle-upon-Tyne">Hostmen</a>. The Hostmen were able gain a monopoly over all of the coal exported from Tyneside, a monopoly which lasted a considerable time. A well-known group of workers on the river were the <a href="/wiki/Keelmen" title="Keelmen">keelmen</a> who handled the keels, boats that carried the coal from the riverbanks to the waiting colliers.<sup id="cite_ref-Finch_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Finch-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Steel_and_shipbuilding">Steel and href="/wiki/River_Derwent,_North_East_England" title="River Derwent, North East England">River Derwent</a>, a major tributary of the Tyne that rises in <a href="/wiki/County_Durham" title="County Durham">County Durham</a>, saw the development of the <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">steel</a> industry from around 1600 onwards. This was led by German immigrant cutlers and sword-makers, probably from around <a href="/wiki/Solingen" title="Solingen">Solingen</a>, who fled from <a href="/wiki/Religious_persecution" title="Religious persecution">religious persecution</a> at home and settled in the then village of <a href="/wiki/Shotley_Bridge" title="Shotley Bridge">Shotley Bridge</a>, near <a href="/wiki/Consett" title="Consett">Consett</a>.\n</p><p>The combination of coal and steel industries in the area was the catalyst for further major industrial development in the 19th century, including the <a href="/wiki/Ship-building" class="mw-redirect" title="Ship-building">shipbuilding</a> industry; at its peak, the Tyneside <a href="/wiki/Shipyard" title="Shipyard">shipyards</a> were one of the largest centres of shipbuilding in the world and built an entire navy for <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> in the first decade of the 20th century. There is still a working shipyard in <a href="/wiki/Wallsend" title="Wallsend">Wallsend</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Professional_competitive_rowing_on_the_Tyne">Professional competitive rowing on the Tyne</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tyneside&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Professional competitive rowing on the Tyne">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>From early in the 19th century, it was a custom to hold boat races on the Tyne. The Tyne had a large number of keelmen and wherrymen, who handled boats as part of their jobs. As on the <a href="/wiki/River_Thames" title="River Thames">River Thames</a>, there were competitions to show who was the best oarsman. As a wherryman did not earn very much, competitive rowing was seen as a quick way of earning extra money. Regattas were held, and provided modest prizes for professionals, but the big money was made in challenge races, in which <a href="/wiki/Sculling" title="Sculling">scullers</a> or boat crews would challenge each other to a race over a set distance for a side stake. The crews would usually have backers, who would put up the stake money, as they saw the chance of financial gain from the race. In the days before mass attendances at football matches, races on the river were enormously popular, with tens of thousands attending. Betting would go on both before and during a race, the odds changing as the fortunes of the contestants changed. Contestants who became champions of the Tyne would often challenge the corresponding champions of the River Thames, and the race would be arranged to take place on one of the two rivers.\n</p><p>Rivalry between the Tyne and the Thames was very keen, and rowers who upheld the honour of the Tyne became local heroes. Three such oarsmen, who came from humble backgrounds and became household names in the North East, were <a href="/wiki/Harry_Clasper" title="Harry Clasper">Harry Clasper</a>, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Chambers_(oarsman)" title="Robert Chambers (oarsman)">Robert Chambers</a> and <a href="/wiki/James_Renforth" title="James Renforth">James Renforth</a>. <a href="/wiki/Harry_Clasper" title="Harry Clasper">Clasper</a> was a champion rower in fours, as well as an innovative boat designer and a successful rowing coach. <a href="/wiki/Robert_Chambers_(oarsman)" title="Robert Chambers (oarsman)">Chambers</a> and <a href="/wiki/James_Renforth" title="James Renforth">Renforth</a> were oarsmen who excelled at <a href="/wiki/Sculling" title="Sculling">sculling</a>. Both held the World Sculling Championship at different times.\nThe popularity of all three men was such that when they died, many thousands attended their funeral processions, and magnificent funeral monuments were provided by popular subscription in all three cases. At the end of the 19th century professional competitive rowing on the Tyne began a gradual decline and would die out entirely leaving the amateur version.<sup id="cite_ref-Whitehead_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Whitehead-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rapper_dancing">Rapper dancing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tyneside&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Rapper dancing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Despite its rapid growth in the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a>, Tyneside developed one peculiar local custom, the <a href="/wiki/Rapper_sword" title="Rapper sword">rapper sword</a> dance, which later spread to neighbouring areas of Northumberland and County Durham.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Industrial_decline_and_regeneration">Industrial decline and regeneration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tyneside&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Industrial decline and regeneration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>During the 1970s and 1980s, there was major <a href="/wiki/Deindustrialization" title="Deindustrialization">industrial decline</a> in the traditional British <a href="/wiki/Heavy_industries" class="mw-redirect" title="Heavy industries">heavy industries</a>, and Tyneside was hit hard. High unemployment rates and the national government\'s resolve to push through with economic transformation led to great social unrest with strikes and occasional rioting in depressed areas.\n</p><p>From the late 1980s onward, an improving national economy and local regeneration helped the area to recover, and although unemployment is still a problem compared with some other areas of Britain, expansion of new industries such as <a href="/wiki/Tourism" title="Tourism">tourism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">science</a> and <a href="/wiki/High-technology" class="mw-redirect" title="High-technology">high-technology</a>, has fuelled local development, especially in Newcastle upon Tyne and Gateshead.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">Definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tyneside&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Office_for_National_Statistics" title="Office for National Statistics">ONS</a> 2011 census had 774,891 census respondents inside the "Tyneside Built-up Area" or "Tyneside Urban Area".<sup id="cite_ref-BUA_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BUA-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> This was a significant decline on the 2001 return of 879,996<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> this loss was mainly due to fewer people returning the form; <a href="/wiki/Hetton-le-Hole" title="Hetton-le-Hole">Hetton-le-Hole</a>, <a href="/wiki/Houghton-le-Spring" title="Houghton-le-Spring">Houghton-le-Spring</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chester-le-Street" title="Chester-le-Street">Chester-le-Street</a> and <a href="/wiki/Washington,_Tyne_and_Wear" title="Washington, Tyne and Wear">Washington</a> no longer forming part of the built-up area and instead forming part of <a href="/wiki/Wearside" title="Wearside">Wearside</a>.
