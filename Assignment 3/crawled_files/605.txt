(January 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> are fees charged by education institutions for instruction or other services. Besides public spending (by governments and other public bodies), private spending via tuition payments are the largest revenue sources for education institutions in some countries. In most countries, especially countries in Scandinavia and Continental Europe, there are no or only nominal tuition fees for all forms of education, including university and other higher education.<sup id="cite_ref-Garritzmann_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Garritzmann-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tuition_payments&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: By location">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Countries such as <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> have \xe2\x80\x9cup-front tuition policies."<sup id="cite_ref-Marcucci/Johnstone_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marcucci/Johnstone-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> These policies generally include a tuition fee that is large enough to give parents or <a href="/wiki/Legal_guardian" title="Legal guardian">guardians</a> "a responsibility to cover some portion of their children\xe2\x80\x99s higher education costs."<sup id="cite_ref-Marcucci/Johnstone_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marcucci/Johnstone-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> This responsibility can make it difficult for a low-income student to attend college without requiring a grant or one or more <a href="/wiki/Student_loan" title="Student loan">loans</a>.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Tuition_fees_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Tuition fees in the United Kingdom">Tuition fees in the United Kingdom</a> were introduced in 1998, with a maximum permitted fee of \xc2\xa31,000. Since then, this maximum has been raised to \xc2\xa39,000 in most of the United Kingdom, while Scotland has abolished tuition.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/College_tuition_in_the_United_States" title="College tuition in the United States">College tuition in the United States</a> is one of the costs of a post-<a href="/wiki/Secondary_education" title="Secondary education">secondary education</a>. The total cost of college is called the <a href="/wiki/Cost_of_attendance" title="Cost of attendance">cost of attendance</a> (or, informally, the "sticker price") and, in addition to tuition, can include <a href="/wiki/Room_and_board" title="Room and board">room and board</a> and fees for facilities such as books, transportation, or commuting provided by the college.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Tuition_fees_in_France" title="Tuition fees in France">French tuition fees</a> are capped based on the level of education pursued, from 183 Euros per year for undergraduate up to 388 for doctorates. Some public universities have autonomous status, meaning that they can charge much higher tuition, and all private universities charge tuition.\n</p><p>In the  <a href="/wiki/Education_in_Germany" title="Education in Germany">German Education system</a> almost all universities and most universities of applied sciences are funded by the state and do not charge tuition fees. In exceptional cases universities may offer courses for professionals (e.g. executive MBA programmes) which may require tuition payment. Some of the local governments have recently decided that students from non-EU countries can be charged, although <a href="/wiki/ERASMUS" class="mw-redirect" title="ERASMUS">ERASMUS</a> students, students from developing countries and other special groups are exempt. <sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> In addition there are private institutions of higher education which are run on a tuition-based model.   \n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Education_in_Greece" title="Education in Greece">In Greece</a> there are no tuition fees as Bachelor-level higher education and Master-level post-graduate education is provided for free to all Hellene (Greek) citizens as a benefit of citizenship paid by taxes. However, universities accept very few students who have excelled at high school, with the selection being done through the Panhellenic Examinations, a system of state-administered examinations in which the chance of failure is too high and thus only a small percentage of students is able to pass and there is a limit to the number of students that can be accepted each year. Furthermore, it is difficult for <a href="/wiki/Mature_student" class="mw-redirect" title="Mature student">mature students</a> to be accepted at universities. Doctorate-level higher education is often also provided for free, but some universities may charge some fees for PhD degrees. Students who weren\'t able to pass the Panhellenic Examinations in a few attempts are effectively banned for life from the Greek university system and those who wish to have a higher education must resort to registering at private universities (called colleges, \xce\xba\xce\xbf\xce\xbb\xce\xad\xce\xb3\xce\xb9\xce\xb1) which charge tuition fees or emigrate to other countries in order to get an education. Sometimes it may happen that a student was academically excellent in school but failed to get a good "ethics performance mark" or was disqualified from entering the Panhellenic Examinations because of non-academic factors, since the Greek educational system also marks the student\'s "ethics performance" (\xce\xb4\xce\xb9\xce\xb1\xce\xbb\xce\xbf\xce\xb3\xce\xae, dialogi) rather than only his or her academic performance: for example, an unruly student or a student caught cheating or drinking may receive a disqualifying mark in his or her "ethics performance" which can disqualify the student from entering university (or in extreme cases from continuing in the high school) even if the student has excellent academic skills.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="By_institution">By institution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tuition_payments&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: By institution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Tuition is charged at different rates from one type of institution to the next. Net tuition indices mark an increase in the \xe2\x80\x9crelative real burden\xe2\x80\x9d for payments at various types of institutions for higher education; in the period between 1980 and 1995, for example, this burden increased by approximately 80 percent for students at <a href="/wiki/Public_university" title="Public university">public universities</a> and by 148 percent for students at <a href="/wiki/Private_university" title="Private university">private universities</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Most students or their families who pay for tuition and other education costs don\'t have enough savings to pay in full while they are in school.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Some students must work or borrow money to afford an education. In the United States, <a href="/wiki/Student_financial_aid_(United_States)" title="Student financial aid (United States)">student financial aid</a> is available to defray the cost of a post-secondary education: \xe2\x80\x9cFinancial aid is typically thought to exert the most influence in [attendance], when admitted students consider whether to enroll in a particular institution.\xe2\x80\x9d<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> It is often the case that the lower the cost of the school, the more likely a student is to attend.\n</p><p>Developed countries have adopted a dual scheme for education: while basic (i.e. high-school) education is supported by taxes rather than tuition, higher education usually requires tuition payments or fees.\n</p><p>People may purchase <a href="/wiki/Tuition_insurance" title="Tuition insurance">tuition insurance</a> to protect themselves from fees related to involuntary withdrawal (illness, death of a parent or guardian, etc.)\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tuition_payments&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:College_Tuition_State_Funding.svg" class="image"><img alt="" class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:College_Tuition_State_Funding.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Study comparing college revenue per student by tuition and state funding in 2008 dollars.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>\n<p>In <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">medieval</a> <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, universities were mainly institutions of the <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Catholic Church</a>. As they mainly trained clergy, most of these universities did not have any need to exact fees from the students<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> with one notable exception: during the 12th century, while under the supervision of <a href="/wiki/Petrus_Comestor" title="Petrus Comestor">Pierre le Mangeur</a>, the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Paris" title="University of Paris">University of Paris</a> began collecting two <a href="/wiki/Sou_(coin)" class="mw-redirect" title="Sou (coin)">sous</a> weekly in tuition. \n</p><p>Later, the main duty of universities in most <a href="/wiki/Protestant" class="mw-redirect" title="Protestant">Protestant</a> countries was the training of future <a href="/wiki/Civil_servant" class="mw-redirect" title="Civil servant">civil servants</a>. Again, it was not in the interest of the state to charge tuition fees, as this would have decreased the quality of civil servants. On the other hand, the number of students from the lower classes was usually kept in check by the expenses of living during the years of study, although as early as the mid-19th century there were calls for limiting the university entrance by middle-class persons.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> A typical family, however, could not afford educating a child or young adult, even if the education itself was free. A similar situation exists today in many <a href="/wiki/Third_World" title="Third World">Third World</a> countries, where the expenses of "free" schooling (food, books, school uniform, etc.) prevent some children from attending any school.\n</p><p>After <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> the tuition systems of all of today\'s advanced democracies still were highly similar: Education institutions in all countries charged no or only very low tuition fees.<sup id="cite_ref-Garritzmann_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Garritzmann-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> It was not before the 1950s that the countries\' education systems developed in different directions. Some countries, especially the Anglo-Saxon countries (for example the United States) but also Asian countries such as Japan, introduced considerable tuition payments already in the early post-war period.<sup id="cite_ref-Garritzmann_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Garritzmann-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Other countries, particularly in Scandinavia and continental Europe, in contrast remained tuition-free. These developments were unrelated to the massive educational expansion that took place at the same time.\n</p><p>Since the early 1970s, the average cost of tuition has steadily outpaced the growth of the average American household. This trend continued particularly under President Reagan\'s higher education policies in the 1980s. Likewise, there has been a steady decrease in <a href="/wiki/Administration_of_federal_assistance_in_the_United_States" title="Administration of federal assistance in the United States">federal funding</a> for grants and a rise in the <a href="/wiki/Interest_rate" title="Interest rate">interest rates</a> of most major student loans, leaving many students struggling to pay debt for years after graduation.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="College_tuition_for_undocumented_students">College tuition for undocumented students</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tuition_payments&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: College tuition for undocumented students">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The Development, Relief, and Education for Alien Minors (DREAM) Act is legislation introduced in the U.S. Senate.  It would allow the estimated 50,000 to 65,000 <a href="/wiki/Undocumented_students_in_the_United_States" class="mw-redirect" title="Undocumented students in the United States">undocumented students in the United States</a> to gain in-state tuition as well as a path towards <a href="/wiki/Citizenship_in_the_United_States" class="mw-redirect" title="Citizenship in the United States">American citizenship</a>.  The Act would apply only to those students with tangible proof of residence in the United States before the age of 18.  This Act has stirred debate in numerous groups, including institutions, families, and the Senate itself.\n</p><p>As of March 2013, undocumented students in most States were required to pay the higher out-of-state students\' tuition charged at public universities, often between <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">$</a>20,000 and $35,000 at a local public university.  In addition, these students were denied federal assistance as they lacked valid <a href="/wiki/Social_Security_number" title="Social Security number">Social Security numbers</a>.  Because such students often come from comparatively poor families, the costs were too high to allow many undocumented students to seek university education in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span
