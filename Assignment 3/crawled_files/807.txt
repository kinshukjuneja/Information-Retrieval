class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Broadsheet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Comparison_newspaper_size.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Comparison_newspaper_size.svg/300px-Comparison_newspaper_size.svg.png" width="300" height="240" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Comparison_newspaper_size.svg/450px-Comparison_newspaper_size.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Comparison_newspaper_size.svg/600px-Comparison_newspaper_size.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="410" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Comparison_newspaper_size.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Comparison of some newspaper sizes with metric paper sizes. Approximate nominal dimensions are in millimetres.</div></div></div>\n<p>Many broadsheets measure approximately <span class="frac nowrap">29<span class="visualhide">&#160;</span><sup>1</sup>&#8260;<sub>2</sub></span> by <span class="frac nowrap">23<span class="visualhide">&#160;</span><sup>1</sup>&#8260;<sub>2</sub></span> inches (749 by 597&#160;mm) per full broadsheet spread, twice the size of a standard tabloid. Australian and New Zealand broadsheets always have a paper size of <a href="/wiki/ISO_216" title="ISO 216">A1</a> per spread (841 by 594&#160;mm or 33.1 by 23.4&#160;in). South African broadsheet newspapers have a double-page spread sheet size of 820 by 578&#160;mm or 32.3 by 22.8&#160;in (single-page live print area of 380 x 545&#160;mm). Others measure 22 inches or 560 millimetres vertically.\n</p><p>In the United States, the traditional dimensions for the front page half of a broadsheet are 15 inches (381&#160;mm) wide by <span class="frac nowrap">22<span class="visualhide">&#160;</span><sup>3</sup>&#8260;<sub>4</sub></span> inches (578&#160;mm) long. However, in efforts to save <a href="/wiki/Newsprint" title="Newsprint">newsprint</a> costs many U.S. newspapers <sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template"><span style="white-space: nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Link_rot" title="Wikipedia:Link rot"><span title="&#160;Dead link since August 2018">dead link</span></a></i>&#93;</span></sup> have downsized to 12 inches (305&#160;mm) wide by <span class="frac nowrap">22<span class="visualhide">&#160;</span><sup>3</sup>&#8260;<sub>4</sub></span> inches (578&#160;mm) long for a folded page.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template"><span style="white-space: nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Link_rot" title="Wikipedia:Link rot"><span title="&#160;Dead link since August 2018">dead link</span></a></i>&#93;</span></sup>\n</p><p>Many rate cards and specification cards refer to the "broadsheet size" with dimensions representing the front page "half of a broadsheet" size, rather than the full, unfolded broadsheet spread. Some quote actual page size and others quote the "printed area" size.\n</p><p>The two versions of the broadsheet are:\n</p>\n<ul><li>Full broadsheet&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 The full broadsheet typically is folded vertically in half so that it forms four pages (the front page front and back and the back page front and back). The four pages are called a spread. Inside broadsheets are nested accordingly.</li>\n<li>Half broadsheet&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 The half broadsheet is usually an inside page that is not folded vertically and just includes a front and back.</li></ul>\n<p>In uncommon instances, an entire newspaper can be a two-page half broadsheet or four-page full broadsheet. Totally self-contained advertising circulars inserted in a newspaper in the same format are referred to as broadsheets.\n</p><p>Broadsheets typically are also folded horizontally in half to accommodate newsstand display space. The horizontal fold however does not affect the page numbers and the content remains vertical. The most important newspaper stories are placed "above the (horizontal) fold."  This contrasts with tabloids which typically do not have a horizontal fold (although tabloids usually have the four page to a sheet spread format).\n</p><p>The broadsheet has since emerged as the most popular format for the dissemination of printed news. The world\'s most widely circulated English-language daily broadsheet is <i><a href="/wiki/The_Times_of_India" title="The Times of India">The Times of India</a></i>, a leading English-language <a href="/wiki/Daily_newspaper" class="mw-redirect" title="Daily newspaper">daily newspaper</a> from <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, followed closely by <i><a href="/wiki/Wall_Street_Journal" class="mw-redirect" title="Wall Street Journal">Wall Street Journal</a></i> from the United States, according to the <a href="/wiki/Audit_Bureau_of_Circulations_(North_America)" class="mw-redirect" title="Audit Bureau of Circulations (North America)">Audit Bureau of Circulations</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Broadsheet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The broadsheet, <a href="/wiki/Broadside_(printing)" title="Broadside (printing)">broadside</a>, was used as a format for <a href="/wiki/Broadside_ballad" title="Broadside ballad">musical</a> and <a href="/wiki/Popular_print" title="Popular print">popular prints</a> in the 17th century. \nEventually the people began using the broadsheet as a source for political activism by reprinting speeches.\n</p><p>Broadsheet newspapers developed after the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Great_Britain" title="Kingdom of Great Britain">British</a> in 1712 placed a <a href="/wiki/Tax" title="Tax">tax</a> on newspapers based on the number of their pages. Larger formats, however, had long been signs of status in printed objects, and still are in many places, and outside Britain the broadsheet developed for other reasons, including style and authority, unrelated to the British tax structure.\n</p><p>With the early mechanization of the 19th century came an increase in production of printed materials including the broadside as well as the competing <a href="/wiki/Penny_dreadful" title="Penny dreadful">penny dreadful</a>. In this period newspapers all over Europe began to print their issues on broadsheets. However, in the  <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, the main competition for the broadside was the gradual reduction of the newspaper tax, beginning in the 1830s, and eventually its dismissal in 1855.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>With the increased production of newspapers and literacy, the demand for visual reporting and journalists led to the blending of broadsides and newspapers, creating the modern broadsheet <a href="/wiki/Newspaper" title="Newspaper">newspaper</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Printing_considerations">Printing considerations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Broadsheet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Printing considerations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Modern printing facilities most efficiently print broadsheet sections in multiples of eight pages (with four front pages and four back pages). The broadsheet is then cut in half during the process. Thus the newsprint rolls used are defined by the width necessary to print four front pages. The width of a newsprint roll is called its <i>web</i>. Thus the new 12-inch-wide front page broadsheet newspapers in the United States use a <i>48-inch web</i> newsprint roll.\n</p><p>With profit margins narrowing for newspapers in the wake of competition from broadcast, cable television, and the internet, newspapers are looking to standardize the size of the newsprint roll. <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wall_Street_Journal" title="The Wall Street Journal">The Wall Street Journal</a></i> with its 12-inch wide frontpage was printed on <i>48-inch web</i> newsprint. Early adopters in the downsizing of broadsheets used a <i>50-inch web</i> (&#x200b;<span class="frac nowrap">12<span class="visualhide">&#160;</span><sup>1</sup>&#8260;<sub>2</sub></span>-inch front pages). However the <i>48-inch web</i> is now rapidly becoming the definitive standard in the U.S. <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> held out on the downsizing until July 2006, saying it would stick to its <i>54-inch web</i> (&#x200b;<span class="frac nowrap">13<span class="visualhide">&#160;</span><sup>1</sup>&#8260;<sub>2</sub></span>-inch front page)<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>. However, the paper adopted the narrower format beginning Monday, 6 August 2007.\n</p><p>The smaller newspapers also have the advantage of being easier to handle, particularly among commuters.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Connotations">Connotations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Broadsheet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Connotations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In some countries, especially Australia, Canada, the UK, and the U.S., broadsheet newspapers are commonly perceived to be more intellectual in content than their <a href="/wiki/Tabloid_(newspaper_format)" title="Tabloid (newspaper format)">tabloid</a> counterparts.  They tend to use their greater size to publish stories exploring topics in depth, while carrying less <a href="/wiki/Sensationalism" title="Sensationalism">sensationalist</a> and <a href="/wiki/Celebrity" title="Celebrity">celebrity</a>-oriented material. This distinction is most obvious on the front page: whereas tabloids tend to have a single story dominated by a headline, broadsheets allow two or more stories to be displayed, of which the most important sit at the top of the page\xe2\x80\x94"<a href="/wiki/Above_the_fold" title="Above the fold">above the fold</a>". In other countries, such as Spain, a small format is the universal standard for newspapers\xe2\x80\x94a popular, sensational press has had difficulty taking root\xe2\x80\x94and the tabloid size does not carry pejorative connotations.\n</p><p>On the other hand, a few newspapers, such as the German <i><a href="/wiki/Bild-Zeitung" class="mw-redirect" title="Bild-Zeitung">Bild-Zeitung</a></i> and others throughout central Europe are clearly tabloids in terms of content, but use the physical broadsheet format.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom_broadsheets">United Kingdom format and start printing in tabloid format was <i><a href="/wiki/Svenska_Dagbladet" title="Svenska Dagbladet">Svenska Dagbladet</a></i>, on 16 November 2000. As of August 2004, there were 26 broadsheet newspapers in total, with a combined circulation of 1,577,700 and 50 newspapers in tabloid with a combined circulation of 1,129,400. On 5 October 2004, the morning newspapers <i><a href="/wiki/G%C3%B6teborgs-Posten" title="G\xc3\xb6teborgs-Posten">G\xc3\xb6teborgs-Posten</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Dagens_Nyheter" title="Dagens Nyheter">Dagens Nyheter</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Sydsvenskan" title="Sydsvenskan">Sydsvenskan</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/%C3%96stersunds-Posten" title="\xc3\x96stersunds-Posten">\xc3\x96stersunds-Posten</a></i> all switched to tabloid, thus making it the leading format for morning newspapers in Sweden by volume of circulation. Most other broadsheet newspapers have followed since. The last daily Swedish newspaper to switch to tabloid was J\xc3\xb6nk\xc3\xb6pings-Posten, 6 November 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Thailand">Thailand</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Broadsheet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=59" title="Edit section: Thailand">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<ul><li><i><a href="/wiki/Thairath" class="mw-redirect" title="Thairath">Thairath</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/Thai_language" title="Thai language">Thai</a>: <span lang="th">\xe0\xb9\x84\xe0\xb8\x97\xe0\xb8\xa2\xe0\xb8\xa3\xe0\xb8\xb1\xe0\xb8\x90</span>)</li>\n<li><i><a href="/wiki/The_Bangkok_Post" class="mw-redirect" title="The Bangkok Post">The Bangkok Post</a></i></li></ul>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Turkey">Turkey</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Broadsheet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=60" title="Edit section: Turkey">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Most of the newspapers in <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a> are printed on this format.
