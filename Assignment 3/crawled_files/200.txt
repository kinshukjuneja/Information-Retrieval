/>.uk</a></span></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><b>Cornwall Council</b> (<a href="/wiki/Cornish_language" title="Cornish language">Cornish</a>: <i lang="kw">Konsel Kernow</i>) is the <a href="/wiki/Unitary_authority" title="Unitary authority">unitary authority</a> for the county of <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a> in the United Kingdom, not including the <a href="/wiki/Isles_of_Scilly" title="Isles of Scilly">Isles of Scilly</a>, which has its own council. The council, and its predecessor Cornwall County Council, has a tradition of large groups of <a href="/wiki/Independent_(politician)" class="mw-redirect" title="Independent (politician)">independent</a> councillors, having been controlled by independents in the 1970s and 1980s. Since the 2013 elections, it is run by an Independent-Liberal Democrat coalition.\n</p><p>Cornwall Council provides a wide range of services to more than half a million Cornish residents. In 2014 it had an annual budget of more than \xc2\xa31 billion and was the biggest employer in Cornwall with a staff of 12,429 salaried workers.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> It is responsible for services including: schools, social services, rubbish collection, roads, planning and more.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input Establishment of the unitary authority">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Before April 2009, Cornwall was administered as a <a href="/wiki/Non-metropolitan_county" title="Non-metropolitan county">non-metropolitan county</a> by the <b>Cornwall County Council</b> with\nsix districts, <a href="/wiki/Caradon" title="Caradon">Caradon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carrick,_Cornwall" title="Carrick, Cornwall">Carrick</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kerrier" title="Kerrier">Kerrier</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_Cornwall" title="North Cornwall">North Cornwall</a>, <a href="/wiki/Penwith" title="Penwith">Penwith</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Restormel" title="Restormel">Restormel</a> (a borough).\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Council_of_the_Isles_of_Scilly" title="Council of the Isles of Scilly">Council of the Isles of Scilly</a> was and still remains a separate unitary authority.\n</p><p>On 5 December 2007, the Government confirmed that Cornwall was one of five councils that would move to unitary status.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> This was enacted by <a href="/wiki/Statutory_instrument" title="Statutory instrument">statutory instrument</a> as part of the <a href="/wiki/2009_structural_changes_to_local_government_in_England" title="2009 structural changes to local government in England">2009 structural changes to local government in England</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The changes took effect on 1 April 2009. On that date the six districts and Cornwall County Council were abolished and were replaced by <b>Cornwall Council</b>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Number_of_councillors">Number of councillors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cornwall_Council&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Number of councillors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Currently (2017,) the council has 123 councillors, the independent Local Government Boundary Commission for England is proposing that Cornwall Council should have 87 councillors in future.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Logo_controversy">Logo controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cornwall_Council&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Logo controversy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Cornwall_Council_logo.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/c/cf/Cornwall_Council_logo.png/220px-Cornwall_Council_logo.png" width="220" height="110" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/c/cf/Cornwall_Council_logo.png/330px-Cornwall_Council_logo.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/c/cf/Cornwall_Council_logo.png/440px-Cornwall_Council_logo.png 2x" data-file-width="444" data-file-height="221" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Cornwall_Council_logo.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The proposed new logo, dropped in January 2009</div></div></div>\n<p>The original proposals for a new logo and motto for Cornwall\'s new unitary authority were met with widespread criticism from the general public with demands that the old logo and motto be kept.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> On 29 January 2009, the Cornwall Council Implementation Executive decided to revert to using the former County Council logo with just a change in name from "Cornwall County Council" to "Cornwall Council".<sup id="cite_ref-CCLogo_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CCLogo-11">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In March 2009, the leader of Cornwall County Council David Whalley announced he would be standing down as a councillor, complaining of personal attacks against him.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The current logo features a <a href="/wiki/Cornish_chough" class="mw-redirect" title="Cornish chough">Cornish chough</a> and the 15 Cornish golden <a href="/wiki/Bezants" class="mw-redirect" title="Bezants">bezants</a> on a black field as used in the arms of the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Cornwall" title="Duchy of Cornwall">Duchy of Cornwall</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-CCLogo_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CCLogo-11">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Name">Name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cornwall_Council&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>On the creation of the new unitary authority it was decided that the name of the new council would be Cornwall Council (Konsel Kernow).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Devolution">Devolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cornwall_Council&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Devolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The campaign for Cornish devolution began in 2000 with the founding of the <a href="/wiki/Cornish_Constitutional_Convention" title="Cornish Constitutional Convention">Cornish Constitutional Convention</a>, a cross-party, cross-sector association that campaigns for devolution to Cornwall.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> In 2009, Liberal Democrat MP <a href="/wiki/Dan_Rogerson" title="Dan Rogerson">Dan Rogerson</a> introduced a bill in parliament seeking to take power from Whitehall and regional quangos and pass it to the new Cornwall Council, with the intention of transforming the new council into an assembly along the lines of <a href="/wiki/National_Assembly_for_Wales" title="National Assembly for Wales">National Assembly for Wales</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Rogerson_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rogerson-14">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> In November 2010, British Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/David_Cameron" title="David Cameron">David Cameron</a> suggested in comments to the local press that his government would "devolve a lot of power to Cornwall - that will go to the Cornish unitary authority."<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> In 2011, the then Deputy Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Nick_Clegg" title="Nick Clegg">Nick Clegg</a> said he would meet a cross party group, including the six Cornish MPs, to look at whether more powers could be devolved to Cornwall.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The subsequent <a href="/wiki/Localism_Act_2011" title="Localism Act 2011">Localism Act 2011</a> was expected to achieve this but it proved incapable. However, the <a href="/wiki/Cities_and_Local_Government_Devolution_Act_2016" title="Cities and Local Government Devolution Act 2016">Cities and Local Government Devolution Act 2016</a> is intended to devolve some powers to Cornwall Council, helping to bring social and care services together, and giving control over bus services and local investment.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_services">Cultural services</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cornwall_Council&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Cultural services">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Among the services provided by the council is a <a href="/wiki/Public_library" title="Public library">public library</a> service which consists of a main library in Truro and smaller libraries in towns and some villages throughout Cornwall. There are also the following special libraries: Cornwall Learning Library, <a href="/wiki/Cornish_Studies_Library" class="mw-redirect" title="Cornish Studies Library">Cornish Studies Library</a>, the Education Library Service, and the Performing Arts Library, as well as a mobile library service based at <a href="/wiki/Threemilestone" title="Threemilestone">Threemilestone</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_projects">Cultural projects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cornwall_Council&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Cultural projects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Cornwall Council is promoting ten cultural projects as part of a five-year culture strategy. One project is the development of a <a href="/wiki/National_Theatre_of_Cornwall" title="National Theatre of Cornwall">National Theatre of Cornwall</a>, a collaboration of the <a href="/wiki/Hall_for_Cornwall" title="Hall for Cornwall">Hall for Cornwall</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kneehigh_Theatre" title="Kneehigh Theatre">Kneehigh Theatre</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eden_Project" title="Eden Project">Eden Project</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wildworks" title="Wildworks">Wildworks</a>. Cornwall Council has based its idea on the successful National Theatres of <a href="/wiki/National_Theatre_of_Scotland" title="National Theatre of Scotland">Scotland</a> and <a href="/wiki/National_Theatre_Wales" title="National Theatre Wales">Wales</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Another of the projects is the proposed creation of a <a href="/wiki/Cornish_National_Library" title="Cornish National Library">National Library of Cornwall</a> to resolve inadequacies with the current storage of archives.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> It is hoped that this will bring some important documents concerning Cornish history back to Cornwall as well as providing better public access to those records already held. Cornwall Council is also involved in the project to build a <a href="/wiki/Stadium_for_Cornwall" title="Stadium for Cornwall">Stadium for Cornwall</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cornish_ethnic_and_national_identity">Cornish ethnic and national identity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cornwall_Council&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Cornish ethnic and national identity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Cornwall Council backs the campaign for the <a href="/wiki/Cornish_people" title="Cornish people">Cornish</a> to be recognised as a <a href="/wiki/Framework_Convention_for_the_Protection_of_National_Minorities" title="Framework Convention for the Protection of National Minorities">National Minority</a> in the UK. The council\'s then chief executive Kevin Lavery wrote a letter to the Government in 2010, writing, "Cornwall Council firmly believes that the UK Government should recognise the Cornish as a national minority under the terms of the Framework Convention." Adding that, "Cornwall Council believes that the Government\'s current restricted interpretation is discriminatory against the Cornish and contradicts the support it gives to Cornish culture and identity through its own departments."<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Cornwall Council\'s support was officially reaffirmed as council policy in 2011 with the publication of the Cornish National Minority Report 2, signed and endorsed by the then leaders of every political grouping on the council.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> The council took an active role in the promotion of the options for registering Cornish ethnicity and national identity on the <a href="/wiki/2011_UK_Census" class="mw-redirect" title="2011 UK Census">2011 UK Census</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> The Cornish people were finally recognised as a National Minority by the British Government on 24 April 2014 and incorporated into the European Framework Convention for the Protection of National Minorities giving the Cornish the same status as the United Kingdom\'s other Celtic peoples, the Scots, the Welsh and the Irish.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="International_relations">International relations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cornwall_Council&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: International relations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Since 2008 Cornwall Council and the former county council, together with Cornwall Enterprise, and Cornwall Sustainable Energy Partnership, have been involved with a Protocol of Cooperation between Cornwall and the Conseil g\xc3\xa9n\xc3\xa9ral du <a href="/wiki/Finist%C3%A8re" title="Finist\xc3\xa8re">Finist\xc3\xa8re</a> in <a href="/wiki/Brittany" title="Brittany">Brittany</a>. The protocol aims to allow the two regions to work more closely on topics of common interest and engage in a knowledge exchange with the possibility of jointly applying for European funding.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> Cornwall is also a member of the Conference of Peripheral Maritime Regions, a partnership of European regions, which aims to promote and highlight the value of these regions to Europe. Cornwall comes under the Atlantic Arc Commission sub-division of 30 regions, which has been used to advertise the potential of renewable energy off the Cornish coast to Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>A scheme arising from these partnerships is MERiFIC (Marine Energy in Far Peripheral and Island Communities) which seeks to advance the adoption of marine energy across the two regions, including the <a href="/wiki/Isles_of_Scilly" title="Isles of Scilly">Isles of Scilly</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> The project has received \xc2\xa34 million of European funding that will be spent in Cornwall and Brittany.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Cornwall County Council organised an event in <a href="/wiki/Brussels" title="Brussels">Brussels</a> in 2008 to promote various aspects of Cornwall, including the Cornish language, food and drink and showcasing Cornwall\'s design industry. This was part of the Celtic Connections programme of events put together by the <a href="/wiki/Celtic_nations" title="Celtic nations">Celtic nations</a> as a showcase for culture in Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Various fact finding missions have been organised by councillors to study how other regions and small nations of Europe govern themselves successfully. <a href="/wiki/Independent_(politician)" class="mw-redirect" title="Independent (politician)">Independent</a> councillor, <a href="/wiki/Bert_Biscoe" title="Bert Biscoe">Bert Biscoe</a>, organised a fact finding mission to <a href="/wiki/Guernsey" title="Guernsey">Guernsey</a> in 2011 to see if the island\'s system of government could be adapted to work in Cornwall.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Since 2010 Cornwall Council has been a full observer member of the <a href="/wiki/British%E2%80%93Irish_Council" title="British\xe2\x80\x93Irish Council">British\xe2\x80\x93Irish Council</a> due to the <a href="/wiki/Cornish_language" title="Cornish language">Cornish language</a> falling under the BIC\'s areas of work.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economic_projects">Economic projects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cornwall_Council&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Economic projects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Cornwall Council, in partnership with the <a href="/wiki/Eden_Project" title="Eden Project">Eden Project</a>, is bidding to have the world\'s first <a href="/wiki/Green_Investment_Bank" class="mw-redirect" title="Green Investment Bank">Green Investment Bank</a> based in Cornwall. The Council is also working with the NHS and Eden to tackle fuel poverty by creating a Cornwall Together co-op which will buy electricity at lower-than-market prices.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> No further progress has been made on this since it was originally mooted.\n</p><p>Cornwall Council are servicing nearly 30 long term <a href="/wiki/Lender_option_borrower_option" title="Lender option borrower option">lender option borrower option</a> loans (LOBOs) totalling \xc2\xa3394 million. The council is locked into some of the deals until the year 2078, paying interest at more than double the current market rate.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cornwall_Council&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: 2009 Cornwall Council elections">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cornwall_Council_election,_2009" title="Cornwall Council election, 2009">Cornwall Council election, 2009</a></div>\n<p>Elections for the new unitary Cornwall Council were held on 4 June 2009 and there were 123 members elected, replacing the previous 82 councillors on Cornwall County Council and the 249 on the six district councils.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> The outgoing Cornwall County Council had 48 <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Democrats_(UK)" title="Liberal Democrats (UK)">Liberal Democrat</a> members, nine <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Conservative Party (UK)">Conservatives</a>, five <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">Labour</a>, one from the small <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Party_(UK,_1989)" title="Liberal Party (UK, 1989)">Liberal Party</a> with the remaining 19 seats held by Independent candidates. <a href="/wiki/Mebyon_Kernow" title="Mebyon Kernow">Mebyon Kernow</a> had no county councillors, but nine district councillors, before the two-tier system was abolished.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Lib Dems lost overall control of Cornwall Council to \'no overall control\' - this means that no single party has overall control of the new council despite the Conservatives have the largest number of councillors, however they do not have enough for a majority control.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> The cabinet of the council was therefore formed as a coalition between the Conservatives and the Independent bloc.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> The Conservatives received 34% of the vote (50 seats), followed by the Liberal Democrats on 28% (38 seats), the Independents on 23% (32 seats) and Mebyon Kernow on 4% (3 seats). The turnout was 41%. Labour, the Green Party, UKIP and the BNP failed to secure any seats in Cornwall.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span id="By-elections_and_defections.2C_2009_to_2013"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="By-elections_and_defections,_2009_to_2013">By-elections and defections, 2009 to 2013</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cornwall_Council&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: By-elections and defections, 2009 to 2013">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In August 2010, Councillor Neil Plummer of Stithians left the Independent group and joined <a href="/wiki/Mebyon_Kernow" title="Mebyon Kernow">Mebyon Kernow</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> He later left Mebyon Kernow and stood for re-election as an independent.\n</p><p>In June 2011 Liskeard North councillor Jan Powell defected from the Conservatives to join the Liberal Democrats.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In May 2012 two Liberal Democrat councillors left the Liberal Democrat group to join the Independent Group. Chris Pascoe, the councillor for Threemilestone and Gloweth, resigned in protest over the national actions of the Liberal Democrat party and the introduction of the "pasty tax".<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>  Graham Walker, councillor for <a href="/wiki/St_Austell" title="St Austell">St Austell</a> Bethel, defected in protest over the coalition government\'s education policies.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In September 2012 another Liberal Democrat councillor resigned from the party. Tamsin Williams, the member for <a href="/wiki/Penzance" title="Penzance">Penzance</a> Central, defected to Mebyon Kernow, having previously been a member of it in the 1990s. She was the second member to defect to Mebyon Kernow since 2009, and her change of allegiance came after "bad decisions made by the London parties."<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> During the same month of September 2012, one Independent councillor, Lisa Dolley, left the council\'s Independent Group to become an ungrouped independent.\n</p><p>In March 2013 Conservative cabinet member for Looe East, Armand Toms, defected to the Independents over the party\'s decision to freeze Council Tax rather than increase it.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>A total of 5 by-elections were held to Cornwall Council in the 2009-2013 term of office. Rogerson and was held on 8 June, remaining vacant in the interim.<sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80">&#91;79&#93;</a></sup> The subsequent election was won by <a councillor, Paula Dolphin, resigned from the Liberal Democrats on 20 February 2018, continuing to sit as a standalone Independent.<sup id="cite_ref-85" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-85">&#91;84&#93;</a></sup> On 1 March 2018 the leader of the Labour group on the council and councillor for <a href="/wiki/Penzance" title="Penzance">Penzance East</a>, Tim Dwelly, He will now sit with the <a href="/wiki/Independent_politician" title="Independent politician">Independent group</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Labour_Leader_Dwelly_Quits_and_joins_Independents_86-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Labour_Leader_Dwelly_Quits_and_joins_Independents-86">&#91;85&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Council_history">Council history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cornwall_Council&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Council history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Old_County_Hall_Truro.jpeg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Old_County_Hall_Truro.jpeg/220px-Old_County_Hall_Truro.jpeg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Old_County_Hall_Truro.jpeg/330px-Old_County_Hall_Truro.jpeg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Old_County_Hall_Truro.jpeg/440px-Old_County_Hall_Truro.jpeg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="480" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Old_County_Hall_Truro.jpeg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Old County Hall in <a href="/wiki/Truro" title="Truro">Truro</a>, which used to be the Council HQ, but is now awaiting conversion to a high quality hotel.</div></div></div>\n<p><b>Cornwall County Council</b> was established in 1889 and abolished on 31 March 2009.
