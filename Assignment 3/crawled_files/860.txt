scope="row">Children</th><td>\n1</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><b>Sir Carol Reed</b> (30 December 1906 \xe2\x80\x93 25 April 1976) was an English <a href="/wiki/Film_director" title="Film director">film director</a> best known for <i><a href="/wiki/Odd_Man_Out" title="Odd Man Out">Odd Man Out</a></i> (1947), <i><a href="/wiki/The_Fallen_Idol_(film)" title="The Fallen Idol (film)">The Fallen Idol</a></i> (1948), <i><a href="/wiki/The_Third_Man" title="The Third Man">The Third Man</a></i> (1949), and <i><a href="/wiki/Oliver!_(film)" title="Oliver! (film)">Oliver!</a></i> (1968).<sup id="cite_ref-Malcolm_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Malcolm-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> For <i>Oliver!</i>, he  received the <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Director" title="Academy Award for Best Director">Academy Award for Best Director</a>.\n</p><p><i>Odd Man Out</i> was the first recipient of the <a href="/wiki/BAFTA_Award_for_Best_British_Film" title="BAFTA Award for Best British Film">BAFTA Award for Best British Film</a>; filmmaker <a href="/wiki/Roman_Polanski" title="Roman Polanski">Roman Polanski</a> has repeatedly cited it as his favourite film.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> <i>The Fallen Idol</i> won the second BAFTA Award for Best British Film. The <a href="/wiki/British_Film_Institute" title="British Film Institute">British Film Institute</a> voted <i>The Third Man</i> the <a href="/wiki/BFI_Top_100_British_films" title="BFI Top 100 British films">greatest British film of the 20th century</a>. \n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" style="display:none" class="toclevel-1 tocsection-9"><a href="#External_links"><span class="tocnumber">9</span> <span class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_career">Early life and career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carol_Reed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Carol Reed was born in <a href="/wiki/Putney" title="Putney">Putney</a>, south-west London.<sup id="cite_ref-Kemp_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kemp-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>  He was the out-of-wedlock son of actor-producer <a href="/wiki/Herbert_Beerbohm_Tree" title="Herbert Beerbohm Tree">Sir Herbert Beerbohm Tree</a> and his <a href="/wiki/Mistress_(lover)" title="Mistress (lover)">mistress</a>, May Pinney Reed.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> He was educated at <a href="/wiki/The_King%27s_School,_Canterbury" title="The King&#39;s School, Canterbury">The King\'s School, Canterbury</a>.\n</p><p>He embarked on an acting career while still in his late teens. A period in the theatrical company of the thriller writer <a href="/wiki/Edgar_Wallace" title="Edgar Wallace">Edgar Wallace</a> followed, and Reed became his personal assistant in 1927.<sup id="cite_ref-Hogg1_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hogg1-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Apart from acting in a few Wallace derived films himself, Reed became involved in adapting his work for the screen during the day while he was a stage manager in the evenings. The connection with Wallace ended with his death in Hollywood during February 1932. Taken on by <a href="/wiki/Basil_Dean" title="Basil Dean">Basil Dean</a>, Reed worked for his <a href="/wiki/Associated_Talking_Pictures" class="mw-redirect" title="Associated Talking Pictures">Associated Talking Pictures</a>, successively for ATP as a dialogue director, second-unit director and then assistant director.<sup id="cite_ref-Feehan_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Feehan-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> His films in the later role working under Dean were <i><a href="/wiki/Autumn_Crocus_(film)" title="Autumn Crocus (film)">Autumn Crocus</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Lorna_Doone_(1934_film)" title="Lorna Doone (1934 film)">Lorna Doone</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Loyalties_(1933_film)" title="Loyalties (1933 film)">Loyalties</a></i> and (with <a href="/wiki/Thorold_Dickinson" title="Thorold Dickinson">Thorold Dickinson</a>) <i><a href="/wiki/Java_Head_(1934_film)" title="Java Head (1934 film)">Java Head</a></i>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_films">Early films</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carol_Reed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early films">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>His earliest films as director were "<a href="/wiki/Quota_quickies" class="mw-redirect" title="Quota quickies">quota quickies</a>".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Of his experience making <i><a href="/wiki/Midshipman_Easy" title="Midshipman Easy">Midshipman Easy</a></i> (1935) his first solo directorial project he was harsh on himself. "I was indefinite and indecisive", he said later. "I thought I had picked up a lot about cutting and camera angles, but now, when I had to make all the decisions myself and was not just mentally approving or criticising what somebody else decided, I was pretty much lost. Fortunately, I realised that this was the only way to learn \xe2\x80\x93 by making mistakes."<sup id="cite_ref-Hogg1_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hogg1-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Graham_Greene" title="Graham Greene">Graham Greene</a>, then reviewing films for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Spectator" title="The Spectator">The Spectator</a></i>, was much more forgiving, commenting that Reed "has more sense of the cinema than most veteran British directors".<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>  Of Reed\'s comedy <i><a href="/wiki/Laburnum_Grove" title="Laburnum Grove">Laburnum Grove</a></i> (1936), he wrote: "Here at last is an English film one can unreservedly praise". He was perceptive about Reed\'s potential, describing the film as "thoroughly workmanlike and unpretentious, with just the hint of a personal manner which makes one believe that Mr. Reed, when he gets the right script, will prove far more than efficient."<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Reed\'s career began to develop with <i><a href="/wiki/The_Stars_Look_Down_(film)" title="The Stars Look Down (film)">The Stars Look Down</a></i> (1940), from the <a href="/wiki/A._J._Cronin" title="A. J. Cronin">A. J. Cronin</a> novel, which features <a href="/wiki/Michael_Redgrave" title="Michael Redgrave">Michael Redgrave</a> in the lead role. Greene wrote that Reed "has at last had his chance and magnificently taken it." He observed that "one forgets the casting altogether: he [Reed] handles his players like a master, so that one remembers them only as people."<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="War_years">War years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carol_Reed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: War years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The scripts of several of Reed\'s films in this period were written by <a href="/wiki/Frank_Launder" title="Frank Launder">Frank Launder</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sidney_Gilliat" title="Sidney Gilliat">Sidney Gilliat</a>, with the screenwriters and director working for producer <a href="/wiki/Edward_Black_(producer)" title="Edward Black (producer)">Edward Black</a>, who released through the British subsidiary of  <a href="/wiki/20th_Century_Fox" title="20th Century Fox">20th Century Fox</a>. The best known of these films are probably <i><a href="/wiki/Night_Train_to_Munich" title="Night Train to Munich">Night Train to Munich</a></i> (1940), with <a href="/wiki/Rex_Harrison" title="Rex Harrison">Rex Harrison</a>; <i><a href="/wiki/Kipps_(1941_film)" title="Kipps (1941 film)">Kipps</a></i> (1941), again with Michael Redgrave; and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Young_Mr_Pitt" class="mw-redirect" title="The Young Mr Pitt">The Young Mr Pitt</a></i> (1942), with <a href="/wiki/Robert_Donat" title="Robert Donat">Robert Donat</a> in the title role. The later film, although historically inaccurate, is set during the <a href="/wiki/French_Revolutionary_Wars" title="French Revolutionary Wars">French Revolutionary</a> and <a href="/wiki/Napoleonic_Wars" title="Napoleonic Wars">Napoleonic Wars</a>.\n</p><p>From 1942, Reed served in the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Army_Ordnance_Corps" title="Royal Army Ordnance Corps">Royal Army Ordnance Corps</a>: he was granted the rank of Captain and placed with the film unit, and then with the Directorate of Army Psychiatry.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> For the latter body a training film, <i>The New Lot</i> (1943),was made, recounting the experiences of five new recruits. It had a script by <a href="/wiki/Eric_Ambler" title="Eric Ambler">Eric Ambler</a> and <a href="/wiki/Peter_Ustinov" title="Peter Ustinov">Peter Ustinov</a>, with contributions from Reed, and was produced by <a href="/wiki/Thorold_Dickinson" title="Thorold Dickinson">Thorold Dickinson</a>. It was remade as <i><a href="/wiki/The_Way_Ahead" title="The Way Ahead">The Way Ahead</a></i> (1944).\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-war">Post-war</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carol_Reed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Post-war">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Reed made his three most highly regarded films just after the war, beginning with <i><a href="/wiki/Odd_Man_Out" title="Odd Man Out">Odd Man Out</a></i> (1947), with <a href="/wiki/James_Mason" title="James Mason">James Mason</a> in the lead. It is the tale of an injured IRA leader\'s last hours in an unidentified Northern Irish city. In fact, <a href="/wiki/Belfast" title="Belfast">Belfast</a> was used for the location work, but it remains unnamed in the film.\n</p><p>It was the producer <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Korda" title="Alexander Korda">Alexander Korda</a>, to whom Reed was now signed, who introduced the director to the novelist <a href="/wiki/Graham_Greene" title="Graham Greene">Graham Greene</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Hogg2_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hogg2-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The next two films were made from screenplays by Greene: <i><a href="/wiki/The_Fallen_Idol_(film)" title="The Fallen Idol (film)">The Fallen Idol</a></i> (1948) and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Third_Man" title="The Third Man">The Third Man</a></i> (1949).\n</p><p><i>The Third Man</i> was co-produced by <a href="/wiki/David_O._Selznick" title="David O. Selznick">David O. Selznick</a> and Korda, with the American actors <a href="/wiki/Orson_Welles" title="Orson Welles">Orson Welles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Cotten" title="Joseph Cotten">Joseph Cotten</a> in two of the leading roles. Reed insisted on casting Welles as Harry Lime, although Selznick had wanted <a href="/wiki/No%C3%ABl_Coward" title="No\xc3\xabl Coward">No\xc3\xabl Coward</a> for the role. The film required six weeks of location work in <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">Vienna</a>, during which time it was Reed himself who accidentally discovered <a href="/wiki/Anton_Karas" title="Anton Karas">Anton Karas</a>, the zither player responsible for the film\'s music, in a courtyard outside a small Viennese restaurant.<sup id="cite_ref-Hogg2_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hogg2-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Reed once said: "A picture should end as it has to. I don\xe2\x80\x99t think anything in life ends \'right\'". While Greene wanted Holly Martins (Cotten) and Anna Schmidt (<a href="/wiki/Alida_Valli" title="Alida Valli">Alida Valli</a>) to reconcile at the end of the film, after Lime, her lover, is killed by Martins, Reed insisted that Anna should ignore him and walk on. "The whole point of the Valli character in that film is that she\xe2\x80\x99d experienced a fatal love \xe2\x80\x93 and then comes along this silly American!"<sup id="cite_ref-Hogg2_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hogg2-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>According to the film critic <a href="/wiki/Derek_Malcolm" title="Derek Malcolm">Derek Malcolm</a>, <i>The Third Man</i> is the "best film noir ever made out of Britain".<sup id="cite_ref-Malcolm_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Malcolm-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The film won the Grand Prix at the <a href="/wiki/Cannes_Film_Festival" title="Cannes Film Festival">Cannes Film Festival</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Feehan_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Feehan-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> the predecessor of the <a href="/wiki/Palme_d%27Or" title="Palme d&#39;Or">Palme d\'Or</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_career">Later career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carol_Reed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Later career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><i><a href="/wiki/Outcast_of_the_Islands" title="Outcast of the Islands">Outcast of the Islands</a></i> (1952), based on a novel by <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Conrad" title="Joseph Conrad">Joseph Conrad</a>, is often thought to mark the start of his creative decline.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Man_Between" title="The Man Between">The Man Between</a></i> (1953) is dismissed as a rehash of <i>The Third Man</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Kemp_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kemp-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> It "makes no startling impact, such as we have learned to expect from its director, on either the mind or the heart", complained Virginia Graham in <i>The Spectator</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> While the fable <i><a href="/wiki/A_Kid_for_Two_Farthings_(film)" title="A Kid for Two Farthings (film)">A Kid for Two Farthings</a></i> (1955), Reed\'s first colour film, set in the <a href="/wiki/East_End_of_London" title="East End of London">East End of London</a>, has been claimed as one of very few authentic cinematic depictions of an Anglo-Jewish community,<sup id="cite_ref-Brooke_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brooke-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> it suffers from the stereotyping of Jews<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> and is no more than a "whimsical curiosity" according to Michael Brooke.<sup id="cite_ref-Brooke_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brooke-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> It was the last film Reed made for Korda\'s <a href="/wiki/London_Films" title="London Films">London Films</a>; the producer died at the beginning of 1956.\n</p><p><i><a href="/wiki/Trapeze_(film)" title="Trapeze (film)">Trapeze</a></i> (1956), was Reed\'s first venture into the then relatively new <a href="/wiki/CinemaScope" title="CinemaScope">CinemaScope</a> wide screen process, and, although largely shot in Paris, was made for the US <a href="/wiki/Hecht-Hill-Lancaster_Productions" class="mw-redirect" title="Hecht-Hill-Lancaster Productions">Hecht-Hill-Lancaster Productions</a> company and was a success at the box-office. <i><a href="/wiki/Our_Man_in_Havana_(film)" title="Our Man in Havana (film)">Our Man in Havana</a></i> (1959) reunited him with Graham Greene who adapted his own novel.\n</p><p>He was contracted to direct a remake of <i><a href="/wiki/Mutiny_on_the_Bounty_(1962_film)" title="Mutiny on the Bounty (1962 film)">Mutiny on the Bounty</a></i> (1962) by <a href="/wiki/Metro-Goldwyn-Mayer" title="Metro-Goldwyn-Mayer">MGM</a>, but then <a href="/wiki/Marlon_Brando" title="Marlon Brando">Marlon Brando</a> was cast as <a href="/wiki/Fletcher_Christian" title="Fletcher Christian">Fletcher Christian</a>, and problems with the mock <i>Bounty</i> and the weather at the locations caused delays.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Brando had insisted on creative control,<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> and the two men argued incessantly. Reed left the production at a relatively early stage of production and was replaced by <a href="/wiki/Lewis_Milestone" title="Lewis Milestone">Lewis Milestone</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Agony_and_the_Ecstasy_(film)" title="The Agony and the Ecstasy (film)">The Agony and the Ecstasy</a></i> (1965), made in the United States, was a box-office failure, and was the last film over which Reed also served as producer. <i><a href="/wiki/Oliver!_(film)" title="Oliver! (film)">Oliver!</a></i> (1968), made at <a href="/wiki/Shepperton_Studios" title="Shepperton Studios">Shepperton</a> in <a href="/wiki/Surrey" title="Surrey">Surrey</a>, was financially backed by <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Pictures" title="Columbia Pictures">Columbia</a>, and won the <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Director" title="Academy Award for Best Director">Academy Award for Best Director</a>. "The movie may have been over-produced but it seemed everyone liked it that way", writes Thomas Hischak.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carol_Reed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:213_and_215_King%27s_Road_05.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/213_and_215_King%27s_Road_05.JPG/220px-213_and_215_King%27s_Road_05.JPG" width="220" height="293" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/213_and_215_King%27s_Road_05.JPG/330px-213_and_215_King%27s_Road_05.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/213_and_215_King%27s_Road_05.JPG/440px-213_and_215_King%27s_Road_05.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1944" data-file-height="2592" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:213_and_215_King%27s_Road_05.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/213_and_215_King%27s_Road" title="213 and 215 King&#39;s Road">213 King\'s Road</a></div></div></div>\n<p>From 1943 until 1947, he was married to the British actress <a href="/wiki/Diana_Wynyard" title="Diana Wynyard">Diana Wynyard</a>. After their divorce, he married, in 1948, the actress <a href="/wiki/Penelope_Dudley-Ward" title="Penelope Dudley-Ward">Penelope Dudley-Ward</a>, also known as Pempie, the elder daughter of <a href="/wiki/Freda_Dudley_Ward" title="Freda Dudley Ward">Freda Dudley Ward</a>, who had been a mistress of the <a href="/wiki/Edward_VIII_of_the_United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="Edward VIII of the United Kingdom">Duke of Windsor</a> (Edward VIII) when he was <a href="/wiki/Prince_of_Wales" title="Prince of Wales">Prince of Wales</a>. They had one son, Max. His stepdaughter <a href="/wiki/Tracy_Reed_(English_actress)" title="Tracy Reed (English actress)">Tracy Reed</a>, Ward\'s daughter, also had an acting career.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Actor <a href="/wiki/Oliver_Reed" title="Oliver Reed">Oliver Reed</a> was his nephew.\n</p><p>In 1953, he became only the second <a href="/wiki/Film_director" title="Film director">British film director</a> to be knighted for his craft. The first was Sir <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Korda" title="Alexander Korda">Alexander Korda</a> in 1942, who was the producer of some of Reed\'s most admired films.\n</p><p>Reed died from a <a href="/wiki/Myocardial_infarction" title="Myocardial infarction">heart attack</a> on 25 April 1976 at his home at <a href="/wiki/213_and_215_King%27s_Road" title="213 and 215 King&#39;s Road">213 King\'s Road</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chelsea,_London" title="Chelsea, London">Chelsea</a>, aged 69. He had lived there since 1948. He is buried in <a href="/wiki/Gunnersbury_Cemetery" title="Gunnersbury Cemetery">Kensington Cemetery</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gunnersbury" title="Gunnersbury">Gunnersbury</a>, West London, and there is a <a href="/wiki/Blue_plaque" title="Blue plaque">blue plaque</a> on his former home in his honour.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Filmography">Filmography</span><span
