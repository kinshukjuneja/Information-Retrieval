style="text-align:right;font-size:115%;padding-top: 0.6em;"><div class="plainlinks hlist navbar mini"><ul><li class="nv-view"><a href="/wiki/Template:Politics_of_the_Republic_of_Ireland" <b>Government of Ireland</b> (<a href="/wiki/Irish_language" title="Irish language">Irish</a>: <i lang="ga">Rialtas na h\xc3\x89ireann</i>) is the <a href="/wiki/Cabinet_(government)" title="Cabinet (government)">cabinet</a> that exercises <a href="/wiki/Executive_(government)" title="Executive (government)">executive authority</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Republic of Ireland</a>.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_Ireland" title="Constitution of Ireland">Constitution of Ireland</a> vests executive authority in a government which is headed by the Taoiseach, the <a href="/wiki/Head_of_government" title="Head of government">head of government</a>. The government is composed of government ministers, all of whom must be members of the Irish parliament. The Taoiseach must be nominated and approved by the <a href="/wiki/D%C3%A1il_%C3%89ireann" title="D\xc3\xa1il \xc3\x89ireann">D\xc3\xa1il \xc3\x89ireann</a>, the lower house of the <a href="/wiki/Oireachtas" title="Oireachtas">Oireachtas</a>, the Irish legislature. Following the D\xc3\xa1il\'s nomination, the <a href="/wiki/President_of_Ireland" title="President of Ireland">President of Ireland</a> appoints the Taoiseach to his role. The President also appoints members of the government, including the <a href="/wiki/T%C3%A1naiste" title="T\xc3\xa1naiste">T\xc3\xa1naiste</a>, the deputy head of government, on nomination of the Taoiseach. The government is dependent upon the Oireachtas to make primary legislation and as such, the government needs to command a majority in the D\xc3\xa1il in order to ensure support and confidence for budgets and government bills to pass. Collectively, the government is known as "the cabinet".\n</p><p>The current Taoiseach is <a href="/wiki/Leo_Varadkar" title="Leo Varadkar">Leo Varadkar</a> who took office on 14 June 2017. He is the leader of <a href="/wiki/Fine_Gael" title="Fine Gael">Fine Gael</a>, the party with the highest number of seats in the D\xc3\xa1il. Varadkar\'s government is a minority coalition, made up of Fine Gael and independent members. His T\xc3\xa1naiste is <a href="/wiki/Simon_Coveney" title="Simon Coveney">Simon Coveney</a> who took office on 30 November 2017.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" href="/w/index.php?title=Government_of_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Membership">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Membership of the cabinet is regulated by Article 28 of the <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_Ireland" title="Constitution of Ireland">Constitution of Ireland</a> and by the <a href="/wiki/Ministers_and_Secretaries_Acts" title="Ministers and Secretaries Acts">Ministers and Secretaries Acts 1924 to 2017</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ministers_act_1924_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ministers_act_1924-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The Irish constitution requires the government to consist of between seven and fifteen members,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> all of whom must be a member of the <a href="/wiki/Oireachtas" title="Oireachtas">Oireachtas</a>. \n</p><p>Since the formation of the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_the_18th_D%C3%A1il#12th_Government_of_Ireland" title="Government of the 18th D\xc3\xa1il">12th Government of Ireland</a> in 1966,<sup id="cite_ref-DTaoiseach-18th-Dail_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DTaoiseach-18th-Dail-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> all Irish cabinets have been formed with the constitutional maximum of fifteen ministers.  The total sometimes falls below this number for brief periods following the resignation of individual ministers or the withdrawal of a party from a coalition.\n</p><p>No more than two members of the Government may be members of <a href="/wiki/Seanad_%C3%89ireann" title="Seanad \xc3\x89ireann">Seanad \xc3\x89ireann</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Upper_house" title="Upper house">upper house</a> of the legislature.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> As a result, all other members of the government must be members of <a href="/wiki/D%C3%A1il_%C3%89ireann" title="D\xc3\xa1il \xc3\x89ireann">D\xc3\xa1il \xc3\x89ireann</a>, the lower house. The Taoiseach, T\xc3\xa1naiste and <a href="/wiki/Minister_for_Finance_(Ireland)" title="Minister for Finance (Ireland)">Minister for Finance</a> must be members of the D\xc3\xa1il.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In practice, however, the members of the government are invariably members of the D\xc3\xa1il. Since the adoption of the 1937 constitution, two ministers have been appointed from the Seanad: only <a href="/wiki/Se%C3%A1n_Moylan" title="Se\xc3\xa1n Moylan">Se\xc3\xa1n Moylan</a> who served in 1957 as <a href="/wiki/Minister_for_Agriculture,_Food_and_the_Marine" title="Minister for Agriculture, Food and the Marine">Minister for Agriculture</a> and <a href="/wiki/James_Dooge" title="James Dooge">James Dooge</a> who served as <a href="/wiki/Minister_for_Foreign_Affairs_and_Trade" title="Minister for Foreign Affairs and Trade">Minister for Foreign Affairs</a> from 1981 to 1982.<sup id="cite_ref-otoole-dooney_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-otoole-dooney-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> (<a href="/wiki/Joseph_Connolly_(Irish_politician)" title="Joseph Connolly (Irish politician)">Joseph Connolly</a>, a member of the <a href="/wiki/Seanad_%C3%89ireann_(Irish_Free_State)" title="Seanad \xc3\x89ireann (Irish Free State)">Free State Seanad</a>, had served in the <a href="/wiki/Executive_Council_of_the_Irish_Free_State" title="Executive Council of the Irish Free State">Executive Council of the Irish Free State</a><sup id="cite_ref-otoole-dooney_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-otoole-dooney-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> from 1932 to 1933 as <a href="/wiki/Minister_for_Posts_and_Telegraphs" title="Minister for Posts and Telegraphs">Minister for Posts and Telegraphs</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-DTaoiseach-7th-Dail_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DTaoiseach-7th-Dail-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> and from 1933 to 1936 as <a href="/wiki/Minister_for_Communications,_Climate_Action_and_Environment" title="Minister for Communications, Climate Action and Environment">Minister for Lands and Fisheries</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-DTaoiseach-8th-Dail_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DTaoiseach-8th-Dail-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>)\n</p><p>Members of the government in charge of <a href="/wiki/Department_of_State_(Ireland)" title="Department of State (Ireland)">Department of State</a> are designated "<a href="/wiki/Minister_(government)" title="Minister (government)">Ministers of Government</a>" (before 1977 a "Minister of State").<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> For distinction, "<a href="/wiki/Minister_of_State_(Ireland)" title="Minister of State (Ireland)">Ministers of State</a>" (known before 1977 as "<a href="/wiki/Parliamentary_secretary" title="Parliamentary secretary">Parliamentary Secretaries</a>") \xe2\x80\x94 informally called "junior ministers" \xe2\x80\x94 are not members of Government. A Government Minister (before 1977 a "Minister of State") is usually in charge of a <a href="/wiki/Department_of_state_(Ireland)" class="mw-redirect" title="Department of state (Ireland)">Department of State</a> and thus technically a "Minister of Government". A <a href="/wiki/Minister_without_portfolio#Ireland" title="Minister without portfolio">minister without portfolio</a> may be appointed to the Government who is not the head of a Department of State; this occurred in 1939 during the period of <a href="/wiki/The_Emergency_(Ireland)" title="The Emergency (Ireland)">the Emergency</a> when <a href="/wiki/Frank_Aiken" title="Frank Aiken">Frank Aiken</a> served as <a href="/wiki/Minister_for_the_Co-ordination_of_Defensive_Measures" title="Minister for the Co-ordination of Defensive Measures">Minister for the Co-ordination of Defensive Measures</a> until 1945.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-members_attending_cabinet">Non-members attending cabinet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Government_of_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Non-members attending cabinet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Non members have no voting rights at Cabinet but may otherwise participate fully and normally receive circulated Cabinet Papers on the same basis as a full member of Government.  Votes are rare however, with the cabinet usually following the Taoiseach or working by consensus.\n</p><p>The Government is advised by the <a href="/wiki/Attorney_General_of_Ireland" title="Attorney General of Ireland">Attorney General</a>, who is not formally a member of the Government, but who participates in cabinet meetings as part of their role as legal advisor to the Government.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Minister_of_State_at_the_Department_of_the_Taoiseach" title="Minister of State at the Department of the Taoiseach">Chief Whip</a> may attend meetings of the cabinet, but is not a member of the Government.<sup id="cite_ref-juniorministers_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-juniorministers-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In addition, the Government can choose other <a href="/wiki/Minister_of_State_(Ireland)" title="Minister of State (Ireland)">Ministers of State</a> (junior ministers), who may attend cabinet meetings. This person is informally known as a Super Junior Minister".<sup id="cite_ref-juniorministers_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-juniorministers-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The current (2016) Super Junior Ministers are <a href="/wiki/Paul_Kehoe" title="Paul Kehoe">Paul Kehoe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Finian_McGrath" title="Finian McGrath">Finian McGrath</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Term_of_office">Term of office</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Government_of_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Term of office">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Normally, the Government serves in office until the nomination of a new Taoiseach by D\xc3\xa1il \xc3\x89ireann.  The maximum term is 5 years by law, though the constitution allows seven.  Most governments in recent years have served 4\xe2\x80\x935 years.\n</p><p>The Government must enjoy the confidence of D\xc3\xa1il \xc3\x89ireann if it is to remain in office. If the Taoiseach ceases "to retain the support of a majority in D\xc3\xa1il \xc3\x89ireann", either D\xc3\xa1il \xc3\x89ireann must be <a href="/wiki/Dissolution_of_parliament" title="Dissolution of parliament">dissolved</a> or the Taoiseach must resign.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> This applies only in cases of a no-confidence vote or loss of supply (rejection of a budget), rather than a government bill being rejected.  The President may refuse to grant a dissolution to a Taoiseach who does not enjoy the support of the D\xc3\xa1il, thus forcing the resignation of the Taoiseach.\n</p><p>When the Taoiseach resigns, the entire Government is deemed to have resigned as a collective. However, in such a scenario, according to the Constitution, "the Taoiseach and the other members of the Government shall continue to carry on their duties until their successors shall have been appointed". The Taoiseach can also direct the President to dismiss or accept the resignation of individual ministers.\n</p><p>Upon the dissolution of D\xc3\xa1il \xc3\x89ireann, ministers are no longer members of the Oireachtas, and therefore at first glance ineligible for office. However, under a different clause in the Constitution, they "shall continue to hold office until their successors shall have been appointed".<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Authority_and_powers">Authority and powers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Government_of_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Authority and powers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Unlike the cabinets in other parliamentary systems, the Government is both the <i>de jure</i> and <i>de facto</i> executive authority in Ireland. The Constitution explicitly vests executive authority in the Government, not the President. In other parliamentary regimes, the head of state is usually the nominal chief executive, though bound by convention to act on the advice of the cabinet.\n</p><p>The executive authority of the Government is subject to certain limitations. In particular:\n</p>\n<ul><li>The state may not <a href="/wiki/Declaration_of_war" title="Declaration of war">declare war</a>, or participate in a war, without the consent of the D\xc3\xa1il \xc3\x89ireann. In the case of "actual invasion", however, "the Government may take whatever steps they may consider necessary for the protection of the State"<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Treaty" title="Treaty">Treaties</a> must be laid before D\xc3\xa1il \xc3\x89ireann.</li>\n<li>The Government must act in accordance with the Constitution.</li></ul>\n<p>Government ministers are collectively responsible for the actions of the government. Each minister is responsible for the actions of his or her department. Departments of State do not have legal personalities. Actions of departments are carried out under the title of ministers even, as is commonly the case, when the minister has little knowledge of the details of these actions. This contradicts the rule in <a href="/wiki/Common_law" title="Common law">common law</a> that a person given a statutory power cannot delegate that power.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> This leads to a phrase in correspondence by government departments, "the Minister has directed me to write", on letters or documents that the minister in question may never have seen.\n</p><p>The functions of government ministers are frequently transferred between departments during <a href="/wiki/Cabinet_reshuffle" title="Cabinet reshuffle">cabinet reshuffles</a> or after elections. On occasion, a ministerial position will cease to exist, as its powers are entirely transferred to another office. Such defunct ministerial positions include the Ministers for <a href="/wiki/Minister_for_Labour" title="Minister for Labour">Labour</a>, <a href="/wiki/Minister_for_Posts_and_Telegraphs" title="Minister for Posts and Telegraphs">Posts and Telegraphs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Minister_for_the_Public_Service" title="Minister for the Public Service">Public Service</a> and <a href="/wiki/Minister_for_Supplies" title="Minister for Supplies">Supplies</a>.\n</p><p>If the Government should fail to fulfill its constitutional duties, it may be ordered to do so by a court of law, by a writ of <a href="/wiki/Mandamus" title="Mandamus">mandamus</a>. Ministers who fail to comply may, ultimately, be found to be in <a href="/wiki/Contempt_of_court" title="Contempt of court">contempt of court</a>, and even imprisoned.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Government_of_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Government_of_the_9th_D%C3%A1il#1st_Government_of_Ireland" title="Government of the 9th D\xc3\xa1il">First Government of Ireland</a> took office on 29 December 1937 on the coming into force of the <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_Ireland" title="Constitution of Ireland">Constitution of Ireland</a>. It was preceded by the <a href="/wiki/Executive_Council_of_the_Irish_Free_State" title="Executive Council of the Irish Free State">Executive Council of the Irish Free State</a>, which operated from 1922 to 1937 as the <a href="/wiki/Executive_(government)" title="Executive (government)">executive</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Free_State" title="Irish Free State">Irish Free State</a>.\n</p><p>The detail and structure of the Government of Ireland had its legislative basis in the <i>Ministers and Secretaries Act, 1924</i>; it has been amended on a number occasions, and these may be cited together as the <a href="/wiki/Ministers_and_Secretaries_Acts" title="Ministers and Secretaries Acts">Ministers and Secretaries Acts 1924 to 2017</a> and are construed together as one Act.\n</p><p>All Governments from 1989 to 2016 were <a href="/wiki/Coalition_government" title="Coalition government">coalitions</a> of two or more parties. The first coalition government was formed in 1948. The Taoiseach has always been a member of the largest party in the coalition. The Taoiseach has almost always been the leader of that party, with <a href="/wiki/John_A._Costello" title="John A. Costello">John A. Costello</a>, Taoiseach from 1948 to 1951 and from 1954 to 1957, the only exception to this rule.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_service">Public service</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Government_of_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Public service">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Public_service_of_the_Republic_of_Ireland" title="Public service of the Republic of Ireland">Public service of the Republic of Ireland</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Gbuildings.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/Gbuildings.jpg/220px-Gbuildings.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/Gbuildings.jpg/330px-Gbuildings.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/fc/Gbuildings.jpg 2x" data-file-width="350" data-file-height="262" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Gbuildings.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Government_Buildings" title="Government Buildings">Government Buildings</a> in Dublin.</div></div></div>\n<p>The public service in Ireland refers to the totality of <a href="/wiki/Public_administration" title="Public administration">public administration</a> in Ireland. As of Q3, 2016 the total number of employees in the Irish public service stands at 304,472 people. The <a href="/wiki/Department_of_Public_Expenditure_and_Reform" title="Department of Public Expenditure and Reform">Department of Public Expenditure and Reform</a> defines the public service as comprising seven sectors: the <a href="/wiki/Civil_service_of_the_Republic_of_Ireland" class="mw-redirect" title="Civil service of the Republic of Ireland">Civil Service</a>, <a href="/wiki/Irish_Defence_Forces" class="mw-redirect" title="Irish Defence Forces">Defence Sector</a>, <a href="/wiki/Education_in_the_Republic_of_Ireland" title="Education in the Republic of Ireland">Education Sector</a>, <a href="/wiki/Healthcare_in_the_Republic_of_Ireland" title="Healthcare in the Republic of Ireland">Health Sector</a>, <a href="/wiki/Law_enforcement_in_the_Republic_of_Ireland" title="Law enforcement in the Republic of Ireland">Justice Sector</a>, <a href="/wiki/Local_government_in_the_Republic_of_Ireland" title="Local government in the Republic of Ireland">Local Authorities</a> and <a href="/wiki/State_Agencies_of_the_Republic_of_Ireland" title="State Agencies of the Republic of Ireland">Non-Commercial State Agencies</a>; such as <a href="/wiki/Bord_Bia" title="Bord Bia">Bord Bia</a>, <a href="/wiki/IDA_Ireland" title="IDA Ireland">IDA Ireland</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Commission_for_Energy_Regulation" class="mw-redirect" title="Commission for Energy Regulation">Commission for Energy Regulation</a>.  Commercial <a href="/wiki/State-sponsored_bodies_of_the_Republic_of_Ireland" title="State-sponsored bodies of the Republic of Ireland">state-owned bodies</a> such as <a href="/wiki/RT%C3%89" class="mw-redirect" title="RT\xc3\x89">RT\xc3\x89</a>, <a href="/wiki/ESB_Group" title="ESB Group">ESB Group</a> and <a href="/wiki/An_Post" title="An Post">An Post</a> are not considered part of the public service in Ireland.\n</p><p>The largest sector is the health sector with over 105,000 employees (largely in the <a href="/wiki/Health_Service_Executive" title="Health Service Executive">Health
