class="toclevel-1 tocsection-8"><a href="#External_links"><span class="tocnumber">7</span> <span class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Howard_Hodgkin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Gordon Howard Eliot Hodgkin was born on 6 August 1932<sup id="cite_ref-p-chron_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p-chron-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> in <a href="/wiki/Hammersmith" title="Hammersmith">Hammersmith</a>, London, the son of Eliot Hodgkin (1905\xe2\x80\x931973), a manager for the chemical company <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Chemical_Industries" title="Imperial Chemical Industries">ICI</a> and a noted amateur <a href="/wiki/Horticulture" title="Horticulture">horticulturist</a>, and his wife Katherine, a <a href="/wiki/Botanical_illustrator" title="Botanical illustrator">botanical illustrator</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-The_colour_of_emotion_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_colour_of_emotion-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> During the <a href="/wiki/Second_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Second World War">Second World War</a>, Eliot Hodgkin was an <a href="/wiki/RAF_officer_ranks" title="RAF officer ranks">RAF officer</a>, rising to Wing Commander, and was assistant to <a href="/wiki/Sefton_Delmer" title="Sefton Delmer">Sefton Delmer</a> in running his <a href="/wiki/Black_propaganda" title="Black propaganda">black propaganda</a> campaign against Nazi Germany.<sup id="cite_ref-Richards_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Richards-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>His maternal grandfather <a href="/wiki/Gordon_Hewart,_1st_Viscount_Hewart" title="Gordon Hewart, 1st Viscount Hewart">Gordon Hewart, 1st Viscount Hewart</a> was a <a href="/wiki/Journalist" title="Journalist">journalist</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lawyer" title="Lawyer">lawyer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Member_of_parliament" title="Member of parliament">MP</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lord_Chief_Justice_of_England_and_Wales" title="Lord Chief Justice of England and Wales">Lord Chief Justice</a>; and the scientist <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Hodgkin" title="Thomas Hodgkin">Thomas Hodgkin</a> was his great-great-grandfather\'s older brother.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Hodgkin was a cousin of the English <a href="/wiki/Still_life" title="Still life">still life</a> painter <a href="/wiki/Eliot_Hodgkin" title="Eliot Hodgkin">Eliot Hodgkin</a> (1905\xe2\x80\x931987),<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>During the Second World War, Hodgkin was evacuated with his mother and sister to the USA, where they lived on <a href="/wiki/Long_Island" title="Long Island">Long Island</a>, New York.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC_obit_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC_obit-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> On returning, he was educated at <a href="/wiki/Eton_College" title="Eton College">Eton College</a><sup id="cite_ref-Howard_Hodgkin_interview_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Howard_Hodgkin_interview-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> and then at <a href="/wiki/Bryanston_School" title="Bryanston School">Bryanston School</a> in <a href="/wiki/Dorset" title="Dorset">Dorset</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-p-chron_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p-chron-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> He had decided on a career in art in early childhood and ran away from school to pursue this.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC_obit_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC_obit-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>He studied at the <a href="/wiki/Camberwell_College_of_Arts" title="Camberwell College of Arts">Camberwell Art School</a><sup id="cite_ref-Howard_Hodgkin_interview_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Howard_Hodgkin_interview-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> and later at the <a href="/wiki/Bath_Academy_of_Art" class="mw-redirect" title="Bath Academy of Art">Bath Academy of Art</a> in <a href="/wiki/Corsham" title="Corsham">Corsham</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-p-chron_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p-chron-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> where <a href="/wiki/Edward_Piper" title="Edward Piper">Edward Piper</a> studied drawing under him.<sup id="cite_ref-Handbook_of_Modern_British_Painting_and_Printmaking_1900-1990_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Handbook_of_Modern_British_Painting_and_Printmaking_1900-1990-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-The_Artist_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Artist-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Swimming_poster_by_Howard_Hodgkin.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" href="#cite_note-baacorsham-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1980, Hodgkin was invited by <a href="/wiki/John_Hoyland" title="John Hoyland">John Hoyland</a> to exhibit work as part of the Hayward Annual at the <a href="/wiki/Hayward_Gallery" title="Hayward Gallery">Hayward Gallery</a> along with <a href="/wiki/Gillian_Ayres" title="Gillian Ayres">Gillian Ayres</a>, <a href="/wiki/Basil_Beattie" title="Basil Beattie">Basil Beattie</a>, <a href="/wiki/Terry_Setch" title="Terry Setch">Terry Setch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Caro" title="Anthony Caro">Anthony Caro</a>, <b>Pulcinella</b> in 1987. \n</p><p><br />\nIn 1984, Hodgkin represented Britain at the <a href="/wiki/Venice_Biennale" title="Venice Biennale">Venice Biennale</a>, in 1985 he won the <a href="/wiki/Turner_Prize" title="Turner Prize">Turner Prize</a>, and in 1992 he was <a href="/wiki/Knight" title="Knight">knighted</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-The_colour_of_emotion_2-1" class="reference"><a 2x" data-file-width="1280" data-file-height="720" /></a></div>\n<p>Hodgkin was invited to design a mural for the front of Charles Correa\'s 1992 headquarters for the <a href="/wiki/British_Council,_India" title="British Council, India">British Council in India</a>.  Hodgkin\'s mural is of a banyan tree spreading its branches across the walls.  It is a symbol of the British Council\'s work rooted in the Indian cultural scene.  Hodgkin said of Correa:    \xe2\x80\x9cCharles Correa is the most perfect architect you could imagine. He first suggested that I think about the mural as an Indian flag turning into a Union Jack. I said no.\xe2\x80\x9d <sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1995, Hodgkin printed the <i>Venetian Views</i> series, which depict the same view of <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">Venice</a> at four different times of day. <i>Venice, Afternoon</i> \xe2\x80\x93 one of the four prints \xe2\x80\x93 uses 16 sheets, or fragments, in a hugely complex printing process that creates a colourful, painterly effect. This piece was given to the <a href="/wiki/Yale_Center_for_British_Art" title="Yale Center for British Art">Yale Centre of British Art</a> in June 2006 by its Israeli family owners in order to complement the museum\'s already-impressive collection of Hodgkins.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>A major exhibition of his work was mounted at <a href="/wiki/Tate_Britain" title="Tate Britain">Tate Britain</a>, London, in 2006. Also in 2006, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Independent" title="The Independent">The Independent</a></i> declared him one of the 100 most influential gay people in Britain, as his work has helped many people express their emotions to others.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In September 2010, Hodgkin and five other British artists, <a href="/wiki/John_Hoyland" title="John Hoyland">John Hoyland</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Walker_(painter)" title="John Walker (painter)">John Walker</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ian_Stephenson" title="Ian Stephenson">Ian Stephenson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Patrick_Caulfield" title="Patrick Caulfield">Patrick Caulfield</a> and <a href="/wiki/R.B._Kitaj" class="mw-redirect" title="R.B. Kitaj">R.B. Kitaj</a>, were in an exhibition entitled <i>The Independent Eye: Contemporary British Art From the Collection of Samuel and Gabrielle Lurie,</i> at the <a href="/wiki/Yale_Center_for_British_Art" title="Yale Center for British Art">Yale Center for British Art</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Before his death on 9 March 2017 he was working on two UK exhibitions, one at the Hepworth Wakefield,<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> and another at the National Portrait Gallery.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>His prints were hand-painted etchings and he worked with the master printer Jack Shirreff at 107 Workshop.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>A feature of his painting was that he made the frame intrinsic to the work " incorporated physically into the painting as part of its making, or created as an illusion to give definition to his subject." <sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/National_Life_Stories" title="National Life Stories">National Life Stories</a> conducted an oral history interview (C466/286) with Howard Hodgkin between 2008 and 2017 for its Architects Lives\' collection held by the British Library.<sup id="cite_ref-oralhistory_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oralhistory-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Honours">Honours</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Howard_Hodgkin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Honours">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Hodgkin was awarded the CBE in 1977, and he was knighted in 1992.<sup id="cite_ref-The_colour_of_emotion_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_colour_of_emotion-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> He received an honorary fellowship from the London Institute in 1999.<sup id="cite_ref-The_colour_of_emotion_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_colour_of_emotion-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> In 2000, he was awarded an honorary <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Letters" title="Doctor of Letters">DLitt</a> by the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Oxford" title="University of Oxford">University of Oxford</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-The_colour_of_emotion_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_colour_of_emotion-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> He was made a Companion of Honour in the <a href="/wiki/2003_New_Year_Honours" title="2003 New Year Honours">2003 New Year Honours</a> for his services to art.<sup id="cite_ref-New_Year_Honours_-_part_4_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-New_Year_Honours_-_part_4-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life_and_death">Personal life and death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Howard_Hodgkin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Personal life and death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In 1955, Hodgkin married Julia Lane, by whom he had two children.<sup id="cite_ref-baacorsham_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-baacorsham-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Hodgkin knew he was gay, even when he married, and later left his wife. In 2009, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Independent" title="The Independent">The Independent</a></i> reported that he had been with his partner, the music writer <a href="/wiki/Antony_Peattie" title="Antony Peattie">Antony Peattie</a>, for 20 years.<sup id="cite_ref-independent_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-independent-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> They lived in a four-storey Georgian house in <a href="/wiki/Bloomsbury" title="Bloomsbury">Bloomsbury</a>, near the <a href="/wiki/British_Museum" title="British Museum">British Museum</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>On 9 March 2017, Hodgkin died at the age of 84 in a hospital in London.<sup id="cite_ref-theguardian_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-theguardian-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-telegraph_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-telegraph-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> Tributes to him were made by several figures in British art, including <a href="/wiki/Tate" title="Tate">Tate</a> director <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_Serota" title="Nicholas Serota">Nicholas Serota</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC_obit_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC_obit-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Howard_Hodgkin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist columns references-column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 30em;
