Mikhail Kapoor</b>, <span class="noexcerpt" style="font-size:100%;"><a href="/wiki/Commander_of_the_Order_of_the_British_Empire" class="mw-redirect" title="Commander of the Order of the British Empire">CBE</a>,&#32;<a href="/wiki/Royal_Academician" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal Academician">RA</a></span> (born 12&#160;March 1954) is a British<sup id="cite_ref-Nationality_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nationality-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Sculpture" title="Sculpture">sculptor</a>. Born in <a href="/wiki/Bombay" class="mw-redirect" title="Bombay">Bombay</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Kapoor has lived and worked in London since the early 1970s when he moved to study art, first at the <a href="/wiki/Hornsey_College_of_Art" title="Hornsey College of Art">Hornsey College of Art</a> and later at the <a href="/wiki/Chelsea_School_of_Art_and_Design" class="mw-redirect" title="Chelsea School of Art and Design">Chelsea School of Art and Design</a>.\n</p><p>He represented Britain in the XLIV <a href="/wiki/Venice_Biennale" title="Venice Biennale">Venice Biennale</a> in 1990, when he was awarded the Premio Duemila Prize. In 1991, he received the <a href="/wiki/Turner_Prize" title="Turner Prize">Turner Prize</a> and in 2002 received the Unilever Commission for the Turbine Hall at <a href="/wiki/Tate_Modern" title="Tate Modern">Tate Modern</a>. Notable public sculptures include <i><a href="/wiki/Cloud_Gate" title="Cloud Gate">Cloud Gate</a></i> (colloquially known as "the Bean") in Chicago\'s <a href="/wiki/Millennium_Park_(Chicago)" class="mw-redirect" title="Millennium Park (Chicago)">Millennium Park</a>; <i><a href="/wiki/Sky_Mirror" title="Sky Mirror">Sky Mirror</a></i>, exhibited at the <a href="/wiki/Rockefeller_Center" title="Rockefeller Center">Rockefeller Center</a> in New York City in 2006 and <a href="/wiki/Kensington_Gardens" title="Kensington Gardens">Kensington Gardens</a> in London in 2010;<sup id="cite_ref-serpentinegalleries.org_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-serpentinegalleries.org-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Tees_Valley_Giants" title="Tees Valley Giants">Temenos</a></i>, at Middlehaven, <a href="/wiki/Middlesbrough" title="Middlesbrough">Middlesbrough</a>; <i>Leviathan</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-anishkapoor.com_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-anishkapoor.com-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> at the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Palais" title="Grand Palais">Grand Palais</a> in Paris in 2011; and <i><a href="/wiki/ArcelorMittal_Orbit" title="ArcelorMittal Orbit">ArcelorMittal Orbit</a></i>, commissioned as a permanent artwork for London\'s <a href="/wiki/Olympic_Park,_London" class="mw-redirect" title="Olympic Park, London">Olympic Park</a> and completed in 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> In 2017 Kapoor designed the statuette for the <a href="/wiki/2018_Brit_Awards" title="2018 Brit Awards">2018 Brit Awards</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Kapoor received a <a href="/wiki/Knight_Bachelor" title="Knight Bachelor">knighthood</a> in the 2013 <a href="/wiki/Birthday_Honours" title="Birthday Honours">Birthday Honours</a> for services to visual arts. He was awarded an honorary doctorate degree from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Oxford" title="University of Oxford">University of Oxford</a> in 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> In 2012, he was awarded the <a href="/wiki/Padma_Bhushan" title="Padma Bhushan">Padma Bhushan</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Indian_government" class="mw-redirect" title="Indian government">Indian government</a> which is India\'s third-highest civilian award.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> An image of Kapoor features in the <a href="/wiki/British_culture" class="mw-redirect" title="British culture">British cultural icons</a> section of the newly designed <a href="/wiki/British_passport" title="British passport">British passport</a> in 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> In 2016, he was announced as a recipient of the <a href="/wiki/LennonOno_Grant_for_Peace" title="LennonOno Grant for Peace">LennonOno Grant for Peace</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> In February 2017 Kapoor, who is <a href="/wiki/Jewish" class="mw-redirect" title="Jewish">Jewish</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Jeffries_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jeffries-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> was announced as the recipient of the USD$1 million <a href="/wiki/Genesis_Prize" title="Genesis Prize">Genesis Prize</a>, which "recognises individuals who have attained excellence and international renown in their fields and whose actions and achievements express a commitment to Jewish values, the Jewish community and the State of Israel".<sup id="cite_ref-Times_of_Israel_14-0" class="reference"><a class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anish_Kapoor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Anish Kapoor was born in Bombay, India (now <a href="/wiki/Mumbai" title="Mumbai">Mumbai</a>), to a <a href="/wiki/Jewish" class="mw-redirect" title="Jewish">Jewish</a> mother<sup id="cite_ref-Jeffries_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jeffries-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> and a <a href="/wiki/Hindu" title="Hindu">Hindu</a> father. His maternal grandfather served as <a href="/wiki/Hazzan" title="Hazzan">cantor</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Synagogue" title="Synagogue">synagogue</a> in <a href="/wiki/Pune" title="Pune">Pune</a>. At this time, <a href="/wiki/Baghdadi_Jews" title="Baghdadi Jews">Baghdadi Jews</a> constituted the majority of the <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Jews_in_Mumbai" title="History of the Jews in Mumbai">Jewish community in Mumbai</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-WeinerJulia_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WeinerJulia-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> His father was a <a href="/wiki/Hydrographer" class="mw-redirect" title="Hydrographer">hydrographer</a> and applied physicist who served in the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Navy" title="Indian Navy">Indian Navy</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Higgins_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Higgins-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Kapoor is the brother of Canada-based academic <a href="/wiki/Ilan_Kapoor" title="Ilan Kapoor">Ilan Kapoor</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Kapoor attended <a href="/wiki/The_Doon_School" title="The Doon School">The Doon School</a>, an all-boys <a href="/wiki/Boarding_school" title="Boarding school">boarding school</a> in <a href="/wiki/Dehradun" title="Dehradun">Dehradun</a>, then in the state of <a href="/wiki/Uttar_Pradesh" title="Uttar Pradesh">Uttar Pradesh</a>, India. He is said to have "hated" his time at Doon.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> In 1971 he moved to Israel with one of his two brothers, initially living on a <a href="/wiki/Kibbutz" title="Kibbutz">kibbutz</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Lunch_with_the_FT:_Anish_Kapoor_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lunch_with_the_FT:_Anish_Kapoor-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> He began to study <a href="/wiki/Electrical_engineering" title="Electrical engineering">electrical engineering</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-WeinerJulia_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WeinerJulia-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> but had trouble with mathematics and quit after six months.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> In Israel, he decided to become an artist.<sup id="cite_ref-WeinerJulia_17-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WeinerJulia-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> In 1973, he left for Britain to attend <a href="/wiki/Hornsey_College_of_Art" title="Hornsey College of Art">Hornsey College of Art</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chelsea_School_of_Art_and_Design" class="mw-redirect" title="Chelsea School of Art and Design">Chelsea School of Art and Design</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Higgins_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Higgins-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> There he found a role model in <a href="/wiki/Paul_Neagu" title="Paul Neagu">Paul Neagu</a>, an artist who provided a meaning to what he was doing.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> Kapoor went on to teach at <a href="/wiki/Wolverhampton_Polytechnic" class="mw-redirect" title="Wolverhampton Polytechnic">Wolverhampton Polytechnic</a> in 1979 and in 1982 was Artist in Residence at the <a href="/wiki/Walker_Art_Gallery" title="Walker Art Gallery">Walker Art Gallery</a>, Liverpool. He has lived and worked in London since the early 1970s.<sup id="cite_ref-royalacademy_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-royalacademy-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anish_Kapoor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Kapoor became known in the 1980s for his geometric or biomorphic sculptures using simple materials such as <a href="/wiki/Granite" title="Granite">granite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marble" title="Marble">marble</a>, pigment and <a href="/wiki/Plaster" title="Plaster">plaster</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-publicartfund.org_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-publicartfund.org-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> These early sculptures are frequently simple, curved forms, usually monochromatic and brightly coloured, using powder <a href="/wiki/Pigment" title="Pigment">pigment</a> to define and permeate the form. "While making the pigment pieces, it occurred to me that they all form themselves out of each other. So I decided to give them a generic title, <i>A Thousand Names</i>, implying <a href="/wiki/Infinity" title="Infinity">infinity</a>, a thousand being a symbolic number. The powder works sat on the floor or projected from the wall. The powder on the floor defines the surface of the floor and the objects appear to be partially submerged, like icebergs. That seems to fit inside the idea of something being partially there."<sup id="cite_ref-bombsite1990_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bombsite1990-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> Such use of pigment characterised his first high-profile exhibit as part of the <i>New Sculpture</i> exhibition at the <a href="/wiki/Hayward_Gallery" title="Hayward Gallery">Hayward Gallery</a> London in 1978.\n</p><p>In the late 1980s and 1990s, Kapoor was acclaimed for his explorations of matter and non-matter, specifically evoking the void in both free-standing sculptural works and ambitious installations. Many of his sculptures seem to recede into the distance, disappear into the ground or distort the space around them. In 1987, he began working in stone.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> His later stone works are made of solid, quarried stone, many of which have carved apertures and cavities, often alluding to, and playing with dualities (earth-sky, matter-spirit, lightness-darkness, visible-invisible, conscious-unconscious, male-female, and body-mind). "In the end, I\xe2\x80\x99m talking about myself. And thinking about making nothing, which I see as a void. But then that\xe2\x80\x99s something, even though it really is nothing."<sup id="cite_ref-bombsite1990_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bombsite1990-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Since 1995, he has worked with the highly reflective surface of polished <a href="/wiki/Stainless_steel" title="Stainless steel">stainless steel</a>. These works are <a href="/wiki/Mirror" title="Mirror">mirror-like</a>, reflecting or distorting the viewer and surroundings. Over the course of the following decade Kapoor\'s sculptures ventured into more ambitious manipulations of form and space. He produced a number of large works, including <i>Taratantara </i>(1999),<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> a 35-metre-high piece installed in the Baltic Flour Mills in <a href="/wiki/Gateshead" title="Gateshead">Gateshead</a>, England, before renovation began there; and <i><a href="/wiki/Marsyas_(sculpture)" title="Marsyas (sculpture)">Marsyas</a></i> (2002), a large work consisting of three steel rings joined by a single span of <a href="/wiki/Polyvinyl_chloride" title="Polyvinyl chloride">PVC</a> membrane that reached end to end of the 3,400-square-foot (320&#160;m<sup>2</sup>) Turbine Hall of <a href="/wiki/Tate_Modern" title="Tate Modern">Tate Modern</a>. Kapoor\'s <i>Eye in Stone</i> (Norwegian: <i>\xc3\x98ye i stein</i>) is permanently placed at the shore of the <a href="/wiki/Fjord" title="Fjord">fjord</a> in <a href="/wiki/L%C3%B8dingen" title="L\xc3\xb8dingen">L\xc3\xb8dingen</a> in northern Norway as part of <a href="/wiki/Artscape_Nordland" title="Artscape Nordland">Artscape Nordland</a>. In 2000, one of Kapoor\'s works, <i>Parabolic Waters</i>, consisting of rapidly rotating coloured water, was shown outside the <a href="/wiki/Millennium_Dome" title="Millennium Dome">Millennium Dome</a> in London.\n</p><p>The use of red <a href="/wiki/Wax" title="Wax">wax</a> is also part of his repertoire, evocative of flesh, blood, and transfiguration. In 2007, he showed <i>Svayambh</i> (which translated from <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">Sanskrit</a> means "self-generated"), a 1.5-metre block of red wax that moved on rails through the <a href="/wiki/Nantes" title="Nantes">Nantes</a> <a href="/wiki/Mus%C3%A9e_des_Beaux-Arts_de_Nantes" title="Mus\xc3\xa9e des Beaux-Arts de Nantes">Mus\xc3\xa9e des Beaux-Arts</a> as part of the Biennale estuaire; this piece was shown again in a major show at the <a href="/wiki/Haus_der_Kunst" title="Haus der Kunst">Haus der Kunst</a> in Munich and in 2009 at the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Academy" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal Academy">Royal Academy</a> in London.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> Some his work blurs the boundaries between architecture and art. In 2008, Kapoor created <i>Memory</i> in <a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">Berlin</a> and New York for the <a href="/wiki/John_Simon_Guggenheim_Memorial_Foundation" title="John Simon Guggenheim Memorial Foundation">Guggenheim Foundation</a>, his first piece in <a href="/wiki/Cor-Ten" class="mw-redirect" title="Cor-Ten">Cor-Ten</a>, which is formulated to produce a protective coating of rust.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> Weighing 24 tons and made up of 156 parts, it calls to mind <a href="/wiki/Richard_Serra" title="Richard Serra">Richard Serra</a>\xe2\x80\x99s huge, rusty steel works, which also invite viewers into perceptually confounding interiors.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 2009, Kapoor became the first Guest Artistic Director of <a href="/wiki/Brighton_Festival" title="Brighton Festival">Brighton Festival</a>. Kapoor installed four sculptures during the festival: <i><a href="/wiki/Sky_Mirror" title="Sky Mirror">Sky Mirror</a></i> at <a href="/wiki/Brighton_Pavilion" class="mw-redirect" title="Brighton Pavilion">Brighton Pavilion</a> gardens; <i>C-Curve</i><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> at <a href="/wiki/Chattri_(Brighton)" class="mw-redirect" title="Chattri (Brighton)">The Chattri</a>, <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blood_Relations_(book)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Blood Relations (book) (page does not exist)">Blood Relations</a></i> (a collaboration with author <a href="/wiki/Salman_Rushdie" title="Salman Rushdie">Salman Rushdie</a>); and <i>1000 Names</i>, both at Fabrica. He also created a large site-specific work titled <i>The Dismemberment of Jeanne d\xe2\x80\x99Arc</i> and a performance-based installation: <i>Imagined Monochrome</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> The public response was so overwhelming that police had to re-divert traffic around <i>C Curve</i> at the Chattri and exercise crowd control.\n</p><p>In September 2009, Kapoor was the first living artist to have a solo exhibition at the Royal Academy of Arts. As well as surveying his career to date, the show also included new works. On display were <i>Non-Object</i> mirror works, cement sculptures previously unseen, and <i>Shooting into the Corner</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> a cannon that fires pellets of wax into the corner of the gallery. Previously shown at MAK, Vienna, in January 2009, it is a work with dramatic presence and associations and  also continues Kapoor\'s interest in the self-made object, as the wax builds up on the walls and floor of the gallery the work slowly oozes out its form.\n</p><p>In early 2011, Kapoor\'s work, <i>Leviathan</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-anishkapoor.com_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-anishkapoor.com-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> was the annual <i>Monumenta</i> installation for the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Palais" title="Grand Palais">Grand Palais</a> in Paris.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> Kapoor described the work as: "A single object, a single form, a single colour...My ambition is to create a space with in a space that responds to the height and luminosity of the Nave at the Grand Palais. Visitors will be invited to walk inside the work, to immerse themselves in colour, and it will, I hope, be a contemplative and poetic experience."\n</p><p>In 2011, Kapoor exhibited <i>Dirty Corner</i> at the Fabbrica del Vapore in <a href="/wiki/Milan" title="Milan">Milan</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> Fully occupying the site\'s "cathedral" space, the work consists of a huge steel volume, 60 metres long and 8 metres high, that visitors enter. Inside, they gradually lose their perception of space, as it gets progressively darker and darker until there is no light, forcing people to use their other senses to guide them through the space. The entrance of the tunnel is goblet-shaped, featuring an interior and exterior surface that is circular, making minimal contact with the ground. Over the course of the exhibition, the work was progressively covered by some 160 cubic metres of earth by a large mechanical device, forming a sharp mountain of dirt which the tunnel appears to be running through.\n</p><p>In 2016, his art exposition in <a href="/wiki/Museo_Universitario_Arte_Contempor%C3%A1neo" title="Museo Universitario Arte Contempor\xc3\xa1neo">MUAC</a> (<a href="/wiki/Mexico_City" title="Mexico City">Mexico City</a>) was a success, with literary contributions from Catherine Lampert, Cecilia Delgado, and Mexican writer <a href="/wiki/Pablo_Soler_Frost" title="Pablo Soler Frost">Pablo Soler Frost</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" Regeneration</a> (TVR) to produce five pieces of public art, collectively known as the <i><a href="/wiki/Tees_Valley_Giants" title="Tees Valley Giants">Tees Valley Giants</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> The first of these sculptures, <i>Tememos</i>, was unveiled to the public in June 2010. <i>Temenos</i> stands 50 metres high and is 110 metres in length. A steel wire mesh pulled taut between two enormous steel hoops, it remains an ethereal and an uncertain form despite its colossal scale.\n</p><p>In 2010, <i>Turning the World Upside Down, Jerusalem</i> was commissioned and installed at the <a href="/wiki/Israel_Museum" title="Israel Museum">Israel Museum</a> in <a href="/wiki/Jerusalem" title="Jerusalem">Jerusalem</a>.  The sculpture is described as a "16-foot tall polished-steel hourglass" and it "reflects and reverses the Jerusalem sky and the museum\'s landscape, a likely reference to the city\'s duality of celestial and earthly, holy and profane".<sup id="cite_ref-Bronner_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bronner-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Also in June, Kapoor\'s <i>Orbit</i><sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> was announced as the winning proposal for an artwork for the <a href="/wiki/2012_Olympic_Games" class="mw-redirect" title="2012 Olympic Games">2012 Olympic Games</a>. The Greater London Authority selected Kapoor\'s sculpture from a shortlist of five artists as the permanent artwork for the Olympic Park. At 115 metres tall, <i>Orbit</i> is the tallest sculpture in the UK.\n</p><p>A granite monument, a memorial garden, is to commemorate the British victims of <a href="/wiki/9/11" class="mw-redirect" title="9/11">9/11</a> in New York\xe2\x80\x99s <a href="/wiki/Hanover_Square_(Manhattan)" title="Hanover Square (Manhattan)">Hanover Square</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>When asked if engagement with people and places is the key to successful public art, Kapoor said:\n</p>\n<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>I\xe2\x80\x99m thinking about the mythical wonders of the world, the Hanging Gardens of Babylon and the Tower of Babel. It\xe2\x80\x99s as if the collective will comes up with something that has resonance on an individual level and so becomes mythic. I can claim to take that as a model for a way of thinking. Art can do it, and I\xe2\x80\x99m going to have a damn good go. I want to occupy the territory, but the territory is an idea and a way of thinking as much as a context that generates objects.\n</p></blockquote>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Architectural_projects">Architectural projects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anish_Kapoor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Architectural projects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Throughout his career, Kapoor has worked extensively with architects and engineers. id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> installed in New Zealand at the <a href="/wiki/Gibbs_Farm" title="Gibbs Farm">Gibbs Farm</a> sculpture park, owned by id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> New York, in collaboration with architects <a href="/wiki/Herzog_and_de_Meuron" class="mw-redirect" title="Herzog and de Meuron">Herzog and de Meuron</a>.</li>\n<li>Two subway stations in <a href="/wiki/Naples" title="Naples">Naples</a> at Monte San Angelo<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a (1995) in collaboration with Philip Gumuchdjian.</li>\n<li>Building for a Void,<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> created for Expo \'92, Seville, in noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Anish_Kapoor&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup>), Kapoor has said:\n</p>\n<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>It\xe2\x80\x99s very vulva-like. The tradition of the Paris or Moscow metro is of palaces of light, underground. I wanted to do exactly the opposite&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 to acknowledge that we are going underground. So it\xe2\x80\x99s dark, and what I\xe2\x80\x99ve done is bring the tunnel up and roll it over as a form like a sock.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup>\n</p></blockquote>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Working_with_text">Working with text</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anish_Kapoor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Working with text">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>A collaboration with author Salman Rushdie, Kapoor  conceived a sculpture consisting of two bronze boxes conjoined with red wax and inscribed around the outside with the first two paragraphs of Rushdie\'s text; "Blood Relations"<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> or an "Interrogation of the Arabian Nights" in 2006.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_projects">Other projects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anish_Kapoor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Other projects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Kapoor has designed <a href="/wiki/Stage_set" class="mw-redirect" title="Stage set">stage sets</a> that include: the opera <i><a href="/wiki/Idomeneo" title="Idomeneo">Idomeneo</a></i> at Glyndebourne in 2003; <i><a href="/wiki/Pell%C3%A9as_et_M%C3%A9lisande_(opera)" title="Pell\xc3\xa9as et M\xc3\xa9lisande (opera)">Pell\xc3\xa9as et M\xc3\xa9lisande</a></i> for <a href="/wiki/La_Monnaie" title="La Monnaie">La Monnaie</a> in Brussels, and a dance-theatre piece called <i>in-i</i> for <a href="/wiki/Akram_Khan_(dancer)" title="Akram Khan (dancer)">Akram Khan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Juliette_Binoche" title="Juliette Binoche">Juliette Binoche</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Royal_National_Theatre" title="Royal National Theatre">National Theatre</a> in London.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vantablack_controversy">Vantablack controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anish_Kapoor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Vantablack controversy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In 2014 he began working with <a href="/wiki/Vantablack" title="Vantablack">Vantablack</a>, a substance thought to be one of the least reflective substances known. His exclusive licence to the material has been criticized, but he has defended the agreement, saying: "Why exclusive? Because it\'s a collaboration, because I am wanting to push them to a certain use for it. I\'ve collaborated with people who make things out of stainless steel for years and that\'s exclusive."<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Vantablack S-VIS, a sprayable paint that uses randomly-aligned carbon nanotubes and only has high absorption in the <a href="/wiki/Visible_light" class="mw-redirect" title="Visible light">visible light</a> band, also called the "blackest black" color, has been <a href="/wiki/Exclusive_license" class="mw-redirect" title="Exclusive license">exclusively licensed</a> to Anish Kapoor\'s studio for artistic use.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup> Kapoor appropriating the "blackest black" for himself has caused outrage among many artists, including <a href="/wiki/Christian_Furr" title="Christian Furr">Christian Furr</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stuart_Semple" title="Stuart Semple">Stuart Semple</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> In retaliation, Semple developed the "pinkest pink" color and made it available to everyone, except Anish Kapoor and anyone affiliated with him.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup> He later stated that the move was itself like performance art and that he did not anticipate the amount of attention it received.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_65-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup> In December 2016, Kapoor posted an Instagram post of his <a href="/wiki/Middle_finger" title="Middle finger">middle finger</a> dipped in Semple\'s pink which he had acquired.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup> Semple developed more products such as "Black 2.0", which has similar qualities to Vantablack despite being acrylic, and "Diamond Dust" which is made of glass shards and comes with a warning to not get glass shards in your fingers or hands.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup> Nanolab, a <a href="/wiki/Waltham,_Massachusetts" title="Waltham, Massachusetts">Waltham, Massachusetts</a>-based carbon nanotube manufacturer, partnered with <a href="/wiki/Boston,_Massachusetts" class="mw-redirect" title="Boston, Massachusetts">Boston</a> artist Jason Chase to release a nanotube-based black paint called Singularity Black.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup> During the first showing of the colour, Chase, alluding to Vantablack, stated that "its possibilities have been stunted by not being available to experiment with," and Singularity Black\'s release was important to create access.<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Exhibitions">Exhibitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anish_Kapoor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Exhibitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Kapoor initially began exhibiting as part of <a href="/wiki/New_British_Sculpture" title="New British Sculpture">New British Sculpture</a> art scene, along with fellow British sculptors <a href="/wiki/Tony_Cragg" title="Tony Cragg">Tony Cragg</a> and <a href="/wiki/Richard_Deacon_(sculptor)" title="Richard Deacon (sculptor)">Richard Deacon</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-publicartfund.org_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-publicartfund.org-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> His first solo exhibition took place at Patrice Alexandra, Paris, in 1980.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup> He achieved widespread recognition when he represented Britain at the 1990 <a href="/wiki/Venice_Biennale" title="Venice Biennale">Venice Biennale</a><sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup>, and recounts the experience in <a href="/wiki/Sarah_Thornton" title="Sarah Thornton">Sarah Thornton</a>\'s <i>Seven Days in the Art World</i><sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup>. In 1992 Kapoor contributed to <a href="/wiki/Documenta" title="Documenta">documenta IX</a> with the <i>Building Descent into Limbo</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup> In 2004, he participated in The 5th <a href="/wiki/Gwangju_Biennale" title="Gwangju Biennale">Gwangju Biennale</a> in Gwangju, Korea. Solo exhibitions of his work have since been held in the <a href="/wiki/Tate" title="Tate">Tate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hayward_Gallery" title="Hayward Gallery">Hayward Gallery</a> in London, <a href="/wiki/Kunsthalle_Basel" title="Kunsthalle Basel">Kunsthalle Basel</a> in <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Museo_Nacional_Centro_de_Arte_Reina_Sof%C3%ADa" title="Museo Nacional Centro de Arte Reina Sof\xc3\xada">Reina Sofia</a> in Madrid, the <a href="/wiki/National_Gallery_of_Canada" title="National Gallery of Canada">National Gallery of Canada</a> in <a href="/wiki/Ottawa" title="Ottawa">Ottawa</a>, Musee des arts contemporains (Grand-Hornu) in Belgium, the <a href="/wiki/CAPC_Museum_of_Contemporary_Art" class="mw-redirect" title="CAPC Museum of Contemporary Art">CAPC Museum of Contemporary Art</a> in <a href="/wiki/Bordeaux" title="Bordeaux">Bordeaux</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Centro_Cultural_Banco_do_Brasil" title="Centro Cultural Banco do Brasil">Centro Cultural Banco do Brasil</a> in Brazil, and the <a href="/wiki/Guggenheim_Museum_Bilbao" title="Guggenheim Museum Bilbao">Guggenheim</a> in <a href="/wiki/Bilbao" title="Bilbao">Bilbao</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> and <a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">Berlin</a>.\n</p><p>In 2008, the <a href="/wiki/Institute_of_Contemporary_Art,_Boston" title="Institute of Contemporary Art, Boston">Institute of Contemporary Art</a> in <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a> exhibited Kapoor\'s first US mid-career survey.<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">&#91;75&#93;</a></sup> That same year, Kapoor\xe2\x80\x99s <i>Islamic Mirror</i> (2008), a circular concave mirror, was installed in a 13th-century Arab palace now being used as by the Convent of Santa Clara in Murcia.<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Kapoor was the first living British artist to take over the Royal Academy, London, in 2009;<sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">&#91;77&#93;</a></sup> the show attracted 275,000 visitors, making it the most successful exhibition ever by a living artist in London.<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">&#91;78&#93;</a></sup> This show subsequently travelled to the Guggenheim Museum Bilbao. In 2010, Kapoor retrospective exhibitions were held at the National Gallery of Modern Art (NGMA) in New Delhi and Mumbai\xe2\x80\x99s <a href="/wiki/Mehboob_Studio" title="Mehboob Studio">Mehboob Studio</a>, the first showcase of his work in the country of his birth.<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">&#91;79&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80">&#91;80&#93;</a></sup> In 2011 Kapoor has had a solo touring exhibition with the Arts Council, part of their "Flashback " series of shows. In May he exhibited <i>Leviathan</i> at the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Palais" title="Grand Palais">Grand Palais</a>, and two concurrent shows in Milan at the <a href="/wiki/Rotonda_della_Besana" title="Rotonda della Besana">Rotonda della Besana</a> and Fabbrica del Vapore.  He had a major exhibition at the <a href="/wiki/Museum_of_Contemporary_Art,_Sydney" class="mw-redirect" title="Museum of Contemporary Art, Sydney">Museum of Contemporary Art, Sydney</a> (MCA) from December 2012 to April 2013 as part of the Sydney International Art Series.<sup id="cite_ref-81" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-81">&#91;81&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><i>Dirty Corner</i>, exhibited at the <a href="/wiki/Palace_of_Versailles" title="Palace of Versailles">Palace of Versailles</a> in 2015, was a topic of controversy due to its "blatantly sexual" nature.
