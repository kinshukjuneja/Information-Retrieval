2013</div></div></div>\n<p>The <b>total fertility rate</b>  (<b>TFR</b>), sometimes also called the <b>fertility rate</b>, <b>absolute/potential natality</b>,  <b>period total fertility rate</b> (<b>PTFR</b>), or <b>total period fertility rate</b> (<b>TPFR</b>) of a population is the average number of children that would be born to a woman over her lifetime if:\n</p>\n<ol><li>She were to experience the exact current age-specific <a href="/wiki/Fertility" title="Fertility">fertility</a> rates (ASFRs) through her lifetime, and</li>\n<li>She were to survive from birth to the end of her reproductive life.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup></li></ol>\n<p>It is obtained by summing the single-year age-specific rates at a given time. align="center">2.36\n</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>The TFR is a synthetic rate, not based on the fertility of any real group of women since this would involve waiting until they had completed childbearing. Nor is it based on counting up the total number of children actually born over their lifetime. Instead, the TFR is based on the age-specific fertility rates of women in their "child-bearing years", which in conventional international statistical usage is ages 15\xe2\x80\x9344 or 15\xe2\x80\x9349.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The TFR is, therefore, a measure of the fertility of an <i>imaginary</i> woman who passes through her reproductive life subject to <i>all</i> the age-specific fertility rates for ages 15\xe2\x80\x9349 that were recorded for a given population in a given year. The TFR represents the average number of children a woman <i>would</i> potentially have, were she to fast-forward through all her childbearing years in a single year, under all the age-specific fertility rates for that year. In other words, this rate is the number of children a woman would have if she was subject to prevailing fertility rates at all ages from a single given year, and survives throughout all her childbearing years.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Related_parameters">Related parameters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Total_fertility_rate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Related parameters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Net_reproduction_rate">Net reproduction rate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Total_fertility_rate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Net reproduction rate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>An alternative fertility measure is the <a href="/wiki/Net_reproduction_rate" title="Net reproduction rate">net reproduction rate</a> (NRR), which measures the number of <i>daughters</i> a woman would have in her lifetime if she were subject to prevailing age-specific fertility <i>and mortality</i> rates in the given year. When the NRR is exactly <b>one</b>, then each generation of women is exactly reproducing itself. The NRR is less widely used than the TFR, and the United Nations stopped reporting NRR data for member nations after 1998. But the NRR is particularly relevant where the number of male babies born is very high due to <a href="/wiki/Gender_imbalance" class="mw-redirect" title="Gender imbalance">gender imbalance</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sex_selection" title="Sex selection">sex selection</a>. This is a significant factor in world population, due to the high level of gender imbalance in the very populous nations of <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> and <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Gross_reproduction_rate" title="Gross reproduction rate">gross reproduction rate</a> (GRR), is the same as the NRR, except that\xe2\x80\x94like the TFR\xe2\x80\x94it ignores <a href="/wiki/Life_expectancy" title="Life expectancy">life expectancy</a>.                                    \n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Total_period_fertility_rate">Total period fertility rate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Total_fertility_rate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Total period fertility rate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The TFR (or TPFR\xe2\x80\x94total period fertility rate) is a better index of fertility than the <a href="/wiki/Crude_birth_rate" class="mw-redirect" title="Crude birth rate">crude birth rate</a> (annual number of births per thousand population) because it is independent of the age structure of the population, but it is a poorer estimate of actual completed family size than the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Total_cohort_fertility_rate&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Total cohort fertility rate (page does not exist)">total cohort fertility rate</a>, which is obtained by summing the age-specific fertility rates that actually applied to each cohort as they aged through time. In particular, the TFR does not necessarily predict how many children young women now will eventually have, as their fertility rates in years to come may change from those of older women now. However, the TFR is a reasonable summary of current fertility levels.                                                       \n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Tempo_effect">Tempo effect</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Total_fertility_rate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Tempo effect">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Sub-replacement_fertility#Tempo_effect" title="Sub-replacement fertility">Sub-replacement fertility \xc2\xa7&#160;Tempo effect</a></div>\n<p>The TPFR (total <i>period</i> fertility rate) is affected by a <a href="/wiki/Sub-replacement_fertility#Tempo_effect" title="Sub-replacement fertility">tempo effect</a>\xe2\x80\x94if age of childbearing increases (and life cycle fertility is unchanged) then while the age of childbearing is increasing, TPFR will be lower (because the births are occurring later), and then the age of childbearing stops increasing, the TPFR will increase (due to the deferred births occurring in the later period) even though the life cycle fertility has been unchanged. In other words, the TPFR is a misleading measure of life cycle fertility when childbearing age is changing, due to this statistical artifact. This is a significant factor in some countries, such as the <a href="/wiki/Czech_Republic" title="Czech Republic">Czech Republic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> in the 1990s. Some measures seek to adjust for this timing effect to gain a better measure of life-cycle fertility.                                                                                                      \n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a href="/wiki/File:CIA_WFB_TotFertilityRate-GDP-Population_-_Simplified_2016.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/CIA_WFB_TotFertilityRate-GDP-Population_-_Simplified_2016.png/350px-CIA_WFB_TotFertilityRate-GDP-Population_-_Simplified_2016.png" width="350" height="244" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/CIA_WFB_TotFertilityRate-GDP-Population_-_Simplified_2016.png/525px-CIA_WFB_TotFertilityRate-GDP-Population_-_Simplified_2016.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/CIA_WFB_TotFertilityRate-GDP-Population_-_Simplified_2016.png/700px-CIA_WFB_TotFertilityRate-GDP-Population_-_Simplified_2016.png 2x" data-file-width="8354" data-file-height="5835" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:CIA_WFB_TotFertilityRate-GDP-Population_-_Simplified_2016.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Log-log graph of total fertility rate (TFR) vs. <a href="/wiki/List_of_countries_by_GDP_(PPP)_per_capita" title="List of countries by GDP (PPP) per capita">GDP (PPP) per capita</a> with <a href="/wiki/Population_size" title="Population size">population size</a> shown as bubble area, for all countries having population greater than 2 million (2016 estimates; 30 largest countries bold).<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Replacement_rates">Replacement rates <span id="Replacement_rate"></span></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Total_fertility_rate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Replacement rates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Sub-replacement_fertility" title="Sub-replacement fertility">Sub-replacement fertility</a></div>\n<p>Replacement fertility is the total fertility rate at which women give birth to enough babies to sustain population levels.\n</p><p>If there were no mortality in the female population from birth to the end of the childbearing years, the replacement level of TFR would be very close to 2.0. The <a href="/wiki/Sub-replacement_fertility" title="Sub-replacement fertility">replacement fertility</a> rate is indeed only slightly above 2.0 births per woman for most industrialized countries (2.075 in the UK, for example), but ranges from 2.5 to 3.3 in developing countries because of higher mortality rates, especially <a href="/wiki/Child_mortality" title="Child mortality">child mortality</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The global average for the replacement total fertility rate (leading to a stable global population) was 2.33 children per woman in 2003.<sup id="cite_ref-variation_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-variation-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Lowest-low_fertility">Lowest-low fertility</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Total_fertility_rate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Lowest-low fertility">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The term "lowest-low fertility" is defined as TFR at or below  1.3.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> This is characteristic of some Eastern European, Southern European and East Asian countries.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> In 2001, more than half of the population of Europe lived in countries with lowest-low TFR, but TFRs have since slightly increased there.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Population-lag_effect">Population-lag effect</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Total_fertility_rate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Population-lag effect">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>A population that maintained a TFR of 3.8 over an extended period without a correspondingly high death or emigration rate would increase rapidly (doubling period ~ 32 years), whereas a population that maintained a TFR of 2.0 over a long time would decrease, unless it had a large enough immigration. However, it may take several generations for a change in the total fertility rate to be reflected in <a href="/wiki/Birth_rate" title="Birth rate">birth rate</a>, because the age distribution must reach equilibrium. For example, a population that has recently dropped below replacement-level fertility will continue to grow, because the recent high fertility produced large numbers of young couples who would now be in their childbearing years.\n</p><p>This phenomenon carries forward for several generations and is called <a href="/wiki/Population_momentum" title="Population momentum">population momentum</a>, <i>population inertia</i> or <i>population-lag effect</i>. This time-lag effect is of great importance to the growth rates of human populations.\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:102px;"><a href="/wiki/File:TFR_vs_population_growth_rate.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/8/85/TFR_vs_population_growth_rate.png/100px-TFR_vs_population_growth_rate.png" width="100" height="67" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/8/85/TFR_vs_population_growth_rate.png/150px-TFR_vs_population_growth_rate.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/8/85/TFR_vs_population_growth_rate.png/200px-TFR_vs_population_growth_rate.png 2x" data-file-width="1500" data-file-height="1000" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:TFR_vs_population_growth_rate.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Symbol radius reflect population size in country</div></div></div>\n<p>TFR (net) and long term population growth rate, g, are closely related. For a population structure in a steady state and with zero migration, g equals log(TFR/2)/Xm, where Xm is mean age for childbearing women and thus P(t) = P(0)<span style="white-space:nowrap">&#8201;</span>exp(gt). At the left side is shown the empirical relation between the two variables in a cross-section of countries with most recent y-y growth rate. The parameter 1/b should be an estimate of the Xm; here equal to 1/0.02 = 50 years, way off the mark because of population momentum. E.g. for log(TFR/2) = 0, g should be exactly zero, which is seen not to be the case.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Developed_or_developing_countries">Developed or developing Darker is higher.</div></div></div>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Income_and_fertility" title="Income and fertility">Income and fertility</a></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Developed_countries" class="mw-redirect" title="Developed countries">Developed countries</a> usually have a significantly lower fertility rate, often correlated with greater wealth, education, urbanization, or other factors. <a href="/wiki/Mortality_rate" title="Mortality rate">Mortality rates</a> are low, <a href="/wiki/Birth_control" title="Birth control">birth control</a> is understood and easily accessible, and costs are often deemed very high because of education, clothing, feeding, and social amenities. With wealth, contraception becomes affordable. In countries like <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a> where contraception was subsidized before the economy accelerated, birth rate also rapidly declined. Further, longer periods of time spent getting higher education often mean women have children later in life.  Female labor participation rate also has substantial negative impact on fertility, but not in all countries (for countries in the <a href="/wiki/Organisation_for_Economic_Co-operation_and_Development" class="mw-redirect" title="Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development">OECD</a>, increased female labor participation has been associated with increased fertility<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>).\n</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Undeveloped_countries" class="mw-redirect" title="Undeveloped countries">undeveloped countries</a> on the other hand, families desire children for their labour and as caregivers for their parents in old age. Fertility rates are also higher due to the lack of access to <a href="/wiki/Contraceptives" class="mw-redirect" title="Contraceptives">contraceptives</a>, stricter adherence to traditional religious beliefs, generally lower levels of <a href="/wiki/Female_education" title="Female education">female education</a>, and lower rates of female employment in industry. The total fertility rate for the world has been declining very rapidly since the 1990s. Some forecasters like <a href="/wiki/Sanjeev_Sanyal" title="Sanjeev Sanyal">Sanjeev Sanyal</a> argue that, adjusted for gender imbalances, the effective global fertility will fall below replacement rate in the 2020s. This will stabilize world population by 2050, which is much sooner than the UN Population Division expects.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>This situation of wealthy countries usually having a lower fertility rate than poor countries is part of the <a href="/wiki/Income_and_fertility" title="Income and fertility">fertility-income paradox</a>, as the very high fertility countries are very poor, and it may seem counter-intuitive for families there to have so many children. The inverse relationship between income and fertility has been termed a <i>demographic-economic "<a href="/wiki/Paradox" title="Paradox">paradox</a>"</i> by the notion that greater means would enable the production of more offspring, as first suggested by demographic scholar <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Malthus" class="mw-redirect" title="Thomas Malthus">Thomas Malthus</a> in 1798.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Politics">Politics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Total_fertility_rate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Politics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Governments have often set population targets, to either increase or decrease the total fertility rate; or to have certain ethnic or socioeconomic groups have a lower of higher fertility rate. Often such policies have been interventionist, and abusive. The most notorious <a href="/wiki/Natalist" class="mw-redirect" title="Natalist">natalist</a> policies of the 20th century  include those in <a href="/wiki/Communist_Romania" class="mw-redirect" title="Communist Romania">communist Romania</a> and <a href="/wiki/Communist_Albania" class="mw-redirect" title="Communist Albania">communist Albania</a>, under <a href="/wiki/Nicolae_Ceau%C8%99escu" title="Nicolae Ceau\xc8\x99escu">Nicolae Ceau\xc8\x99escu</a> and <a href="/wiki/Enver_Hoxha" title="Enver Hoxha">Enver Hoxha</a> respectively. The policy of Romania  (1967-1990) was very aggressive, including outlawing abortion and contraception, routine pregnancy tests for women, <a href="/wiki/Tax_on_childlessness" title="Tax on childlessness">taxes on childlessness</a>, and legal discrimination against childless people; and resulted in large numbers of children put into <a href="/wiki/Romanian_orphanages" class="mw-redirect" title="Romanian orphanages">Romanian orphanages</a> by parents who couldn\'t cope with raising them, <a href="/wiki/Street_children" title="Street children">street children</a> in the 1990s (when many orphanages were closed and the children ended up on the streets), <a href="/wiki/Overcrowding" title="Overcrowding">overcrowding</a> in homes and schools, and over 9,000 women who died due to <a href="/wiki/Illegal_abortion" title="Illegal abortion">illegal abortions</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Kligman_short_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kligman_short-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Conversely, in <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> the government sought to lower the fertility rate, and, as such, enacted the <a href="/wiki/One_child_policy" class="mw-redirect" title="One child policy">one child policy</a> (1978-2015), which included abuses such as <a href="/wiki/Forced_abortions" class="mw-redirect" title="Forced abortions">forced abortions</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Some governments have sought to regulate which groups of society could reproduce through <a href="/wiki/Eugenic" class="mw-redirect" title="Eugenic">eugenic</a> policies of <a href="/wiki/Forced_sterilizations" class="mw-redirect" title="Forced sterilizations">forced sterilizations</a> of \'undesirable\' population groups. Such policies were carried out against ethnic minorities in Europe and North America in the first half of the 20th century, and more recently in Latin America against the Indigenous population in the 1990s; in <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a>, President <a href="/wiki/Alberto_Fujimori" title="Alberto Fujimori">Alberto Fujimori</a> (in office from 1990 to 2000) has been accused of <a href="/wiki/Genocide" title="Genocide">genocide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Crimes_against_humanity" title="Crimes against humanity">crimes against humanity</a> as a result of a sterilization program put in place by his administration targeting indigenous people (mainly the <a href="/wiki/Quechua_people" title="Quechua people">Quechuas</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Aymara_people" title="Aymara people">Aymaras</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Within this historical contexts, the notion of <a href="/wiki/Reproductive_rights" title="Reproductive rights">reproductive rights</a> has developed. Such rights are based on the concept that each person freely decides if, when, and how many children to have - not the state or church. According to the <a href="/wiki/OHCHR" class="mw-redirect" title="OHCHR">OHCHR</a> reproductive rights "rest on the recognition of the basic rights of all couples and individuals to decide freely and responsibly the number, spacing and timing of their children and to have the information and means to do so, and the right to attain the highest standard of sexual and reproductive health. It also includes the right to make decisions concerning reproduction free of discrimination, coercion and violence, as expressed in human rights documents".<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Total_fertility_rate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:192px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Map_of_U.S._states_by_total_fertility_rate_(TFR)_in_2013.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Map_of_U.S._states_by_total_fertility_rate_%28TFR%29_in_2013.svg/190px-Map_of_U.S._states_by_total_fertility_rate_%28TFR%29_in_2013.svg.png" width="190" height="120" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Map_of_U.S._states_by_total_fertility_rate_%28TFR%29_in_2013.svg/285px-Map_of_U.S._states_by_total_fertility_rate_%28TFR%29_in_2013.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Map_of_U.S._states_by_total_fertility_rate_%28TFR%29_in_2013.svg/380px-Map_of_U.S._states_by_total_fertility_rate_%28TFR%29_in_2013.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="1000" data-file-height="630" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Map_of_U.S._states_by_total_fertility_rate_(TFR)_in_2013.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Map of U.S. states by total fertility rate (TFR) in 2013.</div></div></div>\n<p>The total fertility rate in the US after <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> peaked at about 3.8 children per woman in the late 1950s and by 1999 was at 2 children. The fertility rate of the total US population was just below the replacement level of about 1.9 children per woman in 1979.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> However, the fertility of the population of the United States is below replacement among those native born, and above replacement among <a href="/wiki/Immigrant" class="mw-redirect" title="Immigrant">immigrant</a> families, most of whom come to the U.S. from countries with higher fertility than that of the U.S. However, the fertility rates of immigrants to the U.S. have been found to decrease sharply in the second generation, correlating with improved education and income.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> As of the beginning of 2016, there are 59.8 births per 1,000 women aged 15\xe2\x80\x9344; this is the lowest number since records have been kept since 1909.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:192px;"><a href="/wiki/File:US_TFR_History_1933_to_Present.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/US_TFR_History_1933_to_Present.png/190px-US_TFR_History_1933_to_Present.png" width="190" height="177" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/US_TFR_History_1933_to_Present.png/285px-US_TFR_History_1933_to_Present.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/US_TFR_History_1933_to_Present.png/380px-US_TFR_History_1933_to_Present.png 2x" data-file-width="607" data-file-height="566" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:US_TFR_History_1933_to_Present.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Source: UN Population Division</div></div></div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="World_extreme_lows">World extreme lows</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Total_fertility_rate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: World extreme lows">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The lowest TFR recorded anywhere in the world in recorded history is for Xiangyang district of <a href="/wiki/Jiamusi" title="Jiamusi">Jiamusi</a> city (<a href="/wiki/Heilongjiang" title="Heilongjiang">Heilongjiang</a>, China) which had a TFR of 0.41.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Outside China, the lowest TFR ever recorded was 0.80 for Eastern Germany in 1994. The low Eastern German value was influenced by a change to higher age at birth, with the consequence that neither older cohorts (e.g. women born until the late 1960s), who often already had children, nor younger cohort, which often gave childbirth at a higher age (i.e. after 1994), had many children during that time. The total cohort fertility rate of each age <a href="/wiki/Cohort_(statistics)" title="Cohort (statistics)">cohort</a> of woman in East German did not drop as significantly.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Europe">Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Total_fertility_rate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ageing_of_Europe" title="Ageing of Europe">Ageing of Europe</a></div>\n<p>The average total fertility rate in the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> (EU-28) is calculated at 1.58 children per woman in 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-europa.eu_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-europa.eu-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Most Southern European countries have very low TFR (Portugal 1.31, Cyprus, 1.32, Greece 1.33, Spain 1.33, Italy 1.35).<sup id="cite_ref-europa.eu1_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-europa.eu1-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> Poland also has a very low TFR (1.32).<sup id="cite_ref-europa.eu1_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-europa.eu1-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> France has the highest TFR at 1.96, followed by Ireland (1.92), Sweden (1.85), the UK (1.80) and Denmark (1.71). The other non-EU Scandinavian countries also have high TFR (Iceland 1.80, Norway 1.72).<sup id="cite_ref-europa.eu_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-europa.eu-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In the non-EU European post-Soviet states group in 2016, Russia has a TFR of 1.61,  Moldova 1.56, Ukraine 1.54, and Belarus 1.48.<sup id="cite_ref-cia.gov_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cia.gov-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> In former Yugoslavian states, Bosnia has a very low TFR, at only 1.28, the lowest of the whole of Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-cia.gov_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cia.gov-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Emigration of young adults from Eastern Europe to the West aggravates the demographic problems of those countries. People from countries such as Moldova, Romania,  Hungary, and Bulgaria are particularly moving abroad.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="East_Asia">East Asia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a South Korea are the lowest in the world, with Singapore and Macau having a TFR below 1 in 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-cia.gov_12-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cia.gov-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Japan too has a low TFR (1.45 in 2016<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>). Singapore mitigates this through <a href="/wiki/Immigration_to_Singapore" title="Immigration to Singapore">immigrant workers</a>, but in Japan there is a serious demographic problem.\n</p><p>In South Korea, too, a low birthrate is one of its most urgent socio-economic challenges.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>\nRising housing expenses, shrunken job opportunities for younger generations, insufficient support to the families with newborns either from government or working places are among the major explanations for its crawling TFR, from 1.08 in 2005 to 1.17 in 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> Koreans are yet to find viable solutions to make the birthrate rebound, even after trying out dozens of programs over a decade, including subsidizing rearing expenses, giving priorities for public rental housing to couples with multiple children, funding day care centers, reserving seats in public transportation for pregnant women, etc.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Africa">Africa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Total_fertility_rate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Africa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>This region of the world has the highest TFR (<a href="/wiki/Niger" title="Niger">Niger</a>, <a href="/wiki/Burundi" title="Burundi">Burundi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mali" title="Mali">Mali</a>, <a href="/wiki/Somalia" title="Somalia">Somalia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Uganda" title="Uganda">Uganda</a> are highest).<sup id="cite_ref-cia.gov_12-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cia.gov-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The poverty of the region, and the high <a href="/wiki/Maternal_mortality" class="mw-redirect" title="Maternal mortality">maternal mortality</a> and <a href="/wiki/Infant_mortality" title="Infant mortality">infant mortality</a> had led to calls from WHO of <a href="/wiki/Family_planning" title="Family planning">family planning</a> and encouragement of  smaller families.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Factors">Factors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Total_fertility_rate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Factors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Fertility_factor_(demography)" title="Fertility factor (demography)">fertility factor (demography)</a></div>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Sub-replacement_fertility" title="Sub-replacement fertility">Sub-replacement fertility</a></div>\n<p>A parent\'s number of children strongly correlates with the number of children that each person in the next generation will eventually have.<sup id="cite_ref-Murphy2013_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Murphy2013-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> Factors generally associated with increased fertility include <a href="/wiki/Religiosity" title="Religiosity">religiosity</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> intention to have children,<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> and maternal support.<sup id="cite_ref-SchaffnitSear2014_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SchaffnitSear2014-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> Factors generally associated with decreased fertility include <a href="/wiki/Income_and_fertility" title="Income and fertility">wealth</a>, education,<sup id="cite_ref-rai2013_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rai2013-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Female_labor_participation" class="mw-redirect" title="Female labor participation">female labor participation</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Urban_area" title="Urban area">urban</a> residence,<sup id="cite_ref-sato2006_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sato2006-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Intelligence_and_fertility" class="mw-redirect" title="Intelligence and fertility">intelligence</a>, widespread <a href="/wiki/Birth_control" title="Birth control">birth control</a> usage, <a href="/wiki/Age_and_female_fertility" title="Age and female fertility">increased female age</a> and (to a lesser degree) <a href="/wiki/Paternal_age_effect" title="Paternal age effect">increased male age</a>. Many of these factors however are not universal, and differ by region and social class. For instance, at a global level, religion is correlated with increased fertility, but in the West less so: Scandinavian countries and France are among the least religious in the EU, but have the highest TFR, while the opposite is true about Portugal, Greece, Cyprus, Poland and Spain.<sup id="cite_ref-europa.eu_23-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-europa.eu-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Table_Fertility_Rates">Table Fertility Rates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span
