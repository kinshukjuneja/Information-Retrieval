enlargement of the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a></b> was the largest single <a href="/wiki/Enlargement_of_the_European_Union" title="Enlargement of the It occurred on 1 May 2004.\n</p><p>The simultaneous accessions concerned the following countries (sometimes referred to as the "A10" countries<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>): <a href="/wiki/Cyprus" title="Cyprus">Cyprus</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Czech_Republic" title="Czech Republic">Czech Republic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Estonia" title="Estonia">Estonia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a>, <a href="/wiki/Latvia" title="Latvia">Latvia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lithuania" title="Lithuania">Lithuania</a>, <a href="/wiki/Malta" title="Malta">Malta</a>, <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Slovakia" title="Slovakia">Slovakia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Slovenia" title="Slovenia">Slovenia</a>. Union</a> and four were and still are members of the Central European alliance <a href="/wiki/Visegr%C3%A1d_Group" title="Visegr\xc3\xa1d Group">Visegr\xc3\xa1d Group</a>), one of the former <a href="/wiki/Yugoslavia" title="Yugoslavia">Yugoslavia</a> (together sometimes referred to as the <a href="/wiki/A8_countries" title="A8 countries">"A8" countries</a>), and the remaining two were <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean" class="mw-redirect" title="Mediterranean">Mediterranean</a> islands and former <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British colonies</a>. \n</p><p>Part of the same wave of enlargement was the <a href="/wiki/2007_enlargement_of_the_European_Union" title="2007 enlargement of the European Union">accession of Bulgaria and Romania in 2007</a>, who were Union">History of the European Union</a></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2004_enlargement_of_the_European_Union&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>With the end of the <a href="/wiki/Second_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Second World War">Second World War</a> in 1945, Europe found itself divided between a capitalist <a href="/wiki/Western_Bloc" title="Western Bloc">Western Bloc</a> and a communist <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Bloc" title="Eastern Bloc">Eastern Bloc</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Third_World" title="Third World">Third World</a> neutral countries. The <a href="/wiki/European_Economic_Community" title="European Economic Community">European Economic Community</a> (EEC) was created in 1957 between <a href="/wiki/Inner_Six" title="Inner Six">six countries</a> within the Western Bloc and later expanded to twelve countries across Europe. European communist countries had a looser economic grouping with the USSR known as <a href="/wiki/Comecon" title="Comecon">Comecon</a>. To the south there was a <a href="/wiki/Non-Aligned_Movement" title="Non-Aligned Movement">non-aligned</a> communist federated country \xe2\x80\x93 <a href="/wiki/Yugoslavia" title="Yugoslavia">Yugoslavia</a>.\n</p><p>In 1989, the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a> between the two superpowers was coming to an end, with the USSR\'s influence over communist Europe collapsing. As the communist states began their transition to <a href="/wiki/Free_market" title="Free market">free market</a> <a href="/wiki/Democracy" title="Democracy">democracies</a>, aligning to <a href="/wiki/European_integration" title="European integration">Euro-Atlantic integration</a>, the question of enlargement into the continent was thrust onto the EEC\'s agenda.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Negotiations">Negotiations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2004_enlargement_of_the_European_Union&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Negotiations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Phare" title="Phare">Phare</a> strategy was launched soon after to adapt more the structure of the <a href="/wiki/Central_and_Eastern_Europe" title="Central and Eastern Europe">Central and Eastern European</a> countries (<i>Pays d\'Europe Centrale et Orientale (PECO)</i>) to the <a href="/wiki/European_Economic_Community" title="European Economic Community">European Economic Community</a>. width:1.5em; height:1.5em; margin:1px 0; border:1px solid black; background-color: #f3f; color:black; font-size:100%; text-align:center;">&#160;</span>&#160;situation totally incompatible with EU acquis</div>\n<p><br />\n</p>\n</td></tr>\n</tbody></table>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Free_movement_issues">Free movement issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2004_enlargement_of_the_European_Union&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Free movement issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Freedom_of_movement_for_workers_in_the_European_Union" title="Freedom of movement for workers in the European Union">Freedom of movement for workers in the European Union</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Polish_Plumber.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/c/cc/Polish_Plumber.jpg/220px-Polish_Plumber.jpg" width="220" height="222" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/c/cc/Polish_Plumber.jpg/330px-Polish_Plumber.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/c/cc/Polish_Plumber.jpg 2x" data-file-width="400" data-file-height="404" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Polish_Plumber.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The "Polish Plumber" clich\xc3\xa9 adopted by Poland\'s tourism board to advertise Poland as a tourist destination on the French market. (English translation: "I am staying in Poland, come in large numbers")</div></div></div>\n<p>As of May 2011, there are no longer any special restrictions on the free movement of citizens of these new member states.\n</p><p>With their original accession to the EU, <a href="/wiki/Four_Freedoms_(European_Union)" class="mw-redirect" title="Four Freedoms (European Union)">free movement of people</a> between all 25 states would naturally have applied. However, due to concerns of <a href="/wiki/Mass_migration" title="Mass migration">mass migration</a> from the new members to the old EU-15, some transitional restrictions were put in place. Mobility within the EU-15 (plus Cyprus) and within the new states (minus Cyprus) functioned as normal (although the new states had the right to impose restrictions on travel between them). Between the old and new states, transitional restrictions up to 1 May 2011 could be put in place, and EU workers still had a preferential right over non-EU workers in looking for jobs even if restrictions were placed upon their country. No restrictions were placed on Cyprus or Malta. The following restrictions were put in place by each country;<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<ul><li>Austria and Germany: Restriction on free movement and to provide certain services. Work permits still needed for all countries. In Austria, to be employed the worker needs to have been employed for more than a year in his home country prior to accession. Germany had bilateral quotas which remained in force.</li>\n<li>Cyprus: No restrictions.</li>\n<li>Malta: No restrictions on its workers, but does have the right to migration into the country.</li>\n<li>Netherlands: Initially against restrictions, but tightened up its policies in early 2004 and said it would tighten its policies if more than 22,000 workers arrived per year.</li>\n<li>Finland: 2 years of transitional arrangements where a work permit would be granted only where a Finnish national cannot be found for the job. Does not apply to students, part-time workers, entrepreneurs, people living in Finland for non-work purposes, people who were already living in Finland for a year or people who would be entitled to work anyway if they were from a third country.</li>\n<li>Denmark: Two years where only full-time workers can get a <a href="/wiki/Work_permit" title="Work permit">work permit</a>, if they had a <a href="/wiki/Residence_permit" title="Residence permit">residence permit</a>. Workers did not get welfare but restrictions only apply to wage earners (all the EU-10 citizens can set up a business).</li>\n<li>France: Five years of restrictions depending on sector and region. Students, researchers, self-employed and service providers were exempt from the restrictions.</li>\n<li>Spain: Two years.</li>\n<li>Portugal: Two years, annual limit of 6,500.</li>\n<li>Sweden: No restrictions.</li>\n<li>Czech Republic and Slovakia: No restrictions.</li>\n<li>Poland: Reciprocal limits, only British and Irish citizens had free access. Countries with looser or tighter limits face similar limits in Poland.</li>\n<li>Belgium, Greece and Luxembourg: Two years.</li>\n<li>United Kingdom: Welfare restrictions only, need to register though. This decision was controversial: politicians later admitted that they had only decided against barring freedom of movement under the assumption that all of the other EU countries would make the same decision: in fact only Ireland and Sweden followed suit.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>This decision led to a rise of anti-migrant sentiment, and is credited with inspiring Brexit.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>Ireland: No restrictions.</li>\n<li>Hungary: Reciprocal limits for seven years.</li></ul>\n<p>Despite the fears, migration within the EU concerns less than 2% of the population.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> However, the migration did cause controversy in those countries which saw a noticeable influx, creating the image of a "<a href="/wiki/Polish_Plumber" title="Polish Plumber">Polish Plumber</a>" in the EU, caricaturing the cheap manual labour from <a href="/wiki/Worker_Registration_Scheme#A8_Countries" class="mw-redirect" title="Worker Registration Scheme">A8 countries</a> making an imprint on the rest of the EU. The extent to which E8 immigration generated a lasting public backlash has been debated. Ten years after the enlargement, a study showed that increases in E8 migrants in Western Europe over the last ten years had been accompanied by a more widespread acknowledgement of the economic benefits of immigration.<sup id="cite_ref-Anne-Marie_Jeannet_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Anne-Marie_Jeannet-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Following the 2007 enlargement, most countries placed restrictions on the new states, including the most open in 2004 (Ireland and the United Kingdom) with only Sweden, Finland and the 2004 members (minus Malta and Hungary).<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> But by April 2008, these restrictions on the eight members had been dropped by all members except Germany and Austria.<sup id="cite_ref-EUO_TEN_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EUO_TEN-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Remaining_areas_of_inclusion">Remaining areas of inclusion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2004_enlargement_of_the_European_Union&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Remaining areas of inclusion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Cyprus, Czech Republic, Estonia, Hungary, Latvia, Lithuania, Malta, Poland, Slovakia, and Slovenia became members on 1 May 2004, but some areas of cooperation in the European Union will apply to some of the EU member states at a later date. These are:\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Schengen_Area" title="Schengen Area">Schengen Area</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Schengen_Area#Prospective_members" title="Schengen Area">Enlargement of the Schengen Area</a>; Cyprus is still not a member of the Schengen Area)</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Eurozone" title="Eurozone">Eurozone</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Enlargement_of_the_eurozone" title="Enlargement of the eurozone">Enlargement of the eurozone</a>; Czech Republic, Hungary, Poland are still not members of the Eurozone)</li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="New_member_states">New member states</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2004_enlargement_of_the_European_Union&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: New member states">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cyprus">Cyprus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2004_enlargement_of_the_European_Union&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Cyprus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Cyprus_dispute#EU_accession_and_the_settlement_process,_1997\xe2\x80\x93present" title="Cyprus dispute">Cyprus dispute \xc2\xa7&#160;EU accession and the settlement process, 1997\xe2\x80\x93present</a></div>\n<p>Since 1974 Cyprus has been divided between the <a href="/wiki/Greeks" title="Greeks">Greek</a> south (the Republic of Cyprus) and the northern areas under Turkish military occupation (the self-proclaimed Turkish Republic of Northern Cyprus). The Republic of Cyprus is recognised as the sole legitimate government by most countries, including the entire European Union, while the northern occupied area is recognised only by Turkey.\n</p><p>Cyprus began talks to join the EU, which provided impetus to solve the dispute. With the agreement of the <a href="/wiki/Annan_Plan_for_Cyprus" title="Annan Plan for Cyprus">Annan Plan for Cyprus</a>, it was hoped that the two countries would join the EU together as a single <i>United Cyprus Republic</i>. Turkish Cypriots supported the plan. However, in a <a href="/wiki/Cypriot_Annan_Plan_referendum,_2004" class="mw-redirect" title="Cypriot Annan Plan referendum, 2004">referendum on 24 April 2004</a> the Greek Cypriots rejected the plan. Thus, a week later, the Republic of Cyprus joined the EU with political issues unresolved. Legally, as the northern republic is not recognised by the EU, the entire island is a member of the EU as part of the Republic of Cyprus, though the <i>de facto</i> situation is that the Government is unable to extend its controls into the occupied areas.\n</p><p>Efforts to reunite the island continue as of 2014.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Poland">Poland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2004_enlargement_of_the_European_Union&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Poland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Poland_in_the_European_Union" title="Poland in the European Union">Poland in the European Union</a></div>\n<p>Accession of Poland to the European Union took place in May 2004. Poland had been negotiating with the EU since 1989.\n</p><p>With the <a href="/wiki/Fall_of_communism" class="mw-redirect" title="Fall of communism">fall of communism</a> in 1989/1990 in Poland, Poland embarked on a series of reforms and changes in foreign policy, intending to join the EU and <a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">NATO</a>. On 19 September 1989 Poland signed the agreement for trade and trade co-operation with the (then) <a href="/wiki/European_Community" class="mw-redirect" title="European Community">European Community</a> (EC). Polish intention to join the EU was expressed by Polish Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Tadeusz_Mazowiecki" title="Tadeusz Mazowiecki">Tadeusz Mazowiecki</a> in his speech in the <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament" title="European Parliament">European Parliament</a> in February 1990 and in June 1991 by <a href="/wiki/Minister_of_Foreign_Affairs_of_the_Republic_of_Poland" class="mw-redirect" title="Minister of Foreign Affairs of the Republic of Poland">Polish Minister of Foreign Affairs</a> <a href="/wiki/Krzysztof_Skubiszewski" title="Krzysztof Skubiszewski">Krzysztof Skubiszewski</a> in <a href="/wiki/Sejm" title="Sejm">Sejm</a> (Polish Parliament).\n</p><p>On 19 May 1990 Poland started a procedure to begin negotiations for an <a href="/wiki/European_Union_Association_Agreement" title="European Union Association Agreement">association agreement</a> and the negotiations officially began in December 1990. About a year later, on 16 December 1991 the <a href="/wiki/European_Union_Association_Agreement" title="European Union Association Agreement">European Union Association Agreement</a> was signed by Poland. The Agreement came into force on 1 February 1994 (its III part on the mutual trade relations came into force earlier on 1 March 1992).\n</p><p>As a result of diplomatic interventions by the central European states of the <a href="/wiki/Visegr%C3%A1d_group" class="mw-redirect" title="Visegr\xc3\xa1d group">Visegr\xc3\xa1d group</a>, the <a href="/wiki/European_Council" title="European Council">European Council</a> decided at its <a href="/wiki/Copenhagen" title="Copenhagen">Copenhagen</a> summit in June 1993 that: "the associate member states from <a href="/wiki/Central_and_Eastern_Europe" title="Central and Eastern Europe">Central and Eastern Europe</a>, if they so wish, will become members of the EU. To achieve this, however, they must fulfil the appropriate conditions." Those conditions (known as the Copenhagen criteria, or simply, membership criteria) were:\n</p>\n<ol><li>That candidate countries achieve stable institutions that guarantee democracy, legality, human rights and respect for and protection of minorities.</li>\n<li>That candidate countries have a working market economy, capable of competing effectively on EU markets.</li>\n<li>That candidate countries are capable of accepting all the membership responsibilities, political, economic and monetary.</li></ol>\n<p>At the Luxembourg summit in 1997, the EU accepted the Commission\'s opinion to invite Poland, Czech Republic, Hungary, Slovenia, Estonia and Cyprus to start talks on their accession to the EU. The negotiation process started on 31 March 1998. Poland finished the accession negotiations in December 2002. Then, the Accession Treaty was signed in <a href="/wiki/Athens" title="Athens">Athens</a> on 16 April 2003 (<a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Accession_2003" title="Treaty of Accession 2003">Treaty of Accession 2003</a>). After the ratification of that Treaty in the <a href="/wiki/Polish_European_Union_membership_referendum,_2003" title="Polish European Union membership referendum, 2003">Polish European Union membership referendum, 2003</a>, Poland and other 9 countries became the members of EU on 1 May 2004.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact">Impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span years after the enlargement, the EU is still "digesting" the change. The influx of new members has effectively put an end to the <a href="/wiki/Franco-German_engine" class="mw-redirect" title="Franco-German engine">Franco-German engine</a> behind the EU, as its relatively newer members, Poland and Sweden, set the policy agenda, for example <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Partnership" title="Eastern Partnership">Eastern Partnership</a>. Despite fears of paralysis, the decision making process has not been hampered by the new membership and if anything the legislative output of the institutions has increased, however justice and home affairs (which operates by unanimity) has suffered. In 2009 the Commission sees the enlargement as a success, however until the enlargement is fully accepted by the public future enlargements may be slow in coming.<sup id="cite_ref-EUO_TEN_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EUO_TEN-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> In 2012 data published by the Guardian shows that that process is complete.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The internal impact has also been relevant. The arrival of additional members has put an additional stress on the governance of the Institutions, and increased significantly overheads (for example, through the multiplication of official languages). Furthermore, there is a division of staff, since the very same day of the enlargement was chosen to enact an in-depth reform of the Staff Regulation, which was intended to bring significant savings in administrative costs. As a result, employment conditions (career &amp; retirement perspectives) worsened for officials recruited after that date. Since by definition officials of the "new" Member States were recruited after the enlargement, these new conditions affected all of them (although they also affect nationals of the former 15 Member States who have been recruited after 1 May 2004).\n</p><p>Before the 2004 enlargement, the EU had twelve treaty languages: Danish, Dutch, English, Finnish, French, German, Greek, Irish, Italian, Portuguese, Spanish and Swedish. However, due to the 2004 enlargement, nine new official languages were added: Polish, Czech, Slovak, Slovene, Hungarian, Estonian, Latvian, Lithuanian and Maltese.\n</p><p>A 2007 study in the journal <i>Post-Soviet Affairs</i> argued that the 2004 enlargement of the EU contributed to the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_consolidation" title="Democratic consolidation">consolidation of democracy</a> in the new member states.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> In 2009, <a href="/wiki/Free_University_of_Berlin" title="Free University of Berlin">Freie Universit\xc3\xa4t Berlin</a> political scientist Thomas Risse wrote, "there is a consensus in the literature on Eastern Europe that the EU membership perspective had a huge anchoring effects for the new democracies."<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span
