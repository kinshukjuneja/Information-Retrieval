class="mw-empty-elt">\n\n</p><p><b>Indian English</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Register_(sociolinguistics)" title="Register (sociolinguistics)">register</a> of the <a href="/wiki/English_language" Indian English (IndE) is one of the oldest.\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" style="display:none" /><div English in India</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indian_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Status of English in India">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Hindi" title="Hindi">Hindi</a> is the official language of the Union Government of India. However, even after 70 years of <a href="/wiki/Indian_independence_movement" title="Indian independence movement">Indian Independence from Britain</a>, English is still retained with a status of the "subsidiary" official language.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Only a few hundred thousand Indians, or less than 0.1% of the total population, have English as their first language.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>According to the <a href="/wiki/2001_Census_of_India" title="2001 Census of India">2001 Census</a>, 12.6% of Indians know English.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> An analysis of the 2001 Census of India<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> concluded that approximately 86 million Indians reported English as their second language, and another 39 million reported it as their third language.  No data were available whether these individuals were English speakers or users.\n</p><p>According to the 2005 <a href="/wiki/India_Human_Development_Survey" title="India Human Development Survey">India Human Development Survey</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> of the 41,554 surveyed, households reported that 72% of men (29,918) did not speak any English, 28% (11,635) spoke at least some English, and 5% (2,077, roughly 17.9% of those who spoke at least some English) spoke fluent English. Among women, the corresponding percentages were 83% (34,489) speaking no English, 17% (7,064) speaking at least some English, and 3% (1,246, roughly 17.6% of those who spoke at least some English) speaking English fluently.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>  According to statistics of District Information System for Education (DISE) of <a href="/wiki/National_University_of_Educational_Planning_and_Administration" class="mw-redirect" title="National University of Educational Planning and Administration">National University of Educational Planning and Administration</a> under <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Human_Resource_Development" title="Ministry of Human Resource Development">Ministry of Human Resource Development</a>, <a href="/wiki/Government_of_India" title="Government of India">Government of India</a>, enrollment in English-medium schools increased by 50% between 2008\xe2\x80\x9309 and 2013\xe2\x80\x9314. The number of English-medium school students in India increased from over 15 million in 2008\xe2\x80\x9309 to 29 million by 2013\xe2\x80\x9314.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>India ranks 22 out of 72 countries in the 2016 <a href="/wiki/EF_English_Proficiency_Index" title="EF English Proficiency Index">EF English Proficiency Index</a> published by the <a href="/wiki/EF_Education_First" title="EF Education First">EF Education First</a>. The index gives the country a score of 57.30 indicating "moderate proficiency". India ranks 4th out of 19 Asian countries included in the index.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Among Asian countries, Singapore (63.52), Malaysia (60.70) and the Philippines (60.33) received higher scores than India.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Court_language">Court language</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indian_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Court language">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>English, according to the Indian Constitution, is the language of the Supreme Court and all the High Courts of India. The legal system in India is based on British Common Law.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The Indian parliament has the power to change this law, but it has yet to do so. However, in Bihar, Madhya Pradesh, Uttar Pradesh, and Rajasthan there is use of Hindi in courts because of Presidential approval.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> In 2018, the Punjab and Haryana High Court also await Presidential approval for Hindi use as well.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Features">Features</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indian_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Features">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Indian English generally uses the <a href="/wiki/Indian_numbering_system" title="Indian numbering system">Indian numbering system</a>. Idiomatic forms derived from Indian literary languages and vernaculars have been absorbed into Indian English. Nevertheless, there remains general homogeneity in phonetics, vocabulary, and phraseology between various dialects of Indian English.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indian_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>English language public instruction began in India in the 1830s during the <a href="/wiki/Company_rule_in_India#Education" title="Company rule in India">rule of the East India Company</a> (India was then, and is today, one of the most linguistically diverse regions of the world<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>).  In 1835, English <a href="/wiki/English_Education_Act_1835" title="English Education Act 1835">replaced</a> <a href="/wiki/Persian_language" title="Persian language">Persian</a> as the official language of the Company. <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Babington_Macaulay,_1st_Baron_Macaulay" class="mw-redirect" title="Thomas Babington Macaulay, 1st Baron Macaulay">Lord Macaulay</a> played a major role in introducing English and western concepts to education in India. He supported the replacement of Persian by English as the official language, the use of English as the medium of instruction in all schools, and the training of English-speaking Indians as teachers.<sup id="cite_ref-John_MacKenzie_2013_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-John_MacKenzie_2013-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Throughout the 1840s and 1850s, primary-, middle-, and high-schools were opened in many districts of <a href="/wiki/Presidencies_and_provinces_of_British_India" title="Presidencies and provinces of British India">British India</a>, with most high schools offering English language instruction in some subjects.  In 1857, just before the end of Company rule, universities modelled on the <a href="/wiki/University_of_London" title="University of London">University of London</a> and using English as the medium of instruction were established in <a href="/wiki/Mumbai" title="Mumbai">Bombay</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kolkata" title="Kolkata">Calcutta</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chennai" title="Chennai">Madras</a>. During subsequent <a href="/wiki/The_Crown" title="The Crown">Crown Rule</a> in India, or the <a href="/wiki/British_Raj" title="British Raj">British Raj</a>, lasting from 1858 to 1947, English language penetration increased throughout India.  This was driven in part by the gradually increasing hiring of Indians in the <a href="/wiki/Civil_services" class="mw-redirect" title="Civil services">civil services</a>.  At the time of India\'s independence in 1947, English was the only functional <a href="/wiki/Lingua_franca" title="Lingua franca">lingua franca</a> in the country.\n</p><p>After <a href="/wiki/Partition_of_India" title="Partition of India">Indian Independence</a> in 1947, <a href="/wiki/Hindi" title="Hindi">Hindi</a> was declared the first official language, and attempts were made to declare Hindi the sole national language of India. Due to <a href="/wiki/Anti-Hindi_agitations_of_Tamil_Nadu" title="Anti-Hindi agitations of Tamil Nadu">protests from Tamil Nadu</a> and other non-Hindi-speaking states, it was decided to temporarily retain English for official purposes until at least 1965.  By the end of this period, however, opposition from non-Hindi states was still too strong to have Hindi declared the sole language.  With this in mind, the English Language Amendment Bill declared English to be an associate language "until such time as all non-Hindi States had agreed to its being dropped."  This has not yet occurred, and it is still widely used. For instance, it is the only reliable means of day-to-day communication between the central government and the non-Hindi states.\n</p><p>The view of the <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English language</a> among many Indians has gone from associating it with colonialism to associating it with economic progress, and English continues to be an official language of India.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>While there is an assumption that English is readily available in India, available studies show that its usage is actually restricted to the elite,<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> because of inadequate education to large parts of the Indian population. The use of outdated teaching methods and the poor grasp of English exhibited by the authors of many guidebooks, disadvantage students who rely on these books.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hinglish">Hinglish</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indian_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Hinglish">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hinglish" title="Hinglish">Hinglish</a></div>\n<p>The term, "Hinglish", is a portmanteau of the languages <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hindi" title="Hindi">Hindi</a>. This typically refers to the <a href="/wiki/Macaronic_language" title="Macaronic language">macaronic</a> hybrid use of Hindi, or any South Asian language and English. It is often the growing preferred language of the urban and semi-urban educated Indian youth, as well as the Indian diaspora abroad.<sup id="cite_ref-The_Times_of_India_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Times_of_India-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> The Hindi film industry, more popularly known as Bollywood, incorporates considerable amounts of Hinglish as well.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Many internet platforms and voice commands on Google also recognize Hinglish.<sup id="cite_ref-The_Times_of_India_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Times_of_India-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Phonology">Phonology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indian_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Phonology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Most Indians speak with a native tinted accent for their English speech, whereas the modern generation tends to speak with an accent similar to the <a href="/wiki/Received_Pronunciation" title="Received Pronunciation">Received Pronunciation</a>. \n</p><p>Indian English phonology is ultimately based on Received Pronunciation. \n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vowels">Vowels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indian_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Vowels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In general, Indian English has fewer peculiarities in its vowel sounds than the consonants, especially as spoken by native speakers of languages like Hindi, the vowel phoneme system having some similarities with that of English. Though the trap-bath split is prevalent in Indian English, it varies greatly. Many younger Indians who read and listen to American English do not have this split. The distribution is somewhat similar to <a href="/wiki/Australian_English" title="Australian English">Australian English</a> in <a href="/w/index.php?title=Regional_Indian_English&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Regional Indian English (page does not exist)">Regional Indian English</a> varieties, but it has a complete split in <a href="/wiki/Cultivated_Indian_English" class="mw-redirect" title="Cultivated Indian English">Cultivated Indian English</a> and <a href="/wiki/Standard_Indian_English" class="mw-redirect" title="Standard Indian English">Standard Indian English</a> varieties.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. articulate few vowels\n</p>\n<ul><li>Pronunciation of <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/<a href="/wiki/Open-mid_back_rounded_vowel" title="Open-mid href="/wiki/Open_front_unrounded_vowel" title="Open front unrounded vowel">a</a>/</span></li></ul>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Consonants">Consonants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indian_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Consonants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The following are the characteristics of dialect of Indian English most similar to RP:\n</p>\n<ul><li>It is <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/non-rhotic" class="extiw" title="wikt:non-rhotic">non-rhotic</a>.</li>\n<li>The voiceless <a href="/wiki/Stop_consonant" title="Stop consonant">plosives</a> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/p/, /t/, /k/</span> are always unaspirated in Indian English,  (aspirated in cultivated form) whereas in RP, General American and most other English accents they are aspirated in word-initial or stressed syllables. Thus "pin" is pronounced <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[p\xc9\xaan]</span> in Indian English but <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[p\xca\xb0\xc9\xaan]</span> in most other dialects. In native Indian languages (except in <a href="/wiki/Dravidian_languages" title="Dravidian languages">Dravidian languages</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Tamil_language" title="Tamil language">Tamil</a>), the distinction between aspirated and unaspirated plosives is phonemic, and the English stops are equated with the unaspirated rather than the aspirated phonemes of the local languages.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> The same is true of the voiceless postalveolar afficate <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/t\xca\x83/</span>.</li>\n<li>The <a href="/wiki/Alveolar_consonant" title="Alveolar consonant">alveolar</a> stops English <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/d/</span>, <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/t/</span> are often <a href="/wiki/Retroflex" class="mw-redirect" title="Retroflex">retroflex</a> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[\xc9\x96]</span>, <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[\xca\x88]</span>, especially in the South of India.<sup id="cite_ref-wells_628_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wells_628-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> In Indian languages there are two entirely distinct sets of coronal plosives: one <a href="/wiki/Dental_consonant" title="Dental consonant">dental</a> and the other retroflex. Native speakers of Indian languages prefer to pronounce the English alveolar plosives sound as more retroflex than dental,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBallMuller2014_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBallMuller2014-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> and the use of retroflex consonants is a common feature of Indian English.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBallMuller2014289b_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBallMuller2014289b-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESailaja2007252_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESailaja2007252-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/Devanagari" title="Devanagari">Devanagari</a> script of Hindi, all alveolar plosives of English are transcribed as their retroflex counterparts. One good reason for this is that unlike most other native Indian languages, Hindi does not have <i>true</i> retroflex plosives (Tiwari, [1955] 2001). The so-called retroflexes in Hindi are actually articulated as apical <a href="/wiki/Postalveolar_consonant" title="Postalveolar consonant">post-alveolar</a> plosives, sometimes even with a tendency to come down to the alveolar region. So a Hindi speaker normally cannot distinguish the difference between their own apical post-alveolar plosives and English\'s alveolar plosives. Languages such as Tamil have <i>true</i> retroflex plosives, however, wherein the articulation is done with the tongue curved upwards and backwards at the <a href="/wiki/Palate" title="Palate">roof of the mouth</a>. This also causes (in parts of <a href="/wiki/Uttar_Pradesh" title="Uttar Pradesh">Uttar Pradesh</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bihar" title="Bihar">Bihar</a>) the <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/s/</span> preceding alveolar <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/t/</span> to allophonically change to <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[\xca\x83]</span> (&lt;stop&gt; <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/st\xc9\x92p/</span> \xe2\x86\x92 <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/\xca\x83\xca\x88ap/</span>). Mostly in south India, some speakers allophonically further change the voiced retroflex plosives to voiced <a href="/wiki/Retroflex_flap" title="Retroflex flap">retroflex flap</a>, and the nasal <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/n/</span> to a nasalised retroflex flap.</li>\n<li>All major native languages of India (except Bengali) lack the dental fricatives (<span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/\xce\xb8/</span> and <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/\xc3\xb0/</span>; spelled with <i>th</i>). Usually, the <a href="/wiki/Aspiration_(phonetics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Aspiration (phonetics)">aspirated</a> <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_dental_plosive" class="mw-redirect" title="Voiceless dental plosive">voiceless dental plosive</a> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[t\xcc\xaa\xca\xb0]</span> is substituted for <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/\xce\xb8/</span> in the north (it would be unaspirated in the south) and the unaspirated <a href="/wiki/Voiced_dental_plosive" class="mw-redirect" title="Voiced dental plosive">voiced dental plosive</a> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[d\xcc\xaa]</span>, or possibly the aspirated version <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[d\xcc\xaa\xca\xb1]</span>, is substituted for <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/\xc3\xb0/</span>.<sup id="cite_ref-wells_629_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wells_629-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> For example, "thin" would be realised as <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[t\xcc\xaa\xca\xb0\xc9\xaan]</span> instead of <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/\xce\xb8\xc9\xaan/</span> for North Indian speakers, whereas it would be pronounced unaspirated in the south.</li></ul>\n<p>The following are the variations in Indian English\n</p>\n<ul><li>Pronunciations vary between rhotic and non-rhotic; with pronunciations leaning towards native phonology being generally rhotic, and others being non-rhotic.</li>\n<li>Most Indian languages (except <a href="/wiki/Punjabi_language" title="Punjabi language">Punjabi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marathi_language" title="Marathi language">Marathi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Assamese_language" title="Assamese language">Assamese</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bengali_language" title="Bengali language">Bengali</a>), including Standard Hindi, do not differentiate between <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/v/</span> (<a href="/wiki/Voiced_labiodental_fricative" title="Voiced labiodental fricative">voiced labiodental fricative</a>) and <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/w/</span> (<a href="/wiki/Voiced_labiovelar_approximant" class="mw-redirect" title="Voiced labiovelar approximant">voiced labiovelar approximant</a>). Instead, many Indians use a frictionless <a href="/wiki/Labiodental_approximant" title="Labiodental approximant">labiodental approximant</a> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[\xca\x8b]</span> for words with either sound, possibly in free variation with <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[v]</span> and/or <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[w]</span> depending upon region. Thus, <i>wet</i> and <i>vet</i> are often homophones.<sup id="cite_ref-wells_627_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wells_627-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>Related to the previous characteristic, many Indians prefer to pronounce words such as &lt;flower&gt; as [fla\xcb\x90(r)], as opposed to [fla\xca\x8a\xc9\x99(r)], and &lt;our&gt; as [a\xcb\x90(r)] as opposed to [a\xca\x8a\xc9\x99(r)].</li>\n<li>South Indians tend to curl the tongue (retroflex accentuation) more for <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/l/</span> and <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/n/</span>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup></li>\n<li>Sometimes, Indian speakers interchange <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/s/</span> and <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/z/</span>, especially when plurals are being formed, unlike speakers of other varieties of English, who use [s] for the pluralisation of words ending in a voiceless consonant, [z] for words ending in a voiced consonant or vowel, and [\xc9\xa8z] for words ending in a sibilant.</li>\n<li>In case of the postalveolar affricates <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/t\xca\x83/</span> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/d\xca\x92/</span>, native languages like Hindi have corresponding affricates articulated from the palatal region, rather than postalveolar, and they have more of a stop component than fricative; this is reflected in their English.</li>\n<li>Whilst retaining <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/\xc5\x8b/</span> in the final position, many Indian speakers add the <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[\xc9\xa1]</span> sound after it when it occurs in the middle of a word. Hence <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/\xcb\x88ri\xc5\x8bi\xc5\x8b/</span> \xe2\x86\x92 <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/\xcb\x88ri\xc5\x8b\xc9\xa1i\xc5\x8b/</span> (<i>ringing</i>).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Syllabic_consonant" title="Syllabic consonant">Syllabic</a> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/l/</span>, <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/m/</span> and <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/n/</span> are usually replaced by the VC clusters <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[\xc9\x99l]</span>, <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[\xc9\x99m]</span> and <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[\xc9\x99n]</span> (as in <i>button</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/\xcb\x88bu\xca\x88\xca\x88\xc9\x99n/</span>), or if a <a href="/wiki/Close_vowel" title="Close vowel">high vowel</a> precedes, by <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[il]</span> (as in <i>little</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/\xcb\x88li\xca\x88\xca\x88il/</span>). Syllable nuclei in words with the spelling <i>er</i>/<i>re</i> (a <a href="/wiki/Schwa" title="Schwa">schwa</a> in RP and an r-coloured schwa in <a href="/wiki/General_American" title="General American">GA</a>) are also replaced VC clusters. e.g., <i>metre</i>, <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/\xcb\x88mi\xcb\x90t\xc9\x99r/</span> \xe2\x86\x92 <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/\xcb\x88mi\xcb\x90\xca\x88\xc9\x99r/</span>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup></li>\n<li>Indian English uses clear [l] in all instances like <a href="/wiki/Hiberno-English" title="Hiberno-English">Irish English</a> whereas other varieties use clear [l] in syllable-initial positions and dark [l] (velarised-L) in coda and syllabic positions.</li></ul>\n<p>The following are the variations in Indian English that are often discouraged\n</p>\n<ul><li>Most Indian languages (except <a href="/wiki/Urdu" title="Urdu">Urdu</a> varieties and <a href="/wiki/Assamese_language" title="Assamese language">Assamese</a>) lack the <a href="/wiki/Voiced_alveolar_fricative" title="Voiced alveolar fricative">voiced alveolar fricative</a> /z/. A significant portion of Indians thus, even though their native languages do have its nearest equivalent: the unvoiced /s/, often use the voiced palatal affricate (or postalveolar) /d\xca\x92/, just as with a <a href="/wiki/Korean_language" title="Korean language">Korean</a> accent. This makes words such as &lt;zero&gt; and &lt;rosy&gt; sound as [\xcb\x88d\xca\x92i\xcb\x90ro] and [\xcb\x88ro\xcb\x90d\xca\x92i\xcb\x90] (the latter, especially in the North). This replacement is equally true for Persian and <a href="/wiki/Arabic" title="Arabic">Arabic</a> loanwords into Hindi. The probable reason is the confusion created by the use of the Devanagari grapheme &lt; \xe0\xa4\x9c &gt; (for /d\xca\x92/) with a dot beneath it to represent the loaned /z/ (as &lt; \xe0\xa4\x9c\xe0\xa4\xbc &gt;). This is common among people without formal English education.</li>\n<li>In <a href="/wiki/Assamese_language" title="Assamese language">Assamese</a>, <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/t\xca\x83/</span> and <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/\xca\x83/</span> is pronounced as <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/s/</span>; and <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/d\xca\x92/</span> and <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/\xca\x92/</span> is pronounced as <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/z/</span>. Retroflex and dental consonants are not present and only alveolar consonants are used unlike other Indian languages. Similar to <a href="/wiki/Bengali_language" title="Bengali language">Bengali</a>, <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/v/</span> is pronounced as <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/b\xca\xb1/</span> and <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/\xce\xb2/</span> in Assamese. For example; change is pronounced as <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[s\xc9\x9b\xc9\xaanz]</span>, vote is pronounced as <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[b\xca\xb1\xca\x8at]</span> and English is pronounced as <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[i\xc5\x8blis]</span>.</li>\n<li>Again, in <a href="/wiki/Assamese_language" title="Assamese language">Assamese</a> and dialects like <a href="/wiki/Bhojpuri" class="mw-redirect" title="Bhojpuri">Bhojpuri</a>, all instances of <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/\xca\x83/</span> are spoken like <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[s]</span>, a phenomenon that is also apparent in their English. Exactly the opposite is seen for many <a href="/wiki/Bengali_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Bengali people">Bengalis</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup></li>\n<li>Inability to pronounce certain (especially word-initial) <a href="/wiki/Consonant_cluster" title="Consonant cluster">consonant clusters</a> by people of rural backgrounds, as with some Spanish-speakers. This is usually dealt with by <a href="/wiki/Epenthesis" title="Epenthesis">epenthesis</a>. e.g., <i>school</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/is\xcb\x88ku\xcb\x90l/</span>.</li>\n<li>Many Indians with lower exposure to English also may pronounce /f/ as aspirated <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_bilabial_plosive" class="mw-redirect" title="Voiceless bilabial plosive">voiceless bilabial plosive</a> [p\xca\xb0]. Again note that in Hindi (Devanagari) the loaned /f/ from Persian and Arabic is written by putting a dot beneath the grapheme for native [p\xca\xb0] &lt; \xe0\xa4\xab &gt;: &lt; \xe0\xa4\xab\xe0\xa4\xbc &gt;. This substitution is rarer than that for [z], and in fact in many Hindi /f/ is used by native speakers instead of /p\xca\xb0/, or the two are used interchangeably.</li>\n<li>Many speakers of Indian English do not use the <a href="/wiki/Voiced_postalveolar_fricative" title="Voiced postalveolar fricative">voiced postalveolar fricative</a> (<span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/\xca\x92/</span>). Some Indians use <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/z/</span> or <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/d\xca\x92/</span> instead, e.g. <i>treasure</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/\xcb\x88tr\xc9\x9bz\xc9\x99\xcb\x90r/</span>,<sup id="cite_ref-wells_628_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wells_628-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> and in the south Indian variants, with <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/\xca\x83/</span> as in &lt;"sh\'"ore&gt;, e.g. <i>treasure</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/\xcb\x88tr\xc9\x9b\xca\x83\xc9\x99r/</span>.</li></ul>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Spelling_pronunciation">Spelling pronunciation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indian_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Spelling pronunciation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>A number of distinctive features of Indian English are due to "<a href="/wiki/English_orthography" title="English orthography">the vagaries of English spelling</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-wells_629_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wells_629-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> Most Indian languages, unlike English, have a nearly phonetic spelling, so the spelling of a word is a highly reliable guide to its modern pronunciation. Indians\' tendency to pronounce English phonetically as well can cause divergence from Western English.\n</p>\n<ul><li>In words where the digraph &lt;gh&gt; represents a <a href="/wiki/Voiced_velar_plosive" class="mw-redirect" title="Voiced velar plosive">voiced velar plosive</a> (<span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/\xc9\xa1/</span>) in other accents, some Indian English speakers supply a murmured version <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[\xc9\xa1\xca\xb1]</span>, for example &lt;ghost&gt; <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[\xc9\xa1\xca\xb1o\xcb\x90st]</span>. No other accent of English admits this voiced aspiration.<sup id="cite_ref-wells_628_27-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wells_628-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>Similarly, the digraph &lt;wh&gt; may be aspirated as <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[\xca\x8b\xca\xb1]</span> or <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[w\xca\xb1]</span>, resulting in realisations such as &lt;which&gt; <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[\xca\x8b\xca\xb1\xc9\xaat\xca\x83]</span>, found in no other English accent.<sup id="cite_ref-wells_630_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wells_630-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> This is somewhat similar to the traditional distinction between <i>wh</i> and <i>w</i> present in English, however, wherein the former is /\xca\x8d/, whilst the latter is /w/.</li>\n<li>In unstressed syllables, which speakers of American English would realise as a <a href="/wiki/Schwa" title="Schwa">schwa</a>, speakers of Indian English would use the spelling vowel, making &lt;sanity&gt; sound as [\xcb\x88s\xc3\xa6niti] instead of [\xcb\x88s\xc3\xa6n\xc9\x99ti]. This trait is also present in other South Asian dialects (i.e. <a href="/wiki/Pakistani_English" title="Pakistani English">Pakistani</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lankan_English" title="Sri Lankan English">Sri Lankan English</a>), and in <a href="/wiki/Received_Pronunciation" title="Received Pronunciation">RP</a>, etc.</li>\n<li>The word "of" is usually pronounced with a <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/f/</span> instead of a <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/v/</span> as in most other accents.<sup id="cite_ref-wells_629_31-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wells_629-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>Use of <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[d]</span> instead of <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[t]</span> for the "-ed" ending of the past tense after voiceless consonants, for example "developed" may be <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[\xcb\x88d\xc9\x9b\xca\x8bl\xc9\x99pd]</span> instead of RP <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/d\xc9\xaa\xcb\x88v\xc9\x9bl\xc9\x99pt/</span>.<sup id="cite_ref-wells_628_27-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wells_628-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>Use of <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[s]</span> instead of <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[z]</span> for the "-s" ending of the plural after voiced consonants, for example &lt;dogs&gt; may be <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[da\xc9\xa1s]</span> instead of <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[d\xc9\x92\xc9\xa1z]</span>.<sup id="cite_ref-wells_629_31-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wells_629-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup></li>\n<li>Pronunciation of &lt;house&gt; as [hauz] in both the noun and the verb, instead of [haus] as noun and [hauz] as verb.</li>\n<li>In RP, <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/r/</span> occurs only before a vowel. But some speakers of Indian English, primarily in <a href="/wiki/South_India" title="South India">the South</a>, use <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/r/</span> in almost all positions in words using the letter \'r\',<sup id="cite_ref-wells_629_31-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wells_629-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> similar to most <a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">American</a> and some Irish dialects. The <a href="/wiki/Allophony" class="mw-redirect" title="Allophony">allophone</a> used is a mild trill or a tap. Indian speakers do not typically use the <a href="/wiki/Retroflex_approximant" title="Retroflex approximant">retroflex approximant</a> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/\xc9\xbb/</span> for &lt;r&gt;, which is common for American English speakers.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup></li>\n<li>In certain words, especially Latinate words ending in <i>ile</i>, is pronounced [\xc9\xaa] in America and [a\xc9\xaa] in Britain. Indian English, like most other <a href="/wiki/English_in_the_Commonwealth_of_Nations" title="English in the Commonwealth of Nations">Commonwealth dialects</a>, will invariably use the British pronunciation. Thus, &lt;tensile&gt; would be pronounced as [\xcb\x88t\xc9\x9bnsa\xc9\xaal] like the British, rather than [\xcb\x88t\xc9\x9bns\xc9\xaal] like the American; &lt;anti&gt;, on the other hand, use <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">i</span>, as [\xcb\x88\xc3\xa6nti] like in Britain, rather than [\xcb\x88\xc3\xa6nta\xc9\xaa] like in America. Similar effects of British colonisation are \'re\', \'ise\', and \'our\' spellings in words like \'metre\', \'realise\', and \'endeavour\', respectively, which Americans would spell as \'meter\', \'realize\' and \'endeavor\'.</li>\n<li>Deletion is not commonly used. For example, "salmon" is usually pronounced with a distinct "l".</li></ul>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Supra-segmental_features">Supra-segmental features</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indian_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Supra-segmental features">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>English is a <a href="/wiki/Stress-timed_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Stress-timed language">stress-timed language</a>, and both syllable stress and <a href="/wiki/Stress_(linguistics)" title="Stress (linguistics)">word stress</a>, where only certain words in a sentence or phrase are stressed, are important features of received pronunciation. Indian native languages are actually <a href="/wiki/Syllable-timed_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Syllable-timed language">syllable-timed languages</a>, like French. Indian-English speakers usually speak with a syllabic rhythm.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> Further, in some Indian languages, stress is associated with a low pitch,<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> whereas in most English dialects, stressed syllables are generally pronounced with a higher pitch. Thus, when some Indian speakers speak, they appear to put the stress accents at the wrong syllables, or accentuate all the syllables of a long English word. Certain Indian accents are of a "sing-song" nature, a feature seen in a few English dialects in Britain, such as <a href="/wiki/Scouse" title="Scouse">Scouse</a> and <a href="/wiki/Welsh_English" title="Welsh English">Welsh English</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Numbering_system">Numbering system</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indian_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Numbering system">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Indian_numbering_system" title="Indian numbering system">Indian numbering system</a> is preferred for digit grouping.
