class="toclevel-1 tocsection-37"><a href="#External_links"><span class="tocnumber">12</span> <span class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geology_of_Great_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Seismology" title="Seismology">Seismographical</a> research shows that the <a href="/wiki/Crust_(geology)" title="Crust (geology)">crust</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> below <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a> is from 27 to 35&#160;km (17 to 22 miles) thick. The oldest surface rocks are found in north west <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> and are more than half as <a href="/wiki/Age_of_the_Earth" title="Age of the Earth">old as the planet</a>. These rocks are thought to underlie much of <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a> (although boreholes have only penetrated the first few kilometres), but next appear extensively at the surface in <a href="/wiki/Brittany" title="Brittany">Brittany</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Channel_Islands" title="Channel Islands">Channel Islands</a>. The youngest rocks are found in south east <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bedrock_and_past_volcanism">Bedrock and past volcanism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geology_of_Great_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Bedrock and past volcanism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Bedrock" title="Bedrock">bedrock</a> geology consists of a complex mix of generally older <a href="/wiki/Metamorphic" class="mw-redirect" title="Metamorphic">metamorphic</a> rocks overlain by varying sequences of <a href="/wiki/Sedimentary" class="mw-redirect" title="Sedimentary">sedimentary</a> rocks, into both of which <a href="/wiki/Igneous" class="mw-redirect" title="Igneous">igneous</a> rocks have been intruded at different times. The complexity of Great Britain\'s geology is due ultimately to its being subject to a variety of <a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonic" class="mw-redirect" title="Plate tectonic">plate tectonic</a> processes over a very extended period of time. Changing latitude and sea levels have been important factors in the nature of sedimentary sequences, while successive continental collisions have affected its <a href="/wiki/Geological_structure_of_Great_Britain" title="Geological structure of Great Britain">geological structure</a> with major faulting and folding being a legacy of each <a href="/wiki/Orogeny" title="Orogeny">orogeny</a> (mountain-building period), often associated with <a href="/wiki/Volcanism" title="Volcanism">volcanic</a> activity and the metamorphism of existing rock sequences.\n</p><p>Great Britain does not have any active volcanoes now, but has had an active volcanic past. The last active volcanoes stopped erupting about 60 million years ago and the islands are no longer located upon any <a href="/wiki/List_of_tectonic_plate_interactions" title="List of tectonic plate interactions">tectonic boundary</a> or active volcanic region, nor are there other geological phenomena in the island group able to give rise to active volcanism.<sup id="cite_ref-BGS_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BGS-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> However, on a geological timescale, a considerable degree of active and large-scale volcanism occurred in Great Britain, and many famous landmarks are formed of the <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">eroded</a> <a href="/wiki/Igneous" class="mw-redirect" title="Igneous">igneous</a> rocks formed during these <a href="/wiki/Orogeny" title="Orogeny">orogenies</a> (mountain-building periods).<sup id="cite_ref-BGS_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BGS-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/British_Geological_Survey" title="British Geological Survey">British Geological Survey</a> lists <a href="/wiki/Snowdonia" title="Snowdonia">Snowdonia</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Lake_District" class="mw-redirect" title="The Lake District">the Lake District</a> as having extremely large volcanic eruptions around 450 million years ago (mya), Edinburgh Castle lying upon the remains of a volcano dating back 350 mya, and some islands of western Scotland as being remnants of volcanoes from around 60 mya.<sup id="cite_ref-BGS_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BGS-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Superficial_deposits">Superficial deposits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geology_of_Great_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Superficial deposits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Overlain on this <a href="/wiki/Bedrock" title="Bedrock">bedrock</a> geology ("solid geology" in the terminology of maps) is a varied distribution of unconsolidated material of more recent origin. It includes material deposited by glaciers (<a href="/wiki/Boulder_clay" title="Boulder clay">boulder clay</a>, and other forms of <a href="/wiki/Glacial_landforms#Depositional_landforms" class="mw-redirect" title="Glacial landforms">glacial drift</a> such as sand and gravel). "Drift" geology is often more important than "solid" geology when considering building works, drainage, siting water boreholes, sand and gravel resources and soil fertility. Although "drift" strictly refers to glacial and fluvio-glacial deposits, the term on geological maps has traditionally included other material including alluvium, river terraces, etc. Recent maps use the terms "bedrock" and "superficial" in place of "solid" and "drift".\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geological_history">Geological history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geology_of_Great_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Geological history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>This description of the geological history of Great Britain is based on that of P.&#160;Toghill.<sup id="cite_ref-Toghill_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Toghill-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Precambrian">Precambrian</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geology_of_Great_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Precambrian">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Archaean_eon">Archaean eon</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geology_of_Great_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Archaean eon">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Lewisian_gneiss" class="mw-redirect" title="Lewisian gneiss">Lewisian gneiss</a>, the oldest rocks in <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a>, date from at least 2,700 Ma (Ma = million years ago) in the <a href="/wiki/Archean" title="Archean">Archaean</a> eon, the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> itself being about 4,600 Ma old. They are found in the far north west of <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> and in the <a href="/wiki/Hebrides" title="Hebrides">Hebrides</a>, with a few small outcrops elsewhere. Formed from rock originally deposited at the surface of the planet, the rocks were later buried deep in the Earth\'s crust and <a href="/wiki/Metamorphic_rock" title="Metamorphic rock">metamorphosed</a> into crystalline gneiss.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Proterozoic_eon">Proterozoic eon</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geology_of_Great_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Proterozoic eon">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>South of the gneisses are a complex mixture of rocks forming the North West <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Highlands" title="Scottish Highlands">Highlands</a> and <a href="/wiki/Grampian" title="Grampian">Grampian</a> Highlands in Scotland. These are essentially the remains of folded <a href="/wiki/Sedimentary_rock" title="Sedimentary rock">sedimentary rocks</a> that were originally 25&#160;km thick, deposited over the gneiss on what was then the floor of the <a href="/wiki/Iapetus_Ocean" title="Iapetus Ocean">Iapetus Ocean</a>. The process started in about 1,000 Ma, with a notable 7&#160;km thick layer of <a href="/wiki/Torridon_Sandstone" class="mw-redirect" title="Torridon Sandstone">Torridon Sandstone</a> being deposited about 800 Ma, as well as the <a href="/wiki/Moraine" title="Moraine">debris</a> deposited by an <a href="/wiki/Ice_sheet" title="Ice sheet">ice sheet</a> 670 Ma.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Palaeomagnetism" class="mw-redirect" title="Palaeomagnetism">Palaeomagnetic</a> evidence indicates that 520 Ma, what is now Great Britain was split between two <a href="/wiki/Continent" title="Continent">continents</a>, separated by 7,000&#160;km (4,300&#160;mi) of ocean. The north of Scotland was located at about 20\xc2\xb0 south of the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equator</a> on the continent of <a href="/wiki/Laurentia" title="Laurentia">Laurentia</a> near the <a href="/wiki/Tropic_of_Capricorn" title="Tropic of Capricorn">Tropic of Capricorn</a>, while the rest of the country was at about 60\xc2\xb0 south on the continent of <a href="/wiki/Gondwana" title="Gondwana">Gondwana</a> near the <a href="/wiki/Antarctic_Circle" title="Antarctic Circle">Antarctic Circle</a>.\n</p><p>In Gondwana, England and <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a> were near a <a href="/wiki/Subduction" title="Subduction">subduction</a> zone. Both countries were largely submerged under a shallow sea studded with <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcanic</a> islands. The remains of these islands underlie much of central England with small outcrops visible in many places. Around 600 Ma, the <a href="/wiki/Cadomian_Orogeny" title="Cadomian Orogeny">Cadomian Orogeny</a> (mountain building period) caused the English and Welsh landscape to be transformed into a mountainous region, along with much of north west <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Palaeozoic_era">Palaeozoic era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geology_of_Great_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Palaeozoic era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Cambrian_period">Cambrian period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geology_of_Great_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Cambrian period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>In the early <a href="/wiki/Cambrian" title="Cambrian">Cambrian</a> period, the volcanoes and mountains of England and Wales were eroded as the land became flooded by a rise in sea level, and new layers of <a href="/wiki/Sediment" title="Sediment">sediment</a> were laid down. Much of central England formed a stable block of crust, which has remained largely undeformed ever since. <a href="/wiki/Sandstone" title="Sandstone">Sandstones</a> were deposited in the north of Scotland. The first animals with hard shells evolved at this time, consequently, <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">fossils</a> become much more common in rocks formed during this and later periods.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Ordovician_period">Ordovician period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geology_of_Great_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Ordovician period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>500 million years ago, in the <a href="/wiki/Ordovician" title="Ordovician">Ordovician</a> period, southern Great Britain, the east coast of <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> and south-east <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_(island)" title="Newfoundland (island)">Newfoundland</a> broke away from Gondwana to form the continent of <a href="/wiki/Avalonia" title="Avalonia">Avalonia</a>, which by 440 Ma had drifted (by the mechanisms of <a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">plate tectonics</a>) to about 30\xc2\xb0 south.\n</p><p>During this period, north Wales was subject to <a href="/wiki/Volcanism" title="Volcanism">volcanic</a> activity. The remains of these volcanoes are still visible, one example of which is <a href="/wiki/Rhobell_Fawr" title="Rhobell Fawr">Rhobell Fawr</a> dating from 510 Ma. Large quantities of volcanic <a href="/wiki/Lava" title="Lava">lava</a> and ash known as the <a href="/wiki/Borrowdale_Volcanics" class="mw-redirect" title="Borrowdale Volcanics">Borrowdale Volcanics</a> covered the <a href="/wiki/Lake_District" title="Lake District">Lake District</a> and this can still be seen in the form of mountains such as <a href="/wiki/Helvellyn" title="Helvellyn">Helvellyn</a> and <a href="/wiki/Scafell_Pike" title="Scafell Pike">Scafell Pike</a>.\n</p><p>The Ordovician also saw the formation of the <a href="/wiki/Skiddaw" title="Skiddaw">Skiddaw</a> <a href="/wiki/Slate" title="Slate">slate</a> deposits around 500 Ma.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Silurian_period">Silurian period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geology_of_Great_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Silurian period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Deposition continued into the early part of the <a href="/wiki/Silurian" title="Silurian">Silurian</a> period, with mudstones and sandstones being laid down, notably in Wales.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Avalonia" title="Avalonia">Avalonia</a> had now joined with the continent of <a href="/wiki/Baltica" title="Baltica">Baltica</a>, and the combined landmass collided with <a href="/wiki/Laurentia" title="Laurentia">Laurentia</a> at about 20\xc2\xb0 south between 425 and 400 Ma, joining the southern and northern halves of <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a> together. The resulting <a href="/wiki/Caledonian_Orogeny" class="mw-redirect" title="Caledonian Orogeny">Caledonian Orogeny</a> produced an <a href="/wiki/Alps" title="Alps">Alpine</a>-style mountain range in much of north and west Great Britain. The continental collision was probably at an oblique angle rather than a head-on collision, and this probably led to movement along <a href="/wiki/Fault_(geology)#Strike-slip_faults" title="Fault (geology)">strike-slip faults</a> trending north-east to south-west across Scotland, the <a href="/wiki/Great_Glen_Fault" title="Great Glen Fault">Great Glen Fault</a> being the best example (some of these fault zones may have been old lines of weakness from earlier earth movements).\n</p><p>Volcanic ashes and lavas deposited during the Silurian are still found in the <a href="/wiki/Mendip_Hills" title="Mendip Hills">Mendip Hills</a> and in <a href="/wiki/Pembrokeshire" title="Pembrokeshire">Pembrokeshire</a>.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Devonian_period">Devonian period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geology_of_Great_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Devonian period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Euramerica_en.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Euramerica_en.svg/300px-Euramerica_en.svg.png" width="300" height="214" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Euramerica_en.svg/450px-Euramerica_en.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Euramerica_en.svg/600px-Euramerica_en.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="831" data-file-height="594" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Euramerica_en.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Old Red Sandstone Continent in the Devonian</div></div></div>\n<p>The collision between continents continued during the <a href="/wiki/Devonian_period" class="mw-redirect" title="Devonian period">Devonian period</a>, with continuing uplift, and more volcanic deposits such as those now forming <a href="/wiki/Ben_Nevis" title="Ben Nevis">Ben Nevis</a>. Sea levels varied considerably, with the coastline advancing and retreating from north to south across England. The uplifted region was gradually eroded down, resulting in the deposition of numerous sedimentary rock layers in lowlands and seas. The rocks of marine origin in the county of <a href="/wiki/Devon" title="Devon">Devon</a> gave the period its name, though deposits of this age are found in many other places, such as the <a href="/wiki/Brecon_Beacons" title="Brecon Beacons">Brecon Beacons</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Central_Lowlands" title="Central Lowlands">Midland Valley</a> of Scotland, and the <a href="/wiki/Orkney_Islands" class="mw-redirect" title="Orkney Islands">Orkney Islands</a>. Most of these are of terrestrial origin and are informally known as the <a href="/wiki/Old_Red_Sandstone" title="Old Red Sandstone">Old Red Sandstone</a>.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Caledonian_mountains" class="mw-redirect" title="Caledonian mountains">Caledonian mountains</a> had largely been <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">eroded</a> away by the end of the period during which the country would have experienced an arid <a href="/wiki/Desert" title="Desert">desert</a> <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a> as it was located close to the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equator</a> at between 10\xc2\xb0 and 15\xc2\xb0 south.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Carboniferous_period">Carboniferous period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geology_of_Great_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Carboniferous period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Around 360 Ma, at the start of the <a href="/wiki/Carboniferous" title="Carboniferous">Carboniferous</a> period, Great Britain was lying at the equator, covered by the warm shallow waters of the <a href="/wiki/Rheic_Ocean" title="Rheic Ocean">Rheic Ocean</a>, during which time the Carboniferous Limestone was deposited, as found in the <a href="/wiki/Mendip_Hills" title="Mendip Hills">Mendip Hills</a>, north and south Wales, in the <a href="/wiki/Peak_District" title="Peak District">Peak District</a> of <a href="/wiki/Derbyshire" title="Derbyshire">Derbyshire</a>, north <a href="/wiki/Lancashire" title="Lancashire">Lancashire</a>, the northern <a href="/wiki/Pennines" title="Pennines">Pennines</a> and southeast Scotland. <a href="/wiki/Cave#Solutional_cave" title="Cave">Caves</a> developed more recently in the limestone of some of these areas by the action of <a href="/wiki/Carbonic_acid" title="Carbonic acid">carbonic acid</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Organic_acids" class="mw-redirect" title="Organic acids">organic acids</a> in rainwater and <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a>.\n</p><p>These were followed by dark marine <a href="/wiki/Shales" class="mw-redirect" title="Shales">shales</a>, <a href="/wiki/Siltstones" class="mw-redirect" title="Siltstones">siltstones</a>, and coarse sandstones of the <a href="/wiki/Millstone_Grit" title="Millstone Grit">Millstone Grit</a>. Later, <a href="/wiki/River_delta" title="River delta">river deltas</a> formed and the sediments deposited were colonised by <a href="/wiki/Swamp" title="Swamp">swamps</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rain_forest" class="mw-redirect" title="Rain forest">rain forest</a>. It was in this environment that the <a href="/wiki/Cyclic_sediments" title="Cyclic sediments">cyclic</a> <a href="/wiki/Coal_Measures" class="mw-redirect" title="Coal Measures">Coal Measures</a> were formed, the source of the majority of Great Britain\'s extensive <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a> reserves that powered the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a>. Coal can be found in many areas of Great Britain, as far north as the Midland Valley of <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, as far south as <a href="/wiki/Kent" title="Kent">Kent</a>, although coal mining has largely been concentrated in the Scottish Midlands, <a href="/wiki/English_Midlands" class="mw-redirect" title="English Midlands">English Midlands</a>, northern England and Wales.\n</p><p>Throughout the period, southwest England in particular was affected by the collision of <a href="/wiki/Continental_plates" class="mw-redirect" title="Continental plates">continental plates</a>. The mountain building period known as the <a href="/wiki/Variscan_orogeny" title="Variscan orogeny">Variscan orogeny</a>, which occurred around 280 Ma, caused major deformation in south west England. Towards the end of this period <a href="/wiki/Granite" title="Granite">granite</a> was formed beneath the overlying rocks of <a href="/wiki/Devon" title="Devon">Devon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a>, now exposed at <a href="/wiki/Dartmoor" title="Dartmoor">Dartmoor</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bodmin_Moor" title="Bodmin Moor">Bodmin Moor</a>, giving rise to mineralised deposits of <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tin" title="Tin">tin</a>. The general region of Variscan folding was south of an east\xe2\x80\x93west line roughly from south <a href="/wiki/Pembrokeshire" title="Pembrokeshire">Pembrokeshire</a> to <a href="/wiki/Kent" title="Kent">Kent</a>. The main <a href="/wiki/Tectonic" class="mw-redirect" title="Tectonic">tectonic</a> pressure was from the south or south-east, and there developed <a href="/wiki/Fault_(geology)#Strike-slip_faults" title="Fault (geology)">dextral strike-slip</a> faulting. The <a href="/wiki/Devon" title="Devon">Devon</a>-<a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a> massif may originally have been some distance further east, then to be moved westwards. Lesser Variscan folding took place as far north as <a href="/wiki/Derbyshire" title="Derbyshire">Derbyshire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Berwick-upon-Tweed" title="Berwick-upon-Tweed">Berwick-upon-Tweed</a>.\n</p><p>By the end of the Carboniferous period, the various continents of the Earth had fused to form the super-continent of <a href="/wiki/Pangaea" title="Pangaea">Pangaea</a>. Great Britain was located in the interior of Pangea, where it was again subject to a hot arid desert climate with frequent flash floods leaving deposits that formed beds of red <a href="/wiki/Sedimentary" class="mw-redirect" title="Sedimentary">sedimentary</a> rock, somewhat similar to the later, <a href="/wiki/Triassic" title="Triassic">Triassic</a> <a href="/wiki/New_Red_Sandstone" title="New Red Sandstone">New Red Sandstone</a>.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Permian_period">Permian period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geology_of_Great_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Permian period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Permian" title="Permian">Permian</a> was characterised for 30 million years by arid desert conditions and the erosion of the land that had uplifted in the Variscan Orogeny, southwest England and adjacent areas of the present-day English Channel. Later, much of Great Britain was submerged in shallow waters as the polar ice sheets melted and the <a href="/wiki/Tethys_Ocean" title="Tethys Ocean">Tethys Ocean</a> and <a href="/wiki/Zechstein_Sea" class="mw-redirect" title="Zechstein Sea">Zechstein Sea</a> formed, depositing <a href="/wiki/Shale" title="Shale">shale</a>, <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gravel" title="Gravel">gravel</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Marl" title="Marl">marl</a>, before finally receding to leave a flat desert with <a href="/wiki/Dry_lake" title="Dry lake">salt pans</a>.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mesozoic_era">Mesozoic era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geology_of_Great_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Mesozoic era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Triassic_period">Triassic period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geology_of_Great_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Triassic period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>As Pangaea drifted during the <a href="/wiki/Triassic" title="Triassic">Triassic</a>, Great Britain moved away from the equator until it was between 20\xc2\xb0 and 30\xc2\xb0 north. Red beds, including <a href="/wiki/Sandstones" class="mw-redirect" title="Sandstones">sandstones</a> and red <a href="/wiki/Mudstone" title="Mudstone">mudstones</a> form the main sediments of the <a href="/wiki/New_Red_Sandstone" title="New Red Sandstone">New Red Sandstone</a>. The remnants of the Variscan uplands in <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> to the south were eroded down, resulting in layers of the New Red Sandstone being deposited across central England, and in faulted basins in <a href="/wiki/Cheshire" title="Cheshire">Cheshire</a> and the Irish Sea. A basin developed in the <a href="/wiki/Hampshire" title="Hampshire">Hampshire</a> region around this time. <a href="/wiki/Rift" title="Rift">Rifting</a> occurred within and around Great Britain, prior to the breakup of the super-continent in the <a href="/wiki/Jurassic" title="Jurassic">Jurassic</a> period.\n</p><p>Rock fragments found near <a href="/wiki/Bristol" title="Bristol">Bristol</a> appear to indicate that, in 214 Ma, Great Britain was showered with a fine layer of debris from an <a href="/wiki/Impact_event" title="Impact event">asteroid impact</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Manicouagan_Impact_Crater" class="mw-redirect" title="Manicouagan Impact Crater">Manicouagan Impact Crater</a> in <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, although this is still being debated.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Jurassic_period">Jurassic period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geology_of_Great_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Jurassic period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>As the <a href="/wiki/Jurassic" title="Jurassic">Jurassic</a> period began, <a href="/wiki/Pangaea" title="Pangaea">Pangaea</a> began to break up, sea levels rose and Great Britain drifted on the <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_Plate" title="Eurasian Plate">Eurasian Plate</a> to between 30\xc2\xb0 and 40\xc2\xb0 north. With much of Great Britain under water again, <a href="/wiki/Sedimentary_rock" title="Sedimentary rock">sedimentary rocks</a> were deposited and can now be found underlying much of England from the <a href="/wiki/Cleveland_Hills" title="Cleveland Hills">Cleveland Hills</a> of <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire" title="Yorkshire">Yorkshire</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Jurassic_Coast" title="Jurassic Coast">Jurassic Coast</a> in <a href="/wiki/Geology_of_Dorset" title="Geology of Dorset">Dorset</a>. These include <a href="/wiki/Sandstone" title="Sandstone">sandstones</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greensand_(geology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Greensand (geology)">greensands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oolite" title="Oolite">oolitic</a> <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Cotswold_Hills" class="mw-redirect" title="Cotswold Hills">Cotswold Hills</a>, <a href="/wiki/Corallian_limestone" class="mw-redirect" title="Corallian limestone">corallian limestone</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Vale_of_White_Horse" title="Vale of White Horse">Vale of White Horse</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Portland_Limestone" class="mw-redirect" title="Portland Limestone">Isle of Portland</a>.\n</p><p>The burial of <a href="/wiki/Algae" title="Algae">algae</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a> below the mud of the sea floor during this time resulted in the formation of <a href="/wiki/North_Sea_oil" title="North Sea oil">North Sea oil</a> and <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>, much of it trapped in overlying sandstone by <a href="/wiki/Salt" title="Salt">salt</a> deposits formed as the sea levels fell to form the swamps and salty lakes and lagoons that were home to <a href="/wiki/Dinosaur" title="Dinosaur">dinosaurs</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Cretaceous_period">Cretaceous period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geology_of_Great_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Cretaceous period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>The modern continents having formed, the <a href="/wiki/Cretaceous" title="Cretaceous">Cretaceous</a> saw the formation of the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a>, gradually separating northern Scotland from <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>. The land underwent a series of uplifts to form a fertile plain.\n</p><p>After 20 million years or so, the seas started to flood the land again until much of Great Britain was again below the sea, though sea levels frequently changed. <a href="/wiki/Chalk" title="Chalk">Chalk</a> and <a href="/wiki/Flint" title="Flint">flints</a> were deposited over much of Great Britain, now notably exposed at the <a href="/wiki/White_Cliffs_of_Dover" title="White Cliffs of Dover">White Cliffs of Dover</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Seven_Sisters,_Sussex" title="Seven Sisters, Sussex">Seven Sisters</a>, and also forming <a href="/wiki/Salisbury_Plain" title="Salisbury Plain">Salisbury Plain</a>. The high sea levels left only small areas of land exposed, which accounts for the general lack of land-origin sand, mud or clay sediments found from around this time. Some of the late Cretaceous strata are, in fact, almost pure chalk.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cenozoic_era">Cenozoic era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geology_of_Great_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Cenozoic era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Palaeogene_period">Palaeogene period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geology_of_Great_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Palaeogene period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>In the early <a href="/wiki/Palaeogene" class="mw-redirect" title="Palaeogene">Palaeogene</a> period, between 63 and 52 Ma, the last volcanic rocks in Great Britain were formed. The major eruptions at this time produced the <a href="/wiki/County_Antrim" title="County Antrim">Antrim Plateau</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Basalt" title="Basalt">basaltic</a> columns of the <a href="/wiki/Giant%27s_Causeway" title="Giant&#39;s Causeway">Giant\'s Causeway</a> and the lavas and igneous <a href="/wiki/Intrusion" class="mw-redirect" title="Intrusion">intrusions</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Inner_Hebrides" title="Inner Hebrides">Inner Hebrides</a> of Scotland.\n</p><p>An early phase of the <a href="/wiki/Alpine_Orogeny" class="mw-redirect" title="Alpine Orogeny">Alpine Orogeny</a>, about 50 Ma, was responsible for the shaping of the <a href="/wiki/London_Basin" title="London Basin">London Basin</a> <a href="/wiki/Syncline" title="Syncline">syncline</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Weald-Artois_Anticline" class="mw-redirect" title="Weald-Artois Anticline">Weald-Artois Anticline</a> to the south, the North Downs, South Downs and Chiltern Hills.\n</p><p>During the period, the <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">North Sea</a> formed and Great Britain was <a href="/wiki/Tectonic_uplift" title="Tectonic uplift">uplifted</a>. Some of this uplift was along old lines of weakness left from the Caledonian and Variscan Orogenies long before. The uplifted areas were then eroded, and further sediments, such as the <a href="/wiki/London_Clay" title="London Clay">London Clay</a>, were deposited over southern England, while the <a href="/wiki/English_Channel" title="English Channel">English Channel</a> was characterised by <a href="/wiki/Mud_flat" class="mw-redirect" title="Mud flat">mud flats</a> and river-deposited <a href="/wiki/Sand" title="Sand">sands</a>. Much of the midlands and north of England may have been covered by Jurassic and Cretaceous deposits at the start of the Palaeogene, but these were lost through erosion. By 35 Ma, the landscape was colonised by trees such as <a href="/wiki/Beech" title="Beech">beech</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oak" title="Oak">oak</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sequoia_sempervirens" title="Sequoia sempervirens">redwood</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arecaceae" title="Arecaceae">palm</a>, along with <a href="/wiki/Grass" class="mw-redirect" title="Grass">grasses</a>.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Neogene_period">Neogene period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geology_of_Great_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Neogene period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Miocene_and_Pliocene_epochs">Miocene and Pliocene epochs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geology_of_Great_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Miocene and Pliocene epochs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>\n<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Miocene" title="Miocene">Miocene</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pliocene" title="Pliocene">Pliocene</a> epochs of the <a href="/wiki/Neogene" title="Neogene">Neogene</a>, further uplift and erosion occurred, particularly in Wales, the Pennines, and the Scottish Highlands. Plant and animal types developed into their modern forms, and by about 2 million years ago the landscape would have been broadly recognisable today.\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Quaternary_period">Quaternary period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geology_of_Great_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Quaternary period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Pleistocene_epoch">Pleistocene epoch</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geology_of_Great_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Pleistocene epoch">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>\n<div Merton Stone, one of the largest glacial erratics in England</div></div></div>\n<p>The major changes during the <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">Pleistocene</a> were brought about by several recent <a href="/wiki/Ice_age" title="Ice age">ice ages</a>. The most severe was the <a href="/wiki/Anglian_Stage" class="mw-redirect" title="Anglian Stage">Anglian Glaciation</a>, with ice up to 1,000&#160;m (3300&#160;ft) thick that reached as far south as <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bristol" title="Bristol">Bristol</a>. This took place between about 478,000 to 424,000 years ago, and was responsible for the diversion of the <a href="/wiki/River_Thames" title="River Thames">River Thames</a> onto its present course.\n</p><p>There is extensive evidence in the form of stone tools that southern England was colonised by <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">human</a> populations during the warm <a href="/wiki/Hoxnian_Stage" title="Hoxnian Stage">Hoxnian Stage</a> that followed the Anglian Glaciation. It is possible that the <a href="/wiki/English_Channel" title="English Channel">English Channel</a> repeatedly opened and closed during this period, causing Great Britain to become an island from time to time. The oldest human <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">fossils</a> found to date in the British Isles, which include the skull of <a href="/wiki/Swanscombe_Man" class="mw-redirect" title="Swanscombe Man">Swanscombe Man</a> from 250,000 years ago, and the earlier <a href="/wiki/Clactonian_Man" class="mw-redirect" title="Clactonian Man">Clactonian Man</a>, also date from this period.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Wolstonian_Stage" title="Wolstonian Stage">Wolstonian Glaciation</a>, between about 352,000 to 130,000 years ago, which is thought to have peaked around 150,000 years ago, was named after the village of <a href="/wiki/Wolston" title="Wolston">Wolston</a> southeast of <a href="/wiki/Coventry" title="Coventry">Coventry</a> which is thought to mark the southern limit of the ice.\n</p><p>The Wolstonian Stage was followed by the <a href="/wiki/Eemian_Stage" class="mw-redirect" title="Eemian Stage">Ipswichian Stage</a>, during which <a href="/wiki/Hippopotamus" title="Hippopotamus">hippopotamus</a> are known to have lived as far north as <a href="/wiki/Leeds" title="Leeds">Leeds</a>.\n</p><p>During the most recent <a href="/wiki/Devensian_glaciation" class="mw-redirect" title="Devensian glaciation">Devensian glaciation</a>, which is thought to have started around 115,000 years ago, peaked around 20,000 years ago and ended a mere 10,000 years ago, the <a href="/wiki/River_Usk" title="River Usk">Usk valley</a> and <a href="/wiki/River_Wye" title="River Wye">Wye valley</a> were eroded by <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">glaciers</a>, with the icesheet itself reaching south to <a href="/wiki/Wolverhampton" title="Wolverhampton">Wolverhampton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cardiff" title="Cardiff">Cardiff</a>. The oldest human remains in Great Britain, the <a href="/wiki/Red_Lady_of_Paviland" title="Red Lady of Paviland">Red Lady of Paviland</a> (29,000 years old), date from this time. It is thought that the country was eventually abandoned as the ice sheet reached its peak, being recolonised as it retreated. By 5,000 years ago, it is thought that Great Britain was warmer than it is at present.\n</p><p>Among the features left behind by the ice are the <a href="/wiki/Fjord" title="Fjord">fjords</a> of the west coast of Scotland, the <a href="/wiki/Glaciated_valley" class="mw-redirect" title="Glaciated valley">U-shaped valleys</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Lake_District" title="Lake District">Lake District</a> and <a href="/wiki/Glacial_erratic" title="Glacial erratic">erratics</a> (blocks of rock) that have been transported from the <a href="/wiki/Oslo" title="Oslo">Oslo</a> region of <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a> and deposited on the coast of <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire" title="Yorkshire">Yorkshire</a>.\n</p>\n<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Holocene_epoch">Holocene epoch</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geology_of_Great_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Holocene epoch">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>\n<p>The last twelve thousand years are known as the <a href="/wiki/Holocene" title="Holocene">Holocene</a> Epoch. Amongst the most significant geological features created during this period are the <a href="/wiki/Peat" title="Peat">peat</a> deposits of Scotland, and of coastal and upland areas of England and Wales. Many of the lowland deposits, such as the <a href="/wiki/Somerset_Levels" title="Somerset Levels">Somerset Levels</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Fens" title="The Fens">The Fens</a> and <a href="/wiki/Romney_Marsh" title="Romney Marsh">Romney Marsh</a> have recently been artificially drained.\n</p><p>Since humans began clearing the forest during the <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">New Stone Age</a>, most of the land has now been deforested, speeding the natural processes of <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">erosion</a>. Large quantities of stone, gravel and clay are extracted each year, and by 2000 11% of England was covered by <a href="/wiki/Road" title="Road">roads</a> or <a href="/wiki/Building" title="Building">buildings</a>.\n</p><p>At the present time, Scotland is continuing to rise as a result of the weight of Devensian ice being lifted. Southern and eastern England is sinking, generally estimated at 1&#160;mm (1/25&#160;inch) per year, with the London area sinking at double the speed partly due to the continuing <a href="/wiki/Compaction_(geology)" title="Compaction (geology)">compaction</a> of the recent clay deposits.\n</p><p>In addition, rises in sea level thought to be due to <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a> appear likely to make low-lying areas of land increasingly susceptible to flooding, while in some areas the coastline continues to erode at a geologically rapid rate.\n</p><p>Great Britain continues to be subject to several very minor <a href="/wiki/Earthquake" title="Earthquake">earthquakes</a> each month, and more occasional light to moderate ones. During the 20th century, 25 earthquakes with a moment magnitude of 4.5 to 6.1 were felt,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> many of them originating within the Isles themselves.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geological_features">Geological features</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geology_of_Great_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Geological features">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Cheddar_Gorge" title="Cheddar Gorge">Cheddar Gorge</a> - the largest gorge in Great Britain</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Jurassic_Coast" title="Jurassic Coast">Jurassic Coast</a> - a <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" class="mw-redirect" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage Site</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Great_Glen_Fault" title="Great Glen Fault">Great Glen Fault</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Highland_Boundary_Fault" title="Highland Boundary Fault">Highland Boundary Fault</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Southern_Uplands_Fault" title="Southern Uplands Fault">Southern Uplands Fault</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Central_Lowlands" title="Central Lowlands">Midland Valley of Scotland</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Fingal%27s_Cave" title="Fingal&#39;s Cave">Fingal\'s Cave</a> - line of closure of the Iapetus suture.</li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geological_resources">Geological resources</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geology_of_Great_Britain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Geological resources">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Great Britain\'s complex geology has provided it with a wide range of geological resources including abundant supplies of <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a>, the initial extraction of which powered the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a>. During the 20th century, the exploitation of <a href="/wiki/North_Sea_oil" title="North Sea oil">North Sea oil</a> and gas provided further energy supplies for the nation. <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_power_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Geothermal power in the United Kingdom">Geothermal energy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Geology_of_Dorset#Coastline" title="Geology of
