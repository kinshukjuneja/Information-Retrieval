class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Haakon_Sigurdsson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Haakon was the son of <a href="/wiki/Sigurd_Haakonsson" title="Sigurd Haakonsson">Sigurd Haakonsson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jarl_of_Lade" class="mw-redirect" title="Jarl of Lade">Jarl of Lade</a> and  ruler of <a href="/wiki/Tr%C3%B8ndelag" title="Tr\xc3\xb8ndelag">Tr\xc3\xb8ndelag</a> and <a href="/wiki/H%C3%A5logaland" title="H\xc3\xa5logaland">H\xc3\xa5logaland</a>. His mother was Bergljot Toresdatter, daughter of <a href="https://no.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tore_Ragnvaldsson_M%C3%B8rejarl" class="extiw" title="no:Tore Ragnvaldsson M\xc3\xb8rejarl">Tore Ragnvaldsson</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jarl_of_M%C3%B8re&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Jarl of M\xc3\xb8re (page does not exist)">Jarl of M\xc3\xb8re</a>. <a href="/wiki/Adam_of_Bremen" title="Adam of Bremen">Adam of Bremen</a> wrote that he was "of the stock of Ivar (possibly <a href="/wiki/Ivar_the_Boneless" title="Ivar the Boneless">Ivar the Boneless</a>) and descended from a race of giants".<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> In the sagas, Haakon claimed descent from the divine lineage of <a href="/wiki/Sons_of_Odin" title="Sons of Odin">S\xc3\xa6ming</a>, son of <a href="/wiki/Odin" title="Odin">Odin</a>. title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>Haakon Jarl commands the clergymen to return ashore</i> <br />Christian Krogh (1899)</div></div></div>\n<p>Haakon became earl after his father was killed by King <a href="/wiki/Harald_Greycloak" title="Harald Greycloak">Harald Greycloak</a>\'s men in 961. Haakon Jarl warred with King Harald for some time, until he was forced to flee to <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a>, where he conspired with <a href="/wiki/Harald_Bluetooth" title="Harald Bluetooth">Harald Bluetooth</a> against Harald Greycloak.\n</p><p>The two arranged the death of Harald Greycloak around 971, after which Harald Bluetooth invited his foster-son to be invested with new Danish fiefs. Civil war broke out between Haakon Jarl and the surviving brothers of Harald Greycloak, but Haakon proved victorious.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> After this, Haakon Jarl ruled Norway as a <a href="/wiki/Vassal" title="Vassal">vassal</a> of Harald Bluetooth, but he was in reality an independent ruler. For Harald, he attacked <a href="/wiki/G%C3%B6taland" title="G\xc3\xb6taland">G\xc3\xb6taland</a> and killed its ruler <a href="/wiki/Ottar_Jarl" title="Ottar Jarl">Ottar Jarl</a>.\n</p><p>Around 973-974, he went to Denmark to help Harald Bluetooth of Denmark in his defense against the Holy Roman Emperor <a href="/wiki/Otto_II" class="mw-redirect" title="Otto II">Otto II</a>. Otto\'s forces successfully opposed an attempt by Harald to throw off the German yoke. After that Haakon paid no taxes to Denmark.\n</p><p>Haakon was a strong believer in the old <a href="/wiki/Norse_gods" class="mw-redirect" title="Norse gods">Norse gods</a>, even after Harald Bluetooth forced him to accept baptism around 975 and assigned him clergymen to take to Norway to spread <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a>. When a favourable wind came for Haakon to leave, he commanded the clergymen to return ashore,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> and broke his allegiance to Denmark. In 977 <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_I_of_Kiev" class="mw-redirect" title="Vladimir I of Kiev">Vladimir I of Kiev</a> fled to him, collecting as many of the Viking warriors as he could to assist him to recover <a href="/wiki/Novgorod" class="mw-redirect" title="Novgorod">Novgorod</a>, and on his return the next year marched against <a href="/wiki/Yaropolk_I_of_Kiev" title="Yaropolk I of Kiev">Yaropolk I of Kiev</a>.  In 986, a Danish invasion fleet led by the fabled <a href="/wiki/Jomsvikings" title="Jomsvikings">Jomsvikings</a> was defeated at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Hj%C3%B6rungav%C3%A1gr" title="Battle of Hj\xc3\xb6rungav\xc3\xa1gr">Battle of Hj\xc3\xb6rungav\xc3\xa1gr</a>.\n</p><p>In 995, a quarrel broke out between Haakon and the <a href="/wiki/Tr%C3%B8nder" class="mw-redirect" title="Tr\xc3\xb8nder">Tr\xc3\xb8nders</a> just as <a href="/wiki/Olaf_I_of_Norway" class="mw-redirect" title="Olaf I of Norway">Olaf Tryggvason</a>, a descendant of <a href="/wiki/Harald_I_of_Norway" class="mw-redirect" title="Harald I of Norway">Harald Fairhair</a> arrived. Haakon quickly lost all support, and was killed by his own slave and friend, <a href="/wiki/Tormod_Kark" title="Tormod Kark">Tormod Kark</a>, while hiding in the pig sty in the farm <a href="/wiki/Rimul" title="Rimul">Rimul</a> in <a href="/wiki/Melhus" title="Melhus">Melhus</a>. <a href="/wiki/Jarlshola" title="Jarlshola">Jarlshola</a> is the location in Melhus thought to have been the hiding place of Haakon Jarl and Tormod Kark on their last night before the infamous murder at Rimul. After his death, Haakon Jarl\'s two sons <a href="/wiki/Eirik_H%C3%A5konson" class="mw-redirect" title="Eirik H\xc3\xa5konson">Eirik H\xc3\xa5konson</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sweyn_Haakonsson" title="Sweyn Haakonsson">Sveinn H\xc3\xa1konarson</a>, fled for protection to the king of Sweden, <a href="/wiki/Olof_Sk%C3%B6tkonung" title="Olof Sk\xc3\xb6tkonung">Olof Sk\xc3\xb6tkonung</a>.\n</p><p>A number of (textually related) sources also recount Earl Haakon\'s predilection for raping women, whether the daughters of nobles or of commoners.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Children">Children</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Haakon_Sigurdsson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Korsvikaspillet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Haakon Jarl is a central figure in <i>H\xc3\xa5kon og Kark</i> which is performed annually at the Korsvikaspillet festival in <a href="/wiki/Korsvika" title="Korsvika">Korsvika</a> in <a href="/wiki/Trondheim" title="Trondheim">Trondheim</a>. The play is based on the story of Haakon Jarl and Tormod Kark as portrayed in the <a href="/wiki/Sagas" class="mw-redirect" title="Sagas">Sagas</a> by <a href="/wiki/Snorri_Sturluson" title="Snorri Sturluson">Snorri Sturluson</a>. The first play was a poetic tableau that was made in connection with the 800-year anniversary of the <a href="/wiki/Lade_Church" title="Lade Church">Lade Church</a>  (<i>Lade kirke</i>) in 1989 and repeated two years later. In 1995, <a href="https://no.wikipedia.org/wiki/Idar_Lind" class="extiw" title="no:Idar Lind">Idar Lind</a> wrote a new script. The music is composed by <a href="https://no.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frode_Fjellheim" class="extiw" title="no:Frode Fjellheim">Frode Fjellheim</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Primary_sources">Primary sources</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Haakon_Sigurdsson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Primary sources">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Source basis for Haakon Jarl are considerable. He was given  coverage in several sagas, including by <a href="/wiki/Snorri_Sturluson" title="Snorri Sturluson">Snorri Sturluson</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/Heimskringla" title="Heimskringla">Heimskringla</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/%C3%81grip_af_N%C3%B3regskonungas%C3%B6gum" title="\xc3\x81grip af N\xc3\xb3regskonungas\xc3\xb6gum">\xc3\x81grip af N\xc3\xb3regskonungas\xc3\xb6gum</a></i> and more. According to <i><a href="/wiki/Hallfre%C3%B0ar_saga" title="Hallfre\xc3\xb0ar saga">Hallfre\xc3\xb0ar saga</a></i> the poet <a href="/wiki/Hallfre%C3%B0r" class="mw-redirect" title="Hallfre\xc3\xb0r">Hallfre\xc3\xb0r</a> composed a <i><a href="/wiki/Dr%C3%A1pa" class="mw-redirect" title="Dr\xc3\xa1pa">dr\xc3\xa1pa</a></i> on the earl. Several disjoint stanzas by Hallfre\xc3\xb0r in <i><a href="/wiki/Sk%C3%A1ldskaparm%C3%A1l" title="Sk\xc3\xa1ldskaparm\xc3\xa1l">Sk\xc3\xa1ldskaparm\xc3\xa1l</a></i> are often thought to belong to this otherwise lost poem.\n</p>\n<h2><span id="Oehlenschl.C3.A4ger_tragedy"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Oehlenschl\xc3\xa4ger_tragedy">Oehlenschl\xc3\xa4ger tragedy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Haakon_Sigurdsson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Oehlenschl\xc3\xa4ger tragedy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Haakon Jarl\'s life also received literary treatment by Danish poet <a href="/wiki/Adam_Oehlenschl%C3%A4ger" title="Adam Oehlenschl\xc3\xa4ger">Adam Oehlenschl\xc3\xa4ger</a>, in his tragedy <i>Hakon Jarl</i>, written in six weeks in 1805  during a stay in <a href="/wiki/Halle,_Saxony-Anhalt" class="mw-redirect" title="Halle, Saxony-Anhalt">Halle</a>, after reading Snorri\'s <i>Heimskringla</i>. The theme is the conflict between paganism and Christianity. There is an English translation by J. C. Lindberg.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Oehlenschl\xc3\xa4ger\'s play later formed the basis for <a href="/wiki/Smetana" class="mw-redirect" title="Smetana">Smetana</a>\'s symphonic poem <i><a href="/wiki/Hakon_Jarl_(Smetana)" title="Hakon Jarl (Smetana)">Hakon Jarl</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
