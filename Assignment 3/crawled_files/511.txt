It is headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Geneva" title="Geneva">Geneva</a>, Switzerland,<sup id="cite_ref-About_ISO_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-About_ISO-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> and works href="/w/index.php?title=International_Organization_for_Standardization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The International Organization for Standardization is an independent, non-governmental organization, the members of which are the standards organizations of the 168<sup id="cite_ref-ISO_members_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ISO_members-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> member countries. It is the world\'s largest developer of voluntary international standards and facilitates world trade by providing common standards between nations. Over twenty thousand standards have been set covering everything from manufactured products and technology to food safety, agriculture and healthcare.<sup id="cite_ref-About_ISO_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-About_ISO-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Use of the standards aids in the creation of products and services that are safe, reliable and of good quality. The standards help businesses increase productivity while minimizing errors and waste. By enabling products from different markets to be directly compared, they facilitate companies in entering new markets and assist in the development of global trade on a fair basis. The standards also serve to safeguard consumers and the end-users of products and services, ensuring that certified products conform to the minimum standards set internationally.<sup id="cite_ref-About_ISO_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-About_ISO-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Language_usage">Language usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_Organization_for_Standardization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-languages_2-1" class="reference"><a class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_Organization_for_Standardization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Name and abbreviations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The name of the organization in French is <i><i lang="fr" title="French language text">Organisation internationale de normalisation</i></i>, and in Russian, <span lang="ru" title="Russian language text">\xd0\x9c\xd0\xb5\xd0\xb6\xd0\xb4\xd1\x83\xd0\xbd\xd0\xb0\xd1\x80\xd0\xbe\xd0\xb4\xd0\xbd\xd0\xb0\xd1\x8f \xd0\xbe\xd1\x80\xd0\xb3\xd0\xb0\xd0\xbd\xd0\xb8\xd0\xb7\xd0\xb0\xd1\x86\xd0\xb8\xd1\x8f \xd0\xbf\xd0\xbe \xd1\x81\xd1\x82\xd0\xb0\xd0\xbd\xd0\xb4\xd0\xb0\xd1\x80\xd1\x82\xd0\xb8\xd0\xb7\xd0\xb0\xd1\x86\xd0\xb8\xd0\xb8</span> (<i><i lang="ru-Latn" title="Russian-language transliteration">Mezhdunarodnaya organizatsiya po standartizatsii</i></i>).  <i>ISO</i> is not an <a href="/wiki/Acronym" title="Acronym">acronym</a>. The organization adopted <i>ISO</i> as its abbreviated name in reference to the Greek word <i><i lang="grc-Latn" title="Ancient Greek-language transliteration">isos</i></i> (<span lang="grc" title="Ancient Greek language text">\xce\xaf\xcf\x83\xce\xbf\xcf\x82</span>, meaning "equal"),<sup id="cite_ref-about_iso_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-about_iso-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> as its name in the three official languages would have different acronyms. During the founding meetings of the new organization, the Greek word explanation was not invoked, so this meaning may have been made public later.<sup id="cite_ref-iso_name_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iso_name-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>ISO gives this explanation of the name: "Because \'International Organization for Standardization\' would have different acronyms in different languages (IOS in English, OIN in French), our founders decided to give it the short form <i>ISO</i>. <i>ISO</i> is derived from the Greek <i><i lang="grc-Latn" title="Ancient Greek-language transliteration">isos</i></i>, meaning equal. Whatever the country, whatever the language, the short form of our name is always <i>ISO</i>."<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Both the name <i>ISO</i> and the ISO logo are registered trademarks, and their use is restricted.<sup id="cite_ref-iso_logo_7-0" class="reference"><a It was suspended in 1942<sup id="cite_ref-Brief_history_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brief_history-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War&#160;II</a>, but after the war ISA was approached by the recently formed United Nations Standards Coordinating Committee (UNSCC) with a proposal to form a new global standards body. In October 1946, ISA and UNSCC delegates from 25 countries met in London and agreed to join forces to create the new International Organization for Standardization; the new organization officially began operations in February&#160;1947.<sup id="cite_ref-50_years_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50_years-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_Organization_for_Standardization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>ISO is a voluntary organization whose members are recognized authorities on standards, each one representing one country. Members meet annually at a General Assembly to discuss ISO\'s strategic objectives. The organization is coordinated by a Central Secretariat based in <a href="/wiki/Geneva" title="Geneva">Geneva</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ISOStructureAndGovernance_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ISOStructureAndGovernance-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>A Council with a rotating membership of 20 member bodies provides guidance and governance, including setting the Central Secretariat\'s annual budget.<sup id="cite_ref-ISOStructureAndGovernance_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ISOStructureAndGovernance-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-tech_ctte_list_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tech_ctte_list-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The Technical Management Board is responsible for over 250 <a href="/wiki/List_of_International_Organization_for_Standardization_technical_committees" title="List of International Organization for Standardization technical committees">technical committees</a>, who develop the ISO standards.<sup id="cite_ref-ISOStructureAndGovernance_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ISOStructureAndGovernance-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ISO_members_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ISO_members-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>ISO has three membership categories:<sup id="cite_ref-ISO_members_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ISO_members-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<ul><li><i>Member bodies</i> are national bodies considered the most representative standards body in each country. These are the only members of ISO that have voting rights.</li>\n<li><i>Correspondent members</i> are countries that do not have their own standards organization. These members are informed about ISO\'s work, but do not participate in standards promulgation.</li>\n<li><i>Subscriber members</i> are countries with small economies. They pay reduced membership fees, but can follow the development of standards.</li></ul>\n<p>Participating members are called "P" members, as opposed to observing members, who are called "O" members.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Financing">Financing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_Organization_for_Standardization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Financing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>ISO is funded by a combination of:<sup id="cite_ref-iso_general_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iso_general-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> \n</p>\n<ul><li>Organizations that manage the specific projects or loan experts to participate in the technical work.</li>\n<li>Subscriptions from member bodies. These subscriptions are in proportion to each country\'s <a href="/wiki/Gross_national_product" class="mw-redirect" title="Gross national product">gross national product</a> and trade figures.</li>\n<li>Sale of standards.</li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="International_Standards_and_other_publications"><span id="Standards"></span>International Standards and other publications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_Organization_for_Standardization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: International Standards and other publications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_International_Organization_for_Standardization_standards" title="List of International Organization for Standardization standards">List of International Organization for Standardization standards</a></div>\n<p>ISO\'s main products are international standards. ISO also publishes technical reports, technical specifications, publicly available specifications, technical <a href="/wiki/Erratum" title="Erratum">corrigenda</a>, and guides.<sup id="cite_ref-ISOUS95IECUS95directives2004_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ISOUS95IECUS95directives2004-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-iso-directives_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iso-directives-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><b>International standards</b>\n</p>\n<dl><dd>These are designated using the format <i>ISO[/IEC] [/ASTM] [IS] nnnnn[-p]:[yyyy] Title</i>, where <i>nnnnn</i> is the number of the standard, <i>p</i> is an optional part number, <i>yyyy</i> is the year published, and <i>Title</i> describes the subject. <i>IEC</i> for <i><a href="/wiki/International_Electrotechnical_Commission" title="International Electrotechnical Commission">International Electrotechnical Commission</a></i> is included if the standard results from the work of ISO/IEC JTC1 (the ISO/IEC Joint Technical Committee). <i>ASTM</i> (American Society for Testing and Materials) is used for standards developed in cooperation with <a href="/wiki/ASTM_International" title="ASTM International">ASTM International</a>. <i>yyyy</i> and <i>IS</i> are not used for an incomplete or unpublished standard and may under some circumstances be left off the title of a published work.</dd></dl>\n<p><b>Technical reports</b>\n</p>\n<dl><dd>These are issued when a technical committee or subcommittee has collected data of a different kind from that normally published as an International Standard,<sup id="cite_ref-ISOUS95IECUS95directives2004_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ISOUS95IECUS95directives2004-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> such as references and explanations. The naming conventions for these are the same as for standards, except <i>TR</i> prepended instead of <i>IS</i> in the report\'s name.<br />For example:</dd></dl>\n<ul><li>ISO/IEC TR 17799:2000 Code of Practice for Information Security Management</li>\n<li>ISO/TR 19033:2000 Technical product documentation \xe2\x80\x94 Metadata for construction documentation</li></ul>\n<p><b>Technical and publicly available specifications</b>\n</p>\n<dl><dd>Technical specifications may be produced when "the subject in question is still under development or where for any other reason there is the future but not immediate possibility of an agreement to publish an International Standard". A publicly available specification is usually "an intermediate specification, published prior to the development of a full International Standard, or, in IEC may be a \'dual logo\' publication published in collaboration with an external organization".<sup id="cite_ref-ISOUS95IECUS95directives2004_18-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ISOUS95IECUS95directives2004-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> By convention, both types of specification are named in a manner similar to the organization\'s technical reports.<br />For example:</dd></dl>\n<ul><li>ISO/TS 16952-1:2006 Technical product documentation \xe2\x80\x94 Reference designation system \xe2\x80\x94 Part 1: General application rules</li>\n<li>ISO/PAS 11154:2006 Road vehicles \xe2\x80\x94 Roof load carriers</li></ul>\n<p><b>Technical corrigenda</b>\n</p>\n<dl><dd>ISO also sometimes issues "technical corrigenda" (where "corrigenda" is the plural of <a href="/wiki/Erratum" title="Erratum">corrigendum</a>). These are amendments made to existing standards due to minor technical flaws, usability improvements, or limited-applicability extensions. They are generally issued with the expectation that the affected standard will be updated or withdrawn at its next scheduled review.<sup id="cite_ref-ISOUS95IECUS95directives2004_18-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ISOUS95IECUS95directives2004-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup></dd></dl>\n<p><b>ISO guides</b>\nThese are meta-standards covering "matters related to international standardization".<sup id="cite_ref-ISOUS95IECUS95directives2004_18-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ISOUS95IECUS95directives2004-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> They are named using the format <i>"ISO[/IEC] Guide N:yyyy: Title"</i>.<br />For example:\n</p>\n<ul><li>ISO/IEC Guide 2:2004 Standardization and related activities \xe2\x80\x94 General vocabulary</li>\n<li>ISO/IEC Guide 65:1996 General requirements for bodies operating product certification</li></ul>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Document_copyright">Document copyright</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_Organization_for_Standardization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Document copyright">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>ISO documents are copyrighted and ISO charges for most copies. It does not, however, charge for most draft copies of documents in electronic format. Although they are useful, care must be taken using these drafts as there is the possibility of substantial change before they become finalized as standards. Some standards by ISO and its official U.S. representative (and, via the U.S. National Committee, the <a href="/wiki/International_Electrotechnical_Commission" title="International Electrotechnical Commission">International Electrotechnical Commission</a>) are made freely available.<sup id="cite_ref-FreelyAvailableStandards_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FreelyAvailableStandards-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-free_ansi_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-free_ansi-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Standardization_process">Standardization process</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_Organization_for_Standardization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Standardization process">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>A standard published by ISO/IEC is the last stage of a long process that commonly starts with the proposal of new work within a committee. hlist-separated"><ul><li>ISO</li><li>TR</li><li>TS</li><li>IWA</li><li>Amd</li><li>Cor</li></ul></div></td>\n<td></td></tr>\n\n<tr><td>90</td>\n<td>Review</td>\n<td></td>\n<td></td></tr>\n\n<tr><td>95</td>\n<td>Withdrawal</td>\n<td></td>\n<td></td>\n<td></td></tr>\n</tbody></table>\n<p>It is possible to omit certain stages, if there is a document with a certain degree of maturity at the start of a standardization project, for example a standard developed by another organization. ISO/IEC directives allow also the so-called "Fast-track procedure". In this procedure a document is submitted directly for approval as a draft International Standard (DIS) to the ISO member bodies or as a final draft International Standard (FDIS) if the document was developed by an international standardizing body recognized by the ISO Council.<sup id="cite_ref-iso-stages_24-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iso-stages-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The first step\xe2\x80\x94a proposal of work (New Proposal) is approved at the relevant subcommittee or technical committee (e.g.,&#160;SC29 and JTC1 respectively in the case of <a href="/wiki/Moving_Picture_Experts_Group" title="Moving Picture Experts Group">Moving Picture Experts Group</a> \xe2\x80\x93 ISO/IEC JTC1/SC29/WG11). A working group (WG) of experts is set up by the TC/SC for the preparation of a working draft. When the scope of a new work is sufficiently clarified, some of the working groups (e.g.,&#160;MPEG) usually make open request for proposals\xe2\x80\x94known as a "call for proposals". The first document that is produced for example for audio and video coding standards is called a verification model&#160;(VM) (previously also called a "simulation and test model"). When a sufficient confidence in the stability of the standard under development is reached, a working draft&#160;(WD) is produced. This is in the form of a standard but is kept internal to working group for revision. When a working draft is sufficiently solid and the working group is satisfied that it has developed the best technical solution to the problem being addressed, it becomes committee draft&#160;(CD). If it is required, it is then sent to the P-members of the TC/SC (national bodies) for ballot.\n</p><p>The CD becomes final committee draft (FCD) if the number of positive votes is above the quorum. Successive committee drafts may be considered until consensus is reached on the technical content. When it is reached, the text is finalized for submission as a draft International Standard (DIS). The text is then submitted to national bodies for voting and comment within a period of five months. It is approved for submission as a final draft International Standard&#160;(FDIS) if a two-thirds majority of the P-members of the TC/SC are in favour and not more than one-quarter of the total number of votes cast are negative. ISO will then hold a ballot with National Bodies where no technical changes are allowed (yes/no ballot), within a period of two months. It is approved as an International Standard&#160;(IS) if a two-thirds majority of the P-members of the TC/SC is in favour and not more than one-quarter of the total number of votes cast are negative. After approval, only minor editorial changes are introduced into the final text. The final text is sent to the ISO Central Secretariat, which publishes it as the International Standard.<sup id="cite_ref-about-mpeg_22-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-about-mpeg-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-iso-stages_24-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iso-stages-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Products_named_after_ISO">Products named after ISO</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_Organization_for_Standardization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Products named after ISO">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The fact that many of the ISO-created standards are ubiquitous has led, on occasion, to common use of "ISO" to describe the actual product that conforms to a standard. Some examples of this are:\n</p>\n<ul><li>Many <a href="/wiki/CD_image" class="mw-redirect" title="CD image">CD images</a> end in the <a href="/wiki/File_extension" class="mw-redirect" title="File extension">file extension</a> "<a href="/wiki/ISO_image" title="ISO image">ISO</a>" to signify that they are using the <a href="/wiki/ISO_9660" title="ISO 9660">ISO&#160;9660</a> standard file system as opposed to another file system\xe2\x80\x94hence CD&#160;images are commonly referred to as "ISOs". Virtually all computers with <a href="/wiki/CD-ROM" title="CD-ROM">CD-ROM</a> drives that can read CDs use this standard. Some DVD-ROMs also use ISO&#160;9660 file systems.</li>\n<li>Photographic film\'s sensitivity to light (its "<a href="/wiki/Film_speed" title="Film speed">film speed</a>") is described by <a href="/wiki/ISO_6" class="mw-redirect" title="ISO 6">ISO&#160;6</a>, <a href="/wiki/ISO_2240" class="mw-redirect" title="ISO 2240">ISO&#160;2240</a> and <a href="/wiki/ISO_5800" class="mw-redirect" title="ISO 5800">ISO&#160;5800</a>. Hence, the film\'s speed is often referred to by its ISO&#160;number.</li>\n<li>As it was originally defined in ISO&#160;518, the flash <a href="/wiki/Hot_shoe" title="Hot shoe">hot shoe</a> found on cameras is often called the "ISO&#160;shoe".</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/ISO_11783" title="ISO 11783">ISO 11783</a>, which is marketed as ISOBUS.</li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_Organization_for_Standardization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>With the exception of a small number of isolated standards,<sup id="cite_ref-FreelyAvailableStandards_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FreelyAvailableStandards-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> ISO standards are normally not available free of charge, but for a purchase fee,<sup id="cite_ref-iso_shopFAQ_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iso_shopFAQ-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> which has been seen by some as too expensive for small <a href="/wiki/Open_source" class="mw-redirect" title="Open source">open source</a> projects.<sup id="cite_ref-oreilly2007_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oreilly2007-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/ISO/IEC_JTC1" class="mw-redirect" title="ISO/IEC JTC1">ISO/IEC JTC1</a> fast-track procedures ("Fast-track" as used by <a href="/wiki/Office_Open_XML" title="Office Open XML">OOXML</a> and "PAS" as used by <a href="/wiki/OpenDocument" title="OpenDocument">OpenDocument</a>) have garnered criticism in relation to the <a href="/wiki/Standardization_of_Office_Open_XML" title="Standardization of Office Open XML">standardization of Office Open XML</a> (ISO/IEC&#160;29500). Martin Bryan, outgoing Convenor of <a href="/wiki/ISO/IEC_JTC1/SC34" class="mw-redirect" title="ISO/IEC JTC1/SC34">ISO/IEC JTC1/SC34</a> WG1, is quoted as saying:\n</p>\n<blockquote>\n<p>I would recommend my successor that it is perhaps time to pass WG1\xe2\x80\x99s outstanding standards over to OASIS, where they can get approval in less than a year and then do a PAS submission to ISO, which will get a lot more attention and be approved much faster than standards currently can be within WG1.\n</p>\n</blockquote>\n<blockquote>\n<p>The disparity of rules for PAS, Fast-Track and ISO committee generated standards is fast making ISO a laughing stock in IT circles. The days of open standards development are fast disappearing. Instead we are getting \'standardization by corporation\'.<sup id="cite_ref-jtc1_sc34_0940_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jtc1_sc34_0940-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n</blockquote>\n<p>Computer security entrepreneur and <a href="/wiki/Ubuntu_(operating_system)" class="mw-redirect" title="Ubuntu (operating system)">Ubuntu</a> investor, <a href="/wiki/Mark_Shuttleworth" title="Mark Shuttleworth">Mark Shuttleworth</a>, commented on the <a href="/wiki/Standardization_of_Office_Open_XML" title="Standardization of Office Open XML">Standardization of Office Open XML</a> process by saying "I think it de-values the confidence people have in the standards setting process," and Shuttleworth alleged that ISO did not carry out its responsibility. He also noted that <a href="/wiki/Microsoft" title="Microsoft">Microsoft</a> had intensely lobbied many countries that traditionally had not participated in ISO and stacked technical committees with Microsoft employees, solution providers and resellers sympathetic to Office Open XML.\n</p>\n<blockquote>\n<p>When you have a process built on trust and when that trust is abused, ISO should halt the process... ISO is an engineering <a href="/wiki/Good_ol%27_boy" title="Good ol&#39; boy">old boys club</a> and these things are boring so you have to have a lot of passion \xe2\x80\xa6 then suddenly you have an investment of a lot of money and lobbying and you get artificial results. The process is not set up to deal with intensive corporate <a href="/wiki/Lobbying" title="Lobbying">lobbying</a> and so you end up with something being a standard that is not clear.<sup id="cite_ref-zdnet_2222_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-zdnet_2222-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n</blockquote>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span
