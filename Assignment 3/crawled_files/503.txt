Although a <a href="/wiki/Germans" title="Germans">German</a>, <a href="/wiki/Martin_Luther" title="Martin Luther">Martin Luther</a>, was responsible for the beginnings of the <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Reformation" class="mw-redirect" title="Protestant Reformation">Protestant Reformation</a> in the early 16th century, the United Kingdom, and especially <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, developed the Reformation further and produced many of its most notable figures. Protestantism influenced many of England\'s monarchs in the 16th and 17th centuries, including <a href="/wiki/Henry_VIII" class="mw-redirect" title="Henry VIII">Henry VIII</a>, <a href="/wiki/Edward_VI" class="mw-redirect" title="Edward VI">Edward VI</a>, <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_I" class="mw-redirect" title="Elizabeth I">Elizabeth I</a>, and <a href="/wiki/James_I" class="mw-redirect" title="James I">James I</a>. Violence was commonplace, and persecution was largely dependent on whether the monarch was <a href="/wiki/Catholic" class="mw-redirect" title="Catholic">Catholic</a> or Protestant. Reformers and early church leaders were greatly persecuted in the first centuries of the Reformation, but the non-conformist movement survived. As a result of the Reformation, Protestantism is the most widely practiced religion in the modern United Kingdom, although participation in the church has weakened in recent years.\n</p>\n<div tocsection-11"><a href="#References"><span class="tocnumber">6</span> <span class="toctext">References</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom_before_the_Reformation">United Kingdom before the Reformation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Protestantism_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: United Kingdom before the Reformation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_Christianity_in_Britain" title="History of Christianity in Britain">History of Christianity in Britain</a></div>\n<p>Before Protestantism reached England, the <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Roman Catholic Church</a> was the established state church. <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a> were also closely tied to <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholicism" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholicism">Roman Catholicism</a>, but <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> had been dominated by many pagan religions that the <a href="/wiki/Celts" title="Celts">Celts</a> practiced.<sup id="cite_ref-history_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_Reformation">Early Reformation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Protestantism_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early Reformation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In Catholic England, the only <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">Bible</a> available was written in <a href="/wiki/Latin_Vulgate" class="mw-redirect" title="Latin Vulgate">Latin Vulgate</a>, a translation of proper <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> considered holy by the Roman Catholic Church. As a result, only <a href="/wiki/Clergy" title="Clergy">clergy</a> had access to copies of the Bible. Countrymen were dependent on their local priests for the reading of scripture because they could not read the text for themselves. Early in the Reformation, one of the fundamental disagreements between the Roman Church and Protestant leaders was over the distribution of the Bible in the people\'s common language.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/John_Wycliffe" title="John Wycliffe">John Wycliffe</a> helped make the Bible available to all people, regardless of their wealth or social standing. Wycliffe translated the whole Bible into the <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English language</a> because he believed that Englishmen needed to be familiar with the scriptures on their own terms in order to know <a href="/wiki/Jesus_Christ" class="mw-redirect" title="Jesus Christ">Jesus Christ</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 1526, <a href="/wiki/William_Tyndale" title="William Tyndale">William Tyndale</a> published the first complete Bible in print. This facilitated distribution at a lower cost, and soon the Bible was not only readable to English citizens, but also affordable for most people.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Once the common people had access to the Bible, many more joined the Protestant Church. The revolutionary growth in biblical reading was a notable event of the Reformation, and England was one of the first countries where this occurred. Soon, England\'s foundational convictions were changing, and new Protestant <a href="/wiki/Doctrines" class="mw-redirect" title="Doctrines">doctrines</a> were emerging that challenged the Roman Catholic Church.\n</p><p>Leading reformers and <a href="/wiki/Philosopher" title="Philosopher">philosophers</a> of the time, such as Wycliffe, helped establish these doctrines by preaching to large groups of people. Wycliffe, among others, opposed the Catholic belief of <a href="/wiki/Transubstantiation" title="Transubstantiation">transubstantiation</a>. Catholics believe that when they participate in the <a href="/wiki/Eucharist" title="Eucharist">Eucharist</a>, the bread and wine transform into the literal body and blood of Jesus Christ when the priest prays over it. All Protestant leaders rejected this belief as false.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Many Protestant leaders also disapproved of Catholic <a href="/wiki/Monasticism" title="Monasticism">monasticism</a> because they believed it was unnecessary for <a href="/wiki/Salvation" title="Salvation">salvation</a> and harmful to those who practiced it. The practice of <a href="/wiki/Penance" title="Penance">penance</a> and the belief that <a href="/wiki/Good_works" title="Good works">good works</a> could balance the punishment of <a href="/wiki/Sin" title="Sin">sin</a> or lead to salvation were particularly common among the <a href="/wiki/Monks" class="mw-redirect" title="Monks">monks</a> living in monasteries. Protestants rejected this doctrine, believing that good works alone could not allow one to enter heaven. Rather, Protestants rely on the doctrines of <i><a href="/wiki/Sola_scriptura" title="Sola scriptura">sola scriptura</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Sola_fide" title="Sola fide">sola fide</a></i>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Protestant_influence_on_politics">Protestant influence on politics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Protestantism_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Protestant influence on politics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Monarchs">Monarchs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Protestantism_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Monarchs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>During the 16th and 17th centuries, nearly all the monarchs and resulting governments of <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>, and <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> were defined by either Catholicism or Protestantism.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Henry_VIII" class="mw-redirect" title="Henry VIII">Henry VIII</a> was the first monarch to introduce a new state religion to the English. In 1532, he wanted to divorce his wife, <a href="/wiki/Catherine_of_Aragon" title="Catherine of Aragon">Catherine of Aragon</a>. When <a href="/wiki/Pope_Clement_VII" title="Pope Clement VII">Pope Clement VII</a> refused to consent to the divorce, Henry VIII decided to separate the entire country of England from the Roman Catholic Church. The Pope had no more authority over the people of England. This parting of ways opened the door for Protestantism to enter the country.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Henry VIII established the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">Church of England</a> after his split with the Pope. However, England stayed much the same, even with the new state religion. Its doctrines and practices were, at first, very similar to those of the Catholic Church. The king did not establish the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">Church of England</a> as a result of religious differences with Catholicism; his motives were mainly political, and he persecuted radical Protestants who threatened his church.<sup id="cite_ref-Ferm_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ferm-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Henry VIII\'s successor, <a href="/wiki/Edward_VI" class="mw-redirect" title="Edward VI">Edward VI</a>, supported the Reformation, but his belief in Protestantism was not only political. He was more devout in his faith, and persecution of Protestant subjects ceased.<sup id="cite_ref-history_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Under the next monarch, however, Protestants were persecuted once again. <a href="/wiki/Mary_I_of_England" title="Mary I of England">Queen Mary I</a> was raised Catholic, and she saw it as her duty to purge the evil of Protestantism from her country.<sup id="cite_ref-Ferm_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ferm-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> During her reign, reformers of the church, such as <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Hawkes" title="Thomas Hawkes">Thomas Hawkes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hugh_Latimer" title="Hugh Latimer">Hugh Latimer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_Ridley_(martyr)" title="Nicholas Ridley (martyr)">Nicholas Ridley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Cranmer" title="Thomas Cranmer">Thomas Cranmer</a>, and <a href="/wiki/George_Wishart" title="George Wishart">George Wishart</a>, were executed for their faith. These executions did not destroy the Protestant movement. In fact, many joined the church when they saw how committed these <a href="/wiki/Martyrs" class="mw-redirect" title="Martyrs">martyrs</a> were to their religion.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The next monarch, <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_I" class="mw-redirect" title="Elizabeth I">Elizabeth I</a>, was a Protestant. Under her rule, the Protestant Church flourished. Protestants now filled many leadership positions in government. With this new power, however, came the persecution of many Catholics.<sup id="cite_ref-history_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Similarities between the Catholic and Protestant churches steadily decreased during this time.\n</p><p>The reign of <a href="/wiki/King_James_I" class="mw-redirect" title="King James I">King James I</a> established a definite victory for Protestantism in England. The <a href="/wiki/King_James_Bible" class="mw-redirect" title="King James Bible">King James Bible</a> introduced a new Protestant form of the Bible to church members throughout the country. This translation was in a language and dialect specific to the English people and to their Protestant religion. James I fulfilled the efforts of Protestant reformers who had been supporting the distribution of Bibles in common language for decades.<sup id="cite_ref-history_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_events">Political events</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Protestantism_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Political events">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/English_Civil_War" title="English Civil War">English Civil War</a> (1642\xe2\x80\x931651) was largely influenced by the Protestant Reformation. While England struggled between Catholicism and Protestantism, Scotland was experiencing a significant impact from the Reformation and its ideas. A strong <a href="/wiki/Presbyterian" class="mw-redirect" title="Presbyterian">Presbyterian</a> following had developed, but the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_Scotland" title="Church of Scotland">Church of Scotland</a> did not agree with <a href="/wiki/Charles_I_of_England" title="Charles I of England">King Charles I</a>\'s expectations of the Protestant religion. Charles I threatened to change the Church of Scotland by turning to <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>, which was a strong Catholic state.<sup id="cite_ref-history_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Oliver_Cromwell" title="Oliver Cromwell">Oliver Cromwell</a>, an Englishman born in <a href="/wiki/Huntingdon" title="Huntingdon">Huntingdon</a>, emerged victorious at the end of the Civil War. Once he gained control of England, Cromwell established a radical religious government. He organized the <a href="/wiki/Assembly_of_Saints" class="mw-redirect" title="Assembly of Saints">Assembly of Saints</a>, a firm and strict sect of Protestantism that was very similar to <a href="/wiki/Puritan" class="mw-redirect" title="Puritan">Puritanism</a>. The Assembly remained strong in England until the reign of <a href="/wiki/Charles_II_of_England" title="Charles II of England">Charles II</a>, who ended many of the strict practices of Puritanism.\n</p><p>When <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_England" title="Parliament of England">Parliament</a> passed the <a href="/wiki/Act_of_Toleration_1689" class="mw-redirect" title="Act of Toleration 1689">Act of Toleration of 1689</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dissenters" class="mw-redirect" title="Dissenters">dissenters</a> received freedom of worship within England. Catholics were not included in this act of Parliament, but members of other religions, most notably Protestantism, were officially protected from persecution based on their faith.<sup id="cite_ref-history_2-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Protestantism_in_other_countries_of_the_United_Kingdom">Protestantism in other countries of the United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Protestantism_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Protestantism in other countries of the United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Northern_Ireland">Northern Ireland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Protestantism_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Northern Ireland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Although <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a> is considered more Protestant than the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Republic of Ireland</a>, it has still retained more Catholicism than other nations in the United Kingdom. The Presbyterian Church in Ireland and the Church of Scotland have been closely tied in the past.<sup id="cite_ref-britannicaNI_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-britannicaNI-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Scotland">Scotland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Protestantism_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Scotland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> experienced a much deeper movement of Protestant reformation than any other nation in the UK. <a href="/wiki/John_Knox" title="John Knox">John Knox</a> is credited with introducing the Reformation to Scotland. Knox sparked the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Reformation" title="Scottish Reformation">Scottish Reformation</a> in 1560 when he began preaching about Protestantism to large groups of people throughout the country.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Later on, Scotland became involved in the English Civil War when Charles I threatened the country\'s Presbyterian Church.<sup id="cite_ref-history_2-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wales">Wales</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Protestantism_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Wales">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a> became a part of England when the <a href="/wiki/Tudor_dynasty" class="mw-redirect" title="Tudor dynasty">Tudor dynasty</a> conquered the nation.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The religious and political histories of Wales and England were closely tied during the reign of the Tudor monarchs, and the impact of the Reformation in both nations was similar. In 1588, <a href="/wiki/William_Morgan_(Bible_translator)" title="William Morgan (Bible translator)">William Morgan</a> published the <a href="/wiki/Welsh_Bible" class="mw-redirect" title="Welsh Bible">Welsh Bible</a>. <a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">Welsh</a> is the only non-state language in which the entire Bible was published during the Protestant Reformation.<sup id="cite_ref-reformation_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-reformation-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> For the most part, faithful Catholics made it more difficult for radical Protestantism to advance in the country. However, Protestants and non-conformists still compose the largest religious group in Wales.<sup id="cite_ref-reformation_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-reformation-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Protestantism_in_the_United_Kingdom_today">Protestantism in the United Kingdom today</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Protestantism_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Protestantism in the United Kingdom today">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Statistics show a steady decline in church membership and attendance in the United Kingdom. According to the <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a>, church attendance in the UK has dwindled in the past 50 years, not just in the Church of England or other Protestant churches, but in all religious establishments. The BBC reported in 2011 that 26% of people over the age of 65 attend church, as opposed to 11% of those between the ages of 16 and 44.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Britannica_Online" class="mw-redirect" title="Britannica Online">Britannica Online</a> says that the Church of England has more members than other churches, but there is greater dedication among members of non-conformist congregations.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Office_for_National_Statistics" title="Office for National Statistics">Office for National Statistics</a> confirmed in its 2001 census that 15% of people in England do not claim any religion.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Research in 2005 concluded that the number of citizens who belonged to a religion and attended services at any church had decreased by 41% in 41 years, while those who said they did not belong to any religion and did not attend services increased by 35% in the same amount of time.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> These numbers point to the increasing <a href="/wiki/Secularization" title="Secularization">secularization</a> of the country. According to the 31st British Social Attitudes Survey, the percentage of people identifying as Church of England/Anglican has fallen from 27% in 2003 to 16% in 2013, a drop of 59%. The number of people who say they have no religion has increased by more than 16%, from 43% to 50%, overtaking the proportion of people who claim a religious affiliation. The report also noted that Catholics accounted for 9% of the population and "other Christians" for 16%. <sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p><br />\nScotland has long been dominated by Presbyterianism. Today, the Church of Scotland is weakening as a state church, and church membership in the country is declining.<sup id="cite_ref-britannicaSCO_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-britannicaSCO-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> According to research in the city of <a href="/wiki/Dundee" title="Dundee">Dundee</a>, only 10% of church members attend services regularly.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Although the majority of citizens in Wales are members of Protestant and non-conformist churches, the culture has become increasingly <a href="/wiki/Secular" class="mw-redirect" title="Secular">secular</a>. Roman Catholics are a growing minority.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Northern Ireland is now one of the most diverse regions in the UK. Catholicism is still the largest single church in Northern Ireland, but Presbyterians total one-fifth of the population.
