href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Template:Electoral_systems&amp;action=edit"><abbr title="Edit this template">e</abbr></a></li></ul></div></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><b>Plurality-at-large voting</b>, also known as <b>block vote</b> or <b>multiple non-transferable vote</b> (<b>MNTV</b>),<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> is a non-<a href="/wiki/Proportional_representation" title="Proportional representation">proportional</a> <a href="/wiki/Voting_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Voting system">voting system</a> for electing several representatives from a single multimember <a href="/wiki/Electoral_district" title="Electoral district">electoral district</a> using a series of check boxes and tallying votes similar to a <a href="/wiki/Plurality_voting_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Plurality voting system">plurality election</a>. Multiple winners are elected simultaneously to serve the district. Block voting is not a system for obtaining <a href="/wiki/Proportional_representation" title="Proportional representation">proportional representation</a>; instead the usual result is that where the candidates divide into definitive parties (especially for example where those parties have party lines which are <a href="/wiki/Whip_(politics)" title="Whip (politics)">whipped</a>) the most popular party in the district sees its full <a href="/wiki/Slate_(elections)" title="Slate (elections)">slate</a> of candidates elected, resulting in a <a href="/wiki/Landslide_victory" title="Landslide victory">landslide</a>.\n</p><p>The term "plurality <a href="/wiki/At-large" title="At-large">at-large</a>" is in common usage in elections for representative members of a body who are <a href="/wiki/Elect" class="mw-redirect" title="Elect">elected</a> or appointed to represent the whole membership of the body (for example, a city, state or province, nation, club or association). Where the system is used in a territory divided into multi-member <a href="/wiki/Electoral_district" title="Electoral district">electoral districts</a> the system is commonly referred to as "block voting" or the "bloc vote".\n</p><p>This system is usually based on a single round of voting, but it can sometimes appear in a <a href="/wiki/Two-round_system" title="Two-round system">runoff (two-round) version</a>, as in some <a href="/wiki/Local_elections_in_France" class="mw-redirect" title="Local elections in France">local elections in France</a>, where candidates who do not receive an absolute majority must compete in a second round. In these cases, it is more accurately called "majority-at-large voting".\n</p><p>The term "bloc voting" sometimes means simple plurality election in multimember districts. In such a system, each party introduces a list of candidates and the party winning a plurality of votes wins all the seats. In contrast to such a system, the system described in this article can be called "unlimited voting" (contrary to "limited voting", in which a voter has fewer votes than the number of seats contested).\n</p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc"><input type="checkbox" role="button" id="toctogglecheckbox" class="toctogglecheckbox" style="display:none" /><div class="toctitle" lang="en" links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Casting_and_counting_ballots">Casting and counting ballots</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Plurality-at-large_voting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Casting and counting ballots">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In a block voting election, all candidates run against each other for <i>m</i> number of positions, where m is commonly called the district magnitude. Each voter selects up to <i>m</i> candidates on the ballot (voters are sometimes said to have <i>m</i> votes; however, they are unable to vote for the same candidate more than once as is permitted in <a href="/wiki/Cumulative_voting" title="Cumulative voting">cumulative voting</a><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>). Voters are most commonly permitted to cast their votes across more than one party list.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The <i>m</i> candidates with the most votes (who may or may not obtain a majority of available votes) are the winners and will fill the positions.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Example">Example</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Plurality-at-large_voting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Example">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The Dinertown City Council consists of three seats, and seven candidates are vying for these seats. There are 1,500 voters, and the voters each select a maximum of three candidates.\n</p>\n<table class="wikitable">\n<caption>Election results\n</caption>\n<tbody><tr>\n<th>Candidate</th>\n<th>Votes\n</th></tr>\n<tr>\n<td>Flo</td>\n<td>1,250\n</td></tr>\n<tr>\n<td>Mr. Big</td>\n<td>800\n</td></tr>\n<tr>\n<td>Bernie</td>\n<td>650\n</td></tr>\n<tr>\n<td>Simon</td>\n<td>600\n</td></tr>\n<tr>\n<td>Derek</td>\n<td>500\n</td></tr>\n<tr>\n<td>Rosie</td>\n<td>400\n</td></tr>\n<tr>\n<td>Cookie</td>\n<td>300\n</td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p>Since Flo, Mr. Big, and Bernie received the most votes, they will comprise the Dinertown City Council. Flo and Mr. Big each obtained a majority of the maximum 1,500 votes available per candidate; Bernie obtained only a plurality.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tactical_voting_and_strategic_nomination">Tactical voting and strategic nomination</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Plurality-at-large_voting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Tactical voting and strategic nomination">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Plurality block voting, like single-winner <a href="/wiki/Plurality_voting_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Plurality voting system">plurality voting</a>, is particularly vulnerable to <a href="/wiki/Tactical_voting" title="Tactical voting">tactical voting</a>. Supporters of relatively unpopular <a href="/wiki/Third_party_(politics)" title="Third party (politics)">third parties</a> have a substantial incentive to avoid <a href="/wiki/Wasted_vote" title="Wasted vote">wasted votes</a> by casting <i>all</i> of their votes for a slate of candidates from a major party.\n</p><p>Parties in block voting systems can also benefit from <a href="/wiki/Strategic_nomination" title="Strategic nomination">strategic nomination</a>. Coalitions are actively hurt when they have more candidates than there are seats to fill, as <a href="/wiki/Vote-splitting" class="mw-redirect" title="Vote-splitting">vote-splitting</a> will occur. Similarly, a coalition has a substantial incentive to nominate a full slate of candidates, as otherwise supporting voters may cast some of their remaining votes for opposing candidates.\n</p><p><a href="/wiki/Bullet_voting" title="Bullet voting">Bullet voting</a> is a strategy in which a voter only votes for a single candidate in an attempt to stop them being beaten by additional choices. Because the voter is essentially wasting a portion of their vote, bullet voting is only a good strategy when the voter has a strong preference for their favorite and is unsure of (and/or indifferent to) the other candidates\' relative chances of winning, for example, if the voter supports an independent candidate or a minor party which has only nominated one candidate.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects_of_block_voting">Effects of block voting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Plurality-at-large_voting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Effects of block voting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The block voting system has a number of features which can make it unrepresentative of the voters\' intentions. Block voting regularly produces complete <a href="/wiki/Landslide_victory" title="Landslide victory">landslide</a> majorities for the group of candidates with the highest level of support. Under block voting, a slate of <a href="/wiki/Clone_(voting)" class="mw-redirect" title="Clone (voting)">clones</a> of the top-place candidate may win every available seat. A voter does have the option to select candidates of different political stripes if they wish, though.\n</p><p>Additionally, like <a href="/wiki/First_past_the_post" class="mw-redirect" title="First past the post">first past the post</a> methods, small cohesive groups of voters can overpower larger numbers of disorganised voters who do not engage in <a href="/wiki/Tactical_voting" title="Tactical voting">tactical voting</a>, sometimes resulting in a small minority of voters electing an entire slate of candidates by merely constituting a <a href="/wiki/Plurality_(voting)" title="Plurality (voting)">plurality</a>. \n</p><p>Some uses of this system have fostered the creation of an <a href="/wiki/Electoral_alliance" title="Electoral alliance">electoral alliance</a> between political parties or groups as opposed to a <a href="/wiki/Coalition" title="Coalition">coalition</a>. This has been the case in the <a href="/wiki/National_Assembly_of_Mauritius" class="mw-redirect" title="National Assembly of Mauritius">National Assembly of Mauritius</a>, the <a href="/wiki/New_Hampshire_House_of_Representatives" title="New Hampshire House of Representatives">New Hampshire House of Representatives</a> with the election of multiple <a href="/wiki/Free_State_Project" title="Free State Project">Free State Project</a> as well as <a href="/wiki/New_Hampshire_Liberty_Alliance" title="New Hampshire Liberty Alliance">New Hampshire Liberty Alliance</a> members and in the <a href="/wiki/Vermont_Senate" title="Vermont Senate">Vermont Senate</a> with the elections of <a href="/wiki/Vermont_Progressive_Party" title="Vermont Progressive Party">Vermont Progressive Party</a> members <a href="/wiki/Tim_Ashe" title="Tim Ashe">Tim Ashe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Pollina" title="Anthony Pollina">Anthony Pollina</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Historically, similar situations arose within the <a href="/wiki/Multi-member_constituencies_in_the_Parliament_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Multi-member constituencies in the Parliament of the United Kingdom">multi-member constituencies in the Parliament of the United Kingdom</a>.\n</p><p>While many criticize block voting\'s tendency to create landslide victories, some cite it as a strength. Since the winners of a block voting election generally represent the same slate or group of voters, there is greater agreement amongst those elected, potentially leading to a reduction in political <a href="/wiki/Gridlock_(politics)" title="Gridlock (politics)">gridlock</a>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Variations_of_block_voting">Variations of block voting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Plurality-at-large_voting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Variations of block voting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Limited_voting" title="Limited voting">Limited voting</a> and <a href="/wiki/Preferential_block_voting" title="Preferential block voting">Preferential block voting</a></div>\n<p><b>Partial block voting</b>, also called <b>limited voting</b>, functions similarly to plurality-at-large voting, however in partial block voting each voter receives fewer votes than the number of candidates to be elected. This in turn can enable reasonably sized minorities to achieve some representation, as it becomes impossible for a simple plurality to sweep every seat. Partial bloc voting is used for <a href="/wiki/Elections_in_Gibraltar" title="Elections in Gibraltar">elections</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Gibraltar_Parliament" title="Gibraltar Parliament">Gibraltar Parliament</a>, where each voter has 10 votes and 17 seats are open for election; the usual result is that the most popular party wins 10 seats and forms the ruling administration, while the second most popular wins seven seats and forms the opposition. Partial block voting is also used in the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Senate" class="mw-redirect" title="Spanish Senate">Spanish Senate</a>, where there are four seats per constituency and each voter receives three votes. Historically, partial block voting was used in three- and four-member constituencies in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, where voters received two votes, until multimember constituencies were abolished.\n</p><p>Under partial block voting, the fewer votes each voter is granted the smaller the number of voters needed to win becomes and the more like <a href="/wiki/Proportional_representation" title="Proportional representation">proportional representation</a> the results can be, provided that voters and candidates use proper strategy.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>  At the extreme, if each voter receives only one vote, then the voting system becomes equivalent to the <a href="/wiki/Single_non-transferable_vote" title="Single non-transferable vote">single  non-transferable vote</a> and the minimum proportion needed is the <a href="/wiki/Droop_quota" title="Droop quota">Droop quota</a>.\n</p><p>Block voting, or <i>plurality block voting</i>, is often compared with <b>preferential block voting</b> as both systems tend to produce landslide victories for similar candidates. Instead of a series of checkboxes, preferential block voting uses a <a href="/wiki/Preferential_ballot" class="mw-redirect" title="Preferential ballot">preferential ballot</a>. A slate of clones of the top preferred candidate will win every seat under both systems, however in preferential block voting this is instead the <a href="/wiki/Instant-runoff_voting" title="Instant-runoff voting">instant-runoff</a> winner.\n</p><p>The <b>party block voting</b>, or <b>general ticket</b>, is the party-list version of the bloc vote. If in the classic BV the candidates formally stand as <a href="/wiki/Non-partisan" class="mw-redirect" title="Non-partisan">non-partisan</a> and some minority nominations can be theoretically successful, in the PVB each candidate are linked to their party-list, which is voted by the electors producing a <a href="/wiki/Landslide" title="Landslide">landslide</a>, and any minority representation is excluded. So, the full at-large PBV is considered completely anti-democratic, and it is used only to elect portions of assembly.\n</p><p>The <b>majority-at-large voting</b> is the plurality-at-large voting, but candidates who do not receive an absolute majority must compete in a second round.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Usage_of_block_voting">Usage of block voting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Plurality-at-large_voting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Usage of block voting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>These countries use the block vote:<sup id="cite_ref-BV_Idea_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BV_Idea-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag failed verification of its source citation(s). (June 2016)">not in citation given</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>, for <a href="/wiki/Federal_Senate" title="Federal Senate">Federal Senate</a> elections</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, in many local government elections, and in Senate recommendation elections in Alberta</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Ecuador" title="Ecuador">Ecuador</a> in 1998</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Laos" title="Laos">Laos</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Lebanon" title="Lebanon">Lebanon</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Mauritius" title="Mauritius">Mauritius</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">Mongolia</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>, for <a href="/wiki/Senate_of_the_Philippines" title="Senate of the Philippines">Senate</a> elections</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>, in some local elections, e.g. in <a href="/wiki/Administrative_divisions_of_Moscow" title="Administrative divisions of Moscow">Moscow district councils</a> elections</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a> in <a href="/wiki/Group_representation_constituencies" class="mw-redirect" title="Group representation constituencies">group representation constituencies</a> (GRCs)</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Syria" title="Syria">Syria</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Tonga" title="Tonga">Tonga</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Tuvalu" title="Tuvalu">Tuvalu</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, in some local elections.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, in some state and local elections.</li></ul>\n<p>In <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, the election of municipal councilors takes place by majority vote<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> plurinominal, in two rounds with <a href="/wiki/Panachage" title="Panachage">panachage</a>:\n</p>\n<ul><li>In the first round, candidates are elected if they receive an absolute majority of votes cast and the vote of a <a href="/wiki/Quorum" title="Quorum">quorum</a> of at least a quarter of registered voters;</li>\n<li>In the second round, a simple majority suffices. If multiple candidates receive the same number of votes, the election is won by the older of the candidates when no one can be elected based on the number of seats<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>\n<p>Block voting was used in the <a href="/wiki/Australian_Senate" title="Australian Senate">Australian Senate</a> from 1901 to 1948 (from 1918, this was preferential block voting). It was used for multi-member constituencies in <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Parliament of the United Kingdom">parliamentary</a> elections in the United Kingdom until their abolition, and remains in use throughout <a href="/wiki/England_and_Wales" title="England and Wales">England and Wales</a> for some <a href="/wiki/Local_government_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Local government in the United Kingdom">local elections</a>. It is also used in <a href="/wiki/Elections_in_Jersey" title="Elections in Jersey">Jersey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Elections_in_Guernsey" title="Elections in Guernsey">Guernsey</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Elections_in_the_Isle_of_Man" title="Elections in the Isle of Man">Isle of Man</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Elections_in_the_Cayman_Islands" title="Elections in the Cayman Islands">Cayman Islands</a> (until 2013, FPTP since 2017), the <a href="/wiki/Elections_in_the_Falkland_Islands" class="mw-redirect" title="Elections in the Falkland Islands">Falkland Islands</a> and <a href="/wiki/Elections_in_Saint_Helena" class="mw-redirect" title="Elections in Saint Helena">Saint Helena</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-BV_Idea_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BV_Idea-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Plurality block voting is or was also used in the election of the <a href="/wiki/Senate_of_Poland" title="Senate of Poland">Senate of Poland</a> (until 2011), of the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Lebanon" title="Parliament of Lebanon">Parliament of Lebanon</a>, the plurality seats in the <a href="/wiki/Palestinian_Legislative_Council" title="Palestinian Legislative Council">Palestinian Legislative Council</a> and for the <a href="/wiki/National_Assembly_of_Mauritius" class="mw-redirect" title="National Assembly of Mauritius">National Assembly of Mauritius</a>. In some Lebanese and Palestinian constituencies, there is only one seat to be filled; in the <a href="/wiki/Palestinian_legislative_and_presidential_election,_1996" class="mw-redirect" title="Palestinian legislative and presidential election, 1996">Palestinian election of 1996</a> there were only plurality seats, but in <a href="/wiki/Palestinian_legislative_election,_2006" title="Palestinian legislative election, 2006">2006</a> half the seats were elected by plurality, half by proportional representation nationwide.\n</p><p>A form of plurality block voting was used for the elections of both houses of Parliament in <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a> before proportional representation was implemented in 1900. The system, however, was combined with a system similar to a <a href="/wiki/Runoff_election" class="mw-redirect" title="Runoff election">runoff election</a>; when not enough candidates had the majority of the votes in the first round, a second round was held between the highest ranked candidates of the first round (with two times as many candidates as seats to be filled). In some constituencies there was only one seat to be filled. A similar system to elect part of the <a href="/wiki/State_Great_Khural" title="State Great Khural">Mongolian parliament</a>. 48 Representatives are elected from districts with 1-3 members, the representatives are required to achieve at least 28% of the vote in a district to be elected, if there are unfilled seats after the first round of voting, a second round similar to the Belgian system is held to fill the remaining seat. The remaining representatives are elected separately using party list proportional representation on the national level. <sup id="cite_ref-mongolia_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mongolia-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia" title="British Columbia">British Columbia</a>, Canada, all local governments are elected using bloc voting for city councils and for other multi-member bodies (there called "at-large" voting). In other Canadian provinces, smaller cities are generally elected under plurality-at-large, while larger cities are generally elected under the "ward system" which is a municipal adaptation of single member plurality. The sole exception is London, Ontario which has recently changed to the <a href="/wiki/Alternative_Vote" class="mw-redirect" title="Alternative Vote">Alternative Vote</a>. When Toronto was amalgamated in 1997, the new entity\'s <a href="/wiki/Toronto_municipal_election,_1997" title="Toronto municipal election, 1997">first election</a> used a similar rule. From 1871 to 1988, British Columbia had some multi-member ridings using plurality-at-large, and others elected under <a href="/wiki/Single_member_plurality" class="mw-redirect" title="Single member plurality">single member plurality</a>, with the number of each varying from one election to the next. Other Canadian provincial legislatures have in the past used plurality-at-large or <a href="/wiki/Single_transferable_vote" title="Single transferable vote">single transferable vote</a>, but now all members of provincial legislatures are exclusively elected under single member plurality.\n</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a>, block voting is used for a tiny proportion of the territory\'s population to elect the members of the <a href="/wiki/Election_Committee" title="Election Committee">Election Committee</a>, which is responsible for selecting the territory\'s Chief Executive.\n</p><p>Block voting was used in some constituencies for the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Representatives_of_Japan" class="mw-redirect" title="House of Representatives of Japan">House of Representatives of Japan</a> in the first six general elections between 1890 and 1898: while the majority of seats was elected by plurality in 214 single-member districts, there were 43 two-member districts that elected their representatives by block voting.\n</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a> is the country with the most extensive experience in plurality-at-large voting. Positions where there are multiple winners usually use plurality-at-large voting, the exception is the election for sectoral representatives in the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Representatives_of_the_Philippines" title="House of Representatives of the Philippines">House of Representatives</a>. The members of the <a href="/wiki/Senate_of_the_Philippines" title="Senate of the Philippines">Senate</a> and all local legislatures are elected via this method. The members of the <a href="/wiki/Interim_Batasang_Pambansa" title="Interim Batasang Pambansa">Interim Batasang Pambansa</a> (the parliament) were also elected under this method in <a href="/wiki/Philippine_parliamentary_election,_1978" title="Philippine parliamentary election, 1978">1978</a>.\n</p><p>Block voting is often used in <a href="/wiki/Corporate_governance" title="Corporate governance">corporate elections</a> to elect the boards of directors of corporations including <a href="/wiki/Housing_cooperative" title="Housing cooperative">housing cooperatives</a>, with each shareholder\'s vote being multiplied by the number of shares they own; however, <a href="/wiki/Cumulative_voting" title="Cumulative voting">cumulative voting</a> is also popular.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
