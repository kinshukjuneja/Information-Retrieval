Like <a href="/wiki/Canadian_English" title="Canadian English">Canadian English</a> (also part of the broad <a href="/wiki/North_American_English" title="North American English">North American English</a> classification), it resembles parts of both <a href="/wiki/British_English" title="British English">British English</a> and <a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">American English</a> dialects, but uniquely has many aspects of <a href="/wiki/Hiberno-English" title="Hiberno-English">Irish</a> intonation. Typically, it uses the same spellings as found in <a href="/wiki/British_English" title="British English">British English</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Although the distinction between the two is best described as a continuum rather than a solid line,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> it is not to be confused with <a href="/wiki/Jamaican_Patois" title="Jamaican Patois">Jamaican Patois</a>, also known as Jamaican or Jamaican class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jamaican_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Sociolinguistics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Jamaican Standard English is a variety of International Standard English (see <a href="/wiki/English_English" class="mw-redirect" title="English English">English English</a>). Since the mid-20th century, Jamaica has increasingly developed stronger social and economic ties with the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> and the increasing popularity of U.S. cultural offerings, including <a href="/wiki/Film" title="Film">film</a>, <a href="/wiki/Music" title="Music">music</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Televised" class="mw-redirect" title="Televised">televised</a> dramas and comedies, exposure to <a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">American English</a> has been increasing steadily.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Grammar">Grammar</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jamaican_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Grammar">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Although Jamaica is closer to the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, Jamaica was colonized by the British until 1962. Therefore, Jamaicans follow the British grammar, and British English is taught in school.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Vocabulary">Vocabulary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jamaican_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Vocabulary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Recent American influence is apparent in the <a href="/wiki/Lexicon" title="Lexicon">lexicon</a> (babies sleep in "cribs" and wear "diapers" or "pampers"; some people live in "apartments" or "townhouses", for example). Generally, older vocabulary tends to be British (babies wear "nappies", not "diapers"; cars have "bonnets" and "windscreens"; children study "maths", use "rubbers" to erase their mistakes and wish they were on "holiday"), while newer phenomena are typically "imported" together with their American names.\n</p><p>The American term "trunk" is almost universally used instead of the British term "boot" on cars, while the engine covering is always referred to by the British term "bonnet" (as elsewhere throughout the English-official Caribbean). This is probably because the American term, "hood", is used in Jamaica as a vulgar slang for penis (but not elsewhere in the Caribbean).<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Jamaican Standard English uses many words also used in <a href="/wiki/Jamaican_Patois" title="Jamaican Patois">Jamaican Patois</a>, such as "<a href="/wiki/Duppy" title="Duppy">duppy</a>" for "<a href="/wiki/Ghost" title="Ghost">ghost</a>"; "vendah" for "informal vendor/<a href="/wiki/Hawker_(trade)" title="Hawker (trade)">hawker</a>"; and some terms for Jamaican foods, like "<a href="/wiki/Ackee" title="Ackee">ackee</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Callaloo" title="Callaloo">callaloo</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Guinep" class="mw-redirect" title="Guinep">guinep</a>", and "<a href="/wiki/Bammy" title="Bammy">bammy</a>".\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pronunciation">Pronunciation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jamaican_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Pronunciation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Jamaican Standard English pronunciation, while it differs greatly from <a href="/wiki/Jamaican_Patois" title="Jamaican Patois">Jamaican Patois</a> pronunciation, is nevertheless recognizably <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a>. Features include the characteristic pronunciation of the <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/a\xca\x8a/</span> <a href="/wiki/Diphthong" title="Diphthong">diphthong</a> in words like <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-size: smaller;">MOUTH</span>, which is often more closed and rounded <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[\xc9\xb5\xca\x8a]</span> than in <a href="/wiki/Received_Pronunciation" title="Received Pronunciation">Received Pronunciation</a> or <a href="/wiki/General_American" title="General American">General American</a>; the pronunciation of the <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-size: smaller;">STRUT</span> vowel <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/\xca\x8c/</span> to <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[\xc9\xb5~o]</span> (again, more closed and rounded than the British <a href="/wiki/Received_Pronunciation" title="Received Pronunciation">Received Pronunciation</a> or <a href="/wiki/General_American" title="General American">General American</a> varieties); and the very unique feature of "variable semi-<a href="/wiki/Rhoticity_in_English" title="Rhoticity in English">rhoticity</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Jamaican English\'s rhoticity (the pronunciation of "r" everywhere except before vowels) is variable and can depend upon the phonemic context.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Typically, Jamaican English accents fully preserve the "-r" in words of the <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-size: smaller;">NEAR</span> and <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-size: smaller;">FORCE</span> sets, drop the "-r" in words like <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-size: smaller;">LETTER</span> (at the ends of unstressed <a href="/wiki/Syllables" class="mw-redirect" title="Syllables">syllables</a>), show middling degrees of rhoticity in the <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-size: smaller;">SQUARE</span> and <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-size: smaller;">CURE</span> sets, and smaller degrees with all other sets of words.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> When "r" is followed by a consonant, non-rhoticity is nearly four times more likely than when "r" is at the end of a word.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> However, across the board, more rhoticity is positively correlated with higher levels of education.\n</p><p>Merger of the diphthongs in <a href="/wiki/Near-square_merger" class="mw-redirect" title="Near-square merger">"fair" and "fear"</a> takes place both in Jamaican Standard English and <a href="/wiki/Jamaican_Patois" title="Jamaican Patois">Jamaican Patois</a>, resulting in those two words (and many others, like "bear" and "beer") often becoming <a href="/wiki/Homophones" class="mw-redirect" title="Homophones">homophones</a>: the sound being <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[e\xcb\x90\xc9\xb9]</span>, though often <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[i\xc9\x9b\xc9\xb9]</span> (something like "ee-air"; thus "bear/beer" as "bee-air").<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The short "a" sound (<span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-size: smaller;">TRAP</span>, man, hat, etc.) is very open <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[a]</span>, similar to its Irish versions, while <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-size: smaller;">BATH</span>, <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-size: smaller;">PALM</span>, and <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-size: smaller;">START</span> all use this same sound too, but lengthened,<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> and perhaps slightly backed;<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> this distinction can maintain a London-like <a href="/wiki/Trap-bath_split" title="Trap-bath split"><span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-size: smaller;">TRAP&#8211;BATH</span> split</a>. Both <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-size: smaller;">LOT/CLOTH</span> and <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-size: smaller;">THOUGHT</span> use a rounded <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[\xc9\x94]</span>, though a <a href="/wiki/Cot-caught_merger" class="mw-redirect" title="Cot-caught merger">cot-caught merger</a> is theoretically avoided by the latter set of words being more lengthened;<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> however, in reality, a full merger (of <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-size: smaller;">LOT/CLOTH/THOUGHT</span>) is reportedly increasing in informal contexts.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> For Jamaican Patois speakers, the merged vowel is much lower. <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-size: smaller;">GOAT</span> and <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-size: smaller;">FACE</span> vowels in the standard educated dialect are long <a href="/wiki/Monophthong" title="Monophthong">monophthongs</a>: respectively <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[o\xcb\x90]</span> and <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[e\xcb\x90]</span>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The unstressed <a href="/wiki/Schwa" title="Schwa">schwa</a> phoneme (<span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-size: smaller;">COMMA</span>) appears to be normally produced in the area of <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[a~\xc9\x90]</span>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>Presumably less-educated <a href="/wiki/Jamaican_Patois" title="Jamaican Patois">Jamaican Patois</a> speakers may speak English with several other notable features, including a <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-size: smaller;">TRAP&#8211;LOT</span> merger (e.g. with <i>rat</i> and <i>rot</i> homophones) to <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[\xc9\x94]</span> and a <a href="/wiki/Line-loin_merger" class="mw-redirect" title="Line-loin merger"><span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-size: smaller;">PRIZE&#8211;CHOICE</span> merger</a> (e.g. with <i>line</i> and <i>loin</i> homophones) to <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[\xc9\x94i]</span>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Language_use:_Jamaican_Standard_English_versus_Patois">Language use: Jamaican Standard English versus Patois</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jamaican_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Language use: Jamaican Standard English versus Patois">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Jamaican Standard English and <a href="/wiki/Jamaican_Patois" title="Jamaican Patois">Jamaican Patois</a> exist together in a <a href="/wiki/Post-creole_speech_continuum" class="mw-redirect" title="Post-creole speech continuum">post-creole speech continuum</a>. Jamaican (Creole/Patois) is used by most people for everyday, informal situations - it is the language most Jamaicans use at home and are most familiar with, as well as the language of most local popular music. Standard English, on the other hand, is the language of education, <a href="/wiki/High_culture" title="High culture">high culture</a>, government, the <a href="/wiki/Mass_media" title="Mass media">media</a> and official/formal communications. It is also the native language of a small minority of Jamaicans (typically upper-class and upper/traditional middle-class). Most Creole-dominant speakers have a fair command of English and Standard English, through schooling and exposure to official culture and mass media; their receptive skills (understanding of Standard English) are typically much better than their productive skills (their own intended Standard English statements often show signs of Jamaican Creole interference).\n</p><p>Most writing in Jamaica is done in English (including private notes and correspondence). Jamaican Patois has a standardized orthography,<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> and has only recently been taught in some schools. As a result, the majority of Jamaicans can read and write Standard English only, and have trouble deciphering written Patois (in which the writer tries to reflect characteristic structures and pronunciations to differing degrees, without compromising readability). Written Patois appears mostly in <a href="/wiki/Literature" title="Literature">literature</a>, especially in folkloristic "dialect poems"; in humoristic newspaper columns; and most recently, on <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">internet</a> chat sites frequented by younger Jamaicans, who seem to have a more positive attitude toward their own language use than their parents.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>While, for the sake of simplicity, it is customary<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (October 2011)">by whom?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> to describe Jamaican speech in terms of Standard English versus Jamaican Creole, a clear-cut <a href="/wiki/Dichotomy" title="Dichotomy">dichotomy</a> does not adequately describe the actual language use of most Jamaicans.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Between the two extremes\xe2\x80\x94"broad Patois" on one end of the spectrum, and "perfect" Standard English on the other\xe2\x80\x94there are various in-between varieties. This situation typically results when a Creole language is in constant contact with Standardised English (superstrate or lexifier language) and is called a <a href="/wiki/Post-creole_speech_continuum" class="mw-redirect" title="Post-creole speech continuum">creole speech continuum</a>. The least prestigious (most Creole) variety is called the <a href="/wiki/Basilect" class="mw-redirect" title="Basilect">basilect</a>; Standard English (or high prestige) variety, the <a href="/wiki/Acrolect" class="mw-redirect" title="Acrolect">acrolect</a>; and in-between versions are known as <a href="/wiki/Mesolect" class="mw-redirect" title="Mesolect">mesolects</a>.\n</p><p>Consider, for example, the following forms:\n</p>\n<ul><li>"im/(h)ihn de/da/a wok \xc3\xbaoba d\xc3\xa9s\xc3\xb2" (basilect)</li>\n<li>"im workin ova deso" (low mesolect)</li>\n<li>"(H)e (h)is workin\' over dere" (high mesolect)</li>\n<li>"He is working over there." (acrolect)</li></ul>\n<p>(As noted above, the "r" in "over" is not pronounced in any variety, but the one in "dere" or "there" is.)\n</p><p>Jamaicans choose from the varieties available to them according to the situation. A Creole-dominant speaker will choose a higher variety for formal occasions like official business or a wedding speech, and a lower one for relating to friends; a Standard English-dominant speaker is likely to employ a lower variety when shopping at the market than at their workplace. <a href="/wiki/Code-switching" title="Code-switching">Code-switching</a> can also be <a href="/wiki/Metacommunication" class="mw-redirect" title="Metacommunication">metacommunicative</a> (as when a Standard-dominant speaker switches to a more heavily basilect-influenced variety in an attempt at humor or to express solidarity).\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jamaican_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: See also">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Regional_accents_of_English_speakers" class="mw-redirect" title="Regional accents of English speakers">Regional accents of English speakers</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Nation_language" title="Nation language">Nation language</a></li></ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jamaican_English&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist" style="list-style-type: decimal;">\n<div class="mw-references-wrap mw-references-columns"><ol class="references">\n<li id="cite_note-1"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-1">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Andrea Sand (1999), <i>Linguistic Variation in Jamaica.
