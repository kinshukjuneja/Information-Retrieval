35&#160;km/s)<sup id="cite_ref-user_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-user-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup></td></tr></tbody></table>\n<p><b>SABRE</b> (<b>Synergistic Air-Breathing Rocket Engine</b><sup id="cite_ref-names_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-names-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>) is a concept under development by <a href="/wiki/Reaction_Engines_Limited" title="Reaction Engines Limited">Reaction Engines Limited</a> for a <a href="/wiki/Hypersonic" class="mw-redirect" title="Hypersonic">hypersonic</a> <a href="/wiki/Precooled_jet_engine" title="Precooled jet engine">precooled</a> hybrid <a href="/wiki/Engine#Heat_engine" title="Engine">air-breathing</a> <a href="/wiki/Rocket_engine" title="Rocket engine">rocket engine</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-compare_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-compare-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The engine is being designed to achieve <a href="/wiki/Single-stage-to-orbit" title="Single-stage-to-orbit">single-stage-to-orbit</a> capability, propelling the proposed <a href="/wiki/Skylon_(spacecraft)" title="Skylon (spacecraft)">Skylon</a> <a href="/wiki/Spaceplane" title="Spaceplane">spaceplane</a> to low Earth orbit. SABRE is an evolution of <a href="/wiki/Alan_Bond_(engineer)" title="Alan Bond (engineer)">Alan Bond</a>\'s series of <a href="/wiki/Liquid_air_cycle_engine" title="Liquid air cycle engine">liquid air cycle engine</a> (LACE) and LACE-like designs that started in the early/mid-1980s for the <a href="/wiki/HOTOL" title="HOTOL">HOTOL</a> project.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The design comprises a single <a href="/wiki/Combined_cycle" title="Combined cycle">combined cycle</a> rocket engine with two modes of operation.<sup id="cite_ref-user_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-user-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The air-breathing mode combines a turbo-<a href="/wiki/Gas_compressor" class="mw-redirect" title="Gas compressor">compressor</a> with a lightweight air <a href="/wiki/Precooled_jet_engine" title="Precooled jet engine">precooler</a> positioned just behind the <a href="/wiki/Inlet_cone" title="Inlet cone">inlet cone</a>. At high speeds this precooler cools the hot, ram-compressed air leading to a very high <a href="/wiki/Overall_pressure_ratio" title="Overall pressure ratio">pressure ratio</a> within the engine. The compressed air is subsequently fed into the rocket <a href="/wiki/Combustion_chamber" title="Combustion chamber">combustion chamber</a> where it is ignited along with stored <a href="/wiki/Liquid_hydrogen" title="Liquid hydrogen">liquid hydrogen</a>. The high pressure ratio allows the engine to provide high thrust at very high speeds and altitudes. The low temperature of the air permits light alloy construction to be employed and allow a very lightweight engine\xe2\x80\x94essential for reaching orbit. In addition, unlike the LACE concept, SABRE\'s precooler does not <a href="/wiki/Liquid_air" title="Liquid air">liquefy the air</a>, letting it run more efficiently.<sup id="cite_ref-precooled_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-precooled-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>After shutting the inlet cone off at <a href="/wiki/Mach_number" title="Mach number">Mach</a>&#160;5.14, and at a altitude of 28.5&#160;km,<sup id="cite_ref-user_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-user-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> the system continues as a <a href="/wiki/Staged_combustion_cycle_(rocket)" class="mw-redirect" title="Staged combustion cycle (rocket)">closed-cycle</a> high-performance rocket engine burning <a href="/wiki/Liquid_oxygen" title="Liquid oxygen">liquid oxygen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Liquid_hydrogen" title="Liquid hydrogen">liquid hydrogen</a> from on-board fuel tanks, potentially allowing a hybrid <a href="/wiki/Spaceplane" title="Spaceplane">spaceplane</a> concept like <a href="/wiki/Skylon_(spacecraft)" title="Skylon (spacecraft)">Skylon</a> to reach orbital velocity after leaving the atmosphere on a steep climb.\n</p><p>An engine derived from the SABRE concept called <a href="/wiki/Reaction_Engines_Scimitar" title="Reaction Engines Scimitar">Scimitar</a> has been designed for the company\'s <a href="/wiki/Reaction_Engines_A2" title="Reaction Engines A2">A2</a> <a href="/wiki/Supersonic_transport" title="Supersonic transport">hypersonic passenger jet</a> proposal for the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>-funded <a href="/wiki/LAPCAT" title="LAPCAT">LAPCAT</a> study.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<div class="toclimit-3"><div id="toc" class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=SABRE_(rocket_engine)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The precooler concept evolved from an idea originated by Robert P. Carmichael in 1955.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> This was followed by the <a href="/wiki/Liquid_air_cycle_engine" title="Liquid air cycle engine">liquid air cycle engine</a> (LACE) idea which was originally explored by <a href="/wiki/General_Dynamics" title="General Dynamics">General Dynamics</a> in the 1960s as part of the <a href="/wiki/US_Air_Force" class="mw-redirect" title="US Air Force">US Air Force</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Aerospaceplane" title="Aerospaceplane">aerospaceplane</a> efforts.<sup id="cite_ref-precooled_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-precooled-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The LACE system was to be placed behind a supersonic air intake which would compress the air through ram compression, then a <a href="/wiki/Heat_exchanger" title="Heat exchanger">heat exchanger</a> would rapidly cool it using some of the <a href="/wiki/Liquid_hydrogen" title="Liquid hydrogen">liquid hydrogen</a> fuel stored on board. The resulting liquid air was then processed to separate the <a href="/wiki/Liquid_oxygen" title="Liquid oxygen">liquid oxygen</a> for combustion. The amount of warmed hydrogen was too great to burn with the oxygen, so most was to be expelled, giving useful thrust, but greatly reducing the potential efficiency.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>Instead, as part of the <a href="/wiki/HOTOL" title="HOTOL">HOTOL</a> project, the liquid air cycle engine (LACE) based idea <a href="/wiki/RB545" class="mw-redirect" title="RB545">RB545</a> engine was developed with more efficient cycle. The engine was given the Rolls Royce name "Swallow".<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>  In 1989, after funding for HOTOL ceased, Bond and several others formed <a href="/wiki/Reaction_Engines_Limited" title="Reaction Engines Limited">Reaction Engines Limited</a> to continue research. The RB545\'s precooler had issues with <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_embrittlement" title="Hydrogen embrittlement">embrittlement</a>, excess liquid hydrogen consumption, patents and the <a href="/wiki/Official_Secrets_Act" title="Official Secrets Act">Official Secrets Act</a>, so Bond developed SABRE instead.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 2016 the project received \xc2\xa360m in funds from the UK government and ESA for a demonstrator involving the full cycle.<sup id="cite_ref-bbc_12July2016_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbc_12July2016-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Concept">Concept</span><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sabre_cycle_m.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" engine</div></div></div>\n<p>Like the <a href="/wiki/RB545" class="mw-redirect" title="RB545">RB545</a>, the SABRE design is neither a conventional <a href="/wiki/Rocket_engine" title="Rocket engine">rocket engine</a> nor <a href="/wiki/Turbojet" title="Turbojet">jet engine</a>, but a hybrid that uses air from the environment at low speeds/altitudes, and stored <a href="/wiki/Liquid_oxygen" title="Liquid oxygen">liquid oxygen</a> (LOX) at higher altitude. The SABRE engine "relies on a heat exchanger capable of cooling incoming air to \xe2\x88\x92150&#160;\xc2\xb0C (\xe2\x88\x92238&#160;\xc2\xb0F), to provide oxygen for mixing with hydrogen and provide jet thrust during atmospheric flight before switching to tanked liquid oxygen when in space." \n</p><p>At the front of the engine, a simple translating axisymmetric <a href="/wiki/Inlet_cone" title="Inlet cone">shock cone inlet</a> slows the air to subsonic speeds using two shock reflections. Part of the air then passes through a precooler into the central core where it is compressed by a turbo-<a href="/wiki/Gas_compressor" class="mw-redirect" title="Gas compressor">compressor</a> which runs off the same gaseous <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a> loop <a href="/wiki/Brayton_cycle" title="Brayton cycle">Brayton cycle</a> and then fed it into four high pressure combined cycle rocket engine combustion chambers. The remainder of the intake air continues into a bypass system. As the amount of hydrogen required to cool the incoming air is more than can be burnt in the core engine, the excess is burnt in a ring of <a href="/wiki/Flame_holder" title="Flame holder">flame holders</a> in the bypass system, acting as a <a href="/wiki/Ramjet" title="Ramjet">ramjet</a>. \n</p><p>In 2011, hardware testing of the heat exchanger technology "crucial to [the] hybrid air- and liquid oxygen-breathing [SABRE] rocket motor" was completed, demonstrating that the technology is viable.<sup id="cite_ref-precooltest_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-precooltest-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-fg20110901_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fg20110901-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The tests validated that the heat exchanger could perform as needed for the engine to obtain adequate oxygen from the atmosphere to support the low-altitude, high-performance operation.<sup id="cite_ref-precooltest_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-precooltest-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-fg20110901_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fg20110901-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=SABRE_(rocket_engine)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Tests were carried out in 2008 Airbourne Engineering at <a href="/wiki/Rocket_Propulsion_Establishment" title="Rocket Propulsion Establishment">Westcott</a> on an <a href="/wiki/Expansion_deflection_nozzle" title="Expansion deflection nozzle">expansion deflection nozzle</a> called STERN to provide the data needed to develop an accurate engineering model to overcome the problem of non-dynamic exhaust expansion. This research continuied with the STRICT nozzle in 2011.\n</p><p>Successful tests of an oxidiser (both air and oxygen) cooled combustion chamber were conducted by EADS-Astrium at <a href="/wiki/Lampoldshausen" title="Lampoldshausen">Institute of Space Propulsion</a> in 2010\n</p><p>In November 2012, Reaction Engines announced it had successfully concluded a series of tests that prove the cooling technology of the engine, one of the main obstacles towards the completion of the project. The <a href="/wiki/European_Space_Agency" title="European Space Agency">European Space Agency</a> (ESA) evaluated the SABRE engine\'s precooler heat exchanger, and accepted claims that the technologies required to proceed with the engine\'s development had been fully demonstrated.<sup id="cite_ref-precooltest_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-precooltest-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-avEsa_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-avEsa-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-esar_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-esar-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In June 2013 the United Kingdom government announced further support for the development of a full-scale prototype of the SABRE engine,<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> providing \xc2\xa360M of funding between 2014-2016<sup id="cite_ref-UKgovinvestBBC_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UKgovinvestBBC-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> with the ESA providing an additional \xc2\xa37M.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> The total cost of developing a test rig is estimated at \xc2\xa3200M.<sup id="cite_ref-UKgovinvestBBC_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UKgovinvestBBC-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>By June 2015, SABRE\'s development continued with The Advanced Nozzle Project at Westcott. The test engine, operated by Airborne Engineering Ltd., is being used to analyze the <a href="/wiki/Aerodynamics" title="Aerodynamics">aerodynamics</a> and performance of the advanced nozzles that the SABRE engine will use, in addition to new manufacturing technologies such as the 3D-printed propellant injection system.<sup id="cite_ref-newsupdates_Nov_2015_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-newsupdates_Nov_2015-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In April 2015, the SABRE engine concept passed a theoretical feasibility review  conducted by the U.S. <a href="/wiki/Air_Force_Research_Laboratory" title="Air Force Research Laboratory">Air Force Research Laboratory</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> the lab will reveal two-stage-to-orbit SABRE concepts in the near future, they believe that a single-stage-to-orbit Skylon space plane is "technically very risky as a first application of SABRE engine ," and this is why they are developing two-stage-to-orbit concepts.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> In August 2015 the <a href="/wiki/European_Commission" title="European Commission">European Commission</a> competition authority approved UK government funding of \xc2\xa350 million for further development of the SABRE project. This was approved on the grounds that money raised from <a href="/wiki/Private_equity" title="Private equity">private equity</a> had been insufficient to bring the project to completion.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Then in October 2015, British company <a href="/wiki/BAE_Systems" title="BAE Systems">BAE Systems</a> agreed to buy a 20% stake in the company for <a href="/wiki/Pound_sign" title="Pound sign">\xc2\xa3</a>20.6 million as part of an agreement to help develop the SABRE hypersonic engine.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 2016 Reaction CEO Mark Thomas announced plans to build a quarter sized ground test engine, given limitations of funding.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In September 2016, agents acting on behalf of Reaction Engines applied for planning consent to build a rocket engine test facility at the site of the former <a href="/wiki/Rocket_Propulsion_Establishment" title="Rocket Propulsion Establishment">Rocket Propulsion Establishment</a> in Westcott, UK<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> which was granted in April 2017,<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> and in May 2017 a <a href="/wiki/Groundbreaking" title="Groundbreaking">groundbreaking ceremony</a> was held to announce the beginning of construction of the SABRE TF1 engine test facility, expected to become active in 2020.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Precooler">Precooler</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=SABRE_(rocket_engine)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Precooler">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>As the air enters the engine at <a href="/wiki/Supersonic" class="mw-redirect" title="Supersonic">supersonic</a>/<a href="/wiki/Hypersonic" class="mw-redirect" title="Hypersonic">hypersonic</a> speeds, it becomes very hot due to compression effects. The high temperatures are traditionally dealt with in <a href="/wiki/Jet_engines" class="mw-redirect" title="Jet engines">jet engines</a> by using heavy <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a> or <a href="/wiki/Nickel" title="Nickel">nickel</a>-based materials, by reducing the engine\'s <a href="/wiki/Overall_pressure_ratio" title="Overall pressure ratio">pressure ratio</a>, and by throttling back the engine at the higher airspeeds to avoid melting. However, for a <a href="/wiki/Single-stage-to-orbit" title="Single-stage-to-orbit">single-stage-to-orbit</a> (SSTO) spaceplane, such heavy materials are unusable, and maximum thrust is necessary for orbital insertion at the earliest time to minimise <a href="/wiki/Gravity_drag" title="Gravity drag">gravity losses</a>. Instead, using a gaseous <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a> coolant loop, SABRE dramatically cools the air from 1000&#160;\xc2\xb0C down to \xe2\x88\x92150&#160;\xc2\xb0C in a heat exchanger while avoiding <a href="/wiki/Liquid_air" title="Liquid air">liquefaction</a> of the air or blockage from freezing water vapour.\n</p><p>Previous versions of precoolers such as HOTOL put the hydrogen fuel directly through the precooler. SABRE inserts a helium cooling loop between the air and the cold fuel to avoid problems with <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_embrittlement" title="Hydrogen embrittlement">hydrogen embrittlement</a> in the precooler.\n</p><p>The dramatic cooling of the air created a potential problem: it is necessary to prevent blocking the precooler from frozen water vapour and other air fractions. On October 2012, the cooling solution was demonstrated for 6 minutes using freezing air.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> The cooler consists of a fine pipework heat exchanger and cools the hot in-rushing atmospheric air down to the required \xe2\x88\x92150&#160;\xc2\xb0C in 0.01s.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> The ice prevention system had been a closely guarded secret, but REL disclosed a <a href="/wiki/Methanol" title="Methanol">methanol</a>-injecting 3D-printed de-icer in 2015 through patents, as they needed partner companies and could not keep the secret while working closely with outsiders.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In September 2017 it was announced United States Defense Advanced Research projects (DARPA) had contracted with Reaction Engines Inc to build a high-temperature airflow test facility at Front Range Airport near Watkins, Colorado. The DARPA contract is to test the Sabre engine\xe2\x80\x99s precooler heat exchanger or HTX.  \n</p><p>The test work which is to be started in 2018 will focus on running the heat exchanger at temperatures simulating Mach 5 or around 980&#160;\xc2\xb0C (1,800&#160;\xc2\xb0F).<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>\nIn May 2018, the HTX test unit was being completed in the UK before shipping to Colorado in summer, where a <a href="/wiki/F-4" class="mw-redirect" title="F-4">F-4</a> GE <a href="/wiki/J79" class="mw-redirect" title="J79">J79</a> turbojet exhaust will be mixed with ambient air to replicate Mach 5 inlet conditions.\nA successful HTX test could lead to spin-off precooler applications which could be developed before a scalable SABRE demonstrator is completed; suggested uses are to expand <a href="/wiki/Gas_turbine" title="Gas turbine">gas turbines</a> capabilities, in advanced <a href="/wiki/Turbofan" title="Turbofan">turbofans</a>, hypersonic vehicles, and industrial applications.<sup id="cite_ref-AvWeek15May2018_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AvWeek15May2018-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Compressor">Compressor</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=SABRE_(rocket_engine)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Compressor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Below five times the <a href="/wiki/Speed_of_sound" title="Speed of sound">speed of sound</a> and 25 kilometres of altitude, which is 20% of the speed and 20% of the altitude needed to reach <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">orbit</a>, the cooled air from the precooler passes into a modified turbo-<a href="/wiki/Gas_compressor" class="mw-redirect" title="Gas compressor">compressor</a>, similar in design to those used on conventional jet engines but running at an unusually high <a href="/wiki/Overall_pressure_ratio" title="Overall pressure ratio">pressure ratio</a> made possible by the low temperature of the inlet air. The compressor feeds the compressed air at 140 <a href="/wiki/Atmospheres_(unit)" class="mw-redirect" title="Atmospheres (unit)">atmospheres</a> into the combustion chambers of the main engines.<sup id="cite_ref-how_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-how-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>The turbo-compressor is powered by a <a href="/wiki/Gas_turbine" title="Gas turbine">gas turbine</a> running on a helium loop, rather than by combustion gases as in a conventional jet engine. The turbo-compressor is powered by waste heat collected by the helium loop.<sup id="cite_ref-how_41-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-how-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Helium_loop">Helium loop</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=SABRE_(rocket_engine)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Helium loop">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>The \'hot\' helium from the air precooler is recycled by cooling it in a heat exchanger with the liquid hydrogen fuel. The loop forms a self-starting <a href="/wiki/Brayton_cycle" title="Brayton cycle">Brayton cycle</a> engine, cooling critical parts of the engine and powering turbines.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The heat passes from the air into the helium. This heat energy is used to power various parts of the engine and to vaporise hydrogen, which is then burnt in <a href="/wiki/Ramjet" title="Ramjet">ramjets</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-user_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-user-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Reactionengines.co.uk_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reactionengines.co.uk-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Engine">Engine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=SABRE_(rocket_engine)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Engine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Due to the static thrust capability of the hybrid rocket engine, the vehicle can <a href="/wiki/Takeoff" title="Takeoff">take off</a> under air-breathing mode, much like a conventional <a href="/wiki/Turbojet" title="Turbojet">turbojet</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-user_3-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-user-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> As the craft ascends and the outside air pressure drops, more and more air is passed into the compressor as the effectiveness of the ram compression drops. In this fashion the jets are able to operate to a much higher altitude than would normally be possible.\n</p><p>At Mach 5.5 the air-breathing system becomes inefficient and is powered down, replaced by the on-board stored oxygen which allows the engine to accelerate to orbital velocities (around Mach 25).<sup id="cite_ref-how_41-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-how-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Combustion_Chambers">Combustion Chambers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=SABRE_(rocket_engine)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Combustion Chambers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The combustion chambers in the SABRE engine are cooled by the oxidant (air/liquid oxygen) rather than by liquid hydrogen<sup id="cite_ref-uksa_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uksa-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> to further reduce the system\'s use of liquid hydrogen compared to <a href="/wiki/Air-fuel_ratio" class="mw-redirect" title="Air-fuel ratio">stoichiometric</a> systems.\n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nozzles">Nozzles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=SABRE_(rocket_engine)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Nozzles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The most efficient atmospheric pressure at which a conventional <a href="/wiki/Propelling_nozzle" title="Propelling nozzle">propelling nozzle</a> works is set by the <a href="/wiki/Geometry" title="Geometry">geometry</a> of the <a href="/wiki/De_Laval_nozzle" title="De Laval nozzle">nozzle bell</a>. While the geometry of the conventional bell remains static the <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure#Altitude_atmospheric_pressure_variation" title="Atmospheric pressure">atmospheric pressure changes with altitude</a> and therefore nozzles designed for high performance in the lower atmosphere lose efficiency as they reach higher altitudes. In traditional rockets this is overcome by using multiple stages designed for the atmospheric pressures they encounter. An SSTO engine must use a single set of nozzles. \n</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bypass_burners">Bypass burners</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=SABRE_(rocket_engine)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Bypass burners">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Avoiding <a href="/wiki/Liquid_air" title="Liquid air">liquefaction</a> improves the efficiency of the engine since less <a href="/wiki/Entropy" title="Entropy">entropy</a> is generated and therefore less liquid hydrogen is boiled off. However, simply cooling the air needs more liquid hydrogen than can be burnt in the engine core. The excess is expelled through a series of burners called "spill duct <a href="/wiki/Ramjet" title="Ramjet">ramjet</a> burners",<sup id="cite_ref-user_3-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-user-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Reactionengines.co.uk_42-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reactionengines.co.uk-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> that are arranged in a ring around the central core. These are fed air that bypasses the precooler. This bypass ramjet system is designed to reduce the negative effects of drag resulting from air that passes into the intakes but is not fed into the main rocket engine, rather than generating thrust. At low speeds the ratio of the volume of air entering the intake to the volume that the compressor can feed to the combustion chamber is at its highest, requiring the bypassed air to be accelerated to maintain efficiency at these low speeds. This distinguishes the system from a <a href="/wiki/Air_turborocket#Types" title="Air turborocket">turboramjet</a> where a turbine-cycle\'s exhaust is used to increase air-flow for the ramjet to become efficient enough to take over the role of primary propulsion.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Performance">Performance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=SABRE_(rocket_engine)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Performance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The designed <a href="/wiki/Thrust-to-weight_ratio" title="Thrust-to-weight ratio">thrust-to-weight ratio</a> of SABRE is fourteen compared to about five for conventional jet engines, and two for <a href="/wiki/Scramjet" title="Scramjet">scramjets</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-compare_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-compare-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> This high performance is a combination of the denser, cooled air, requiring less compression, and, more importantly, the low air temperatures permitting lighter alloys to be used in much of the engine. Overall performance is much better than the <a href="/wiki/RB545" class="mw-redirect" title="RB545">RB545</a> engine or scramjets.\n</p><p>Fuel efficiency (known as <a href="/wiki/Specific_impulse" title="Specific impulse">specific impulse</a> in rocket engines) peaks at about 3500&#160;<a href="/wiki/Specific_impulse#Units" title="Specific impulse">seconds</a> within the atmosphere.<sup id="cite_ref-user_3-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-user-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Typical all-rocket systems peak around 450 seconds and even "typical" <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_thermal_rocket" title="Nuclear thermal rocket">nuclear thermal rockets</a> at about 900&#160;seconds.\n</p><p>The combination of high fuel efficiency and low-mass engines permits an SSTO approach, with air-breathing to Mach 5.14+ at 28.5&#160;km (17.7&#160;mi) altitude, and with the vehicle reaching orbit with more payload mass per take-off mass than just about any non-<a href="/wiki/Nuclear_pulse_propulsion" title="Nuclear pulse propulsion">nuclear</a> launch vehicle ever proposed.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>\n</p><p>The precooler adds mass and complexity to the system, and is the most aggressive and difficult part of the design, but the mass of this heat exchanger is an order of magnitude lower than has been achieved previously. The experimental device achieved heat exchange of almost 1&#160;GW/m<sup>3</sup>. The losses from carrying the added weight of systems shut down during the closed cycle mode (namely the precooler and turbo-compressor) as well as the added weight of Skylon\'s wings are offset by the gains in overall efficiency and the proposed flight plan. Conventional launch vehicles such as the <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle" title="Space Shuttle">Space Shuttle</a> spend about one minute climbing almost vertically at relatively low speeds; this is inefficient, but optimal for pure-rocket vehicles. In contrast, the SABRE engine permits a much slower, shallower climb, breathing air and using its wings to support the vehicle therefore increasing <a href="/wiki/Payload_fraction" title="Payload fraction">payload fraction</a>.\n</p><p>A hybrid jet engine like SABRE needs only reach low <a href="/wiki/Hypersonic" class="mw-redirect" title="Hypersonic">hypersonic</a> speeds inside the <a href="/wiki/Stratosphere" title="Stratosphere">lower atmosphere</a> before engaging its closed cycle mode, whilst climbing, to build speed. Unlike <a href="/wiki/Ramjet" title="Ramjet">ramjet</a> or <a href="/wiki/Scramjet" title="Scramjet">scramjet</a> engines, the design is able to provide high thrust from zero speed up to Mach 5.5,<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> with excellent thrust over the entire flight, from the ground to very high altitude, with high efficiency throughout. In addition, this static thrust capability means the engine can be realistically tested on the ground, which drastically cuts testing costs.<sup id="cite_ref-compare_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-compare-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>\n</p><p>In 2012, REL expected test flights by 2020, and operational flights by 2030.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Resources">Resources</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=SABRE_(rocket_engine)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Resources">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul><li><cite class="citation web"><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20110615104534/http://www.reactionengines.co.uk/tech_docs/JBIS_v57_22-32.pdf">"The Skylon Space plane"</a> <span class="cs1-format">(PDF)</span>. <a href="/wiki/Reaction_Engines_Limited" title="Reaction Engines Limited">Reaction Engines Limited</a>.
